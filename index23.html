A Note to Our Readers

Publishing Cadence Change

Starting with this issue, Marine Intelligence Weekly is shifting to a flexible cadence.

The weekly format has served us well through 23 issues. But the maritime regulatory and technology landscape does not move at a fixed weekly pace. Some weeks produce five publishable developments. Other weeks produce one.

From Issue 23 onward:

  • Standard cadence: Bi-weekly (every two weeks)
  • When significant events demand it — an IMO session outcome, a major IACS update, a class advisory that changes operational practice — we will publish a single-focus article between regular issues, outside the scheduled cycle.

What does not change: editorial rigour, practitioner-first framing, and the standard that every item in MIW must be worth a marine professional's time.

If you have been following this work from the beginning, thank you. If this is your first issue, there is a growing archive behind you worth exploring.

— Nixon Antony, Editor

Feature Article

Hydrogen Safety at Sea: What the Engineer Actually Needs to Know

Two documents landed in the same week. One from DNV defining crew competence requirements for hydrogen systems. The other from DNV and EMSA jointly, after a multi-year study, concluding that hydrogen-fuelled ships need a fundamentally different safety philosophy — design-based, not procedure-based.

Neither is mandatory. Both will shape what surveyors look for.

Hydrogen Safety: Two Documents, One Framework — DNV-RP-0703 and the EMSA/DNV Study

The competence gap is the primary risk

DNV-RP-0703 makes an uncomfortable observation: hydrogen introduces failure modes that experienced LNG or conventional fuel engineers have not encountered. The ignition energy threshold for hydrogen is approximately 0.017 mJ — one-tenth of methane, less than one-hundredth of diesel vapour. A hydrogen leak forms an ignitable cloud from a release that may have no visible sign.

The RP requires system-specific, demonstrated competence in operation, maintenance, and emergency response — not general fuel familiarity. It will be referenced by flag administrations and class societies as the benchmark for what "adequate" looks like during SOLAS Chapter I surveys and vetting inspections.

For MEO Class 1: STCW sets the floor. Class and flag-endorsed RPs are increasingly setting the operational standard above it. That is the regulatory architecture worth understanding.

Secondary enclosures are the design answer

The EMSA/DNV study concludes that human response time is inadequate as a primary safety strategy for hydrogen release. Automated protection — gas detection, ventilation interlocks, ESD valves, isolation of non-Ex equipment — must be the first line, not a backup.

The specific recommendation: secondary enclosures for all hydrogen-carrying components, including on open deck. Engineers receiving hydrogen-ready vessels should treat secondary enclosure integrity as a critical maintenance item from day one, not a design-phase detail.

Hydrogen Release — Safety Response Sequence. Automated barriers act before crew response begins.

What engineers on conventional vessels should do now

The global hydrogen-capable orderbook is under 0.5% of total fleet capacity. But RP-0703 is already informing how DNV approaches ammonia competence documentation. The competence architecture being built for hydrogen will apply across alternative fuel types.

If hydrogen or ammonia systems are in your company's pipeline: request the RP, map it against your SMS, and do not wait for the ISM audit.

What to Watch Next — Hydrogen

  • IGF Code amendments extending to hydrogen
  • Class rule updates codifying secondary enclosure requirements
  • Flag state training requirements referencing RP-0703 as the competence baseline

Sources: DNV-RP-0703  ·  EMSA/DNV Hydrogen Safety Study

Regulatory Pulse

What Changed, What's Coming

1. SOLAS II-1/3-13 — Anchor Handling Winches

Mandatory · In Force

New requirements entered into force 1 January 2026. For winches installed on or after that date: mandatory design review, material verification, fabrication survey, and overload testing at 110% brake holding capacity and 110% maximum line pull. For existing winches: compliance required by the first Cargo Ship Safety Construction Renewal Survey on or after 1 January 2026.

Maintenance records and manufacturer manuals must be on board. If manuals are missing, reconstruction and flag approval are required before survey.

Why it matters: The underlying principle — documented brake capacity, verified overload tolerance, tested alarms and emergency stops from all control locations — applies by analogy to all critical mooring and deck machinery. Surveyors are applying the same scrutiny to documentation that they previously reserved for propulsion machinery.

Source: IMO Res. MSC.532(107) · LR Class News

2. DNV-RP-0703 — Hydrogen Competence

Recommended

Defines structured competence requirements for crew, inspectors, and technical personnel on hydrogen-fuelled ships. Supplements STCW and ISM. Expected to serve as the reference framework for future audits, SIRE-2.0-style inspections, and class/flag verifications.

Source: DNV Advisory

3. LR / Alan Turing Institute — MASS Safety Framework

In Development

Open-source systems-engineering framework covering full MASS lifecycle: design, architecture, modelling, ML DevOps, integration, testing, and sustainment. Will influence automation standards, alarm management philosophy, HMI design, override controls, and fail-safe state requirements — for all ships, not only autonomous.

What to Watch Next — MASS

  • MASS Code implementation guidance from flag states
  • Remote operator competence standards
  • Cybersecurity assurance requirements for automated control systems

Source: Lloyd's Register

4. IMO Net-Zero Framework — Delayed

Deferred

Adoption deferred from October 2025 session. Next key date: October 2026 continuation; possible entry into force no earlier than 1 March 2028. The framework will drive well-to-wake fuel intensity compliance and pricing exposure for vessels above 5,000 GT. Operational direction unchanged — only the timeline has shifted.

Source: DNV

5. IMO SSE 12 — Battery Storage, NEV/Containership Fire Safety

In Development

Sub-committee progressed interim guidelines for battery energy storage systems and strengthened fire detection/control for containerships and roll-on/roll-off (ro-ro) cargo ships carrying new-energy vehicles. Increased survey scrutiny expected on battery thermal-runaway response procedures and emergency isolation logic.

Computer vision and deep learning for port automation, cargo monitoring, and visual document intelligence. Represented at India-EU Business Forum, early 2026. Technical work on edge computing in port automation published May 29, 2026. Supports ISPS Code compliance through automated container tracking at terminal gates.

OceanAI / MariApps — Singapore/Kochi

FaultSenseAI for engine failure prediction integrated into the smartPAL ERP. IntelliCrew for STCW crew matrix optimisation. Highlighted at India AI Impact Summit 2026.

The common thread: All three platforms have moved away from dashboards that display data toward tools that recommend operational actions. The focus is no longer presenting information to engineers — it is generating decisions for engineers to verify. That shift has direct implications for how the ISM Code treats AI-generated alerts, and how class will eventually formalise the human oversight requirement. MIW covered the ISM dimension in Issue 21.

MEO Class 1 note: AI predictive alerts do not authorise deferral of statutory overhauls — CBM (Condition-Based Maintenance) notation approval from class is required regardless of what the AI recommends.

Ecosystem: Maritime India Foundation signed MoU with IIT Bombay on 5 May 2026 to establish a Maritime Innovation Hub under the Sagarmala Startup initiative.

Regulatory context: IACS UR E26 and E27 (mandatory for new builds from 2024) require AI network segmentation and type-approval for AI sensor gateways in machinery spaces. Verify class type-approval for any new AI edge devices before installation.

Fuel & Decarbonisation Watch

Alternative Fuel Reality Check — 2026

Source: DNV Alternative Fuels Insight 2026, LR Maritime Forecast 2050

LNG

LR's updated outlook places LNG as the most cost-effective alternative fuel pathway through 2050. Dual-fuel LNG fleet at 1,665 vessels, 982 on order. High-pressure two-stroke engines achieving methane slip as low as 0.2 g/kWh. Focus: engine tuning and methane-slip verification, not fuel switching.

LPG

Immediately deployable — mature technology, global storage infrastructure, EEXI/CII/EU ETS/FuelEU Maritime compatible. Lower-risk option for owners who cannot wait for maturing bunkering ecosystems.

Hydrogen

Sub-0.5% of global orderbook. Infrastructure investment still insufficient for bulk fleet adoption. Safety zoning, ventilation, and secondary containment are the engineering priority for any hydrogen design work.

EU Obligations

FuelEU Maritime in force with well-to-wake reporting requirements. Shore-power compliance windows will tighten. Bunker documentation, voyage records, and fuel quality verification are now as important as propulsion hardware selection.

Voice from the Deck

Three Themes from This Week

Communication failures remain a larger operational risk than technical failures. A superintendent described the job as primarily about translating problems across teams and managing assumptions — not fixing machinery. The most costly mistakes in recent work were coordination failures, not engineering ones.
Trust is still the primary currency of a junior engineer. A marine engineer reflecting on early contracts described the first year as largely about earning the right to be given responsibility — not demonstrating technical knowledge. Competence is demonstrated through reliability on basic tasks, not through unsolicited expertise.
Shore transition preparation has to start years before the final contract. The seafarer who begins translating sea experience into shore language only after signing off for the last time is already behind. The translation takes time. The network takes longer.

"Shipping may have mastered vessel data, but much of what happens onboard is still not visible in a structured way." That gap — between what the system records and what actually occurred — is the problem MIW was built to address.

Oral Exam Corner — MEO Class 1

One question. Sourced. Examiner-relevant.

A vessel's anchor handling winch was installed in 2024. The ship is due for its first Cargo Ship Safety Construction Renewal Survey in March 2027. What are the SOLAS requirements applicable to this winch, and what documentation must the Chief Engineer ensure is on board?

Framework Answer

Under SOLAS Reg. II-1/3-13, as amended by IMO Resolution MSC.532(107) (in force 1 January 2026), separate compliance timelines apply to new and existing installations. For winches installed on or after 1 January 2026: mandatory design review, material verification, fabrication survey, and overload testing at 110% of brake holding capacity and 110% of maximum line pull, plus full onboard functional testing before entering service.

Because this winch was installed in 2024, it is classed as an existing installation. It must therefore comply no later than the first Cargo Ship Safety Construction Renewal Survey on or after 1 January 2026 — making the March 2027 survey the hard compliance deadline.

For that Renewal Survey, the Chief Engineer must ensure:

  1. Documented test records confirming overload testing was completed in accordance with IMO/class requirements
  2. The manufacturer's operation and maintenance manual is on board
  3. The winch is included in the PMS with recorded inspections of foundations, welds, deck structure, hydraulic systems, and control stations
  4. All emergency stops and alarms have been tested from every control location with results recorded

If any manufacturer documentation is missing, reconstruction and flag-state approval are required before survey — not during it.

Source: IMO Res. MSC.532(107) · LR Class News
