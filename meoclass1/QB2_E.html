Lifeboat and rescue boat — location, and why is the rescue boat often positioned on the starboard side?
LSASOLASCOLREG (context)
15-Second Answer: Under SOLAS Chapter III, a rescue boat must be kept ready for direct boarding, launching, and recovery from a position suitable for that purpose. The regulation is functional — readiness and safe recovery — not side-specific; many merchant ships use the starboard boat as the rescue boat for drill standardisation and faster MOB response.
60-Second Answer: A rescue boat is carried under SOLAS Chapter III for man-overboard recovery and liferaft marshalling, with arrangements for direct boarding and launching from the stowed position, and recovery within about 5 minutes in moderate sea conditions at up to 5 knots ship speed. SOLAS does not open by mandating "starboard side" — many ships place the rescue boat on starboard as Boat No.1 for fixed drill routine, bridge-deck coordination, and operational convenience; that is shipboard arrangement, not statutory placement.

1. Governing Basis

The governing framework is SOLAS Chapter III. For cargo ships (including this vessel type), rescue boat carriage is under Regulation 31 — Reg 21 is the passenger-ship carriage provision (one rescue boat each side, ≥500 GT) and does not apply here. Stowage in continuous readiness is under Regulation 14, and embarkation/launching/recovery arrangements under Regulation 17, all read alongside the LSA Code section 5.1 (construction/performance) and section 6.1.1.11 (launching appliance trim/list capability). A rescue boat is a davit-launched, motor-propelled boat used for man-overboard retrieval and liferaft marshalling duties.

2. What the Regulations Actually Require

The regulatory emphasis is that the rescue boat is in continuous readiness, can be boarded and launched directly from its stowed position, and can be recovered promptly — not "which side." Recovery is expected within not more than 5 minutes in moderate sea conditions, with the ship making up to 5 knots.

  • Length: LSA Code specifies rescue boats between 3.8 m and 8.5 m.
  • A lifeboat may also be designated as the rescue boat if it independently meets rescue-boat requirements.

3. Lifeboat and Rescue Boat Location

On cargo ships, lifeboats are normally arranged port and starboard, while one boat (or a dedicated craft) may serve as the rescue boat if it complies with rescue-boat requirements. The exact side is a matter of the ship's approved arrangement and safe operational design — SOLAS does not prescribe a universal side.

4. Why Many Ships Keep the Rescue Boat on Starboard

On many merchant ships, the starboard boat is commonly designated Boat No.1 and used as the rescue boat — giving one fixed, familiar side for crew mustering, drill planning, and bridge communication during a man-overboard situation. Starboard is also a heavily standardised manoeuvring side in navigation practice (head-on vessels alter to starboard under COLREG Rule 14; the give-way vessel in a crossing situation under COLREG Rule 15), but this is operational context, not a SOLAS rescue-boat placement rule.

5. Oral Exam Line

"SOLAS requires the rescue boat to be in continuous readiness and suitably stowed for quick launch and recovery. Starboard side is commonly the designated rescue-boat side on many ships, but I would treat that as shipboard arrangement unless quoting the vessel's approved plan."

CE Oral Tip (Simon): Open with the regulation-principle answer, not a seamanship generalisation: "Rescue boat must be in continuous readiness, suitably stowed, and capable of quick launch and recovery." Then add that starboard is a common arrangement on many ships, not the first statutory point. The common failure is stating an absolute "SOLAS says starboard" without separating regulation from practice.
DEEP-DIVE — CE Relevance · Traps · Failures · Numbers · Casualty Link · Examiner Chain · On My Vessel
CE Relevance

Rescue-boat location is only meaningful if the assigned boat is immediately operable: engine starts reliably, battery/charging is sound, winch/davit is serviceable, and the launch/recovery zone is unobstructed. CE ownership shows up in oral through maintenance records, defect reporting before drills or PSC attendance, and readiness verification.

Trap Questions

Q: Is starboard side compulsory by SOLAS? A: Not as a universal mandate — SOLAS Reg 31 (cargo ships) requires suitable stowage, readiness, and quick launch/recovery; starboard is a common shipboard arrangement, not a statutory placement rule. (Reg 21 is the passenger-ship carriage rule and requires one rescue boat on EACH side — don't confuse the two when questioned.)

Q: Can a lifeboat also act as a rescue boat? A: Yes, provided it independently complies with the rescue-boat requirements.

Q: What is the performance figure you must quote? A: Recovery within 5 minutes in moderate sea conditions.

Common CE Failures

Mixing up lifeboat location with the rescue-boat functional requirement; stating "starboard because SOLAS says so" without legal basis; forgetting to mention launch and recovery; not mentioning direct boarding from the stowed position; ignoring the ship-specific approved arrangement and SMS wording.

Numbers to Memorise
  • 3.8 m – 8.5 m rescue-boat length range
  • Recovery within 5 minutes, moderate sea, ship making up to 5 kt
  • Regulation 21 — carriage; Regulation 17 — embarkation/launch/recovery
Casualty Link

Industry safety flashes (e.g. IMCA) on rescue-boat damage during lowering highlight that rescue-boat safety is about proper preparation, load balance, and command-and-control during launch and recovery — not simply which side the boat sits on.

Examiner Chain

Likely sequence: "Where is the rescue boat located?" → "Why starboard?" → "Show me the regulation" → "Is starboard mandatory?" → "Can a lifeboat be a rescue boat?" → "What is the CE's responsibility before launch?" Prepare to answer in that order: regulation, functional requirement, practical reason, ship-specific arrangement, CE readiness.

On My Vessel (Maersk A/S)

On a container vessel, answer from the approved LSA arrangement plan — identify the designated rescue boat, its launching appliance, and boarding/launch/recovery procedure. If the starboard boat is assigned, note the fixed drill side and bridge-deck coordination benefit, then close with CE ownership: engine readiness, battery charging, release-gear condition, davit/winch availability, and a clear, unobstructed launch/recovery zone.
