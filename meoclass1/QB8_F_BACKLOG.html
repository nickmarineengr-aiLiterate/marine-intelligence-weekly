1
LPG (as an alternative marine fuel) is a hot topic now. Have you experienced any issues onboard?
GHGMARPOL

15-Second Answer

My vessel likely isn't LPG-fuelled, so I'd say that honestly, then explain that LPG-fuel systems fall under the IGF Code, require STCW V/3 gas-fuel training, and that LPG vapour (propane ~1.5, butane ~2.0 relative to air) is heavier than air — unlike LNG/methane (~0.55) — which drives low-level gas-detection design.

60-Second Answer

I'd first clarify my own vessel's actual fuel type rather than claim experience I don't have. LPG is gaining attention as an alternative marine fuel because it has lower sulphur and particulate emissions than HFO and, unlike LNG, liquefies under moderate pressure at ambient temperature, simplifying storage. Industry-wide, LPG-fuelled ships fall under the IGF Code — LNG-specific in its core text, with LPG covered via the Alternative Design Approach — with mandatory STCW V/3 training before independent watchkeeping. The critical safety-design distinction is vapour density: propane is about 1.5 times heavier than air and butane about 2.0 times heavier, so a leak sinks and pools in low-lying enclosed spaces, requiring bilge-level gas detection — the opposite of LNG, where methane at about 0.55 relative density rises and disperses, so detection sits at deckhead level. Other considerations include material compatibility with seals and gaskets, boil-off gas management, and strict bunkering safety protocols with ESD links and exclusion zones. I'd present this as regulatory/technical knowledge rather than personal onboard experience unless my ship is actually gas-fuelled.

Context

LPG (propane/butane) is being adopted as an alternative marine fuel on some newbuild dual-fuel tonnage, driven by its lower sulphur/particulate emissions versus HFO and comparatively simpler storage (liquefies under moderate pressure, ambient temperature) versus LNG. On a standard container vessel running HFO/MGO/VLSFO, LPG-fuel exposure is more likely through cargo/bunkering awareness than day-to-day operation — answer honestly based on your vessel's actual fuel type if this comes up.

Vapour density — the key safety-design distinction

  • LPG: propane has a vapour density of approximately 1.5 relative to air, butane approximately 2.0 — both heavier than air. A leak sinks, pools, and accumulates in low-lying enclosed spaces, double bottoms, and bilge wells.
  • LNG (methane): vapour density approximately 0.55 relative to air — lighter than air, so a leak rises and disperses unless trapped by overhead structure.
  • Design consequence: LPG installations require bilge-level gas detection and low-level extraction/ventilation, the opposite design philosophy to LNG systems, which detect and vent at deckhead level.

Known operational issues associated with LPG as fuel (industry-wide, from class/OEM guidance)

  • Toxicity & asphyxiation risk: as above — gas detection and ventilation must be positioned low, not high, around fuel tanks/pump rooms.
  • Material compatibility: requires compatible seals/gaskets; LPG can affect certain elastomers differently from HFO systems.
  • Boil-off/pressure management: even though LPG is easier to handle than LNG, tank pressure and BOG (boil-off gas) management still needs monitoring during idle periods.
  • Bunkering safety: ship-to-ship/shore LPG bunkering requires ESD (emergency shutdown) links, dedicated PPE, and exclusion zones per the IGF Code.
  • Crew familiarisation: STCW-mandated gas-fuelled ship familiarisation training is required before independent watchkeeping on LPG dual-fuel plant.

Deep-Dive: Trap Questions

“Is LPG the same as LNG for fuel purposes?” — no: different storage conditions (LPG liquefies at moderate pressure/ambient temp; LNG requires cryogenic -162°C storage) AND opposite vapour density behaviour, driving different gas-detection system layouts (low-level for LPG, high-level for LNG).

Deep-Dive: Common CE Failures

Deep-Dive: Numbers to Memorise

Vapour density relative to air: propane ≈1.5, butane ≈2.0 (both heavier than air); methane (LNG) ≈0.55 (lighter than air). These are standard physical constants (molar mass ratios), safe to quote precisely.

Deep-Dive: Casualty Link

2
Describe the frame stripping and purging procedure (scavenge space fire response context).
MACHINERYFIRE

15-Second Answer

Stop the engine and engage turning gear, isolate scavenge air and drains, extinguish with the fixed fire-fighting system, cool the space without opening it, then strip out oil/sludge and purge before re-entry — the golden rule is never open the doors early, since that feeds oxygen to the fire.

60-Second Answer

On a scavenge fire, the sequence is: stop the main engine immediately and engage turning gear to keep even heat distribution, isolate by stopping scavenge blowers and closing drain valves to cut off the air feeding the fire, then apply the fixed extinguishing system (CO2, steam, or water mist) and boundary cooling to the casing from outside without opening the doors — opening early introduces oxygen and risks flashback or explosion. Only once the fire is confirmed extinguished and the casing has cooled does stripping begin: removing accumulated oil, sludge and carbon deposits from the scavenge space, piston rod glands and stuffing box, which removes the fuel source for re-ignition. The space is then purged and ventilated, with atmosphere testing per enclosed-space entry procedures before any hot work or full re-entry. Finally, the piston rod gland and scavenge drain non-return valves are inspected and the root cause of oil leakage addressed before restart — I'd reference the specific engine builder's checklist, such as MAN ES or WinGD guidance, for the exact confirmed safe-to-open condition rather than quoting a generic numeric threshold.

Context

"Frame" here refers to the scavenge air space/piston-rod frame area of a 2-stroke crosshead main engine. Stripping and purging is the recovery sequence after a scavenge fire or when opening the space for inspection — the goal is to safely remove residual oil/sludge (fuel for re-ignition) and inert/ventilate the space before re-entry or restart.

Sequenced procedure

  1. Stop the engine immediately on scavenge fire alarm/indication; stop fuel to the affected unit(s), engage and maintain turning gear once stopped to keep even heat distribution and avoid shaft distortion.
  2. Isolate: stop scavenge air/auxiliary blowers, close blower flaps and scavenge drain valves to prevent air feeding the fire.
  3. Extinguish: apply the fixed fire-fighting system for the scavenge space (CO2, steam, or water mist depending on the installation) rather than opening doors to fight the fire directly.
  4. Boundary cooling & wait: apply external cooling to the casing if accessible, and do not open scavenge space doors until the fire is confirmed fully extinguished and the casing has cooled — opening early introduces oxygen and risks flashback/explosion. Do not rely on a specific universal temperature figure; follow your engine builder's (MAN ES/WinGD) checklist for the confirmed safe-to-open condition.
  5. Stripping: once cooled, open drains/doors carefully, remove accumulated oil, sludge, and carbon deposits from the scavenge space, piston rod glands, and stuffing box area — this removes the fuel source for re-ignition.
  6. Purging/ventilation: ventilate the space to clear residual fumes/oil mist before hot work or re-entry, following enclosed-space entry precautions (gas-free certificate, atmosphere testing).
  7. Inspection: check piston rod gland tightness, scavenge drain non-return valves, and root-cause the oil leakage source (worn rings, gland leakage) before restart.

Deep-Dive: CE Relevance

CE's PMS ensures scavenge drains are regularly checked/cleaned and piston rod gland packing condition is monitored to prevent the oil accumulation that causes scavenge fires in the first place.

Deep-Dive: Trap Questions

“Why not open the scavenge doors immediately to fight the fire directly?” — because introducing air/oxygen intensifies the fire; correct response is stop/isolate/extinguish/cool first, open only once the fire is confirmed out and the casing has cooled.

Deep-Dive: Common CE Failures

Skipping the cooling/wait period and jumping straight to 'open and clean' — this is the exact sequencing error examiners probe for, since it risks a scavenge space explosion on reignition. Also: quoting a precise universal reopening temperature (e.g. a specific °C figure) as if it's a fixed regulatory number — it isn't; it's engine-builder-specific.

Deep-Dive: Numbers to Memorise

No single universal numeric threshold for safe reopening — always reference the specific engine manufacturer's (MAN ES/WinGD) manual for permissible temperature/wait-time; state this as vessel/engine-type specific rather than inventing a figure.

Deep-Dive: Casualty Link

Scavenge fires remain a recurring engine-room casualty type in class/flag investigation reports, typically traced to piston rod gland oil leakage combined with hot spots from blow-by — root-cause analysis after every event is standard practice.

Deep-Dive: On My Vessel (Maersk A/S, container)

On a Maersk container vessel's 2-stroke main engine, this procedure would be run per the engine builder's (e.g., MAN ES/WinGD) scavenge fire checklist posted in the ECR — know where that checklist is physically located as an oral-exam-ready detail.
