What are e-fuels, and how do they relate to shipping's net-zero target?
GHG

15-Second Answer

E-fuels are synthetic fuels made from green hydrogen plus captured carbon or nitrogen using renewable electricity — central to IMO's 2050 net-zero target, though the enforcement mechanism (the Net-Zero Framework) had its adoption postponed and won't be voted on again until October 2026.

60-Second Answer

E-fuels, or electrofuels, are produced by combining green hydrogen — generated via electrolysis using renewable electricity — with a carbon or nitrogen source to make e-methanol, e-ammonia, e-diesel or e-hydrogen. They matter because the IMO's 2023 Revised GHG Strategy targets net-zero shipping emissions by or around 2050, with indicative checkpoints of at least 20% (striving 30%) reduction by 2030 and 70% (striving 80%) by 2040 relative to 2008. The enforcement mechanism is the IMO Net-Zero Framework — a global fuel GHG-intensity standard (GFI, well-to-wake, measured against a 93.3 gCO2eq/MJ 2008 baseline) plus a two-tier pricing/reward mechanism — approved in principle at MEPC 83 in April 2025. Its formal adoption was postponed at the October 2025 extraordinary MEPC session by a 57-49 vote, and the reconvened session is specifically scheduled for October 2026. If adopted then, earliest entry into force is around March 2028. E-fuels remain the intended long-term compliance pathway once the framework is adopted, but I'd flag this adoption timeline explicitly rather than quote a settled compliance date.

Definition

E-fuels (electrofuels) are synthetic fuels produced using renewable electricity: green hydrogen (from electrolysis of water using renewable power) is combined with a carbon or nitrogen source to produce liquid/gaseous fuels — e-methanol, e-ammonia, e-diesel/e-LNG, e-hydrogen itself.

Why they matter for shipping's GHG strategy

  • IMO 2023 GHG Strategy targets net-zero GHG emissions from international shipping by or around 2050, with indicative checkpoints of at least 20% (striving 30%) reduction by 2030 and 70% (striving 80%) by 2040, relative to a 2008 baseline (reference value 93.3 gCO2eq/MJ).
  • e-fuels are central to the "well-to-wake" (lifecycle) GHG intensity approach IMO is adopting — unlike fossil LNG/methanol, e-fuels can approach near-zero lifecycle carbon intensity if produced from genuinely renewable electricity.

Current regulatory status — verify before your exam

The enforcement mechanism is the IMO Net-Zero Framework: a global fuel GHG-intensity standard (GFI) with two compliance tiers, plus a pricing/reward mechanism funding an IMO Net-Zero Fund. It was approved in principle at MEPC 83 (April 2025) and was due for formal adoption at an extraordinary MEPC session in October 2025. That vote did not happen — member states voted 57-49 to adjourn the decision, following strong opposition led by the US. The reconvened session is scheduled for October 2026. If the Framework is adopted then, the earliest realistic entry into force is around March 2028, accounting for the mandatory MARPOL tacit-acceptance period. Technical guideline work (fuel certification, lifecycle assessment methodology, the Net-Zero Fund design) continued through MEPC 84 (April/May 2026) in the interim.

Practical challenges for e-fuels specifically

High production cost (multiple electrolysis + synthesis steps mean significant energy losses versus using electricity directly), limited current production scale, and bunkering infrastructure not yet widespread.

Deep-Dive: CE Relevance

CE evaluates fuel-flexibility of the plant (dual-fuel readiness, fuel changeover procedures) as the Company plans transition fuels — but with NZF adoption timing now uncertain until at least October 2026, near-term compliance planning has become harder to pin down.

Deep-Dive: Trap Questions

“Is e-fuel the same as bio-fuel?” — no: bio-fuels derive from biomass feedstock; e-fuels are synthesised from green hydrogen + captured carbon/nitrogen using renewable electricity. Also: “When does the GFI become mandatory?” — correct answer is “not yet adopted; reconvened vote scheduled October 2026, earliest entry into force ~March 2028 if adopted then.”

Deep-Dive: Common CE Failures

Quoting exact GFI compliance numbers/dates from memory without flagging the postponement and the specific October 2026 reconvene date — this is now the single most likely way to look out of date in this topic area.
