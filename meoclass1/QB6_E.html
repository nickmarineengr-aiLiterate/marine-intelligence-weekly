Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) Explanation
MARPOL Annex VIGHG EmissionsCII
15-Second Answer:

The Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) is an operational efficiency metric mandated by IMO under MARPOL Annex VI that measures how efficiently a ship transports goods in terms of grams of CO2 emitted per cargo-carrying capacity and nautical mile. It applies to ships of 5,000 GT and above, providing an annual rating from A to E. Continuous poor ratings (D for three consecutive years or E for one year) require an audited corrective action plan within the SEEMP Part III.

60-Second Answer:

CII assesses a vessel's operational carbon intensity annually using the Attained CII formula, which divides the total annual mass of CO2 emitted by the vessel's deadweight (or gross tonnage) multiplied by the total distance sailed in a calendar year. This attained value is compared against a Required CII baseline that sharpens every year up to 2030. Based on this comparison, the vessel is assigned a performance rating from A (major overperformer) to E (severe underperformer). As Chief Engineer, keeping the vessel out of the D and E bands involves tight control over fuel quality, optimizing main engine SFC, maintaining hull cleanliness, and coordinating with charterers for optimal routing and speed.

Overview of the Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII)

Introduced under MARPOL Annex VI (Regulation 26, 27, and 28), the Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) is a operational tool designed to drive down greenhouse gas emissions from international shipping. Unlike design indexes like EEDI or EEXI, which evaluate a vessel's theoretical technical efficiency, CII is a continuous operational metric reflecting real-time commercial and technical management choices over a calendar year.

Mathematical Formulation (Attained CII)

The Attained CII is calculated annually using the following basic equation:

Attained CII = frac{Σj (FC_j × CFj)}{Capacity × Distance}

Where:

  • FC_j: Mass of fuel consumption for fuel type j consumed in the calendar year (metric tonnes).
  • CFj: CO2 carbon content conversion factor for fuel type j (e.g., 3.114 for LSMGO, 3.206 for HFO).
  • Capacity: Deadweight Tonnage (DWT) for container vessels and bulkers, or Gross Tonnage (GT) for cruise/passenger vessels.
  • Distance: The actual distance traveled over the ground in nautical miles.

The CII Rating Mechanism (A to E)

Every year, the Attained CII is mapped against the Required CII for that specific ship type and size. The boundaries create five compliance bands:

  • A: Major superior performance level.
  • B: Minor superior performance level.
  • C: Standard/Acceptable performance level.
  • D: Minor inferior performance level.
  • E: Superiorly inferior/unacceptable performance level.

[ A (Superior) ] [ B ] [ C (Target) ] [ D ] [ E (Inferior) ]

Regulatory Consequences of Low Ratings

If a vessel hits a D rating for 3 consecutive years or an E rating for a single year:

  1. The Ship Energy Efficiency Management Plan (SEEMP) Part III must be revised.
  2. A structured Correction Action Plan must be developed and integrated into the SEEMP.
  3. The plan must outline exactly how the vessel will achieve a 'C' rating or better.
  4. The revised SEEMP must be verified and approved by the Administration/Recognized Organization (RO) before a Statement of Compliance (SoC) can be reissued.

Chief Engineer Relevance

On a Maersk A/S container vessel, the Chief Engineer directly influences the numerator of the CII equation via fuel consumption optimization. Key operational levers include:

  • Main Engine Optimization: Monitoring specific fuel consumption (SFC), alpha lubrication rates, and scavenging efficiency.
  • Auxiliary Load Management: Utilizing variable frequency drives (VFDs) on seawater cooling pumps and optimizing reefer container power distribution.
  • Hull Condition: Tracking slip changes and torque increases to prompt underwater hull cleaning/propeller polishing.
  • Boiler Management: Maximizing exhaust gas economizer (EGE) utilization to eliminate auxiliary boiler firing at sea.

Greenhouse Gas Fuel Intensity (GFI) Calculation & Unit
FuelEU MaritimeGHG BasicsAlternative Fuels
15-Second Answer:

Greenhouse Gas Fuel Intensity (GFI) is the core regulatory metric under the FuelEU Maritime framework (and proposed IMO mid-term measures) used to limit the well-to-wake GHG emissions of energy used on board. It is strictly measured in grams of CO2 equivalent per megajoule of energy (gCO2eq/MJ). It mandates a progressive drop in fuel carbon intensity compared to a 2020 baseline.

60-Second Answer:

GFI assesses the entire lifecycle emission footprint of marine energy on a Well-to-Wake (WtW) basis, combining both Well-to-Tank (production/transport) and Tank-to-Wake (onboard combustion) factors. The unit of calculation is gCO2eq/MJ. The calculation aggregates the mass of each fuel type consumed, multiplies it by its respective Well-to-Wake greenhouse gas emissions factor (including CO2, CH4, and N2O adjusted by their Global Warming Potential over 100 years), and divides this by the total energy content delivered in Megajoules. As Chief Engineer, compliance requires deep familiarity with Bunker Delivery Notes (BDN) containing certified sustainability data and managing alternative fuel blends.

Definition & Framework

Greenhouse Gas Fuel Intensity (GFI) shifting focus from simple vessel efficiency to the cleanliness of the fuel energy source itself. Championed under the EU Maritime regulations and heavily integrated into current IMO mid-term market-based measures, GFI penalizes high-carbon fossil fuels while providing compliance credits for renewable fuels of non-biological origin (RFNBOs) and biofuels.

The Unit of Measurement

The statutory unit for GFI calculation is:

mathbf{gCO2eq / MJ}

(Grams of Carbon Dioxide Equivalent per Megajoule of energy content).

Comprehensive Calculation Formula

The GFI is calculated on a Well-to-Wake (WtW) basis using the following equation:

Attained GFI = frac{Σi ( M_i × WtWGHG,i )}{Σi ( M_i × LCV_i )}

Where:

  • M_i: The mass of fuel type i consumed on board within the regulated zone (metric tonnes).
  • LCV_i: Lower Calorific Value of fuel type i, expressed in Megajoules per gram (MJ/g) or Megajoules per metric ton (MJ/t).
  • WtWGHG,i: The total Well-to-Wake Greenhouse Gas emission factor for fuel i.

The WtWGHG,i is further broken down to account for the entire lifecycle and multiple greenhouse gases:

WtWGHG,i = ( WtTCO2eq,i + TtWCO2eq,i )

TtWCO2eq,i = ( CF,CO2,i × 1 ) + ( CF,CH4,i × GWPCH4 ) + ( CF,N2O,i × GWPN2O ) + Slip Emissions

Where:

  • WtT: Well-to-Tank emissions (upstream sourcing, refining, transportation).
  • TtW: Tank-to-Wake emissions (onboard operations and burning).
  • GWP: Global Warming Potential over a 100-year horizon (GWPCH4 = 28, GWPN2O = 265).
  • Slip Emissions: Unburnt fuel escaping into the atmosphere (especially relevant as methane slip in LNG dual-fuel engines).

Chief Engineer Relevance

For a Chief Engineer on a modern container ship, GFI moves compliance parameters past mechanical adjustments into chemical accountability.

  • Bunker Management: C/Es must verify that the Bunker Delivery Note (BDN) is accompanied by a valid sustainability certificate (e.g., ISCC) specifying the feedstock tracking if biofuel is blended.
  • Methane Slip Mitigation: For dual-fuel engines, operational parameters (such as maintaining optimal combustion temperatures and managing pilot fuel injection) must be monitored closely to reduce unburnt methane slip, which drastically spikes the gCO2eq/MJ profile due to methane's high GWP.
⚖ Regulatory References
FuelEU Maritime Regulation (EU) 2023/1805: Establishing mandatory limits on the greenhouse gas intensity of energy used on board ships.
IMO MEPC Guidelines on Life Cycle GHG Intensity of Marine Fuels (LCA Guidelines): Comprehensive framework for calculating WtW emission factors.
ISO 8217 (Latest revisions): Incorporating specifications for biofuel blends (FAME) and alternative fuel profiles.
Class Society Rules (e.g., DNV / ABS): Rules for fuel verification, greenhouse gas data logging, and energy storage system accounting.

Deep-Dive: Traps, Failures, Numbers, Casualty Link, On My Vessel
Trap Questions: “Why can two ships burning the exact same amount of standard VLSFO have different fuel intensity values?” Answer: Under a true GFI lifecycle assessment, if one ship utilizes a certified biofuel blend or uses an onshore power supply (OPS) while at berth, its aggregate energy footprint drops in gCO2eq/MJ compared to a vessel operating solely on fossil fuels. “What role does engine type play in GFI calculation if the fuel is identical?” Answer: The Tank-to-Wake calculation accounts for specific engine type emission factors. For example, an Otto-cycle dual-fuel medium-speed engine has a higher regulatory methane slip factor than a Diesel-cycle high-pressure dual-fuel engine, resulting in a higher (worse) attained GFI for the same LNG fuel consumption.

GFI Calculation and Unit (Duplicate/Deepened Focus)
FuelEU MaritimeGHG BasicsLCA Guidelines
15-Second Answer:

The Greenhouse Gas Fuel Intensity (GFI) of a vessel is calculated as the weighted average of the well-to-wake greenhouse gas footprint of every unit of energy consumed on board. The unit is strictly gCO2eq/MJ. If the examiner is asking from an electrical systems perspective, a Ground Fault Indicator (GFI) calculates leakage current in milliamperes (mA) with standard safety trip values set at 30mA for personnel safety.

60-Second Answer:

To calculate the regulatory GHG Fuel Intensity, we convert the mass of bunkered fuel into its energy equivalent using its Lower Calorific Value (LCV), yielding total Megajoules (MJ). The mass is also multiplied by its combined lifecycle emissions coefficient (WtW), yielding total grams of CO2 equivalent (gCO2eq). Dividing total gCO2eq by total MJ gives the attained GFI. Alternatively, in electrical distribution, a Ground Fault Indicator monitors phase current imbalances using a Core Balance Current Transformer (CBCT) to compute ground fault leakage in milliamperes.

Deep Dive into the Regulatory GFI Calculation Step-by-Step

To illustrate a MEO Class 1 level written calculation answer for Greenhouse Gas Fuel Intensity:

Step 1: Energy Content Determination

Convert total metric tonnes of fuel consumed into Megajoules.

Energy Consumed (MJ) = Mass of Fuel (tonnes) × 106 × LCV ((MJ/g))

Step 2: Well-to-Wake Emissions Aggregation

Calculate individual greenhouse gas components multiplied by their respective 100-year global warming potentials (GWP).

Total Emissions (gCO2eq) = Mass of Fuel × 106 × [ WtT + TtWCO2 + (TtWCH4 × 28) + (TtWN2O × 265) ]

Step 3: Final Division

Divide Step 2 by Step 1 to extract the final intensity metric in gCO2eq/MJ.

Reference Value Comparisons (Target vs. Attained)

The regulatory framework demands a continuous reduction against the reference baseline (91.16 gCO2eq/MJ):

  • 2025/2026 Target: 2% reduction → Max allowed: 89.34 gCO2eq/MJ
  • 2030 Target: 6% reduction → Max allowed: 85.69 gCO2eq/MJ
  • 2035 Target: 14.5% reduction → Max allowed: 77.94 gCO2eq/MJ

Alternative Electrical Context: Ground Fault Indicator (GFI) / Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter (GFCI)

If Examiner Simon shifts the topic to the electrical engineering sub-group within QB6:

Line (I_in) ======================> [ LOAD ] Neutral <====================== ( ) <-- CBCT Senses Imbalance (I_in != I_out) ===> Trip Relay Actuated (mA / ms)

  • Principle of Calculation: It calculates the vector sum of currents flowing through the phase lines and the neutral line using a Core Balance Current Transformer (CBCT). In a healthy system, the vector sum is zero (Σ vec{I} = 0). If insulation breaks down, current leaks to earth, creating an imbalance:

Δ I = Iphase - Ineutral

  • Unit of Calculation: Sensed in Milliamperes (mA); tripping time duration measured in Milliseconds (ms).
  • Standard Values: Personnel protection loops trip at 30 mA within 40 ms. Main switchboard earth fault monitoring uses settings up to 5 A with stepped time delays for discrimination.

Chief Engineer Relevance

  • GHG Perspective: The Chief Engineer must keep track of compliance balances. If the container ship burns low-sulfur fuel oil (VLSFO) with an intensity of 92.1 gCO2eq/MJ, it accumulates a compliance deficit. To avoid steep financial penalties, the C/E must offset this by utilizing onshore power supply (OPS) while at European berths or blending compliant biofuels.
  • Electrical Perspective: C/Es ensure that insulated neutral distribution systems (common on container ships to maintain power to steering gear/reefers during a single earth fault) are kept clean. A continuous ground fault calculation on the group starter panel indicator must read infinity (infty \ Omega).
