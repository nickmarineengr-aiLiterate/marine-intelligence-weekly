Green passport (Inventory of Hazardous Materials) — what is it, what does it contain, and which convention requires it?
MARPOL/Environ Survey IACS/Class
15-Second AnswerThe "Green Passport" is the industry nickname for the Inventory of Hazardous Materials (IHM), required under the Hong Kong International Convention for the Safe and Environmentally Sound Recycling of Ships, 2009, which entered into force on 26 June 2025. It has three parts — Part I (structure/equipment), Part II (operationally generated waste), Part III (stores) — and applies to ships of 500 GT and above.
60-Second AnswerThe IHM lists all hazardous materials present in a ship's structure, equipment, and stores, and is required under the Hong Kong Convention (HKC) 2009, in force from 26 June 2025. It applies to ships ≥500 GT engaged in international voyages. Part I — hazardous materials in structure and fitted equipment (asbestos, PCBs, ozone-depleting substances, heavy metals, TBT anti-fouling) — must be maintained throughout the ship's operating life. Parts II (operationally generated waste) and III (stores) are added only when the ship is prepared for recycling. Existing ships must hold a valid International Certificate on Inventory of Hazardous Materials (ICIHM) by 26 June 2030 or before going for recycling, whichever is earlier; new ships (contracted on/after 26 June 2025) require it from build. IHM is prepared by an approved IHM company, reviewed/approved by the Recognized Organisation (Class, when authorised) or flag Administration, and the certificate is issued by the flag State.

What the IHM / Green Passport Is

The Inventory of Hazardous Materials (IHM) is a ship-specific document identifying, locating, and quantifying hazardous materials contained in the ship's structure, machinery, equipment, and stores — comparable in concept to a building's asbestos register, but comprehensive across the whole vessel. "Green Passport" is the informal industry term IMO and BIMCO used before the Hong Kong Convention formalised the IHM structure; the terms are used interchangeably in oral exams, but the correct formal name is IHM under the Hong Kong Convention.

Governing Convention & Applicability

  • Instrument: Hong Kong International Convention for the Safe and Environmentally Sound Recycling of Ships, 2009 (adopted 15 May 2009).
  • Entry into force: 26 June 2025 — 24 months after Bangladesh and Liberia's ratification in June 2023 met the threshold.
  • Applies to: ships of 500 GT and above on international voyages, flying the flag of, or calling at ports/waters of, a Party State — non-Party-flag ships trading to a Party State's waters must also comply.
  • Related instrument: EU Ship Recycling Regulation (EU) 1257/2013 — imposed near-identical IHM requirements on EU-flagged ships years before the HKC itself entered into force; many owners' existing EU SRR certificates are now being harmonised into HKC certification.

Three-Part Structure

  • Part I — Hazardous materials in structure and equipment: asbestos, PCBs (polychlorinated biphenyls) in cabling/gaskets, ozone-depleting substances in refrigeration/AC/fire-fighting systems, heavy metals (lead, mercury, cadmium, hexavalent chromium), tributyltin (TBT) and other organotin compounds in anti-fouling coatings, brominated flame retardants (PBDE), radioactive substances. Required throughout the ship's operating life — maintained and updated, not a one-time document.
  • Part II — Operationally generated wastes: residues such as sludge, bilge oil, ballast sediment, scale, and cargo residues that accumulate during service. Added when the ship is prepared for recycling.
  • Part III — Stores: paints, chemicals, batteries, and hazardous-material-containing consumable stores on board at the time of recycling. Added at the same stage as Part II.

Certification & Timeline

  • Existing ships (≥500 GT, in service before 26 June 2025): must hold a valid International Certificate on Inventory of Hazardous Materials (ICIHM), or Statement of Compliance (SoC) pending certificate issue, by 26 June 2030 — or before going for recycling, whichever comes first. Harmonised with the next main class renewal survey after 26 June 2025.
  • New ships — building contract placed on/after 26 June 2025 — require a reviewed IHM and valid ICIHM before entering service, per an initial survey under MEPC.222(64) as updated.
  • Preparation and verification chain: IHM prepared by an approved IHM (Hazmat) surveying company → reviewed/approved by the Recognized Organisation (Classification Society, when authorised) or directly by the flag Administration → certificate issued by the flag State.
  • Prior to recycling, when Parts II and III are added and finalised, the ship additionally requires an International Ready for Recycling Certificate (IRRC), issued after a Final Survey, and the recycling facility itself must hold a Document of Authorisation for Ship Recycling (DASR) and prepare a ship-specific Ship Recycling Plan.

Maintenance Obligation

Part I must be kept current throughout the ship's life — any structural modification, retrofit, or equipment change that introduces or removes a listed hazardous material (e.g. replacing PCB-containing cable, retrofitting a scrubber, changing anti-fouling system) requires the IHM to be updated and, where material, re-verified. This is a live, working document, not filed away after delivery.

CE Oral Tip: Nair uses "Green Passport" as the trigger word — accept it, then immediately upgrade to the formal term: "Sir, the Green Passport is formally the Inventory of Hazardous Materials under the Hong Kong Convention 2009, in force since 26 June 2025." State the 500 GT threshold, the three parts (Part I lifelong / Parts II & III at recycling), and who certifies (flag State, verified by an approved IHM company and Class as RO). If pushed further, give the existing-ship certification deadline — 26 June 2030 or before recycling, whichever is earlier.
CE Relevance, Traps, Failures & Exam Strategy
CE Relevance

The CE does not prepare the IHM (a specialist approved surveyor does), but is directly responsible for flagging any machinery/equipment modification that could alter Part I content — e.g. replacing gaskets, cabling, refrigerant type, or anti-fouling system — and ensuring the update is routed through the ship-manager/technical department before the next survey. On PSC inspection, absence of a valid ICIHM (post-2030) or an outdated Part I is a documented deficiency the CE should be able to speak to.

Trap Questions

Q: "Is IHM only required for ships going to the scrapyard?"

A: No. Part I is required throughout the ship's operating life for all ships ≥500 GT — not just at end-of-life. Parts II and III are the only elements added specifically for recycling.

Q: "The Hong Kong Convention was adopted in 2009 — why does the certification deadline only fall in 2030?"

A: The Convention needed ratification by enough States representing sufficient tonnage before it could enter into force; that threshold was met in June 2023, triggering entry into force 24 months later on 26 June 2025. Existing ships are then given a further 5-year compliance window to 26 June 2030 to obtain certification without disrupting ongoing trading.

Common CE Failures

1. Not knowing the Hong Kong Convention's actual entry-into-force date (26 June 2025) and confusing it with the 2009 adoption date.

2. Saying IHM applies "only when going to recycling," missing that Part I is a lifelong requirement.

3. Not being able to name who certifies the document (flag State, via Class as RO) when pressed.

Numbers to Memorise

Hong Kong Convention adopted 15 May 2009 · entered into force 26 June 2025 · applicability: ships ≥500 GT · existing ships certification deadline: 26 June 2030 (or before recycling) · three parts: I (structure/equipment, lifelong), II (operational waste, at recycling), III (stores, at recycling).

Casualty Link

The HKC exists largely because of decades of documented worker deaths and injuries at uncontrolled beaching-yard ship-breaking sites in South Asia, plus asbestos and heavy-metal exposure incidents during unregulated dismantling — the regulatory response IMO adopted after prolonged pressure from ILO and environmental NGOs.

On My Vessel

Maersk container tonnage is well above the 500 GT threshold, so IHM Part I maintenance is mandatory fleet-wide. Maersk's technical/environmental department manages IHM updates centrally when retrofits occur (e.g. scrubber or ballast water system installation); as CE, the practical duty is reporting any structural/equipment change with potential hazardous-material content to the technical superintendent so the IHM stays current ahead of survey.
