⚠ CE Trap: "What if you discover a major machinery defect during takeover?" Answer: Do NOT sign the handover until the defect is documented, disclosed to the company, and either repaired or formally accepted as a known condition with a management of change (MOC) entry. Signing without disclosure creates personal liability.
Q24 — CE as Leader — Leadership Theories
Leadership Theories — CE Application Q24
TheoryCore ConceptCE ApplicationLimitation
Trait TheoryLeaders are born — innate qualities (intelligence, decisiveness, integrity)CE must develop: technical competence, decisiveness under pressure, personal integrityIgnores situation; not teachable
Situational Leadership (Hersey-Blanchard)Adapt leadership style to follower's Competence + Commitment level: S1 Directing / S2 Coaching / S3 Supporting / S4 DelegatingNew 4/E joining = S1 (directing); experienced 2/E planning departure = S4 (delegate and trust)Requires accurate follower diagnosis
Transformational LeadershipInspire followers to exceed self-interest for the group's benefit; change the cultureCE building a safety reporting culture where crew report near-misses voluntarily — beyond rule complianceTime-intensive; high energy demand on leader
Transactional LeadershipExchange-based — reward for compliance, punishment for failureRoutine maintenance performance targets, overtime authorisation for extra work completedCreates compliance culture, not safety culture
Servant LeadershipLeader serves the team — removes obstacles, develops people, prioritises team needsCE ensuring ratings have correct PPE, tools, and training before assigning a taskCan be misread as weak by Theory X teams
AutocraticCE decides alone; directs without consultationEmergency scenarios — main engine breakdown, flooding, fire in ER; no time for consensusDemotivating in normal operations
Democratic / ParticipativeCE consults team before decidingMaintenance planning, bunkering schedule, crew watch allocationSlow in emergencies

Examiner favourite: "Is CE a leader?" → Yes. STCW A-III/2 names "use leadership and managerial skills" as a mandatory CE competency. The CE must apply Situational Leadership — style must match the follower's competence + commitment level at that moment.

Q26 / Q27 — Fatigue vs Fitness for Duty
Fatigue (Q26) vs Fitness for Duty (Q27) — Critical Distinction Q26 Q27
⚠ Rajappan sir trap: "Are fatigue and unfitness for duty the same?" Answer: No. Fatigue is one specific cause of unfitness. Fitness for duty is the broader concept.
Fatigue (Q26)Fitness for Duty (Q27)
DefinitionState of weariness resulting from insufficient rest, excessive work, or mental/physical stress, reducing cognitive performance and physical capabilityOverall state where a seafarer is physically and mentally capable of performing watchkeeping and other duties safely without impairment
CausesInsufficient sleep, disrupted circadian rhythm, workload, port turnarounds, UMS callouts, monotony, emotional stressFatigue, alcohol (BAC >0.05%), drugs/medication, illness, injury, psychological distress
Key RegulationSTCW A-VIII/1 + MLC Reg. 2.3 (rest hours: 10 hrs/24 hrs; 77 hrs/7 days)STCW 2010, Reg. VIII/1 — must not be impaired when assuming watch or duty
Alcohol LimitN/A (fatigue-specific)BAC ≤0.05% (50 mg/100 mL blood) or breath alcohol ≤0.25 mg/L — STCW Reg. VIII/2 §5
CE Action if suspectedReview rest hour records; check UMS callout log; rearrange watch; grant additional rest before critical taskRelieve watchkeeper; not send unfit person on duty; document; inform Master; provide medical assessment if illness
IMO documentMSC-MEPC.7/Circ.6 — Guidance on fatigueSTCW Code Section A-VIII/1; MLC Std. A2.3

CBDR — Constant Bearing, Decreasing Range Q29

CBDR — Risk of Collision Indicator
DefinitionIf compass bearing to another vessel remains constant AND range is decreasing → risk of collision exists
COLREGS Rule 7Every available means shall be used to determine risk of collision. If bearing does not appreciably change — risk deemed to exist (Rule 7(d)(i))
Rule 8 ActionAction must be taken in ample time; must be large, positive, and made in good time
CE relevanceIn UMS — CE called to engine room for maneuvering; must understand bridge CBDR principle to respond correctly to manoeuvring orders
⚠ CBDR Trap: "What if bearing changes slightly?" Answer: A small change in bearing does not eliminate risk — the vessel may still be on a collision course due to CPA (Closest Point of Approach) calculation.
Q30 — PID Controller
PID Controller — Formula, Actions, Symptoms Q30
u(t) = Kp × [ e(t) + (1/Ti) × ∫e(τ)dτ + Td × de(t)/dt ]

u(t) = controller output  |  e(t) = error (SP − PV)  |  Kp = proportional gain  |  Ti = integral time (reset time)  |  Td = derivative time (rate time)

TermCorrectsToo large → symptomToo small → symptomVessel location
P (Proportional)Current errorOscillation / instability / huntingSluggish; large steady-state offset remainsAll loops: ME governor, boiler, temp ctrl
I (Integral)Accumulated past error — eliminates offsetIntegral windup, overshoot, oscillationPersistent offset (setpoint never reached)Boiler drum level, feed water valve
D (Derivative)Rate of change of error — predictive dampingNoise amplification; hunting; instabilitySlow disturbance response; overshoot on load changeUsed sparingly; fuel oil temp, some governors

Onboard PID locations: Main engine speed governor · Boiler feedwater (3-element) · HFO temperature (steam heating valve) · Refrigeration temperature · Turbocharger waste gate (some designs) · Freshwater generator temperature

3-Element Boiler Feed Water Control (Q31)

[ STEAM FLOW ] (Feedforward) │ ▼ [ DRUM LEVEL ]──►[ LEVEL PID ]──►[+]──►[ INNER PID ]──►[ FEED VALVE ] (Master) (Master Loop) (Slave Loop) ▲ ▲ │ └──[ FEED FLOW ]┘ (Slave Feedback)
ElementRole
Drum Level (Master)What we ultimately control
Steam Flow (Feedforward)Anticipates load change before drum level reacts — prevents shrink/swell errors
Feed Flow (Slave feedback)Inner loop maintains feedwater flow setpoint precisely
⚠ At low loads (<25% MCR) switch to single-element (drum level only) — steam/feed flow signals too noisy for accurate control

Ziegler-Nichols Method (Q32)

Procedure: Set I=∞, D=0 → Increase Kp until sustained oscillation → record Ku (Ultimate Gain) and Pu (Ultimate Period)

⚠ ZN gives aggressive initial settings — always de-tune (reduce Kp by 20-30%) before applying to live system. Never run ZN on a vessel at sea without Master's approval and backup manual control ready.
Q33 — IG Alarms, Trips & O₂ Analyser
IG System Alarms / Trips + O₂ Analyser Q33
IG System — Key Alarms & Trips
O₂ content high alarmO₂ >5% in IG supply → alarm; >8% → automatic shutdown of IG plant (oxygen too high to be inert)
Scrubber water low flowAlarm + trip — cooling/scrubbing water insufficient; SO₂/NOx not removed; IG temperature rises
IG pressure lowAlarm — possible deck seal breach or demand exceeding supply; trip if pressure falls below deck seal setpoint
IG pressure highAlarm — pressure relief valve lifts; overpressure risk to cargo tanks
Blower tripFan failure — IG supply stops; switch to standby blower automatically
Deck seal low waterAlarm + trip — water seal integrity lost; risk of cargo vapour flashback to IG plant
High temp alarmIG supply too hot — typically >65°C; scrubber inefficiency; trip to protect cargo tank integrity
O₂ Analyser — Limits & Calibration
Normal IG O₂ level<5% O₂ by volume — inert atmosphere confirmed for cargo operations
Alarm setpoint>5% O₂ — high O₂ alarm; approaching flammable range
Trip setpoint>8% O₂ — IG plant automatic shutdown
Flammable rangeHydrocarbons typically 1–10% in air; below 5% O₂ prevents ignition (below limiting O₂ concentration)
Span gasGas of known O₂ concentration used to set the 100% scale of the analyser (calibration high point)
Zero gasNitrogen (O₂-free) used to set the 0% scale of the analyser (calibration low point)
Test gasGas of known intermediate concentration used to verify analyser accuracy between calibrations
Calibration frequencyPer maker's manual — typically before each cargo operation and at periodic intervals per PMS
⚠ Span gas ≠ Test gas. Span gas calibrates. Test gas verifies. The analyser must be calibrated with span gas and zero gas before verifying with test gas — not the other way around.

15-Second Quick-Fire Index — Q21 to Q36
Q21–Q36 — One-Line Oral Anchors
Q21 — Taking Over as CE
Review vessel history → handover from outgoing CE → independent machinery rounds → safety systems check → manning/cert verification → fuel/lube status → SMS familiarisation → sign CE Takeover Certificate. Never sign without documenting known defects.
Q22 — Conflict Management
Thomas-Kilmann: Competing (safety emergency) / Collaborating (CE default) / Compromising / Avoiding (never for safety) / Accommodating. MLC 5.1.5: CE must not suppress complaints. Document serious conflicts in Official Log Book.
Q23 — HR Management
CE HR functions: recruitment input (appraisals → MLC 1.4), rest hour management (MLC 2.3), appraisal/development, conflict resolution, training planning (Bloom's levels), disciplinary action (MS Act), complaints handling (MLC 5.1.5).
Q24 — CE as Leader
Yes — STCW A-III/2 names "use leadership and managerial skills" as mandatory. Key theories: Situational (Hersey-Blanchard — match style to follower), Transformational (build safety culture), Transactional (routine compliance), Autocratic (emergencies only).
Q25 — CE & ISPS Audit
CE responsibilities: engine dept access control (restricted areas: ER, steering gear, CO₂ room), engine room security procedures in SSP, machinery readiness for emergency departure, security watch records, CCTV/alarm systems in engine scope.
Q26 — Fatigue
Fatigue = weariness from insufficient rest, disrupted circadian rhythm, excessive workload. Rest hours: 10 hrs/24 hrs; 77 hrs/7 days (MLC 2.3 + STCW VIII/1). Mitigation: scheduling, workload prioritisation, culture. MSC-MEPC.7/Circ.6.
Q27 — Fitness for Duty
Broader than fatigue — includes alcohol (BAC ≤0.05%; breath ≤0.25 mg/L per STCW VIII/2), drugs, medication, illness, injury, psychological distress. CE must relieve and not assign duty until fit. Document and inform Master.
Q28 — MACN
Maritime Anti-Corruption Network — founded 2011; 200+ members; >50% global tonnage. Combats facilitation payment demands via collective action, incident reporting, government engagement. UK Bribery Act: paying = criminal offence. Use MACN incident reporting system.
Q29 — CBDR
Constant Bearing, Decreasing Range = risk of collision exists (COLREGS Rule 7(d)(i)). Action per Rule 8: large, positive, early. CE relevance: understand manoeuvring orders in UMS — bridge CBDR triggers engine room actions.
Q30 — PID Controller
P = corrects current error; I = eliminates steady-state offset (integral of past error); D = predictive damping (rate of change). Formula: u(t) = Kp[e(t) + (1/Ti)∫e(τ)dτ + Td·de(t)/dt]. Locations: ME governor, boiler, HFO temp, refrigeration.
Q31 — 3-Element Controller
Boiler drum feedwater control. Three elements: (1) Drum Level (master — what we control), (2) Steam Flow (feedforward — anticipates demand), (3) Feed Flow (slave — inner loop precision). Switch to single-element below ~25% MCR.
Q32 — Ziegler-Nichols
Set I=∞, D=0. Increase Kp to sustained oscillation → record Ku (ultimate gain) and Pu (ultimate period). PID: Kp=0.6Ku, Ti=0.5Pu, Td=0.125Pu. De-tune by 20-30% before applying to live system.
Q33 — IG Alarms & O₂ Analyser
Normal IG O₂: <5%. High O₂ alarm: >5%. Trip: >8%. Other trips: scrubber low water, deck seal low water, high temp (>65°C). O₂ analyser: Span gas = calibrate scale; Zero gas (N₂) = set zero; Test gas = verify accuracy between calibrations.
Q34 — War Zone Crew Management
Apply Maslow Level 2 (safety) first — machinery readiness for evasive manoeuvring, damage control stations. Then Level 3 (social) — open communication, no information vacuum. Apply Vroom — clear timeline to safe passage. BMP5 procedures. ISPS Security Level elevation.
Q35 — Framo Pump RA & PTW
RA: identify hazards (hydraulic hose failure, hot work proximity, enclosed space atmosphere, electrical isolation). PTW scope: LOTO hydraulic + electrical, enclosed space entry if applicable (O₂ 19.5-23.5%, H₂S <1 ppm, CO <25 ppm). Continuous monitoring during operation. IMO A.1050(27).
Q36 — Human Element in Casualty
Human element factors in investigation: fatigue, situational awareness failure, communication breakdown, authority gradient, training gap, procedural violation vs error, workload overload, cultural factors, organisational pressures. Framework: HFACS. ISM §9 + IMO MSC.255(84) Casualty Investigation Code.
