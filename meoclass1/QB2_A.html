CSC Convention — what it covers, container testing, approval methods, certification, and how reefer container testing differs.
CSCSOLASConvention

Governing Instrument

International Convention for Safe Containers (CSC) 1972, as amended. Implemented in India under MS Act Part IXA. Establishes uniform structural regulations and standardised testing to ensure safety during intermodal handling, transport, and stacking.

Approval Methods (CSC Annex II)

  • Design Type Approval (DT): Prototype tested under maximum stress; subsequent units certified by design conformity
  • Individual Approval: Each unit tested individually — used for specialised or heavy-lift frames
  • ACEP: Owner-managed continuous inspection programme; first inspection within 5 years, subsequent cycles ≤ 30 months
  • PES: Fixed next-exam date stamped on plate; inspection every 30 months after initial 5-year period

Structural Tests (CSC Annex II)

  • Stacking: 1.8 × R × (S−1) where R = max gross mass, S = stacking factor (typically 9)
  • Lifting: 2P top corner fittings; 1.25P bottom fork pockets
  • Racking: 150 kN lateral force — frame shear rigidity
  • End Wall: 0.4P longitudinal; Side Wall: 0.6P rolling forces
  • Floor: 5,460 kg forklift axle load; Roof: 300 kg over 600 × 300 mm

Reefer Container — Additional Tests (ISO 1496-2)

  • Thermal Transmittance (K-value) Test: Heat transfer coefficient through insulation layer
  • Air Leakage Test: Volumetric leakage rate at static differential pressure
  • Machinery/Defrost Test: Validates compressor efficiency and evaporator/condenser airflow
  • CSC plate carries “R” designation and temperature rating

Certification

CSC Safety Approval Plate: country of approval, manufacture date, max gross mass, stacking load, racking force, next examination date. A freight container engaged in international transport must carry valid CSC approval or equivalent approved certification. CSC provides for alternative approval arrangements in certain special cases, but these must be formally approved.

ISO 1496-2Reefer container thermal transmittance and air leakage test standards
SOLAS VICarriage requirements — container must carry valid CSC approval
MS Act Part IXAIndian domestic implementation of CSC Convention requirements
CE Oral Tip (Nair/Simon): Simon focuses on ACEP vs PES — know the distinction cold: ACEP = owner manages continuous programme; PES = fixed date stamped on plate. For ACEP inspection items state: corner posts for buckling, corner castings for micro-fractures or ovalization, cross-member detachment, door gasket elasticity, lock-rod engagement. Reefer containers carry “R” on the CSC plate — mention the K-value test when asked what makes reefer testing different.

VGM — who is responsible, two certified methods, CE’s verification duties, master’s authority to reject, and dangers of misdeclaration.
VGMSOLAS VICargo

Governing Instrument

SOLAS Chapter VI, Regulation 2, amended by MSC.380(94). Implementation guidance: MSC.1/Circ.1475. VGM must be provided to master and terminal operator before container can be loaded.

Responsibility

The Shipper named on the Bill of Lading — legally and exclusively responsible for providing accurate VGM.

Two Certified Methods

  • Method 1: Weigh completely packed and sealed container on calibrated, certified weighbridge
  • Method 2: VGM = Σ(cargo packages) + Σ(dunnage/securing material) + container tare weight. Subject to DG Shipping approval in India. Cannot be used for raw bulk materials (scrap metal, grain).

CE’s Verification Duties

  • Verify EDI data or documentation carries shipper’s legal authorising signature
  • Feed VGM into Loadicator — run stress and stability checks before signing loading calculations
  • Golden Rule: No VGM = No Load. Statutory prohibition under SOLAS VI/2

Master’s Authority to Reject

  • If declared VGM does not match terminal weighing, loading must be suspended pending resolution
  • Master retains overriding authority under SOLAS V/34 (master’s discretion for safe operation)

Dangers of Misdeclaration

  • Cell guide structural collapse from overloaded lower-tier containers
  • False KG — intact and damage stability criteria become invalid
  • Dynamic hull girder overstress — permissible bending moments exceeded at sea
  • MOL Comfort (2013): structural failure linked in part to cargo weight misdeclaration
⚖ Regulatory References
SOLAS VI/2VGM obligation on shipper — mandatory before container loading
MSC.380(94)2014 amendment introducing mandatory VGM requirement
MSC.1/Circ.1475Implementation guidance — methods 1 and 2, documentation requirements
SOLAS V/34Master’s discretion — authority to refuse unsafe cargo operation
CE Oral Tip (Nair): Nair asks “What if VGM not received?” — SHALL NOT load. Cite SOLAS VI/2 verbatim. Method 2: DG Shipping approval required in India; cannot be used for bulk materials. MOL Comfort is the incident reference — a 316-metre container ship that broke in two in 2013; cite it when asked about consequences of misdeclared VGM.

Container material — why Corten steel, key properties, wall thickness by component, and why corrugation is used.
ContainerConstruction

Governing Standards

ISO 1496-1 and CSC structural safety design mandates.

Why Corten Steel (Weathering Steel — ASTM A242/A588)

Low-alloy copper-chromium-nickel steel. Forms a self-sealing, dense amorphous iron oxide patina on atmospheric exposure. This micro-layer blocks oxygen from penetrating deeper into the grain matrix — stopping corrosion without continuous industrial coatings.

Key Properties

  • Yield strength approximately 345 MPa (varies by grade and manufacturer)
  • Ultimate Tensile Strength: 480–620 MPa
  • Excellent low-temperature notch toughness — resists brittle fracture on sub-zero routes
  • Corrosion resistance approximately 4–8× better than plain carbon steel

Thickness by Component

  • Side corrugated panels: 1.6–2.0 mm
  • Roof panels: 2.0 mm
  • Corner posts: 6.0–8.0 mm structural channels — carry full compressive load of stack above
  • Floor understructure: 4.0–6.0 mm high-tensile cross-members supporting 28 mm Apitong/Keruing hardwood floor

Why Corrugation

Dramatically increases Section Modulus (Z) and moment of inertia without adding weight — same principle as an I-beam. Transforms a flexible thin plate into a rigid, load-bearing monocoque structure. Vertical side corrugations carry axial compressive loads; end-wall trapezoidal profiles resist dynamic surging forces during ship acceleration.

Weakness of Corten

Once the patina layer is broken (welding, grinding, mechanical damage), standard corrosion resumes — damaged areas require recoating.

ISO 1496-1Series 1 freight containers — specifications and testing including structural requirements
CSC 1972 Annex IIStructural safety design — load criteria driving material and thickness selection
ASTM A242/A588Weathering steel specification for container-grade Corten
CE Oral Tip (Simon): Know “monocoque structure” — same principle as an aircraft fuselage skin: stiffness without mass penalty. Floor is Apitong hardwood over steel cross-members — Simon will ask why not all steel: timber absorbs forklift point loads better, easier to repair, lighter. Know the patina mechanism: not a coating, a self-forming barrier layer. Patina damage from grinding or welding must be recoated immediately.

Container tracking — who tracks them, for what purpose, and what technology is used including ISO 6346.
ContainerTracking

Governing Framework

ISO 6346 (container coding, identification, marking), ISO 18185 (electronic seals), C-TPAT (US customs compliance).

ISO 6346 Container Number Structure

3-letter owner code + 1 product category identifier (U = freight container, J = detachable equipment, Z = trailers) + 6-digit serial number + 1 Check Digit. The check digit is a base-11 weighted system — each letter assigned a numerical value, multiplied by geometric weight factor (2&sup0; to 2¹&sup0; by position). Prevents data-entry errors in loading and terminal management software.

Who Tracks and Why

  • Shipping lines: Box position throughout supply chain
  • Terminal operators: Yard management and crane sequencing
  • Customs authorities: Import/export compliance, DG cargo verification
  • Shippers/consignees: Cargo visibility and delivery scheduling
  • Port state: Security screening (ISPS compliance)

Technology Used

  • RFID tags: Automated gate in/out logging at terminal
  • Active GPS/GNSS on reefers: Real-time position, temperature, humidity, power alarms
  • OCR camera arrays: Automated container number recognition at gantry cranes
  • Electronic seals (ISO 18185): Records door-opening events with timestamp and GPS coordinates
  • Distributed-ledger (DLT) platforms: Immutable supply chain records — TradeLens discontinued 2022; newer DLT-based platforms continue to develop in this space
⚖ Regulatory References
ISO 6346Container coding, identification, marking and check digit verification system
ISO 18185Electronic seal standard — door-opening event records with timestamp
C-TPATUS Customs-Trade Partnership Against Terrorism — supply chain security requirements
ISPS CodePort security screening — affects container tracking data requirements
CE Oral Tip (Nair): Know ISO 6346 check digit concept — “how does the terminal know the number is correct?” Answer: check digit is base-11 mathematical verification of preceding digits; automated systems flag mismatches before gate-in. Be ready to cross to smart Bill of Lading and DLT when asked about new technology. C-TPAT affects documentation requirements for US-bound cargo on Maersk vessels.

Recent container vessel safety developments — latest SOLAS amendments and IMO guidance.
SOLASContainerIMDG

Key Instruments

MSC.421(98), MSC.496(105), MSC.513(105), MSC.1/Circ.1612. This topic updates with every MSC session — state amendments by resolution number and add that you monitor updates through company SMS and flag state notifications.

1 — Mandatory Reporting of Lost Containers (SOLAS VI/5-1)

Real-time radio/satellite reporting mandatory. Report must contain exact coordinates, quantities lost, and IMDG class listings for lost units.

2 — Fire Protection (MSC.1/Circ.1612)

IMO guidance recommends that vessels carrying containers on or above the weather deck carry at least two portable water monitors, with a dedicated fire main branch capable of reaching the highest tier of container stack. MSC.1/Circ.1612 is IMO guidance — not a universal SOLAS regulation applicable to every container ship; verify applicability against flag state requirements for your vessel.

3 — Lithium Battery Provisions (IMDG Amendment 41-22 / 42-24)

Restructured stowage categories for UN 3480 (Li-ion batteries, charged) and UN 3481 (Li-ion batteries in equipment). Restricts under-deck stowage adjacent to machinery spaces or high thermal radiation sources.

4 — Cargo Securing (CSS Code Updates)

Computer-based cargo securing calculations mandatory; class requirements for container stack loads updated to reflect taller vessels with greater bay lengths.

Li-ion battery fire on a container vessel — thermal runaway mechanism and CE’s actions.
IMDG Class 9SOLAS II-2Container

Governing Instruments

IMDG Code Class 9 (UN 3480/3481), EmS F-A, S-I. SOLAS II-2. MSC.1/Circ.1612. IMDG Amendment 41-22/42-24.

Thermal Runaway — Mechanism

Internal exothermic chemical cascade within battery cells — does not require atmospheric oxygen; generates its own oxygen through cathode decomposition. Burns at 400–700°C. Releases HF, CO, and H&sub2; gas. CO&sub2; and dry powder agents fail — they provide no thermal cooling and cannot stop internal oxygen generation.

Primary Extinguishing Approach

Copious water cooling remains the primary shipboard control measure currently recommended by IMO guidance. Water reduces localised cell temperature below the activation energy threshold of adjacent battery packs, arresting the cascade. Emerging agents (F-500, water mist, encapsulants) are under evaluation — for oral exams, state IMO-recommended practice: copious water.

CE’s Actions

  1. Isolate Electrical Power: Physically isolate breakers in deck switch room — prevent cascading electrical faults
  2. Deploy Copious Water: Run all main fire pumps in parallel; deploy portable water monitors for continuous external container cooling
  3. Manage Hold Boundaries: Do NOT activate fixed CO&sub2; prematurely — it will not stop internal battery fire. Boundary cool engine room forward bulkhead
  4. Bilge Management: Monitor hold bilge levels — large water volumes create dangerous Free Surface Effect, reducing GM
  5. Toxic Gas: BA sets mandatory near fire area — HF is lethal at low concentrations
  6. Alert MRCC and coastal state — IMDG reporting obligation
⚖ Regulatory References
IMDG Code — Class 9UN 3480 / UN 3481 — lithium battery classification and stowage requirements
IMDG EmS F-A / S-IFire and spillage schedules for lithium battery incidents
IMDG Amendment 41-22/42-24Revised stowage restrictions for lithium batteries
SOLAS II-2Fire protection — fixed CO2 systems, fire pumps, fire main requirements
MSC.1/Circ.1612IMO guidance on portable water monitors for container fire suppression
CE Oral Tip (Rajappan/Senthil): Key term = “thermal runaway.” Rajappan asks why CO&sub2; fails — answer: battery generates its own internal oxygen through cathode decomposition; smothering external atmosphere does not stop the reaction. If asked about F-500 or water mist — acknowledge they exist and are under evaluation, then state current IMO guidance recommends copious water. Re-ignition risk: thermal runaway can re-activate hours or days after apparent extinguishment.

Container deck fire — major reasons, EmS structure, and firefighting procedure.
IMDG EmSSOLAS II-2Container

Governing Instruments

SOLAS II-2, IMDG Code EmS Supplement, MSC.1/Circ.1612, FSS Code Chapter 15.

Major Reasons for Container Fires

  • Misdeclared / undeclared IMDG cargo — most common cause
  • Exothermic auto-oxidation of self-heating substances (Class 4.2)
  • Undeclared oxidisers (Class 5.1) stowed adjacent to organic compounds
  • Reefer compressor electrical arcing
  • Lithium battery thermal runaway (Class 9)

Identification Chain

Container number → cargo manifest → IMDG declarations → UN number on placard → EmS schedule

EmS Structure

  • Fire Schedules (F-A to F-J): Agent and approach by chemical class. F-G (water-reactive) prohibits water — dry agents only
  • Spillage Schedules (S-A to S-Z): PPE requirements, neutralisation protocols, wash-down rules

Firefighting Procedure

  1. Alarm, muster, BA sets donned
  2. Identify UN number and EmS schedule from manifest
  3. Deploy portable water monitors per MSC.1/Circ.1612 guidance (at least two)
  4. Boundary cool adjacent containers
  5. High-expansion foam for enclosed hold fire (SOLAS II-2 fixed system)
  6. Do NOT open container until confirmed cold
  7. Official Log Book entry; preserve VDR/CCTV
⚖ Regulatory References
IMDG Code EmS SupplementFire (F-A to F-J) and Spillage (S-A to S-Z) emergency schedules by chemical class
SOLAS II-2Fire protection — fixed systems, fire main, portable equipment
FSS Code Chapter 15Fire suppression systems for cargo ships including high-expansion foam
MSC.1/Circ.1612IMO guidance on portable water monitors for container vessel fire response
CE Oral Tip (Nair): MFAG = medical reference for crew chemical exposure, NOT firefighting — Nair distinguishes these. F-G prohibits water for water-reactive cargo — common examiner trap: “you said use water monitors — can you always use water?” Answer: No — check EmS fire schedule first. IMDGSOLAS VIICargo

Governing Instrument

IMDG Code — SOLAS Chapter VII Part A, Regulation 3. Updated biennially. Amendment 41-22 mandatory from 1 January 2024; Amendment 42-24 adopted 2024.

Documentation Required

  • DGD (Dangerous Goods Declaration): Proper Shipping Name, UN number, Hazard Class, Packing Group (I=High / II=Medium / III=Low), flashpoint, emergency contact
  • Container Packing Certificate: Structural integrity and securing compliance — appended to DGD
  • Cargo Manifest: All DG units listed with class and UN number
  • Stowage Plan: Cell coordinates per DG unit

Marking and Labelling

  • Package: UN number, Proper Shipping Name, Class label (100 mm min), Marine Pollutant mark if applicable
  • Container: Class placard 250 × 250 mm all four sides; orange UN number placard 300 × 120 mm

Segregation Codes (IMDG 7.2.4) — Functional Definitions

  • Code 1 — Away from: Minimum separation within same space
  • Code 2 — Separated from: Different holds if under deck; minimum separation on deck
  • Code 3 — Separated by complete compartment: Full structural steel boundary between cargoes
  • Code 4 — Separated longitudinally: Entirely different ship zones — full hold structure between

Latest Amendments — IMDG 41-22 / 42-24

  • Updated lithium battery provisions (UN 3480, 3481, 3090, 3091)
  • New entries for alternative fuels — hydrogen, ammonia
  • Revised EmS schedules for several chemical classes

IMSBC Code — BLU Code, Group A/B/C classification, TML and Moisture Certificate, codes applicable to bulk carriers.
IMSBCSOLAS VIBulk

Governing Instrument

IMSBC Code — SOLAS Chapter VI, Regulation 1-1. Mandatory from 1 January 2011 (MSC.268(85)). Replaces BC Code. Applies to all solid bulk cargoes carried in bulk on seagoing vessels.

Group Classification

  • Group A — Liquefaction Risk: Fine particles with inherent moisture. If moisture exceeds TML, cyclic ship movements increase pore water pressure, destroying internal shear strength — cargo transitions to fluid state, catastrophic free-surface shift. Examples: nickel ore, iron ore fines, coal fines
  • Group B — Chemical Hazard: MHB or IMDG-classified. Self-heating, oxygen depletion (DRI, coal), toxic gas (CO, phosphine), structural corrosion. Examples: coal, sulphur, fishmeal
  • Group C — Low Risk: Neither liquefies nor chemically hazardous. Examples: limestone, cement clinker, quartz

Some cargoes are dual-classified — coal can be Group A + B simultaneously.

TML and Moisture Certificate

TML = 0.90 × FMP (Flow Moisture Point)

Before loading any Group A cargo, shipper must provide: Moisture Content (MC) — actual moisture at time of shipment, and TML Certificate — maximum safe moisture for transport. MC must be less than TML. If MC ≥ TML, cargo cannot be loaded.

BLU Code (MSC.1/Circ.1217)

Best practice guidance. Mandates signed Ship/Shore Safety Checklist between Chief Officer and Terminal Representative — ensures loading sequence does not overstress hull girder.

Codes Applicable to Bulk Carriers

IMSBC Code, SOLAS Chapter XII (additional safety measures for bulk carriers), ESP Code (IACS), Grain Code (MSC.23(59)), MARPOL Annex V.

IMSBC Code — SOLAS VI/1-1Mandatory from 1 January 2011 — solid bulk cargo carriage requirements
MSC.268(85)Resolution adopting IMSBC Code as mandatory instrument
BLU Code — MSC.1/Circ.1217Best practice for bulk loading — Ship/Shore Safety Checklist
SOLAS Chapter XIIAdditional safety measures for bulk carriers — structural requirements
Grain Code — MSC.23(59)International Grain Code — mandatory under SOLAS VI/9
CE Oral Tip (Simon): Two numbers cold: TML = 90% of FMP, and MC must be less than TML. Mechanism: pore water pressure buildup — say this exactly. Simon asks “can a Group A cargo look safe on deck and still liquefy?” — yes: pore water pressure builds internally before any surface change is visible; by the time you see free water, the cargo may already be in fluid state.

Grain Code — intact stability criteria, grain heeling moment mechanism, and Document of Authorisation.
Grain CodeStabilitySOLAS VI

Governing Instrument

International Grain Code — SOLAS Chapter VI, Regulation 9, Resolution MSC.23(59). Applies to all ships loading grain in bulk regardless of ship type. Requires Document of Authorisation (DoA) from flag state or RO, and an approved Grain Loading Manual.

Three Stability Criteria — All Must Be Met Simultaneously

  • Angle of heel due to assumed grain shift: ≤ 12° or deck edge immersion angle, whichever is lower
  • Net residual dynamic stability area: ≥ 0.075 m·rad (between heeling moment curve and GZ curve, up to lesser of 40° or angle of flooding)
  • Initial GM₀ (corrected for all liquid free surfaces): ≥ 0.30 m throughout voyage

Grain Heeling Moment — Three Mechanisms

  • Assumed grain shift: Surface grain moves laterally when ship heels
  • Void migration: Unsupported grain voids allow bulk cargo to shift as a mass
  • Surface grain movement: Top layer is free to move; lower packed grain moves less

The Grain Code provides tables to calculate the heeling moment for each loading condition based on void space, grain type, and hold geometry.

Void Space Mitigation

Minimised by filling with bagged grain, or using shifting boards — temporary wooden dividers that physically restrain surface grain movement, reducing the assumed shift heeling moment.

Grain Code — MSC.23(59)International Grain Code — mandatory under SOLAS VI/9

IMSBC CodeGrain is a solid bulk cargo — IMSBC Code context applies for general handling
CE Oral Tip (Simon/Nair): Know all three criteria with numbers. The 0.075 m·rad residual area is the dynamic stability check — ensures ship has enough righting energy after assumed grain shift. Mechanism: grain heeling moment from assumed shift — not just “cargo shifts.” DoA = mandatory before loading; no DoA = cannot load grain in bulk.

High density cargo in IMSBC — definition, restriction criteria, and local vs global structural strength.
IMSBCStrengthConstruction

Governing Instrument

IMSBC Code Section 7, individual cargo schedules. Stowage factor ≤ 0.56 m³/t (bulk density ≥ 1,250 kg/m³ per IMSBC Section 4.3). Examples: iron ore, pig iron, manganese ore, pyrites.

CE’s Structural Audit — Before Loading

  1. Check IMSBC individual cargo schedule — stowage factor and transport remarks
  2. Verify tank top strength rating (t/m²) from classification files and capacity plans
  3. Check class notation — “Strengthened for Heavy Cargoes” or hold restrictions (e.g. “Holds 2 and 4 may be empty”)
  4. Run Loadicator — shear force and bending moment check before loading

Local vs Global Structural Strength

  • Local strength: Tank top loading — concentrated weight on tank top plating. Expressed in t/m². Checked against hold capacity plans.
  • Global strength: Hull girder loading — longitudinal bending moment and shear force across ship’s length. Checked via Loadicator against class-approved limits.

A high density cargo may respect local tank top strength limits but still cause unacceptable global hull girder stresses if loaded in alternating holds — CE must check both.

Cohesive cargo — definition. Can you carry logs in a bulk carrier and what are the precautions?
IMSBCStabilityCargo

Governing Instruments

IMSBC Code (cohesive cargo), 2011 TDC Code — SOLAS Chapter VI Reg 5 (timber deck cargoes).

Cohesive Cargo

Fine particle moisture surface tension or chemical adhesion causes cargo to clump into a semi-solid mass. Does not flow freely. Primary risk: steep vertical faces during unloading can suddenly collapse — causing structural damage to hold framing and injury. Distinct from Group A: shifts as a mass rather than liquefying.

Timber / Log Carriage

Only permitted if ship has Timber Load Line (LT) under LL Convention Annex II, or special flag state approval. The Timber Load Line permits reduced freeboard in exchange for additional structural and securing requirements.

Stability Risk — Key Mechanism

GT and NT — how are they calculated, why is GT always bigger, and what dues are based on each?
TonnageConvention

Governing Instrument

International Convention on Tonnage Measurement of Ships 1969 (in force 1982). Applies to all ships ≥ 24 m LOA.

Gross Tonnage Formula

GT = K₁ × V
V = total volume of all enclosed spaces (m³)
K₁ = 0.2 + 0.02 × log₁₀(V)
GT is dimensionless.

Net Tonnage Formula

NT = K₂ × Vc × (4d/3D)² + K₃ × (N₁ + N₂/10)
Vc = cargo space volume; d = moulded draught; D = moulded depth
(4d/3D)² must not exceed 1
NT must not be less than 0.30 × GT (Article 4(4))

Why GT is Always Bigger

GT = ALL enclosed spaces (cargo + machinery + accommodation + voids + deck structures). NT = cargo-earning spaces + passenger spaces only. The engine room is a large enclosed space on container ships — it is included in GT but excluded from NT.

Significance — Which Dues Use Which

  • Port dues, canal dues (Suez/Panama), pilotage: Based on GT
  • Lighthouse dues: Based on NT
  • Suez Canal: Uses SCGT (Suez Canal Gross Tonnage) — own calculation method independent of 1969 Convention GT

Parametric rolling — cause, mechanism, why container ships are susceptible, cargo consequences, and CE’s correction.
StabilitySOLAS II-1Container

Governing Instruments

MSC.1/Circ.1228 (parametric rolling of container ships — information to masters). SOLAS II-1 stability.

Mechanism — Parametric Resonance

Occurs when wave encounter period (Te) ≈ 0.5 × natural roll period (Tr) — in head or following seas.

  • Wave CREST amidships: Large bow flare and transom lift out of water → waterplane area reduces → GM drops → GZ falls
  • Wave TROUGH amidships: Hull settles into water → waterplane area increases → GM spikes → strong restoring force

Cyclic GM variation at twice the roll frequency builds roll amplitude — can exceed 30–40° within a few cycles without any beam-sea excitation.

Why Container Ships Are Susceptible

Pronounced bow flare and wide transom produce dramatic waterplane area change — far greater than conventional hull forms with gentle entry and exit.

Compare the failure mechanisms of liquefaction, cohesive cargo collapse, and grain shift — risk and control for each.
IMSBCStabilityCargo

Why Compare These Three

All three are bulk cargo shift/failure mechanisms but operate by completely different physics — examiners use this comparison to test whether the candidate truly understands each, rather than reciting definitions in isolation.

1. Liquefaction (Group A — IMSBC)

  • Mechanism: ship motion builds pore water pressure within fine-particled, moist cargo until it exceeds the cargo’s shear strength — the cargo then behaves as a fluid
  • Risk: sudden large-angle list/capsize, often with little warning
  • Control: confirm Moisture Content < TML (TML = 0.90 × Flow Moisture Point) via certificate before loading; refuse loading if MC ≥ TML

2. Cohesive Cargo Failure

  • Mechanism: fine particles bound by moisture/adhesion form semi-solid blocks; during discharge, near-vertical faces and overhangs can suddenly collapse as large masses
  • Risk: structural impact damage to hold frames/tank top, crew injury — occurs at discharge, not at sea
  • Control: controlled trimming/excavation sequence, no personnel under overhangs, mechanical breakdown of blocks before they become unstable

3. Grain Shift

  • Mechanism: assumed internal shift, surface movement and void migration in free-flowing grain create a heeling moment under Grain Code assumptions
  • Risk: sustained angle of heel reducing residual stability/freeboard margin
  • Control: meet all three Grain Code criteria simultaneously (heel ≤ 12°, residual dynamic stability ≥ 0.075 m·rad, corrected GM ≥ 0.30 m); use shifting boards/bagged grain to reduce voids

Examiner Keyword Triad

Liquefaction = pore water pressure | Cohesive = block collapse | Grain = assumed shift / void migration

Why These Three Differ

Although all three are common bulk cargoes governed by IMSBC/Grain Code, each stresses a different part of the ship’s safety case — structure, gas/heating hazard, or stability — and the CE’s priority checks change accordingly.

Iron Ore / High-Density Ore (very low stowage factor)

  • Primary concern: structural — ores with SF ≤ 0.56 m³/t (bulk density ≥ 1,250 kg/m³) impose very high local tank-top loads (t/m²) and significant global hull girder bending/shear
  • CE checks: tank-top permissible load from class/plan, “Strengthened for Heavy Cargoes” notation, loadicator SF/BM within limits, especially for alternate-hold loading

Coal

  • Primary concern: liquefaction + gas/self-heating — coal can be listed under both Group A (liquefaction) and Group B (chemical hazard)
  • CE checks: MC < TML certification (Group A), continuous methane/CO monitoring and ventilation regime, awareness of self-heating risk (Group B)

Grain

  • Primary concern: stability — free-flowing cargo creates an assumed heeling moment under the Grain Code
  • CE checks: all three Grain Code criteria simultaneously — heel ≤ 12°, residual dynamic stability ≥ 0.075 m·rad, corrected GM ≥ 0.30 m; valid Document of Authorisation on board

One-Line Summary for the Exam

Ore = structure | Coal = liquefaction + gas | Grain = stability

How do the BLU Code, IMSBC Code, and the loadicator work together during bulk cargo loading?
IMSBCBLU CodeStability

Three Layers of the Same Operation

These three references operate at different stages of the same loading operation — cargo identification, operational sequencing, and real-time structural/stability verification — and must be used together, not as alternatives.

1. IMSBC Code — What the Cargo Is

  • Defines cargo Group (A/B/C), stowage factor, TML/MC requirements (Group A) and any special precautions (e.g. gas monitoring, self-heating)
  • Answers: “What hazards does this specific cargo present, and what certificates must accompany it?”

2. BLU Code — How Loading is Sequenced

  • Governs the Ship/Shore Safety Checklist and the agreed loading/unloading sequence between master and terminal
  • Ensures the sequence does not create excessive, localised hull girder bending or shear during the operation (not just at completion)
  • Answers: “In what order and rate will the holds be loaded so the structure stays within limits throughout?”

3. Loadicator — Real-Time Verification

  • CE inputs cargo weights stage-by-stage following the BLU-agreed sequence
  • Continuously checks shear force, bending moment and GM/stability against class-approved limits at every intermediate stage, not only the final condition
  • Answers: “Does the ship actually remain within structural and stability limits as we follow this sequence?”

Exam Synthesis Line

IMSBC defines the cargo and hazards; BLU defines the safe loading sequence; loadicator proves SF/BM and stability stay within limits while following that sequence.

As Chief Engineer, what are your “red lines” — situations where you will not proceed with loading or carriage regardless of commercial pressure?
SOLASIMDGIMSBC

Purpose of This Question

This is a judgement question — the examiner is testing whether the candidate understands that certain statutory requirements override commercial schedule pressure, and can articulate this calmly and with correct references.

Container Operations

  • No VGM = No Load (SOLAS VI/2) — will not load a container without a reconciled Verified Gross Mass, regardless of schedule pressure
  • Will not accept a container showing structural damage or without valid CSC approval/plate
  • Will not open a dangerous-goods container fire before it is fully cooled and the relevant EmS schedule permits it

Bulk Cargo Operations

  • Will not load Group A cargo if Moisture Content ≥ TML — liquefaction risk overrides schedule
  • Will not load grain without a valid Document of Authorisation and all three Grain Code criteria met
  • Will not approve a loading plan that breaches local tank-top or global hull girder (SF/BM) limits for heavy cargo

Chemical / Tanker Operations

  • Will not load a cargo not listed on the ship’s ICOF, or where MSDS shows incompatibility with tank coating/material
  • Will not discharge a Category X/Y persistent floater to sea — shore reception and pre-wash only

Timber / Deck Cargo

  • Will not proceed without accounting for the 20–30% weight-gain effect on KG/GM in the stability plan for the full voyage

SOLAS VI/2VGM — statutory prohibition on loading without verified gross mass
IMSBC CodeMC < TML requirement before loading Group A cargoes
International Grain Code (MSC.23(59))Document of Authorisation and three stability criteria
IBC Code / ICOFCargo must be listed on ICOF before acceptance for chemical carriage
MARPOL Annex IIPersistent floater discharge restrictions — Category X/Y to shore only
CE Oral Tip: Structure the answer as a confident list of 3–4 “I will not proceed if…” statements, each tied to a specific code/convention — this demonstrates judgement, not just memory.
