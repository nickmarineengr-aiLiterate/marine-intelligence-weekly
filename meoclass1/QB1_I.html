What is the ESP Code, in full?
surveyiacsExaminer: Nair
15-Second AnswerThe ESP Code is the International Code on the Enhanced Programme of Inspections during Surveys of Bulk Carriers and Oil Tankers, 2011 — IMO Res A.1049(27), mandatory under SOLAS Reg XI-1/2. It requires close-up survey/thickness measurement/tank testing for bulk carriers ≥500 GT and oil tankers ≥5,000 GT.
60-Second AnswerThe 2011 ESP Code was adopted 30 November 2011 by Assembly Resolution A.1049(27) and made mandatory through SOLAS Reg XI-1/2 amendments (Res MSC.325(90)), in force 1 January 2014. It applies to all oil tankers ≥5,000 GT, all bulk carriers ≥500 GT, and combination carriers. The Code has two Annexes: Annex A (bulk carriers — Part A single-side skin, Part B double-side skin) and Annex B (oil tankers — Part A double-hull, Part B other than double-hull). It defines overall survey, close-up survey (hand's-reach visual range), and thickness measurement extent, and mandates a dry-dock survey as part of the renewal survey with a minimum of two bottom inspections during the 5-year certificate period. It superseded the non-mandatory 1994 Guidelines (Res A.744(18)) and aligns closely with IACS UR Z10.

Full Title and Governing Instrument

International Code on the Enhanced Programme of Inspections during Surveys of Bulk Carriers and Oil Tankers, 2011 (2011 ESP Code) — IMO Res A.1049(27), mandatory under SOLAS Reg XI-1/2 via Res MSC.325(90), in force 1 January 2014.

Application

  • Bulk carriers ≥500 GT
  • Oil tankers ≥5,000 GT
  • Combination carriers (ore/oil, ore/bulk/oil)
  • Does NOT apply to container ships, general cargo ships, or gas carriers

Structure

  • Annex A — Bulk carriers: Part A (single-side skin), Part B (double-side skin)
  • Annex B — Oil tankers: Part A (double-hull), Part B (other than double-hull)

Key Survey Concepts

  • Overall survey: general hull condition, scope for further close-up survey
  • Close-up survey: hand's-reach visual inspection range
  • Thickness measurement: at substantial-corrosion areas or per renewal survey extent tables
  • Dry-dock survey: mandatory part of renewal survey; ≥2 bottom inspections per 5-year cycle

Key Numbers / Regs

  • A.1049(27) — adopted 30 Nov 2011
  • MSC.325(90) — mandatory application, in force 1 Jan 2014
  • Bulk carrier ≥500 GT / oil tanker ≥5,000 GT
  • Superseded A.744(18) (1994 Guidelines)

Chief Engineer Relevance

Even though ESP doesn't apply to container ships, class survey literacy across vessel types is standard MEO Class 1 expectation and directly testable regardless of current vessel type.

Trap Questions

Q: 'Does the ESP Code apply to your container vessel?' A: No — bulk carriers ≥500 GT and oil tankers ≥5,000 GT (and combination carriers); container ships use standard SOLAS Ch I periodical surveys.
Q: 'Difference between overall and close-up survey?' A: Overall = general condition assessment; close-up = hand's-reach detailed visual examination.

Common CE Failures

Calling it 'enhanced survey' without full title/resolution number; not knowing it excludes container ships; confusing close-up survey scope with ordinary Annual/Intermediate/Special survey scope.

Numbers to Memorise

A.1049(27) = 2011 ESP Code; MSC.325(90) = mandatory application; 1 Jan 2014 = in force; bulk carrier ≥500 GT / oil tanker ≥5,000 GT.

Casualty Link

ESP Code traces to the bulk carrier loss crisis of the late 1980s–1990s (structural failures, single-hold flooding losses) — the same pressure that produced SOLAS Ch XII.

Examiner Chain

ESP full title → resolution number → application → Annex structure → survey types → dry-dock requirement → non-applicability to container ships → SOLAS Ch XII cross-reference.

On My Vessel (Maersk A/S container, 2-stroke ME)

My vessel — a conventional Maersk A/S container ship, ABS-classed — falls outside ESP Code scope entirely. Container vessels are surveyed under the standard SOLAS Chapter I periodical survey regime instead: Annual Survey (hull, machinery, safety equipment, cargo ship safety construction), Intermediate Survey (2nd–3rd anniversary, broader than Annual), and Renewal/Special Survey (every 5 years, full re-certification incl. dry-docking, tailshaft/machinery survey extent per ABS class rules). There is no separate close-up survey mandate under SOLAS Ch I in the way the ESP Code requires for bulk/tanker hull structure — close-up examination on a container ship is applied at ABS surveyor discretion in areas of suspected wastage or fatigue (e.g. cargo hold frames, hatch corners, deck lashing bridges) rather than under a codified enhanced programme.

How will you register a second-hand ship acquisition in India?
maritime lawship registrationdgs ordercomplianceExaminer: Nair
15-Second Answer

Registering a second-hand vessel in India involves obtaining a Bill of Sale, securing an absolute release from the foreign registry, and ensuring the vessel strict compliance with the maximum age caps mandated by DGMA Order 01 of 2026. Once vetted, the application is processed through the DGMA (formerly DG Shipping) e-Samudra portal to receive a Certificate of Indian Registry.

60-Second Answer

To register a second-hand ship acquisition under the Indian Flag, the owner must fulfill the requirements of Part III (Registration of Vessels), Sections 14–44 of the Merchant Shipping Act, 2025 (in force 15 March 2026, replacing the equivalent 1958 Act provisions, formerly cited as Sections 24–46). Critically, the vessel must comply with DGS Order 01 of 2026, which establishes strict qualitative parameters and age norms, phasing out older vessels to ensure a modern, low-emission fleet. The acquisition must be audited via an IACS/IRS class society, and an Age Norms compliance plan must be filed via the e-Samudra portal. Upon submitting the Bill of Sale, Certificate of De-registration from the previous flag, and paying the statutory fees, the Principal Officer of the Mercantile Marine Department (MMD) issues a Provisional Certificate of Registry, followed by a Permanent Certificate of Indian Registry.

Governing Instrument

  • The Merchant Shipping Act, 2025, Part III — Registration of Vessels (Sec 14–44): In force 15 March 2026; establishes the legal framework for the registration of vessels (replaces the repealed 1958 Act, then Part V).
  • DGMA Order 01 of 2026 (formerly issued as a DG Shipping / DGS Order; DG Shipping renamed DGMA under the MS Act 2025): Establishes revised age caps and qualitative parameters for vessels operating under or entering the Indian Flag registry.

Definition or Core Concept Ship registration grants Indian nationality to the vessel, providing it with the protection of the Indian flag and requiring full alignment with Indian maritime safety, labor, and environmental laws. The registry ensures that sub-standard or excessively aged tonnage cannot compromise safety in Indian territorial waters.

Key Requirements or Procedure

  1. Age Norms Verification (DGS Order 01 of 2026): Before acquisition, the vessel’s age must be computed from the 'date of Delivery' stated on its Cargo Ship Safety Construction Certificate. It must fall well within the maximum age limits allowed for registration (e.g., specific age restrictions apply to bulk carriers, tankers, and cellular container ships to align with global fleet averages).
  2. Portal Submission: The owner submits an application along with an Age Norms compliance plan via the DGMA (formerly DG Shipping) e-Samudra portal.
  3. Vessel Evaluation & Survey: An authorized classification society (like the Indian Register of Shipping - IRS) executes a comprehensive audit and equivalent rating evaluation of the hull, machinery, and safety systems.
  4. Document Gathering: Secure the essential administrative trail:
  5. Bill of Sale (proof of legitimate ownership transfer).
  6. Certificate of De-registration / De-flagging from the previous foreign registry (preventing dual registration).

  7. Valid International Tonnage Certificate and statutory convention documents.

  8. Issuance of Registry: The MMD processes the file, matches the statutory fees, and issues a Provisional Certificate of Indian Registry to facilitate the vessel’s voyage to an Indian port, followed by the permanent registration.

Any Distinguishing Trap Point

  • Candidates frequently overlook the fact that DGS Order 01 of 2026 carries strict environmental clauses, such as mandatory Ballast Water Management System (BWMS) installation and low-sulfur or low-carbon energy efficiency retrofits. You cannot register a second-hand hull if its structural configuration makes an alternate-fuel or eco-upgrade unviable.

Examiner Trap: The examiner might ask, "If a second-hand container vessel is 22 years old and has been maintained perfectly by an international owner, can you import and register it in India today under a standard grace period?" Correction: No. Under DGMA Order 01 of 2026 (issued under the outgoing DG Shipping name, shortly before the DGMA renaming took effect on 15 March 2026), strict phase-outs target older tonnage immediately. Existing vessels already flagged are granted transition paths until 2029/2031 under Sustainability Indexing, but new second-hand acquisitions must strictly clear the modern entry age filters.

Key Numbers / Regs

  • DGS Order 01 of 2026: Formally enforces strict ship age limits and environmental qualitative rules beginning early 2026.
  • Part III: The current section of the Merchant Shipping Act, 2025 (in force 15 March 2026) governing registration parameters — formerly Part V of the repealed 1958 Act.
  • 31.03.2029: The general operating hard ceiling date for specific older existing vessels under the phased transition plan before requiring structural Sustainability Index approval.

Relation between DWT, NT, GT and lightweight — how are they related?
naval architectureship stabilitytonnagedisplacementExaminer: Simon
15-Second Answer

Displacement represents the total weight of the ship, which is mathematically the sum of its Lightweight and Deadweight (DWT). Conversely, Gross Tonnage (GT) and Net Tonnage (NT) are non-dimensional volumetric measurements of the vessel's internal spaces, unrelated to the physical weight of the ship.

60-Second Answer

The fundamental relationship between a ship's mass parameters is defined by the displacement equation: Δ (Displacement) = Lightweight + Deadweight (DWT). Lightweight is the fixed weight of the empty ship structure and machinery, while DWT represents the variable carrying capacity, including cargo, fuel, ballast, ballast water, and stores. In contrast, Gross Tonnage (GT) and Net Tonnage (NT) are regulated under the International Convention on Tonnage Measurement of Ships, 1969. They are dimensionless indices calculated using formulas based on total enclosed volumes (V) and cargo space volumes (Vc) respectively, and are used primarily for port fees, manning regulations, and taxation.

Governing Instrument

  • International Convention on Tonnage Measurement of Ships, 1969 (ITC 69): Standardizes definitions and equations for calculating GT and NT.
  • Load Line Convention: Governs the maximum draft, which implicitly dictates the maximum allowable displacement and deadweight.

Definition or Core Concept To master ship construction and stability, a Chief Engineer must separate mass-based parameters (which relate to flotation, displacement, and stability) from volume-based indices (which relate to commercial regulations and statutory fees).

Key Requirements or Procedure

  • The Weight Framework (Tons / Tonnes):

Displacement (Δ) = Lightweight + Deadweight (DWT)

  • Lightweight: The weight of the ship as built. Includes the hull structure, main and auxiliary machinery, boilers at working level, piping, and spare parts. It is determined via an inclining experiment.

  • Deadweight (DWT): The weight a ship can carry. It fluctuates dynamically throughout the voyage as cargo is loaded/discharged and fuel or fresh water is consumed.

  • The Volumetric Framework (Dimensionless Indices):

  • Gross Tonnage (GT): A function of the total enclosed volume of all spaces on the ship. Calculated as:

GT = K1 × V

(where V is total volume in m³, and K1 = 0.2 + 0.02 × log₁₀V) * Net Tonnage (NT): An index reflecting the useful capacity of the ship, primarily a function of the enclosed volume of the cargo spaces (Vc), adjusted for draft and passenger numbers. It must not be less than 0.30 × GT.

Any Distinguishing Trap Point

  • The "Tonnage" Misnomer: A classic pitfall is calling GT or NT "weights." If an examiner asks how many tons a ship's GT is, the correct answer is that GT has no units. It is a pure dimensionless index.

Examiner Trap: The examiner will ask, "If you burn 500 tons of fuel during a voyage, does your Gross Tonnage decrease?" Correction: Absolutely not. Burning fuel reduces your variable Deadweight (DWT) and total Displacement (Δ), but it has zero impact on Gross Tonnage (GT), because GT is calculated solely from the permanent, enclosed geometric volume of the vessel.

Key Numbers / Regs

  • 0.30 × GT: The statutory minimum value that Net Tonnage (NT) can fall to under ITC 1969 formulas.
  • 1969: The year of the International Tonnage Convention, which eliminated the older, confusing "register tons" system.
  • Δ = Lightweight + Deadweight: The absolute foundation equation of ship flotation physics.

Anniversary date of a certificate — how is it defined and used?
certificatessurveysolashsscExaminer: Simon
15-Second Answer

The anniversary date is defined as the day and month of each year corresponding to the expiry date of the relevant permanent International Statutory Certificate. It establishes the rigid 6-month window—extending 3 months before to 3 months after that specific date—during which mandatory annual and intermediate surveys must be completed.

60-Second Answer

According to SOLAS Chapter I, Regulation 2(n), and the Harmonized System of Survey and Certification (HSSC), the anniversary date means the day and month of each year which corresponds to the expiry date of the certificate. It functions as the permanent chronological anchor for a ship’s statutory timeline. Under the HSSC framework, annual and intermediate surveys must be executed within a specific +/- 3 month survey window around this date. If an mandatory survey is omitted or completed outside this designated window, the statutory certificate becomes automatically invalid, requiring a comprehensive re-survey and flag administration intervention to restore the vessel's legal trading status.

Governing Instrument

  • SOLAS Chapter I, Regulation 2 (Definitions) and Reg 14: Formally defines the anniversary date framework.
  • IMO Resolution A.1140(31) (Survey Guidelines under the HSSC): Outlines how the anniversary date structures survey windows across major conventions (SOLAS, MARPOL, LOADLINE).

Definition or Core Concept The anniversary date acts as the recurring annual deadline derived from a certificate's final 5-year expiration date. Instead of surveys drifting forward or backward based on scheduling convenience, the anniversary date guarantees that structural, safety, and environmental equipment are audited at standardized, predictable intervals throughout the ship's operational life.

Key Requirements or Procedure

  • The Window Framework: For any certificate with a 5-year validity, the annual and intermediate surveys must be carried out within a 6-month window centered on the anniversary date. This spans from 3 months before to 3 months after the day and month of definition.
  • Usage and Verification:
  • The Chief Engineer tracks all machinery-related certificates (e.g., International Air Pollution Prevention - IAPP, Safety Construction) against this date.

  • Surveyors inspect the vessel within this window, endorse the back of the active certificate, and maintain its operational validity.

  • Impact of Early Completion: If an annual or intermediate survey is completed more than 3 months before the anniversary date, the certificate is endorsed, but the original anniversary date on the certificate is permanently amended to a new date. This shifts all subsequent surveys forward.

Any Distinguishing Trap Point

  • The Expiry vs. Anniversary Trap: Candidates often confuse the actual date the certificate physically expires (at the end of 5 years) with the annual anniversary date. The anniversary date occurs every year, whereas the expiry date occurs only once at the end of the full cycle.

Examiner Trap: The examiner will ask, "If your ship is at sea and you miss the +3 month window after the anniversary date by just two days, can the Class surveyor simply endorse the certificate once you arrive at the next port?" Correction: Absolutely not. Once the +3 month window closes without a successful survey and endorsement, the certificate ceases to be legally valid immediately. The surveyor cannot just endorse it; a formal, more rigorous dynamic assessment/re-survey must be conducted, and the flag administration must be notified to issue a new or restored certificate.

Key Numbers / Regs

  • +/- 3 Months: The rigid operational window surrounding the anniversary date for executing mandatory annual and intermediate surveys.
  • 5 Years: The standard maximum validity period for primary statutory maritime certificates under the HSSC framework.

Explain any renewal survey in detail as Chief Engineer.
surveysafety constructionrenewalcomplianceExaminer: Nair
15-Second Answer

A renewal survey is a comprehensive statutory audit conducted every five years to verify that the vessel's hull, machinery, and critical safety systems fully comply with international conventions. As Chief Engineer, I would focus on the Cargo Ship Safety Construction Renewal Survey, which involves complete dry-dock tank inspections, machinery opening, and functional structural testing.

60-Second Answer

To execute a Cargo Ship Safety Construction Renewal Survey under SOLAS Chapter I, Reg 10, the Chief Engineer must systematically prepare the entire technical plant for a thorough five-year inspection. This survey mandates a rigorous evaluation of the vessel's structural integrity and machinery systems. The process requires cleaning and venting fuel oil, ballast, and void tanks for internal entry; cleaning and measuring wear on sea valves and sea chests; and opening selected components of the 2-stroke main engine, auxiliary generators, and steering gear for internal inspection. The renewal survey concludes with extensive operational testing of emergency systems, bilge/fire pumps, and remote quick-closing valves to verify that the vessel complies with all safety requirements before a new five-year permanent certificate is issued.

Governing Instrument

  • SOLAS Chapter I, Regulation 10: Formally dictates the survey requirements for the hull, machinery, and equipment of cargo ships.
  • IACS Extended Survey Programme (ESP) / HSSC Guidelines: Outlines specific, deep-dive structural thickness gauging and machinery testing mandates required at the 5-year renewal juncture.

Definition or Core Concept Unlike annual surveys which rely primarily on visual inspections and functional checks, a renewal survey is an invasive, bottom-up validation of the ship's physical condition. It confirms that wear, corrosion, and machinery degradation over the past five years remain well within safe, acceptable limits.

Key Requirements or Procedure As Chief Engineer, the execution of a Safety Construction Renewal Survey (typically synchronized with the 5-year dry-docking cycle) follows a strict sequence:

  1. Tank Preparation & Structural Inspection: Ballast tanks, fuel oil tanks, and cofferdams must be de-sludged, washed, gas-freed, and ventilated. Class surveyors enter these spaces to inspect structural stiffeners and floors, and conduct Extensive Ultrasonic Thickness Gauging (UTG) to check for steel wastage.
  2. Machinery Opening & Gauging:
  3. Main Engine & Auxiliaries: Open selected crankcase bearings, verify thrust bearing clearances, and review tailshaft wear-down records.
  4. Sea Inlets & Overboards: Disassemble sea chests, clear straining grids, and open up sea valves for internal inspection and grinding.

  5. Steering Gear: Inspect the rudder carrier bearing, perform hydraulic pressure testing, and check ram clearances.

  6. Safety & Remote Shutdown Systems: Test the operational integrity of all fuel oil quick-closing valves, emergency bilge suctions, remote engine room ventilation dampers, and emergency fire pump configurations.

  7. Testing and Documentation: Conduct full operational load trials of auxiliary machinery. Once the surveyor satisfies all criteria, they issue a clean survey report, which allows the administration to issue a new permanent Cargo Ship Safety Construction Certificate valid for another 5 years.

Any Distinguishing Trap Point

  • The Continuous Survey Misconception: Candidates often assume that if a ship is on a Continuous Machinery Survey (CMS) cycle, it skips the renewal survey. In reality, while CMS spreads out the opening of specific machinery components over 5 years, the structural hull assessments, tank entries, and comprehensive safety system testing must still be finalized during the formal renewal survey window.

Examiner Trap: The examiner may trap you by asking, "If a minor structural fracture or excessive steel wastage is found in a forward ballast tank during the renewal survey, can you ask for a condition assessment scheme (CAS) extension to repair it at the next intermediate survey?" Correction: No. During a statutory Renewal Survey, any structural defect or steel wastage that exceeds classification limits must be repaired immediately before the new 5-year certificate can be issued. Extensions are not granted for known structural failures that compromise the vessel's safety construction framework.

Key Numbers / Regs

  • 5 Years: The maximum validity of the resulting permanent certificate.
  • 2 Instances: The minimum number of dry-dock hull inspections required within any 5-year HSSC cycle (the interval between inspections must not exceed 36 months).
  • 20%: Typical allowable structural steel thickness wastage limit before mandatory replacement is triggered by Class.

IACS — what are its functions?
classiacsinternational standardscomplianceExaminer: Nair
15-Second Answer

The International Association of Classification Societies (IACS) acts as a technical advisory body that harmonizes the rules and standards established by its member societies. Its core functions are to enhance maritime safety and pollution prevention through Unified Requirements, Unified Interpretations, and Quality System Certification.

60-Second Answer

IACS consists of the world's leading classification societies and plays a critical role in global maritime governance. It establishes and reviews harmonized technical standards, known as Unified Requirements (UR) and Unified Interpretations (UI). These ensure that member societies apply international conventions uniformly. IACS also coordinates with the IMO as a primary non-governmental advisory body, developing safety rules like the Common Structural Rules (CSR) for tankers and bulkers. Additionally, it enforces strict quality controls through its Quality System Certification Scheme (QSCS), ensuring that all member societies maintain high standards of survey and inspection.

Governing Instrument

  • IACS Charter and Quality System Certification Scheme (QSCS): Establishes the criteria for membership and uniform quality standards.
  • SOLAS Chapter II-1, Regulation 3-1: Mandates that ships must be designed, constructed, and maintained in compliance with the structural, mechanical, and electrical requirements of a recognized classification society or an organization recognized by the administration acting in accordance with IACS standards.

Definition or Core Concept IACS provides a centralized platform to standardize technical rules across its member societies (such as ABS, DNV, LR, and IRS). This prevents a "race to the bottom," where societies might compete commercially by lowering safety and structural requirements.

Key Requirements or Procedure The core operations and functions of IACS are structured around four pillars:

  1. Developing Unified Requirements (UR): IACS formulates URs on design, construction, and machinery features. Once adopted, each member society must integrate these requirements into their own internal class rules. (e.g., UR E10 for electrical equipment type testing, UR M73 for turbocharger safety).
  2. Formulating Unified Interpretations (UI): When IMO international convention text (SOLAS, MARPOL) is ambiguous or open to interpretation, IACS develops a UI. This ensures that surveyors worldwide enforce the regulation consistently.
  3. Advising the International Maritime Organization (IMO): IACS holds permanent consultative status with the IMO. It contributes technical expertise, structural data, and research to help draft realistic international regulations.
  4. The Quality System Certification Scheme (QSCS): IACS performs robust external quality audits of its members. This ensures that all surveyors, technical estimators, and auditors adhere to high professional standards.

Any Distinguishing Trap Point

  • The Regulatory Status Trap: A common mistake is stating that IACS is an enforcement agency that issues international fines or acts as a flag state. IACS is a technical association. It creates standards and unified requirements, but the legal authority to enforce them remains with the Flag State Administration and its Recognized Organizations (RO).

Examiner Trap: The examiner may ask, "Can IACS directly cancel a ship's statutory Safety Construction Certificate if the ship skips a mandatory annual survey?" Correction: No, IACS itself cannot cancel a certificate. The individual Classification Society acting as the Recognized Organization (RO) on behalf of the Flag State has the authority to suspend class or withdraw certificates. IACS sets the standards and quality frameworks for its members but does not manage individual vessels.

Key Numbers / Regs

  • 11-12 Members: The typical core group of elite international classification societies that meet the strict criteria for IACS membership.
  • SOLAS Ch II-1, Reg 3-1: The structural bridge in international law that gives classification society rules their mandatory statutory authority.

▸ ESP Code applies to which vessels?
Bulk carriers ≥500 GT, oil tankers ≥5,000 GT. NOT container ships — they use standard SOLAS Ch I periodical surveys.
▸ DGS Order 01 of 2026 — what does it govern?
Age norms & qualitative parameters for Indian flag registration. Issued 21 Jan 2026. Existing vessels get until 31.03.2029 (extendable to 2031).
▸ Is GT a unit of weight?
No — GT and NT are dimensionless volumetric indices. Only DWT and Displacement are actual weights (tonnes).
▸ Anniversary date survey window?
±3 months around the certificate's anniversary date (day/month of expiry). Miss it → certificate invalid immediately, no grace period.
▸ Renewal survey — minimum dry-dock inspections in the 5-year cycle?
2 bottom inspections, interval between them ≤36 months.
▸ IACS's 4 core functions?
1) Unified Requirements (mandatory) 2) Unified Interpretations (clarify IMO text) 3) Advise IMO 4) Quality System Certification Scheme (QSCS) — audits its own members.
