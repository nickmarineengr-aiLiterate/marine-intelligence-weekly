Concept Map

One hull carries four different identifiers, each governed by a different regime and each with a different rule for whether it survives a reflagging.

Confusable Pairs — Examiner Favourites

⚠ Confusable Pair: FOC vs Open Registry
Every Flag of Convenience (FOC) ship uses an open registry, but not every open registry is a value-judgement FOC — FOC is the critical/loose-standards framing (ITF-defined); open registry is the neutral administrative description (any state can register any foreign-owned ship).
⚠ Confusable Pair: Official Number vs IMO Number vs Call Sign
Official Number = flag-state registration ID, changes on reflagging. IMO Number = permanent 7-digit hull ID for life, never changes. Call Sign = ITU radio ID, also tied to flag and reissued on reflagging.
⚠ Confusable Pair: Clear Grounds vs Detention
'Clear Grounds' is the PSC officer's threshold test to justify boarding/expanding an inspection (e.g. visible corrosion, expired cert). Detention is the possible outcome if that expanded inspection finds a deficiency serious enough to threaten safety/environment.

Topic Clusters — Tap to Recall

Jurisdiction & Legal Frameworks
Q1. Uniform Law and Conflict of Law in Maritime Context
Tap to reveal the 15-second answer →
Uniform Law refers to standardized international maritime conventions (like Hague-Visby, SOLAS, or MARPOL) designed to create consistent legal frameworks across all seafaring nations. Conflict of Law (or Private International Law) occurs when a maritime dispute involves multiple national jurisdictions, requiring courts to apply specific legal tests to determine which country’s laws apply and which court has the authority to adjudicate the matter.
Q5. Continuous Hot Pursuit
Tap to reveal the 15-second answer →
The Right of Hot Pursuit allows a coastal state to pursue and arrest a foreign vessel on the high seas if there is good reason to believe it has violated the state's laws within its internal waters or territorial sea. The pursuit must be "continuous" to prevent the abuse of state power on the high seas, meaning the chasing visual or radar signals must maintain an unbroken track of the target until the arrest is made.
Q6. Cabotage Law
Tap to reveal the 15-second answer →
Cabotage law refers to the statutory right of a sovereign nation to restrict its domestic coastal trade—the transportation of goods and passengers between ports within the same country—exclusively to locally flagged, owned, and crewed vessels. In India, this protection is enforced through the Coastal Shipping Act, 2025 (Ch II/IV) — GTL/CTL/SPL cabotage licence categories — which superseded Sections 406–407 of the Merchant Shipping Act, 1958 with effect from 15 March 2026.
Registration & Ship Identity
Q4. Fraudulent Registration of Ships
Tap to reveal the 15-second answer →
Fraudulent registration of ships refers to the illegal practice of registering a vessel under a national flag without the knowledge, authorization, or administrative consent of that flag state, or operating under a completely fictitious registry. The consequences include the immediate loss of right to fly a flag, port state detention, denial of insurance coverage, and severe criminal prosecution for the operators under international anti-smuggling and security frameworks.
Q7. STSDSS Full Form and Explanation
Tap to reveal the 15-second answer →
STSDSS stands for Ship-to-Shore Data Security System. It is a specialized cybersecurity and data-transmission framework designed to protect vulnerable information technology (IT) and operational technology (OT) systems on board commercial vessels from cyber threats during real-time data exchanges with shore-based fleet operations centers.
Q8. Flag of Convenience (FOC) / Open Registry
Tap to reveal the 15-second answer →
A Flag of Convenience (FOC) or Open Registry refers to a national ship registry that allows foreign shipowners to register and operate their vessels under its flag, offering low taxation, minimal registration fees, and flexible crew nationality requirements. India does not have an open registry; it operates a strict Closed Registry requiring vessels to be substantially owned by Indian entities and manned by qualified Indian seafarers.
Q11. Official Number
Tap to reveal the 15-second answer →
An Official Number is a unique, mandatory identification number assigned to a commercial vessel by its specific national flag state administration at the time of registration. Unlike a vessel's name, which can change frequently, the Official Number remains unique within that national register and is permanently carved or marked on the vessel's main structural beam as a statutory requirement.
Insurance, Refuge & Inspection
Q2. Port of Refuge
Tap to reveal the 15-second answer →
A Port of Refuge is a port or sheltered anchorage that a vessel seeks when forced by an extraordinary maritime peril to deviate from its course to preserve the ship, crew, and cargo from total destruction. The Master can request entry under the ancient maritime right of distress, and must communicate comprehensive safety, technical, and environmental data to the coastal state authorities.
Q3. Machinery Breakdown Insurance Claims
Tap to reveal the 15-second answer →
When a major machinery breakdown results in a Hull & Machinery (H&M) insurance claim, the primary documents accepted include certified engine room and bridge logbooks, bell books, Planned Maintenance System (PMS) histories, root-cause analysis reports, and surveyor statements. These events must be recorded immediately in the official Engine Room Logbook, the Master’s Bell Book, and the company's digital fleet asset management software.
Q9. RightShip Inspection
Tap to reveal the 15-second answer →
A RightShip Inspection is a highly specialized commercial vetting evaluation targeted predominantly at dry bulk carriers to assess a vessel's safety, environmental standards, and operational risk. It uses the RightShip Inspection Ship Questionnaire (RISQ) framework to provide a dynamic Safety Score (1 to 5 stars) that global charterers use to vet vessels before fixing contracts.
Q10. Clear Grounds
Tap to reveal the 15-second answer →
"Clear Grounds" refers to evidence or objective observations gathered by a Port State Control Officer (PSCO) during an initial shipboard inspection indicating that the operational condition of the vessel, its equipment, or its crew does not substantially comply with the requirements of international maritime conventions. The establishment of clear grounds legally empowers the PSCO to escalate the check into a "More Detailed Inspection."
