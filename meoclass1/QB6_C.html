🔧 Fail Safe, Layup & Machinery

3 questions
1
Fail safe — define fail safe. Give specific examples of fail safe systems onboard.
MachineryISM
15-Second AnswerA fail-safe system is a design principle where any failure of power, signal, or a component automatically forces the system into a pre-determined safe state, minimising risk to personnel, ship and environment. Examples: pneumatic fuel oil quick-closing valves fail CLOSED on loss of control air; ME overspeed trip shuts down fuel via a spring-loaded mechanical trip on signal loss.
60-Second AnswerSir, a fail-safe system defaults to a pre-determined safe condition on loss of power, air or signal. Two logic types: (1) De-energise to Safe — passive mechanical action, e.g. fuel oil cut-off valves fail CLOSED using spring-return on air-pressure loss, and UMS bilge high-level loops trigger alarm on open-circuit failure. (2) Energise to Safe — a loss triggers an alternate energy source, e.g. emergency generator auto-starts on blackout via spring/hydraulic accumulator. Other examples: boiler burner shutdown on low water, steering gear reverting to manual on autopilot power failure.

Definition

A fail-safe system is an engineering design methodology ensuring that if a component, control signal, or power supply fails, the system automatically reverts to a pre-determined, predictable state that prevents machinery damage, environmental pollution, and protects human life.

Principle of Operation

  • De-energise to Safe: relies on stored mechanical energy (springs, gravity) to actuate when holding force (electricity/air) is cut off.
  • Energise to Safe: absence of primary signal actively triggers an autonomous secondary system to restore vital functions.

Specific Onboard Examples

  • Pneumatic fuel oil cut-off valve: fails CLOSED via return spring on loss of control air, isolating fuel supply.
  • Steering gear autopilot: fails to MANUAL OVERRIDE on autopilot power loss.
  • ME overspeed trip: fails to SHUTDOWN via independent mechanical spring-loaded trip on signal loss.
  • UMS bilge high-level alarm: fails to ALARM if sensor cable is cut (de-energise to alarm circuit).
  • Emergency generator: fails to AUTO-START via stored spring/hydraulic energy on blackout.
  • Fixed CO2 system: mechanical cable/pilot-gas trip — remains operable without ship's electrical grid.
  • Boiler low-water level: fails to BURNER SHUTDOWN via fuel solenoid drop-out.
  • IGS pressure control valve: fails CLOSED on loss of instrument air, preventing hydrocarbon gas back-flow into non-hazardous spaces.

Fail Safe vs Fail Secure

Fail safe prioritises hazard mitigation — opens pathways or shuts down processes (quick-closing fuel valve). Fail secure prioritises containment — e.g. a watertight door or secure locker stays LOCKED on power loss.

Exam Test — Apply Fail Safe Logic

Apply the Zero-Power Test: total loss of power/air → what is the physical position/state? → is this the least hazardous state? If the system freezes in last position or drifts unpredictably, it is not fail-safe.

CE Relevance

As Chief Engineer, testing fail-safe logic is a critical safety-verification duty. During PMS/UMS testing, simulate the absolute failure mode (physically disconnect a wire, isolate instrument air) rather than pressing a "test" button, confirming springs, backup relays and downstream trips operate as designed.

Trap Question"Is the ME remote start system fail-safe?" — No: loss of remote start signal must fail to NO START, not to start, otherwise the engine could crank unexpectedly during maintenance or with turning gear engaged.

Common CE Failures

Naming a component without explaining the logic. Say: "loss of control medium causes internal springs to push the valve closed, preventing fuel propagation to a fire zone" — not just "the fuel valve is fail-safe."

Numbers / Regs

SOLAS II-1 Regs 46–52 (UMS block, incl. Reg 49 bridge propulsion control fail-safe logic) · IEC 60092-504 (open-circuit fault alarms)
Casualty LinkA container vessel blackout/grounding (2019) traced to a modified fuel control valve that failed "as-is" (held last position) instead of fail-closed on a 24V DC control-loop short, over-fuelling the engine and tripping the main breaker.

Examiner Chain

Nair → fail-safe definition → examples → "is the ME fuel rack fail-safe?" → UMS open-circuit fault monitoring requirements.

On My Vessel

Container (Maersk): ME remote-stop circuit runs de-energise-to-trip — a broken control wire anywhere between bridge and ECR immediately registers a safety fault, so loop integrity is continuously monitored.

Checks on main engine after layup — what are the mandatory checks before restart?
MachinerySurvey
15-Second AnswerReactivation after layup is a phased sequence: remove preservation → lube oil and cooling water quality checks → line up fuel and air start → slow-turn on turning gear with indicator cocks open → controlled, low-load start under Class Surveyor attendance.
60-Second AnswerSir, restarting requires a strict phased procedure to avoid mechanical damage or hydrostatic lock. First, clear preservation covers/plugs/grease. Second, purify the lube sump to remove moisture and check jacket water treatment. Third, line up and heat the fuel system to viscosity. Fourth, with indicator cocks open, engage turning gear for at least two full revolutions checking for fluid discharge. Finally, blow through on air, disengage turning gear, and execute a controlled low-load start under Class Survey oversight.

Phase 1 — Depreservation and External Checks

  • Remove blanks, silica gel bags, exhaust covers and preservation plugs on turbochargers/manifolds.
  • Clean anti-corrosive grease from piston rods, guides and linkages.
  • Inspect crankcase for corrosion, scaling or biological growth; verify foundation bolts and chocks tight.

Phase 2 — Lubrication System

  • Run lube oil purifier to remove condensation moisture; sample for viscosity, water%, TBN, acid number.
  • Prime lube oil and camshaft pumps; verify oil flow at crossheads, crankpins, main bearings, piston cooling.

Phase 3 — Cooling Water System

Fill jacket water, vent air locks, pressurise and check joints/liner seals for leaks; verify pH (typically 8.5–10), hardness and inhibitor levels.

Phase 4 — Fuel System

Flush preservation oil, line up HFO/MDO, run booster pumps, heat to injection viscosity, inspect injection pumps and high-pressure pipes for weeping.

Phase 5 — Air Start System

Drain water from air bottles/filters, charge receivers to design pressure, function-test remote stop valves and slow-turn interlocks.

Phase 6 — Turning Gear and Slow Turn

Open all indicator cocks fully, engage turning gear, rotate through at least two full revolutions monitoring cocks for water/oil/fuel discharge (hydrostatic lock check), then disengage and confirm the electrical interlock.

Phase 7 — Controlled Start

Air blow-through with cocks open to clear residue, close cocks, start on fuel at minimal load, run dead-slow monitoring exhaust temperatures and crosshead temperatures before increasing load.

Class Surveyor Involvement

A vessel emerging from official (hot or cold) layup undergoes a formal Reactivation Survey — a Class Surveyor must witness the initial start, verify safety trips (overspeed, low lube oil pressure) and confirm operational readiness before class certificate validity is restored.

CE Relevance

The Chief Engineer is accountable for confirming propulsion machinery reliability before departure — log every reactivation step, document Surveyor attendance, and ensure the SMS reactivation checklist is fully signed off.

Trap Question"You see a trickle of water from No.3 indicator cock during turning — can you proceed?" — No: water indicates a cracked liner, leaking cover gasket or exhaust valve housing. Firing air/fuel into that cylinder risks hydraulic lock, a bent rod or a cracked liner. Isolate cooling water and investigate before proceeding.

Common CE Failures

Skipping the turning-gear phase and jumping to start; omitting the Class Surveyor requirement; failing to mention lube oil purification/lab testing.

Numbers / Regs

SOLAS II-1 Reg 26 (propulsion machinery availability) · Class reactivation rules (functional trip checks) · ISM Code Sec. 10
Casualty LinkA multi-purpose vessel (2021) suffered a crankcase explosion and bent crankshaft two hours after leaving a layup berth — the lube purifier was not run long enough to remove sump condensation, causing bearing film failure under load.

Examiner Chain

Nair → ME checks after layup → water found in cylinders → which class certificates need renewal → first sea trial requirement.

On My Vessel

Container (Maersk): a 2-stroke slow-speed engine reactivation follows a dedicated 48-hour SMS sequence — pre-heating jacket water to ~60°C to avoid thermal shock, running cylinder oil alpha lubricators in manual mode to pre-lubricate liners, and verifying ECS backups are aligned before start.

Manual desludging of purifier — explain the procedure step by step.
MachineryMARPOL
15-Second AnswerWith the purifier idling at normal rpm and amps but feed off — bowl already emptied by a few desludge cycles — I take manual control of the water solenoids and step through the maker's timing table: closing water to confirm the bowl is shut, interface water, then opening water for the desludge itself, confirmed by sound and vibration, then closing water again, checking rpm/amps return to normal before repeating or restoring feed.
60-Second AnswerSir, first the correct PPE — face shield, gloves, ear muffs, torch, the specific screwdriver or tool for that maker's solenoid valves, a spanner for the inspection plug, and a stopwatch for timing. If the purifier is already running, I run a few desludge cycles to empty the bowl, then initiate the stopping sequence for the FO feed and let it come down to idle running — I do not switch on a fresh starting sequence with feed on. As a precaution I also shut the manual FO inlet/outlet valves. With the automatic program not started or stopped, I take manual control of the solenoids at the local panel. I confirm bowl-operation water is open using the pressure gauge, then open and shut the closing water per the timing table to confirm the bowl is closed — visible on amps and rpm. I open and shut the interface water per timing, then open the opening water — immediately I hear the desludge sound and feel the vibration — then shut it, then open and shut closing water again, and confirm rpm and amps return to normal. This cycle can be repeated. Finally I open the interface water per the timing chart, check the inspection hole for leaks, and after a satisfactory inspection I open the FO feed valves and start the auto run program on a shortened desludge interval to confirm the whole cycle is working, then restore the interval to 60 minutes or more as required.

When Manual Desludging Is Used

  • Auto desludge program is faulty, not started, or has been deliberately stopped so the engineer can control the sequence by hand.
  • Troubleshooting a suspected bowl-closing or sealing problem.
  • After a major overhaul — to verify the bowl closes properly before returning the unit to full unattended service.
  • Forcing an off-cycle desludge on heavy contamination ahead of the next scheduled auto interval.

PPE & Tools Required

  • Face shield and gloves — protection against water/oil spray from solenoid valves and the inspection plug.
  • Ear muffs — purifier noise at running speed.
  • Torch light — for viewing through the inspection hole and reading local panel indications.
  • Maker-specific tool for the solenoid valves — often a Phillips screwdriver, sometimes a special tool depending on design.
  • Spanner/tool to open the inspection plug.
  • Stopwatch or watch — to time each stage against the maker's timing table.

Precautions Before Starting

  • Feed must be off, not on — this is not the starting sequence. If the purifier is already running, first run a few desludge cycles to empty the bowl, then initiate the normal stopping sequence for the FO feed and let the unit settle to idle running (bowl spinning at normal rpm/amps, no oil feed). As a precaution, shut the manual FO inlet/outlet valves as well.
  • Have the specific unit's desludge sequence and timing table at hand — timings differ by purifier type and size, and even between units from the same maker.
  • Check your own vessel's purifier manual for exact timings on your type/model before the oral — quoting figures from your last vessel's actual maker table (not a generic value) makes for a far more confident, examiner-proof answer. Example — Mitsubishi HBC-1 (Table 5-4, SJ-series): T00 Interval, the gap between each solenoid valve operation, is 15 sec across most SJ models (20 sec on larger SJ160H/SJ220H); T01 Opening bowl (bowl-opening/desludge water) is a fixed 3 sec across all models; T02 Replacement water and T03 Sealing water scale with bowl size (roughly 6–39 sec and 6–34 sec respectively); T04 Bowl washing water is 0 sec for fuel oil but 10–56 sec for lube oil, sized by model; T16 Operating water supply for closing the bowl is a fixed 5 sec. These confirm the general pattern in this answer — closing/interface-type water in single-digit seconds, opening/discharge water only a few seconds, with a set interval gap between each solenoid step — but the candidate must quote their own vessel's actual table, not these Mitsubishi figures, unless that is genuinely their type.
  • Water naming is maker-specific and inconsistent across manufacturers — think in functional terms: water for opening the bowl, water for closing the bowl, and water for creating the interface (oil/water interface inside the bowl) — rather than memorising one maker's terms as universal.
  • Some designs use both high-pressure and low-pressure water for different stages, and solenoid valve throughput (flow rate) also differs by maker — Alfa Laval, Mitsubishi, and Westfalia each have their own values, so always confirm against that unit's table rather than assuming.

Step-by-Step Procedure

1. Prepare Purifier: if already running, complete a few desludge cycles to empty the bowl, then run the normal stopping sequence for the FO feed and let it settle to idle running (normal rpm/amps, feed off); shut manual FO inlet/outlet valves as a precaution.

2. Take Manual Control: confirm the automatic desludge program is not started, or is stopped, so the water solenoid valves can be operated by hand at the local panel.

3. Confirm Bowl-Operation Water: ensure the water supply for bowl operation is open, verified on the water pressure gauge.

4. Confirm Bowl Closed: open the closing water solenoid per the maker's timing table, then shut it — confirm the bowl is fully closed by watching rpm and amps hold steady.

5. Interface Water: open and shut the interface-creating water solenoid per the timing table.

6. Desludge (Opening Water): open the opening water solenoid per the timing table — immediately confirm the desludge by the characteristic sound and vibration felt through the casing — then shut it.

7. Re-close the Bowl: open and shut the closing water again per the timing table, and confirm rpm and normal amps are restored to steady running before proceeding.

8. Repeat if Required: steps 4–7 can be repeated for further desludge cycles as needed.

9. Interface Water & Leak Check: open the interface water once more per the timing chart, then check through the inspection hole/plug for any water leakage.

10. Restore Feed & Auto Program: after satisfactory inspection, open the FO feed valves and initiate the auto run program with a shortened desludge interval to confirm the complete cycle — all sequences — is working correctly; once confirmed, restore the desludge interval to 60 minutes or more as required by the maker/PMS.

Trap Question"Why would you run this manual sequence after a major overhaul, when the purifier hasn't even started producing sludge yet?" — Because the purpose isn't just sludge removal: stepping through the closing/interface/opening water sequence by hand and confirming sound, vibration, and no leakage at the inspection hole is how you verify the bowl closes and seals correctly after reassembly, before trusting the unit to automatic unattended operation.

CE Relevance

The Chief Engineer must ensure watchkeepers know each unit's specific water-naming convention, sequence, and timing table — since terms and timings are not standardised across makers — and understand that this manual sequence is used both for routine desludging and as a functional check after overhaul or during troubleshooting.

Common CE Failures

Starting the manual sequence with feed still on instead of first emptying the bowl and idling; assuming one maker's water-naming convention applies universally; not knowing the model-specific timing table; skipping the inspection-hole leak check; not recognising this manual sequence as a valid post-overhaul functional test.

Numbers / Regs

The manual desludge sequence itself is a maker's-manual operational procedure, not governed by a specific SOLAS/MARPOL clause. MARPOL Annex I Reg 12 and ORB Part I Code C apply to the retention, disposal, and recording of the sludge once ejected.

Examiner Chain

Nair → manual desludging sequence → why feed must be off before starting → why the same sequence is used after overhaul → how you confirm no leakage → sludge destination and ORB entry.

On My Vessel

Container (Maersk): Alfa Laval ALCAP separators in the centralised fuel treatment plant, normally on automatic PLC-timed desludge. When manual control is taken at the local panel, feed is stopped and the unit brought to idle running first, then the closing/interface/opening water sequence is stepped through per that unit's timing table — used for troubleshooting, and always run after a major overhaul to confirm the bowl closes and seals properly before returning to automatic operation.

Then don't lock onto one maker's water names — closing, interface, opening water is the functional sequence, timed per that unit's table. Walk the 10 steps, and mention the post-overhaul functional-check use case if the examiner probes further.

⚡ QB6_C Cheat Sheet — Fail Safe, Layup & Machinery

Q1 Fail Safe: Definition = defaults to safe state on power/signal/component loss. Two logics: de-energise-to-safe (springs — fuel valve fails closed) vs energise-to-safe (emergency gen auto-starts). Give 4–5 examples with failure scenario + resulting position + why it's safe. Trap: ME remote start fails to NO START, not start.
Q2 ME After Layup: 7-phase sequence — depreservation → lube oil (purify, sample TBN/water) → cooling water (vent, pH 8.5–10) → fuel (heat to viscosity) → air start (charge bottles) → turning gear (2 revolutions, cocks open, check for water/oil discharge) → controlled start under Class Surveyor. Trap: any liquid from indicator cocks = stop, investigate, do not proceed.
Q3 Manual Desludging: Bowl running at normal rpm/amps, feed OFF (empty bowl with a few cycles first, then normal stop sequence to idle — never a fresh start with feed on). Auto program stopped/not started → manual solenoids at local panel per maker's timing table: closing water (confirm bowl shut, watch rpm/amps) → interface water → opening water (desludge — confirm by sound + vibration) → closing water again (confirm rpm/amps normal) → repeat as needed → interface water + inspection-hole leak check → open FO feed, run auto program on short interval to test full cycle, then restore interval to 60 min+. Also used post-overhaul/troubleshooting to verify bowl closes properly. Trap: don't fix on one maker's water-naming — think function (open/close/interface), always confirm against that unit's own table.
Common thread: Nair rewards structured, phased, safety-first answers with the regulatory anchor named (SOLAS II-1 UMS block, MARPOL Annex I Reg 12, ISM Sec 7/10) — not just a list of steps.
