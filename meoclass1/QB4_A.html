📋 ISM Code & Safety Management System

4 questions
1
State the objective and functional requirements of the ISM Code. What must a Safety Management System contain?
ISM
15-Second AnswerThe ISM Code, made mandatory by SOLAS Chapter IX, requires every Company to establish a documented Safety Management System (SMS) to ensure safe ship operation and pollution prevention. Its outputs are a Document of Compliance (DOC) issued to the Company and a Safety Management Certificate (SMC) issued to each ship, both based on a functioning SMS that covers safe operation, defined responsibilities, emergency preparedness and continuous improvement.
60-Second AnswerThe ISM Code's objective in §1.2 is to ensure safety at sea, prevent human injury or loss of life and avoid damage to the environment, in particular to the marine environment and to property. To achieve this, §1.4 requires each Company to establish, implement and maintain a Safety Management System that includes: a safety and environmental protection policy; defined levels of authority and lines of communication; documented procedures for safe operation and pollution prevention; procedures for reporting accidents and non-conformities; procedures for emergency preparedness; and procedures for internal audits and management review. The SMS is verified through a DOC issued to the Company and an SMC issued to the ship, both normally valid for 5 years with periodic verifications (annual for DOC, one intermediate between the 2nd and 3rd anniversaries for SMC), and loss of the DOC generally leads to loss of SMC and inability of the ship to trade.

Governing Instrument

  • SOLAS Chapter IX makes the International Safety Management (ISM) Code mandatory for applicable ships and Companies.
  • The ISM Code is adopted by IMO and sets international standards for the safe management and operation of ships and for pollution prevention.

Objective of the ISM Code (ISM §1.2)

  • Ensure safety at sea.
  • Prevent human injury or loss of life.
  • Avoid damage to the environment, in particular to the marine environment, and to property.

Functional Requirements of the SMS (ISM §1.4)

  • A safety and environmental protection policy.
  • Instructions and procedures to ensure safe operation of ships and protection of the environment, in compliance with relevant international and flag State legislation.
  • Defined levels of authority and lines of communication between, and among, shore and shipboard personnel.
  • Procedures for reporting accidents and non-conformities with the provisions of the Code.
  • Procedures to prepare for and respond to emergency situations.
  • Procedures for internal audits and management reviews.

Key Certificates — DOC and SMC

  • Document of Compliance (DOC): Issued to the Company by the Administration or RO on its behalf. Valid for up to 5 years, subject to annual verification. Indicates the Company's SMS complies with ISM for the ship types listed.
  • Safety Management Certificate (SMC): Issued to each ship by the Administration or RO. Valid for up to 5 years, with at least one intermediate verification between the 2nd and 3rd anniversaries. Indicates the Company and shipboard management operate in accordance with the approved SMS.

Loss of the DOC typically leads to withdrawal or invalidation of SMCs for ships operated under that DOC.

Applicability

ISM applies to passenger ships, oil/chemical/gas tankers, bulk carriers, high-speed craft and other cargo ships of 500 GT and above engaged on international voyages, and to mobile offshore drilling units, in accordance with SOLAS Chapter IX.

DOC vs SMC — The Examiner's Trap

  • DOC: Issued to the Company. Covers one or more ship types. 5-year validity with annual verification.
  • SMC: Issued to the ship. Linked to a valid DOC. 5-year validity with at least one intermediate verification between the 2nd and 3rd anniversaries.

If the DOC is suspended or withdrawn, the SMCs depending on it cannot remain valid and the vessels cannot legally operate.

CE Relevance, Traps, Failures & Exam Strategy
CE Relevance

As CE, you operate within the Company's SMS and must know where engine-department responsibilities and procedures are documented (PMS, PTW, ORB, MARPOL, emergency response). You must ensure engine-room practices comply with the SMS, report non-conformities, and support the Master's overriding authority; if safety resources are inadequate, you escalate via the SMS chain up to the Designated Person Ashore (DPA).

Trap Questions

Q: "Who issues the DOC and who issues the SMC?"

A: Both are issued by the flag Administration or a Recognised Organisation on its behalf; the DOC is issued to the Company for defined ship types, the SMC is issued to each individual ship.

Q: "If the company's DOC is withdrawn, can the ship still sail with its SMC?"

A: No. Without a valid DOC, the SMC cannot be maintained; the ship cannot legally trade.

Q: "Is the ISM Code voluntary guidance?"

A: No. ISM is mandatory through SOLAS Chapter IX for applicable ships and Companies.

Common CE Failures

1. Confusing DOC and SMC (who holds them, validity and verification schedules), which undermines confidence in your understanding of the SMS framework.

2. Treating ISM as "paperwork" rather than the system that defines how PMS, PTW, drills and MARPOL compliance are managed in the engine department.

Numbers to Memorise

ISM §1.2 — objective. ISM §1.4 — six functional SMS elements. DOC: 5-year validity, annual verification. SMC: 5-year validity, intermediate verification between 2nd and 3rd anniversaries. Applicability: SOLAS IX — ≥500 GT on international voyages, and MODUs.

Casualty Link

The ISM Code was developed in response to serious casualties where organisational and management failures were key factors (e.g. passenger ship and Ro-Ro losses before the mid-1990s), demonstrating that safety cannot rely solely on technical rules and certificates.

As Chief Engineer, what are your specific roles and responsibilities as defined in the SMS manual?
ISM
15-Second AnswerAs CE I am responsible under the SMS for safe operation and maintenance of all machinery, PMS implementation, permit-to-work (PTW) control, MARPOL compliance in the engine room, near-miss and non-conformity reporting, and ensuring engine department personnel are competent and fit for duty. I support the Master's overriding authority under ISM §5.2 and escalate resource or safety shortfalls through the Designated Person Ashore (DPA) as defined in the SMS.
60-Second AnswerUnder the SMS, the CE is responsible for implementing the Company's maintenance and safety procedures in the engine department: planning and executing PMS, controlling PTW for high-risk work such as hot work, enclosed-space entry and electrical isolation, and ensuring MARPOL operations (bunkering, sludge handling, bilge discharge, incineration) follow documented procedures and are recorded correctly. The CE must verify that engine-room officers and ratings hold valid STCW certificates, are familiar with the SMS and emergency procedures, and are fit for duty; drills and training in machinery-related emergencies must be carried out and recorded. The CE supports the Master's overriding authority under ISM §5.2 and escalates any inadequacy in resources, manning, or safety standards to the DPA, rather than allowing commercial pressure to compromise safety.

Authority Under the SMS

The Master holds overriding authority for the safety of the ship under ISM §5.2. The CE exercises delegated authority within the engine department, defined by the Company's SMS, and is accountable to the Master for engine-room safety, maintenance, and regulatory compliance.

Core CE Responsibilities

  • PMS: Planning, execution, and recording of planned maintenance per the Company's PMS system.
  • PTW: Authorising and controlling permits for hot work, enclosed-space entry, electrical isolation, and working aloft in the engine department.
  • MARPOL compliance: Ensuring bunkering, sludge handling, bilge discharge, and incineration follow documented SMS procedures with correct ORB entries.
  • Crew competency: Verifying STCW certificate validity, SMS familiarisation, and fitness for duty of engine department personnel.
  • Emergency preparedness: Ensuring machinery-related emergency drills (blackout, steering failure, engine room fire, flooding) are conducted and recorded.
  • Reporting: Raising near-misses and non-conformities through the SMS reporting chain without suppression.

Escalation — DPA

If engine department resources, manning, or safety standards are inadequate and cannot be resolved onboard, the CE escalates through the Master to the DPA — the formal SMS escalation route designed to bypass commercial pressure.

A: I would raise my safety concern directly and clearly. The Master holds overriding authority, but safety concerns must still be communicated; if unresolved and safety-critical, the SMS provides for escalation to the DPA.

A: The CE assesses operational adequacy day-to-day, but manning levels themselves are set per the Safe Manning Document and Company policy — inadequacy is escalated, not resolved by working short-handed indefinitely.

An ISM non-conformity or deficiency is raised during an audit. What are the timescales for corrective action and how does the process work?
ISMSurvey

Definitions

  • Non-conformity (NC): Observed situation where objective evidence indicates non-fulfilment of a specified SMS requirement.
  • Major NC: A deviation posing a serious threat to personnel safety/environment, or a lack of effective and systematic implementation of an SMS requirement.

Corrective Action Timescales

  • Corrective Action Plan (Form CAP) must be proposed/submitted to the auditor within 2 weeks of the NC being raised, routed via the DPA.
  • The CAP schedule itself must not exceed 3 months for the corrective actions to be fully implemented — this applies to both Minor and Major NCs.
  • Major NC specifically: in addition to the 3-month implementation window, an additional audit must be carried out within 3 months of the NC being detected, to verify the corrective/preventive actions were effective.
  • Major NC not corrected in time, or CAP not submitted by the specified date: DOC or SMC may be withdrawn/invalidated.
  • Immediate threat: the Administration may take action to prevent the ship from operating.

Corrective Action Process

  1. NC raised by auditor with objective evidence (Form NCN issued).
  2. Ship/Company acknowledges in writing.
  3. Root cause analysis conducted — not symptom-only correction.
  4. Corrective Action Plan (Form CAP) proposed to the auditor within 2 weeks, via the DPA — itemising actions and a schedule not exceeding 3 months.
  5. Evidence of completion submitted as actions are closed out.
  6. Verification and close-out by Flag State/RO — for a Major NC, via an additional audit within 3 months of detection.

NC vs Deficiency — The Terminology Trap

"Non-conformity" is the ISM/audit term — failure against an SMS requirement. "Deficiency" is the PSC term — any observed failure during a Port State Control inspection. A serious PSC deficiency may trigger an ISM NC investigation, but they are separate instruments.

For a Major NC, add that an additional audit within 3 months verifies effectiveness, and that DOC/SMC withdrawal is the consequence of an unresolved major NC.

CE Relevance

NCs raised in the engine department are most commonly tied to PMS overdue items, PTW non-compliance, or ORB discrepancies. As CE you own root cause analysis and the corrective action evidence trail for engine-department NCs.

Trap Questions

A: No — different instruments, different raising authority. A serious PSC deficiency can prompt an internal ISM investigation, but they remain separate.

Q: "What happens if a major NC corrective action is not completed in time?"

A: The DOC or SMC may be withdrawn, and in cases of immediate danger the Administration may prevent the ship from operating. A Major NC also triggers an additional audit within 3 months of detection to verify the corrective action was effective.

Common CE Failures

1. Treating corrective action as closing the paperwork rather than addressing root cause.

2. Confusing "deficiency" and "non-conformity" terminology under exam pressure.

3. Quoting only "3 months" and missing that the CAP itself must be proposed within 2 weeks of the NC being raised.

Numbers to Memorise

Describe the internal audit procedure under the ISM Code. Who conducts it, what is checked, and what is the outcome?
ISM
15-Second AnswerISM Code §12.1 requires each Company to conduct internal safety audits of ships and shore-based activities at intervals not exceeding 12 months. Auditors must be independent of the area being audited; the outcome is an audit report recording NCs and required corrective actions, which feed into the management review under ISM §12.2 to evaluate SMS effectiveness and drive continuous improvement.
60-Second AnswerUnder ISM §12.1, internal safety audits must be carried out at intervals not exceeding 12 months — both onboard and in shore-based offices — and the auditor must be independent of the activities being evaluated. A shipboard internal audit typically covers: safety and environmental policy, PMS records, PTW register, drill records, ORB accuracy, LSA and FFA maintenance, crew STCW certificate currency, previous NC close-out status, and records of incidents and near-misses. The audit report identifies non-conformities requiring corrective action, which is then escalated to the §12.2 management review — a shore-management function where NC trends, incident data, and audit findings are evaluated to determine whether the SMS itself needs amendment or strengthening.

Regulatory Requirement

ISM §12.1 requires the Company to carry out internal safety audits to verify whether SMS activities comply with the planned arrangements. Both shipboard and shore-based operations must be covered. Auditors must be independent of the activities they audit — this is a requirement, not a guideline.

Who Conducts the Audit

  • Shipboard audit: typically conducted by the Master, Superintendent, or DPA-appointed internal auditor — not by the department head being audited.
  • The CE cannot audit their own engine room if it is the subject of the audit; the Master (independent of engine-room daily operations) may audit the engine department.
  • Shore audit: conducted by an internal auditor or DPA team, auditing shore-based SMS implementation.
  • Internal auditors are company-trained and authorised; they do not need to be flag-state appointed.

Audit Scope

  • SMS documentation: correct version onboard, crew familiarisation records.
  • PMS: records of planned, deferred, and corrective maintenance.
  • PTW register: permit issuance, closure, and associated risk assessments.
  • Drill records: fire, abandon ship, enclosed-space rescue, man-overboard — frequency and quality.
  • STCW certificates: currency and applicability for all watchkeepers.
  • ORB and MARPOL records: completeness and consistency with actual operations.
  • LSA and FFA maintenance logs: monthly/weekly tests, annual servicing.
  • Previous NC status: whether outstanding corrective actions have been closed out.
  • Near-miss and incident records: whether reported and analysed.

Audit Outcome

Audit report prepared with objective evidence for each finding. NCs categorised (NC or major NC) and entered into the NC tracking system. Corrective and preventive action plan agreed with timescales. Evidence of implementation collected for close-out. All findings and trends fed into §12.2 Management Review at company level.

CE Relevance, Traps, Failures & Exam Strategy
CE Relevance

Your engine-room records — PMS, PTW, ORB, drill records, STCW certificates — are the primary objective evidence reviewed during an internal audit of the engine department. Maintain these to audit-ready standard at all times, not just before a scheduled audit. If an NC is raised in the engine department, you are responsible for root cause analysis and corrective action; this cannot be delegated — your evidence quality determines whether the NC can be formally closed.

Trap Questions

Q: "Can the Master conduct the internal audit of the engine department?"

A: Yes — the Master is independent of engine-room operations and is a typical internal auditor for the engine department in a shipboard audit. Independence means not auditing one's own direct work area.

Q: "What is the §12.2 management review?"

A: It is the Company's periodic top-level review of SMS effectiveness — a shore management function. It uses audit results, NC trends, incident data, and near-miss reports to determine whether the SMS itself requires amendment. It is above the ship level and is not a shipboard audit.

Q: "Is a PSC inspection the same as an ISM internal audit?"

A: No. A PSC inspection is conducted by the Port State under UNCLOS Art 218/219; an ISM internal audit is company-conducted to verify SMS functioning. A serious PSC deficiency may subsequently prompt an ISM NC investigation, but they are separate instruments.

Common CE Failures

1. Not maintaining PMS and PTW records in audit-ready condition — deferred maintenance older than 30–60 days with no documented root cause or DPA notification gives auditors immediate NC material.

2. Submitting corrective actions that address only the symptom without root cause analysis — results in repeat findings at the next audit cycle.

3. Not knowing that §12.2 management review exists or its function — examiners expect CE candidates to articulate how audit results drive SMS improvement, not just how individual NCs are closed.

Numbers to Memorise

ISM §12.1 — internal safety audits; ≤12 months; independence of auditors. ISM §12.2 — management review of SMS by the Company. ISM §9 — NC reporting and analysis. ISM §13 — external verification.

🔍 PSC, FSI, Flag State & RO

6 questions
5
Describe how a PSC inspection is conducted — how do officers board, what are the types and levels of inspection, and how are deficiency codes used?
PSC / SurveyISM
15-Second AnswerA PSCO boards with official identification and the Master is informed immediately; the initial inspection checks statutory certificates and overall ship condition. If clear grounds exist, a more detailed inspection follows — and for high-risk ship types and ages, an expanded inspection is mandatory under MOU procedures. Deficiencies are coded by convention and action code: Action Code 30 = grounds for detention — the ship cannot sail until all detaining deficiencies are rectified and verified.
60-Second AnswerPSC inspection authority derives from each convention itself — SOLAS Ch I/Reg 19, MARPOL Art 5, STCW Art X, MLC Reg 5.2 — with UNCLOS Articles 218 and 219 providing additional port-State jurisdiction specifically over pollution and seaworthiness violations. A PSCO boards with official identification, immediately informs the Master, and conducts an initial inspection of statutory certificates and overall condition. If certificates are valid and conditions appear satisfactory, no further action is taken; but if there are clear grounds — expired certificates, inadequate crew, reports of deficiencies, or poor condition — a more detailed inspection follows covering any convention area. Expanded inspections are mandatory for passenger ships, bulk carriers, oil tankers, gas tankers and chemical tankers above certain ages; deficiencies are recorded using a convention code plus a Paris/Tokyo/IOMOU-aligned action code — Code 30 = grounds for detention, with Codes 15–19 covering non-detainable rectification timelines (next port, 14 days, before departure, 3 months for ISM NCs).

Legal Basis

Primary basis: each convention grants PSC inspection rights under its own text — SOLAS Ch I/Reg 19, MARPOL Art 5, STCW Art X, MLC Reg 5.2, Load Line Convention Art 21, AFS Art 11, BWM Art 9. Supporting jurisdiction: UNCLOS Art 218 gives the port State enforcement power over pollution-discharge violations even when they occurred outside its own waters; UNCLOS Art 219 gives it authority to require rectification or detain a vessel found unseaworthy and posing a pollution threat. For oral purposes: lead with the convention-specific clause relevant to the deficiency, cite UNCLOS 218/219 as the broader pollution-jurisdiction backstop.

Ship Risk Profile (SRP) — How Ships Get Selected for Inspection

PSC is not random. Every foreign ship calling at a Paris MoU, Tokyo MoU, or IOMOU port is assigned a Ship Risk Profile — High Risk Ship (HRS), Standard Risk Ship (SRS), or Low Risk Ship (LRS) — recalculated continuously on a rolling basis using generic and historical parameters:

  • Flag State performance: historical detention ratio and the flag's ratification/compliance record against IMO conventions (white/grey/black list under Paris MoU).
  • Ship type and age: bulk carriers, oil/chemical/gas tankers, and passenger ships attract higher scrutiny, especially above certain ages.
  • Recognised Organisation (RO/Class) performance: the classification society's own detention/deficiency track record with that flag.
  • Company performance: the ISM-certified operating company's fleet-wide deficiency and detention history.
  • Inspection history: number and severity of deficiencies/detentions over the preceding 36 months.

The SRP dictates inspection frequency and window — HRS ships are targeted every 5–6 months, SRS every 10–12 months, LRS every 24–36 months. This is the New Inspection Regime (NIR) principle adopted by Paris MoU (2011) and mirrored by Tokyo MoU and IOMOU.

Reasons a Ship Gets Inspected — Beyond the Risk Profile

Even a Low Risk ship can be boarded outside its scheduled window if an overriding factor exists. These include:

  • Involvement in a collision, grounding, or stranding en route to port.
  • Alleged violation of discharge/pollution provisions (MARPOL) since its last port.
  • Suspension, withdrawal, or change of class by its Recognised Organisation for safety reasons.
  • Reports or complaints from a pilot, agent, crew member, or another maritime authority alleging unseaworthy conditions or unsafe operation.
  • The ship is due under its risk-profile inspection window, or is on a targeted list (e.g. banned/refused-access ships attempting re-entry).

Concentrated Inspection Campaigns (CIC)

Each year, Paris MoU, Tokyo MoU, and other regional MoUs jointly run a Concentrated Inspection Campaign — a focused, typically 3-month enforcement period where a supplementary questionnaire is applied on top of the standard inspection for a specific high-risk topic (recent examples: fire safety, MARPOL Annex VI/EEXI-CII documentation, Ballast Water Management). Results feed back to IMO and inform the following year's targeting. CE relevance: ahead of a CIC period, ensure the specific documentation/equipment named in that year's campaign focus is inspection-ready — Class circulars and Flag State notices publish the CIC topic and questionnaire items in advance.

Clear Grounds for a More Detailed Inspection

"Clear grounds" means the PSCO has professional evidence, found during the initial inspection or from external information, that the ship, its equipment, or its crew does not substantially meet the requirements of the relevant convention. This is the trigger that escalates a routine document-check into a full detailed inspection. Typical clear grounds include:

  • Certificates found missing, expired, invalid, or not corresponding to the vessel.
  • Visible poor maintenance — corrosion, oil leaks, inoperative equipment observed by the PSCO while walking the deck/engine room.
  • Reports of deficiencies from another port or a previous PSC inspection not properly closed out.
  • Evidence of unsafe cargo loading/discharge operations.
  • Crew failing to demonstrate familiarity with essential procedures (e.g. cannot operate the OWS, cannot locate the SOPEP locker).
  • Crew complaints regarding living conditions, wages, or hours of rest (MLC grounds).
  • Indications of unseaworthiness or a pollution hazard reported by pilots, port authorities, or other credible sources.

Expanded Inspection

Mandatory under MOU procedures (e.g. Paris MOU, Tokyo MOU, Indian Ocean MOU) for passenger ships, bulk carriers, oil tankers, gas tankers and chemical tankers above certain ages — a deeper inspection covering structural and operational areas beyond the standard scope.

Deficiencies are recorded against a convention code (which convention/item is breached) plus a numeric action code describing the required response. This coding is a Paris MoU / Tokyo MoU / IOMOU operational standard (not an IMO instrument — IMO Res. A.1206(34) Appendix 13 records "action taken" as free text). Where Ships and Authorities Check the Risk Profile & Detention Record

PSC authorities, superintendents, and masters check a ship's SRP and detention history through regional databases, global registries, and commercial risk platforms:

  • THETIS (Paris MoU) — operated by EMSA, the live database that calculates and publishes each ship's risk profile and inspection window for the Paris MoU region.
  • APCIS (Tokyo MoU) — the Asia-Pacific Computerised Information System performing the equivalent function for the Tokyo MoU region.
  • IOCIS (Indian Ocean MoU) — the equivalent system for IOMOU, the operative region for Indian ports, shared in real time across all IOMOU member authorities (a detention in Colombo is visible in Mumbai before the ship arrives).
  • MISLE (US Coast Guard) — the USCG's internal targeting and public detention-record system, separate from the MoU network.
  • Equasis — a free, IMO-supported public registry consolidating inspection, detention, and company-performance data across all major MoUs — the standard first check for superintendents and this exam.

Your vessel is detained by PSC. What are the grounds for detention, what actions do you take, and what is the appeal procedure?
PSC / SurveyISM
15-Second AnswerPSC detention requires deficiencies that are clearly hazardous to safety, health, or the environment. As CE I immediately inform Master → DPA → Flag State → Class, obtain the detention report, prepare a corrective action plan, arrange specialist support if needed, and request re-inspection only after all deficiencies are rectified. An appeal does not lift the detention — the ship cannot sail pending outcome.

Grounds for Detention

Detention under SOLAS Ch I/Reg 19(c) applies when deficiencies are individually or cumulatively hazardous to safety, health, or the environment — e.g. inoperative steering gear, expired or absent statutory certificates, failed fire detection/suppression, manning below the Safe Manning Document, or serious MARPOL non-compliance.

CE Immediate Actions

  1. Inform Master immediately — Master initiates the notification chain.
  2. Notification chain: Master → DPA → Flag State → Class (RO).
  3. Obtain the formal detention report listing detaining deficiencies.
  4. Prepare a corrective action plan with timeline.
  5. Arrange specialist support (class surveyor, OEM technician) as required.
  6. Collect evidence of rectification.
  7. Request re-inspection only once all detaining deficiencies are rectified.

A written appeal may be lodged with the Port State authority. Critically, the appeal does NOT automatically lift the detention — the ship remains detained pending the outcome unless the authority is satisfied independently. If the appeal succeeds and detention is found unjust, costs may be recoverable from the Port State.

What is the difference between a Flag State and a Recognised Organisation (RO)? What statutory authority does an RO carry?
IACS / ClassSurvey

Flag State

The country in which a ship is registered. Under UNCLOS Art 94, the Flag State carries primary, non-transferable responsibility for ensuring its ships comply with international conventions.

Recognised Organisation (RO)

An organisation (typically a classification society) authorised by the Flag State under SOLAS Ch I/Reg 6 to carry out statutory surveys and issue certificates on its behalf. The RO acts as the Flag State's delegated agent for these functions.

The Core Legal Boundary

The Flag State can delegate the functions of survey and certification to an RO, but it cannot delegate responsibility. Under UNCLOS Art 94, ultimate accountability for compliance remains with the Flag State even when surveys are physically conducted by an RO.

For Indian-Flagged Ships

The Indian Register of Shipping (IRS) is India's recognised classification society and commonly acts as RO for Indian-flagged vessels, conducting statutory surveys on behalf of the Director General of Shipping (DGS).

What Does 'Statutory' Mean?

A statutory certificate is one mandated by an international convention (SOLAS, MARPOL, Load Lines, ISM, ISPS, MLC, CLC) and issued by or on behalf of the Flag State. It is legally distinct from a class certificate, which is issued by the classification society under its own rules and has no direct treaty force.

Statutory Certificates by Convention

  • SOLAS: Safety Construction Certificate (SCC), Safety Equipment Certificate (SEC), Safety Radio Certificate (SRC), Safety Management Certificate (SMC), International Ship Security Certificate (ISSC), Continuous Synopsis Record (CSR), Minimum Safe Manning Document.
  • Load Lines Convention: International Load Line Certificate (ILLC).
  • MARPOL: International Oil Pollution Prevention Certificate (IOPPC, Annex I), International Air Pollution Prevention Certificate (IAPPC, Annex VI).
  • ISM Code: Document of Compliance (DOC, to Company), Safety Management Certificate (SMC, to Ship).
  • MLC 2006: Maritime Labour Certificate + DMLC Parts I & II.
  • CLC/Bunker Convention: Civil Liability Certificate (insurance evidence for pollution liability).

The DOC Onboard Trap

The DOC is issued to the Company, not the ship. A certified copy must be carried onboard — the original is held ashore by the Company. This is distinct from the SMC, which is issued directly to the ship.
