1
Name other Acts related to shipping besides the MS Act (e.g. Recycling of Ships Act, Admiralty Act) — what does the Admiralty Act contain?
COMMERCIAL LAW

15-Second Answer

Besides the Merchant Shipping Act, Indian shipping is governed by the Admiralty (Jurisdiction and Settlement of Maritime Claims) Act 2017, the Recycling of Ships Act 2019, the Major Port Authorities Act 2021, and the Marine Insurance Act 1963. The Admiralty Act 2017 vests admiralty jurisdiction in coastal High Courts, enumerates maritime claims, and sets out the ship-arrest and maritime-lien-priority framework.

60-Second Answer

India's key shipping-related Acts beyond the Merchant Shipping Act, 2025 (in force 15 March 2026, replacing the 1958 Act) are: the Admiralty (Jurisdiction and Settlement of Maritime Claims) Act 2017, the Recycling of Ships Act 2019 (domestic codification of the Hong Kong Convention), the Coastal Shipping Act 2025 (cabotage/coasting trade, now separate from the MS Act), the Major Port Authorities Act 2021 (port administration/tariffs), and the Marine Insurance Act 1963 (hull/cargo insurance rules). The Admiralty Act 2017 repealed colonial-era admiralty statutes (Admiralty Court Act 1861 etc.) and now: vests exclusive admiralty jurisdiction in designated coastal High Courts (S.3); enumerates maritime claims that can found an admiralty action (S.4); sets the conditions for arresting a vessel in rem, including sister-ship arrest (S.5); governs in personam actions (S.6); and separately defines maritime liens and their own inter se priority, with a one-year extinction period unless the vessel is arrested first (S.9), while the broader ranking of maritime claims in a judicial-sale distribution sits under S.10.

Governing instrument

Admiralty (Jurisdiction and Settlement of Maritime Claims) Act 2017; Recycling of Ships Act 2019; Major Port Authorities Act 2021; Marine Insurance Act 1963; Merchant Shipping Act 1958 (baseline).

Key Acts beyond the MS Act

  • Admiralty Act 2017 — ship arrest, maritime claims, judicial sale; repealed the 1861 Admiralty Court Act and related colonial statutes.
  • Recycling of Ships Act 2019 — domestic enforcement of the Hong Kong Convention for eco-friendly recycling.
  • Major Port Authorities Act 2021 — administration/tariffs at major commercial ports.
  • Marine Insurance Act 1963 — hull, machinery and cargo insurance rules and liabilities.

What the Admiralty Act 2017 contains

  • S.3: vests admiralty jurisdiction in designated coastal High Courts (Bombay, Madras, Calcutta, and — since 2017 — Kerala, Gujarat, Hyderabad, Orissa, Karnataka).
  • S.4: enumerates the maritime claims a High Court can adjudicate (ownership disputes, mortgages, collision/tort, cargo loss, salvage, wages, etc.) — exact clause count varies by source; verify the current total against the Act text before quoting a specific number.
  • S.5: sets the conditions for arresting a vessel in rem (including a sister ship under S.5(2), owned or demise-chartered by the same liable party).
  • S.6: governs in personam actions against the owner/charterer.
  • S.9: defines maritime liens and their own inter se ranking, extinguished after 1 year unless the vessel is arrested/seized first.
  • S.10: sets the broader order of priority among maritime claims generally in a judicial-sale distribution (crew wages/injury claims typically rank highest).

Trap point

Don't say "Section 9 sets the priority order of all claims" — S.9 is specifically about maritime liens and their own ranking/1-year extinction; the broader claims-priority ranking used in a judicial sale is S.10. Also, don't cite obsolete colonial statutes (Admiralty Court Act 1861) as still governing — they were repealed in 2017.

Deep-Dive: CE Relevance

An arrest under the Admiralty Act halts all commercial/technical operations immediately — the CE must preserve evidence (bell books, engine parameters, oil record books), manage restricted fuel/stores during the hold, and liaise closely with the P&I surveyor.

Deep-Dive: Trap Questions

Q: "Can a sister ship be arrested for a claim against another vessel in the same fleet?" A: Yes — S.5(2) permits sister-ship arrest where the same person owned/demise-chartered both vessels when the claim arose. Q: "Do crew wages outrank a registered mortgage in a judicial sale?" A: Yes — maritime liens (including crew wages) rank ahead of mortgages and other registered charges.

Deep-Dive: Common CE Failures

Citing repealed colonial statutes as current law; confusing S.9 (maritime lien definition/ranking) with S.10 (general claims priority); treating all 4(S.4) claims as carrying lien status when only the S.9-listed liens do.

Deep-Dive: Numbers to Memorise

2017 = Admiralty Act enactment year; 1 year = maritime lien extinction period unless the vessel is arrested first; verify the exact S.4 claim count against the Act text rather than quoting a fixed number from memory.

Deep-Dive: On My Vessel

Meticulous retention of BDNs (bunker delivery notes), scavenge/scraper-ring analysis logs, and electronic engine automation logs is critical to defending a Maersk vessel against fuel-quality or engine-breakdown disputes that could trigger an S.4 claim.

What is meant by 'in rem' and 'in personam' in maritime legal proceedings?
COMMERCIAL LAW

15-Second Answer

An action in rem is filed against the vessel itself as the defendant, allowing arrest to secure a claim regardless of where the owner is. An action in personam is filed against a specific person/company (e.g. the shipowner), attaching to their broader assets rather than the ship.

60-Second Answer

Under the Admiralty Act 2017, an in rem action names the vessel itself as defendant (S.5) — the High Court can order its arrest to secure security for the claim, purely on the basis that the ship is physically within Indian jurisdiction, irrespective of where the owner is based. An in personam action (S.6) instead proceeds against a specific legal person — the shipowner or operating company — and a resulting judgment attaches to that company's general assets, not just the ship. Once security is posted to release an arrested vessel, the action typically continues in personam against the owner while the posted security stands in for the ship (the res).

Governing instrument

Admiralty Act 2017, S.5 (in rem) and S.6 (in personam); International Convention on Arrest of Ships 1952/1999 (international baseline).

Core distinction

  • In rem ("against the thing"): proceeding directed at the vessel itself as the wrongdoer, enabling arrest to secure the claim.
  • In personam ("against the person"): proceeding against a specific individual or company based on contract/tort/statutory liability.

Procedure

  1. Claimant files suit naming the vessel; the court issues an arrest warrant.
  2. Arrest forces the owner/P&I Club to post security (bail bond/letter of undertaking).
  3. Once security is posted, the action effectively continues in personam against the owner, with the security replacing the res.
  4. If no security is posted and the claimant prevails, the court can order a judicial sale.

Trap point

In rem jurisdiction does not require the owner to be Indian or the ship Indian-flagged — it is purely territorial: if the ship is physically in Indian waters, it can be arrested. But in rem recovery is capped at the ship's value/freight; anything beyond that needs a separate in personam action against the company's wider assets.

CE Oral Tip: Simon's classic follow-up: "If the ship is arrested, who is the defendant?" Answer: "The ship itself, sir — the suit is filed against the vessel by name, not the owner." Don't say "the owner" first.
Deep-Dive: CE Relevance

An in rem warrant targets the ship itself, so the entire engine department must immediately preserve records — electronic data-logger files, oil record books, maintenance logs — as primary evidence for the P&I lawyers contesting the action.

Deep-Dive: Trap Questions

Q: "Can an in rem arrest happen even if the contract requires arbitration in London?" A: Yes — an Indian High Court can still arrest the vessel to secure the claim pending the foreign arbitration outcome. Q: "Does a judicial-sale buyer inherit the old owner's debts?" A: No — a judicial sale under an in rem decree transfers the ship free of prior liens/mortgages; those claims transfer to the sale proceeds instead.

Deep-Dive: Common CE Failures

Believing in rem means the action is against the crew or Master rather than the vessel itself; assuming in rem requires Indian ownership/flag.

Deep-Dive: Numbers to Memorise

S.5 = in rem arrest conditions; S.6 = in personam jurisdiction; in rem recovery capped at ship's value/freight.

Deep-Dive: On My Vessel

During any port-arrest scenario, the CE must implement the company's port-arrest checklist — safe shutdown of non-essential machinery, strict bunkering/bilge discipline, and zero tolerance for altered logbook entries, which would destroy the evidentiary value of the res.

How is an IMO convention formally adopted? Explain the conference process, protocol, amendments, tacit vs explicit acceptance, the role of committees/sub-committees, the FAL Committee's function, and the difference between ratification, acceptance, approval, and accession.
IMO PROCESS

15-Second Answer

A convention is drafted through IMO's committee/sub-committee structure (MSC, MEPC, LEG, FAL, TC), adopted at a diplomatic conference, then opened for signature and ratification/acceptance/approval/accession by states, entering into force once threshold conditions are met. Once in force, most technical amendments pass via tacit acceptance — deemed adopted unless a set proportion of states object — rather than needing fresh ratification.

60-Second Answer

A convention begins as a proposal debated in an IMO sub-committee, refined and adopted at committee level (MSC for safety, MEPC for environment, LEG for legal, FAL for facilitation, TC for technical cooperation), then formally adopted at a diplomatic conference of member states. It's opened for signature, and states express consent to be bound via ratification (if signed within the open period), acceptance or approval (broadly equivalent alternatives some states use), or accession (joining after the signature period has closed) — all four have identical legal effect under the Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties 1969. Entry into force typically needs a minimum number of states representing a minimum share of world gross tonnage. Once in force, most technical amendments (SOLAS/MARPOL annexes) pass via tacit acceptance — deemed accepted on a set date unless objected to by a specified proportion of Parties — rather than the slower explicit acceptance route requiring active ratification of every amendment.

Governing instrument

Convention on the International Maritime Organization 1948 (establishes IMO's institutional structure); SOLAS Art.VIII and MARPOL Art.16 (amendment/tacit-acceptance procedure); Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties 1969, Art.11 (means of expressing consent to be bound).

The adoption pipeline

  1. Proposal — raised by a member state/industry body.
  2. Sub-Committee — technical drafting.
  3. Committee (MSC/MEPC/LEG/FAL/TC) — refines and adopts the text for conference.
  4. Diplomatic Conference — formal adoption by assembled states.
  5. Signature — convention opened for signature for a defined period.
  6. Ratification / Acceptance / Approval / Accession — states formally consent to be bound.
  7. Entry into force — once threshold conditions (minimum states + minimum world gross tonnage share) are met.

Ratification vs Acceptance vs Approval vs Accession

All four express a state's consent to be bound and have identical legal effect (VCLT Art.11). Ratification is used when a state signed during the open signature period; Accession is used when a state joins after signature has closed; Acceptance/Approval are largely interchangeable alternative terms some states use instead of "ratification" depending on their domestic constitutional procedure.

Tacit vs explicit acceptance (post entry-into-force amendments)

Explicit acceptance requires each Party to actively ratify every amendment — slow. Tacit acceptance (used for most SOLAS/MARPOL technical annexes) deems an amendment accepted on a stated date unless a specified proportion of Parties formally object before the deadline — this is why technical updates propagate quickly across the fleet.

FAL Committee

Handles facilitation of international maritime traffic — simplifying formalities, documentation, and procedures for ships calling at ports (e.g. the FAL Convention 1965 and its Maritime Single Window mandate).

Trap point

Tacit acceptance does not mean states must vote yes — it passes by default unless enough Parties object. Don't confuse the FAL Committee (facilitation/paperwork) with LEG (legal claims/liability) or MEPC (environment).

CE Oral Tip: Simon often drills the Ratification-vs-Accession distinction. Keep it crisp: "Ratification applies if a state signed while the treaty was open for signature; Accession is joining after signature closed — same legal effect either way."
Deep-Dive: CE Relevance

Every major equipment mandate the CE deals with (ECDIS, EEXI/CII, SCR units) reached the fleet because a committee-drafted amendment passed via tacit acceptance — the CE cannot claim ignorance just because the flag state didn't explicitly vote for it; unless the flag state formally objected, it applies on the enforcement date.

Deep-Dive: Trap Questions

Q: "Difference between an IMO Code and an IMO Convention?" A: A Convention is the overarching treaty (e.g. SOLAS); a Code (ISM, IGC, IGF) is the detailed technical regulation made mandatory by reference within a chapter of that Convention. Q: "Can a state ignore an amendment that passed via tacit acceptance?" A: No — once past the objection deadline it's mandatory; ignoring it exposes the fleet to PSC detention.

Deep-Dive: Common CE Failures

Saying tacit acceptance requires unanimous agreement to pass (it's the opposite — passes by default on silence); confusing the FAL Committee with LEG or MEPC.

Deep-Dive: Numbers to Memorise

5 core IMO committees: MSC, MEPC, LEG, FAL, TC. Entry-into-force and amendment-objection thresholds are tonnage/state-count based — verify the current specific percentage for the instrument in question rather than quoting one fixed number for all conventions.

Deep-Dive: On My Vessel

Recent CII/EEXI amendments came through the MEPC track and took effect via tacit acceptance, requiring the CE to adapt engine-load management and fuel-logging practice on schedule regardless of a formal fleet-level vote.

What is an Official Log Book?
ISMMLC

15-Second Answer

The Official Log Book (OLB) is a statutory legal document, in the Master's custody, recording significant administrative, disciplinary, medical and casualty events — distinct from operational logs like the Engine Log Book, and carrying heavy evidentiary weight in court.

60-Second Answer

The Official Log Book is a statutory document required under the Merchant Shipping Act, 2025 (Section 112, Part V — Seafarers; in force 15 March 2026, replacing the 1958 Act's Part VII provisions) that must be kept on every commercial vessel, maintained personally by the Master as a running legal diary of the ship's life. It records crew changes, births/deaths, disciplinary offences and fines, marine casualties, boat/fire drills, and steering-gear tests. Every entry must carry the date/time of the event and of the entry, and be signed by the Master and witnessed (often by the Chief Officer or Chief Engineer). Errors must be struck through with a single line and initialled — never erased or corrected with fluid. The OLB must be handed to the Shipping Master/Port Officer within a set window after final port arrival, and is the primary document examined in marine casualty inquiries, P&I claims, and MLC 2006 audits.

Governing instrument

Merchant Shipping Act, 2025, Section 112 (Part V — Seafarers), in force 15 March 2026, replacing the repealed 1958 Act's Part VII log book provisions; Merchant Shipping (Official Log Books) Rules; MLC 2006.

Core concept

The OLB is a flag-administration-issued legal register — a floating extension of statutory record-keeping that converts everyday shipboard events into admissible legal evidence, distinct from operational technical logs.

Key requirements

  • Custody: the Master personally maintains it; entries made as soon as possible after the event.
  • Entry format: date/time of event + date/time of entry, signed by Master and witnessed (typically Chief Officer/Chief Engineer).
  • Corrections: single-line strike-through with initials — no erasure, no correction fluid, no torn-out pages.
  • Submission: handed to the Shipping Master/Port Officer within the statutory window after final port arrival or crew sign-off.

Key entries required

  • Crew appointments, promotions, desertions, sign-offs.
  • Disciplinary offences, fines, hearings.
  • Casualties: collisions, groundings, major machinery breakdowns, injuries.
  • Births, marriages, deaths at sea, medical treatment given.
  • Statutory drills and periodic steering-gear/emergency-power tests.

Trap point

Don't confuse the OLB with the Deck Log or Engine Log Book — those are operational tools kept by watch officers for routine parameters; they carry no statutory legal status until formally reflected in the OLB. There is only one OLB per vessel, in the Master's custody — the CE does not keep a separate statutory log.

CE Oral Tip: Nair focuses on entry correctness. If asked about an engine-room dispute entry: state you'll conduct an inquiry with the Master, prepare a written technical statement, have the Master write the OLB entry, and sign it yourself as the technical witness — don't claim you'd write the entry yourself.
Deep-Dive: CE Relevance

When a technical incident escalates into a statutory event — blackout, steering-gear failure, a serious disciplinary matter — the CE must give the Master a precise technical timeline and witness signature for the OLB entry to protect the vessel's legal position.

Deep-Dive: Trap Questions

Q: "Can you use white-out to correct an OLB entry before the Master signs?" A: No — strike through with a single line, keep the original legible, write the correction alongside, both Master and witness sign it. Q: "Does the CE keep a separate statutory OLB for the engine room?" A: No — one statutory OLB per vessel, Master's custody; the Engine Log Book is operational, not statutory.

Deep-Dive: Common CE Failures

Confusing the operational Engine Log Book with the statutory OLB; assuming the CE can write OLB entries without the Master's involvement.

Deep-Dive: Numbers to Memorise

One OLB per vessel; submission to the Shipping Master/Port Officer within the statutory window after final port arrival — verify the exact hour figure in force under current DGMA (formerly DG Shipping) guidance before quoting it precisely.

Deep-Dive: On My Vessel

Weekly emergency generator tests, pre-port steering-gear tests, and emergency steering drills must be completed and passed to the bridge for OLB logging.

Maritime Single Window — what is it, and how does it relate to the FAL Convention?
FALDIGITALISATION

15-Second Answer

The Maritime Single Window (MSW) is a centralised digital portal for submitting all port-clearance data in one place instead of separately to customs, immigration, health, and port authorities. It became a mandatory requirement worldwide from 1 January 2024 under amendments to the FAL Convention's Annex.

60-Second Answer

The Maritime Single Window is an integrated electronic data-exchange platform for ship-to-shore reporting. Under the 2022 amendments to the Annex of the IMO Facilitation (FAL) Convention 1965, member states were required to have a functioning electronic single-window system in place by 1 January 2024, so ships or their agents submit standardised data once instead of separately to customs, immigration, health and port authorities, with the system routing the data onward automatically. Data must conform to the IMO Compendium on Facilitation and Electronic Business for international interoperability, and digital authentication replaces handwritten signatures. India has been building out its own national single-window infrastructure to meet this mandate, though the Master remains responsible for data accuracy even when submitted via a shore agent.

Governing instrument

Convention on Facilitation of International Maritime Traffic (FAL Convention) 1965, as amended — the 2022 Annex amendments made electronic Single Window data exchange mandatory from 1 January 2024.

Core concept

A digital environment enabling electronic submission of standardised data to multiple government authorities through one portal, eliminating redundant paper-based reporting.

Key requirements

  • Mandatory electronic exchange from 1 Jan 2024 — public authorities must provide a single electronic window for FAL data.
  • Data harmonisation per the IMO Compendium on Facilitation and Electronic Business.
  • Digital authentication replacing handwritten signatures.
  • Simultaneous processing — port, health, immigration and customs authorities view/approve concurrently, cutting turnaround time.

Key documents typically covered

FAL Form 1 (General Declaration), FAL Form 2 (Cargo Declaration), FAL Form 3 (Ship's Stores Declaration), and related crew/passenger/health declarations.

Trap point

The MSW is not optional infrastructure — a port lacking an operational electronic single window is in non-compliance with the FAL Convention. The Master remains accountable for data accuracy even when a shore agent handles the actual submission.

FAL Convention 1965 (2022 Annex amendments)Mandates electronic Single Window data exchange from 1 Jan 2024

CE Oral Tip: Nair expects the specific date — "mandatory from 1 January 2024" — not a vague "it's a digital system now." Name the FAL Convention explicitly as the legal basis.
Deep-Dive: CE Relevance

While primarily a Master/agent-facing process, the CE supplies accurate technical data (bunker ROB, machinery status) that feeds into FAL Form 3 (Stores Declaration) and related clearance submissions.

Deep-Dive: Trap Questions

Q: "Is the Master still responsible for data accuracy if a port agent submits it?" A: Yes — responsibility for accuracy stays with the Master regardless of who physically submits the data.

Deep-Dive: Common CE Failures

Treating the MSW as an optional convenience rather than a mandatory FAL Convention requirement; not naming the 1 Jan 2024 deadline.

Deep-Dive: Numbers to Memorise

1 January 2024 = mandatory electronic Single Window deadline under the FAL Convention's amended Annex.

Deep-Dive: On My Vessel

Port-entry paperwork on a Maersk container vessel increasingly flows through single-window electronic submission rather than separate paper declarations to each shore authority.

What is the difference between a Convention, a Protocol, and a Treaty in international maritime law?
IMO PROCESS

15-Second Answer

Treaty is the umbrella legal term for any binding international agreement. A Convention is a broad multilateral treaty (SOLAS, MARPOL). A Protocol amends or structurally updates an existing Convention — it carries the same binding force but needs its own separate ratification, and can sometimes absorb a stalled Convention (as MARPOL 73/78 did).

60-Second Answer

Under the Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties 1969, 'Treaty' is the general legal term for any binding agreement between states. A 'Convention' is typically a broad multilateral standard-setting treaty adopted through a body like IMO — SOLAS and MARPOL are Conventions. A 'Protocol' is a separate instrument introduced when major structural changes are needed beyond the routine amendment process — it carries identical binding legal force to a Convention but requires its own independent signature and ratification; a state does not automatically accept a Protocol just because it ratified the parent Convention. The clearest example is MARPOL 73/78: the original 1973 Convention never entered into force on its own, and the 1978 Protocol effectively absorbed and modified it, so the combined instrument is called MARPOL 73/78.

Governing instrument

Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties (VCLT) 1969, Art.2 (definitions).

Hierarchy

Treaty (umbrella term) → Convention (broad multilateral standard, e.g. SOLAS/MARPOL) → Protocol (major structural add-on/update to a Convention, e.g. MARPOL 73/78, SOLAS Protocol 1988).

Key examples

  • MARPOL 73/78: the 1973 Convention never entered into force alone; the 1978 Protocol absorbed and modified it, giving the combined "MARPOL 73/78" instrument.
  • SOLAS Protocol 1988: replaced the survey/certification schedule under SOLAS 1974, creating the Harmonised System of Survey and Certification (HSSC).
  • LLMC Protocol 1996: substantially raised the liability limits set by the 1976 LLMC Convention.
  • 1997 MARPOL Protocol: introduced MARPOL Annex VI (air pollution/fuel sulphur/engine emissions).

Trap point

Ratifying a parent Convention does not automatically bind a state to its Protocols — a state could be party to SOLAS 1974 without having ratified the SOLAS Protocol 1988. A Protocol carries the same binding weight as a Convention, it's simply an add-on requiring its own ratification — it is not a "lower-tier" or non-binding document. Also don't confuse either with an IMO Resolution, which is committee guidance without independent treaty force unless incorporated into a Convention/Protocol by amendment.

Deep-Dive: CE Relevance

Statutory certificates like the IOPP Supplement or IAPP Certificate exist because a Protocol expanded the parent Convention's requirements — the CE must ensure machinery (oil-filtering equipment, sewage plant type-approval) matches the exact Protocol-driven specification.

Deep-Dive: Trap Questions

Q: "Can a state adopt a Protocol without ratifying the parent Convention?" A: Generally no, unless the Protocol explicitly absorbs/replaces the parent (as MARPOL 1978 did). Q: "Is an MEPC Resolution the same as a Convention or Protocol?" A: No — a Resolution is committee guidance, not a treaty instrument, unless formally incorporated by amendment.

Deep-Dive: Common CE Failures

Treating Protocol as a lesser, non-binding document; assuming every MARPOL/SOLAS amendment needs a new Protocol (most routine technical changes go through tacit acceptance instead).

Deep-Dive: Numbers to Memorise

1969 = VCLT; 1978 = MARPOL Protocol (created MARPOL 73/78); 1988 = SOLAS Protocol (HSSC); 1997 = MARPOL Protocol creating Annex VI.

Deep-Dive: On My Vessel

The vessel's IAPP Certificate traces directly to the 1997 Protocol to MARPOL — a concrete daily example of a Protocol's practical effect on engine-room compliance.

What is the difference between General Average and Particular Average?
MARINE INSURANCE

15-Second Answer

General Average is a deliberate, reasonable sacrifice/expenditure made for the common safety of ship and cargo, shared proportionally by all interests under the York-Antwerp Rules. Particular Average is an accidental, partial loss to one specific interest, borne solely by that owner/insurer with no contribution from others.

60-Second Answer

Under the Marine Insurance Act 1963 (Ss.64 and 66) and the York-Antwerp Rules, General Average requires an extraordinary and imminent peril to the whole adventure, a deliberate and voluntary act, a reasonably/prudently made sacrifice or expenditure, aimed at preserving the common safety — for example jettisoning cargo or intentionally flooding a hold to fight a fire; the resulting loss is shared proportionally by ship, cargo and freight interests via a formal declaration and Average Adjuster assessment. Particular Average, by contrast, is an accidental, involuntary partial loss to one specific asset — a container washed overboard, a cracked crankshaft — with no declaration needed; the affected party simply claims against their own Hull & Machinery or Cargo insurance, with zero contribution from other parties.

Governing instrument

Marine Insurance Act 1963 (S.64 defines Particular Average, S.66 defines General Average); York-Antwerp Rules (latest version) for GA adjustment mechanics.

Core distinction

  • General Average: extraordinary + imminent peril to the whole adventure, deliberate/voluntary act, reasonably made, for the common safety — cost shared proportionally by ship, cargo and freight.
  • Particular Average: accidental, partial loss to one specific interest — borne solely by that owner/insurer.

Process

GA: shipowner formally declares General Average; an independent Average Adjuster assesses values and each party's contribution percentage before cargo release. PA: no declaration — Master may file a maritime protest, damage is logged, affected party claims on their own policy.

Comparison

NatureDeliberate, voluntaryAccidental, involuntary
PurposeCommon safety of all interestsIsolated peril, one asset
Cost distributionShared proportionallyBorne solely by affected owner/insurer
Governing rulesYork-Antwerp Rules / Marine Insurance Act S.66Standard policy clauses / S.64

Trap point

A machinery breakdown itself (e.g. a thrown rod) is Particular Average — but the subsequent salvage/towing cost to bring the ship safely to a port of refuge is General Average, shared by all cargo interests. Don't lump the two together.

CE Oral Tip: Nair loves engine-room GA/PA boundary questions. If asked "you use CO2 flooding on a scavenge fire, damaging components — GA or PA?", say: "The CO2 use and resulting collateral damage are General Average, sir — a deliberate act to save the whole ship and cargo. The original fire damage itself is Particular Average."
Deep-Dive: CE Relevance

The CE's technical logs are the legal foundation for the Average Adjuster — if the Master orders the engine run past safe limits, or a deliberate action causes intentional damage to save the ship, the CE must log exact times/parameters/rationale to support the GA claim.

Deep-Dive: Trap Questions

Q: "Refrigerated containers fail from an engineer's oversight and cargo spoils — is that GA?" A: No — accidental, one cargo interest affected, no voluntary sacrifice; it's a cargo claim under Particular Average/P&I. Q: "Seawater used to fight a Hold 3 fire damages unignited cargo in Hold 4 — how classified?" A: General Average — the water reaching Hold 4 was accidental, but it resulted directly from a deliberate act to save the ship and remaining cargo.

Deep-Dive: Common CE Failures

Treating 'average' as a mathematical mean rather than a maritime loss/damage term; assuming any salvage scenario is a total loss rather than a shared GA settlement.

Deep-Dive: Numbers to Memorise

S.64 = Particular Average definition; S.66 = General Average definition (Marine Insurance Act 1963).

Deep-Dive: Casualty Link

Maersk Honam (2018) — a major container-ship fire where General Average was declared; salvor hire, firefighting-tug costs and diversion to a port of refuge were adjusted under GA, requiring cargo owners to post bonds before recovering surviving containers.

Deep-Dive: On My Vessel

If the main engine suffers a severe bearing failure requiring a port-of-refuge stop, the CE must separate the repair cost itself (Particular Average) from port-entry fees, tug assistance and crew overtime incurred during the port-of-refuge stay (General Average).
