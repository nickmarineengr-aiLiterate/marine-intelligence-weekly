What are the latest amendments governing carriage of IMDG cargo?
SOLAS VII|IMDG
15-Second AnswerIMDG Code Amendment 42-24 became mandatory on 1 Jan 2026, tightening rules for charcoal/carbon cargo, lithium and sodium-ion batteries, and updating the EmS Guide to Rev.3.
60-Second AnswerAs Chief Engineer I track IMDG Amendment 42-24 (mandatory since 1 Jan 2026): tighter charcoal self-heating tests, new UN entries for lithium/sodium-ion batteries and battery vehicles replacing UN 3171, and a revised EmS Guide (MSC.1/Circ.1588/Rev.3). I verify hold ventilation, DoC segregation limits and gas detection are matched to the manifest before loading.

Regulatory Context

Carriage of dangerous goods by sea is governed by SOLAS Chapter VII and detailed technically in the IMDG Code. The 2024 Edition, incorporating Amendment 42-24 (adopted under Resolution MSC.556(108)), applied voluntarily from 1 January 2025 and became mandatory from 1 January 2026, fully superseding Amendment 41-22.

Key Changes

  • Carbonaceous/charcoal cargo: tightened testing and weathering-certificate scrutiny under Special Provision 925 following container fires linked to self-heating charcoal.
  • Lithium & sodium-ion batteries: new UN entries (e.g. UN 3551/3552 sodium-ion, UN 3556-3558 battery-powered vehicles) replacing the generic UN 3171 entry, plus State-of-Charge restrictions for bulk transport.
  • EmS Guide: updated to MSC.1/Circ.1588/Rev.3 to align emergency schedules with the new entries.

Operational Segregation

  • Self-reactive/oxidising classes (4.2, 5.1) require documented thermal buffer distance from heat sources such as fuel-oil deep tanks.
  • The vessel's Document of Compliance for carriage of DG must match bay/slot/stack limits for the loaded UN classes.

Key Numbers

Amendment 42-24 mandatory from 1 Jan 2026 (voluntary from 1 Jan 2025); EmS Guide at MSC.1/Circ.1588/Rev.3.
Regulatory References
SOLAS Ch. VII, Reg. 1-2Carriage of dangerous goods in packaged form
IMDG Code, Amdt. 42-24Mandatory technical code from 1 Jan 2026, Res. MSC.556(108)
MSC.1/Circ.1588/Rev.3Revised EmS Guide aligned to Amdt. 42-24
DGS India MS Notice 09/2023National directive on hazardous container handling
CE Oral Tip: Nair frequently tests candidates on charcoal declarations. State clearly: an unverified non-hazardous certificate is not sufficient — the cargo must pass the UN Class 4.2 self-heating test before under-deck stowage is considered, and even then boundary heat sources must be avoided.
CE Relevance

The Chief Engineer verifies hold ventilation is spark-proof, bilge lines serving DG holds are isolated from machinery-space bilges, and gas-detection heads are calibrated to the manifest's specific hazard classes.

Trap Questions

"A shipper certifies the charcoal is non-hazardous — do you load it under deck?" — Only after independent Class 4.2 self-heating test verification, and even then stow with a thermal buffer clear of engine-room bulkheads.

Common CE Failures
  • Quoting the superseded Amendment 41-22 as current after 1 Jan 2026
  • Not knowing which UN entries replaced the generic UN 3171 vehicle entry
Numbers & Regulations to Memorise
  • 1 Jan 2026: Amendment 42-24 becomes mandatory
  • UN 3551/3552: new sodium-ion battery entries
Casualty Link

X-Press Pearl (2021): nitric-acid leak and chain chemical reaction leading to total loss — underlines the risk of misdeclared/reactive DG cargo.

Examiner Chain (Kochi MMD)

Nair: IMDG amendment date → charcoal SP925 → stowage segregation → DoC verification.

On My Vessel (Maersk Container)

Maersk stowage-planning software cross-checks the IMDG digital database against bay-plan segregation; hold water-spray insulation valves are tested daily during DG-heavy transits.

A container catches fire and the UN number shows different EmS symptoms — how do you approach and fight it?
Fire Safety|EmS
15-Second AnswerThe UN number is used to pull the correct EmS Fire Schedule; water-reactive cargo (4.3) is fought differently from self-oxidising cargo (5.1/5.2), which cannot be smothered by CO2.
60-Second AnswerI extract the EmS Fire Schedule from the UN number, then decide tactics: sustained water cooling for oxidisers that supply their own oxygen, dry-agent/controlled-burn with boundary cooling for water-reactive cargo. I run pumps in parallel, isolate reefer power, trip ventilation as directed, and deploy the lance or monitors to attack the seat of fire without direct entry.

Regulatory Context

Firefighting tactics for declared DG cargo are governed by the EmS Guide (IMDG Code Supplement), cross-referenced from the UN number on the manifest, together with SOLAS II-2/10 fire-fighting arrangements.

Reading the EmS Symptoms

  • Class 5.1/5.2 (oxidisers/peroxides): generate their own oxygen — CO2 suffocation fails; sustained water cooling is the primary tactic.
  • Class 4.3 (water-reactive): water triggers explosion/toxic gas; EmS directs dry agents or controlled burn with boundary cooling only.

CE Actions

  • Run main and emergency fire pumps in parallel for monitor pressure at height.
  • Trip hold mechanical ventilation unless EmS calls for continuous exhaust of toxic gas.
  • Isolate reefer power distribution boards in the affected bay.

Tactical Deployment

  • Boundary-cool all six sides of the affected slot.
  • Use a water-mist lance pierced through the container skin to attack seat-of-fire without opening doors.
Examiner Trap"EmS says do not use water, but adjacent containers are igniting — what do you do?" — Do not apply water to the source container; apply massive boundary cooling to surrounding boxes and deck plating instead.
Regulatory References
SOLAS II-2/10Fire-fighting arrangements
IMDG Code Supplement — EmS GuideFire (F) and Spillage (S) emergency schedules keyed to UN number
MSC.1/Circ.1458Guidelines for open-deck container fire-fighting evaluation
CE Oral Tip: Nair probes decision-making under EmS constraints. Show you prioritise hull integrity: never dump water onto a water-reactive fire, but never withhold boundary cooling from adjacent stacks.
CE Relevance

Fire-main hydraulic capacity is the limiting factor — running multiple monitors plus deck lines can drop pressure below the required minimum; coordinate with the Second Engineer on auxiliary generator loading to avoid blackout.

Trap Questions

"Will you release fixed CO2 into an unidentified hold fire immediately?" — No; a Class 5.1/5.2 self-oxidising cargo will not be extinguished by CO2 and the fixed charge would be wasted.

Common CE Failures
  • Forgetting CO2 cannot extinguish self-oxidising (5.1/5.2) cargo fires
  • Not isolating reefer power before deploying water near the bay
Numbers & Regulations to Memorise
  • EmS schedules split into Fire (F) and Spillage (S) categories
  • Minimum fire-main pressure benchmark: 0.27 MPa at deck monitors
Casualty Link

Maersk Honam (2018): catastrophic under-deck chemical-cargo fire — illustrates the danger of oxidising cargo reacting against conventional tactics.

Examiner Chain (Kochi MMD)

Nair: UN number → EmS code → extinguishing medium suitability → boundary cooling decision.

On My Vessel (Maersk Container)

The EmS pocket-guide is kept at the cargo office terminal and bridge emergency desk; quarterly drills include water-lance deployment on scrap container panels.

What are the latest SOLAS Chapter II amendments and their key changes?
SOLAS II-2|PFOS
15-Second AnswerSOLAS Chapter II-2 amendments effective 1 Jan 2026 (Res. MSC.532(107)) prohibit PFOS-containing fire-extinguishing media above 10 mg/kg, alongside enhanced Ro-Ro detection and cargo control room alarm requirements.
60-Second AnswerThe headline 2026 change is the PFOS ban under Resolution MSC.532(107), inserting SOLAS II-2/10.11: no fire-extinguishing media above 10 mg/kg PFOS may be used or stored, with existing ships complying by first survey after 1 Jan 2026. Chapter II-2 also adds individually addressable detectors in Ro-Ro/vehicle spaces and mandatory detection in cargo control rooms; II-1 continues refining watertight-door indication and mooring SWL documentation.

Regulatory Context

SOLAS Chapter II is split into II-1 (construction — structure, stability, machinery, electrical) and II-2 (fire protection, detection and extinction). Several amendments entered into force on 1 January 2026.

Chapter II-2 — Fire Protection

  • PFOS ban: Resolution MSC.532(107) inserts Regulation II-2/10.11, prohibiting use or storage of fire-extinguishing media (including foam) containing perfluorooctane sulfonic acid (PFOS) above 10 mg/kg (0.001% by weight). Ships keel-laid on/after 1 Jan 2026 must comply on delivery; existing ships by their first survey on/after that date.
  • Ro-Ro/vehicle space detection: individually addressable smoke/heat detectors required in new-ship Ro-Ro, special-category and vehicle spaces.
  • Cargo control rooms: fire detection/alarm now mandatory in all control stations.

Chapter II-1

  • Continued refinement of watertight-door status indication and mooring/towing equipment SWL documentation (Reg. 3-8).

Key Numbers

PFOS threshold: 10 mg/kg (0.001% wt). Entry into force: 1 Jan 2026. Governing resolution: MSC.532(107).
Regulatory References
SOLAS II-2, Reg. 10.11PFOS prohibition — fixed and portable fire-extinguishing media, Res. MSC.532(107)
SOLAS II-2, Reg. 10.7.3Container-deck fire-fighting arrangements
MSC.1/Circ.1694Unified interpretations of SOLAS II-2 PFOS amendments
IACS UI SC309Unified interpretation on PFOS fire-extinguishing media
CE Oral Tip: Nair targets PFOS compliance evidence. If asked how you prove compliance: state that the flag/RO reviews the manufacturer's PFOS-free declaration or lab test report against the 10 mg/kg threshold, recorded at first survey after 1 Jan 2026, with disposal via shore reception facilities logged in the official log book.
CE Relevance

Update foam inventories, PMS tasks and SMS procedures to reflect PFOS-free certification; ensure disposal receipts from a licensed facility are retained for PSC.

Trap Questions

"Does the PFOS ban apply to all PFAS chemicals?" — No; the amendment specifically targets PFOS above 10 mg/kg, not the wider PFAS family (though EU REACH imposes broader restrictions separately).

Common CE Failures
  • Citing an incorrect or fabricated resolution number for the PFOS ban
  • Confusing the PFOS threshold with a general PFAS ban
Numbers & Regulations to Memorise
  • MSC.532(107): SOLAS II-2 PFOS amendment
  • 10 mg/kg: PFOS concentration threshold
  • 1 Jan 2026: entry into force
Casualty Link

No single casualty directly drove the PFOS ban; it responds to environmental/health persistence data on PFAS "forever chemicals" under the Stockholm Convention framework.

Examiner Chain (Kochi MMD)

Nair: PFOS ban resolution number → threshold value → compliance verification method → disposal procedure.

On My Vessel (Maersk Container)

Foam-system compliance certificates (ClassNK/LR) are held in the ship's safety folder; any legacy PFOS foam is scheduled for flush-and-replace before the first survey after 1 Jan 2026.

A fire breaks out in an undeclared dangerous goods container — how will you fight it?
Fire Safety|DG
15-Second AnswerUndeclared DG fires must be fought defensively: assume worst-case reactivity, boundary-cool all sides, and hold CO2/water application until smoke and thermal signs rule out oxidising or water-reactive cargo.
60-Second AnswerWith no manifest UN number I treat the fire as potentially reactive, toxic or self-oxidising. I maximise fire-main pressure by running pumps in parallel, trip hold ventilation to limit oxygen, isolate local reefer power, and deploy lances/monitors from a protected distance while reading smoke and thermal-imaging signatures before committing to a water or CO2-based tactic.

Regulatory Context

With no manifest UN number, the EmS Guide cannot be consulted directly, so the fire must be treated on a worst-case defensive basis under the general fire-fighting arrangements of SOLAS II-2/10.7.

Identification & Assessment

  • Use thermal imaging to map heat spread across adjacent stacks; assess smoke colour/odour as an indicator (acrid yellow — acids/oxidisers; thick black — plastics/polymers).
  • Treat as reactive/toxic/self-oxidising until proven otherwise — no direct manual entry.

Engineering Actions

  • Run main and emergency fire pumps in parallel, targeting 0.27-0.35 MPa at open-deck monitors.
  • Seal hold ventilation flaps if under deck; hold CO2 release until an oxidising cargo is ruled out.
  • Align cargo-hold bilge 3-way valves to keep hazardous runoff out of machinery-space bilge tanks.
Examiner Trap"Do you release CO2 immediately since the cargo is unknown?" — No — if the cargo is a self-oxidising Class 5.1/5.2 substance, CO2 will not extinguish it and the fixed charge is wasted; boundary cooling and smoke assessment come first.
Regulatory References
SOLAS II-2/10.7Firefighting arrangements for ships carrying containers on/above the weather deck
IMDG Code General GuidanceContingency protocols for unmanifested/undeclared hazardous reactions
MSC.1/Circ.1458Evaluation guidelines for open-deck container fire-fighting systems
CE Oral Tip: Nair pressures candidates on the water-lance decision for unknown cargo. If sparks and white smoke suggest a reactive metal fire, state clearly you will NOT pierce with water — maintain boundary cooling only and let the internal reaction burn out.
CE Relevance

Manage electrical load configuration to prevent an automatic blackout if a deck reefer cable short-circuits under fire conditions, protecting propulsion and manoeuvring availability.

Trap Questions

"Bright sparks and white smoke — will you pierce with a water lance?" — No; this pattern suggests a Class 4.3 water-reactive metal fire; piercing with water risks a hydrogen explosion. Maintain external boundary cooling only.

Common CE Failures
  • Failing to isolate electrical power feeders to the whole bay before deploying water near cable trunks
  • Treating an undeclared fire the same as a known Class A cargo fire
Numbers & Regulations to Memorise
  • Target fire-main pressure: 0.27-0.35 MPa
  • Safe stand-off for unverified chemical fires: at least 2 container lengths
Casualty Link

NNCI Arauco (2016): fatal container fire from undeclared hazardous material reacting unpredictably to heat during welding operations.

Examiner Chain (Kochi MMD)

Nair: worst-case assumption → thermal/smoke assessment → boundary cooling → CO2 decision gate.

On My Vessel (Maersk Container)

Quarterly emergency drills simulate undeclared-cargo scenarios; the safety squad uses thermal cameras to map the internal fire boundary before choosing offensive or defensive posture.

Explain the water mist lance and mobile water monitor: how to use them, on which fires, where the requirement is mentioned, and the functional requirements.
SOLAS II-2|FSS Code
15-Second AnswerSOLAS II-2/10.7.3 mandates lances (piercing 2 mm steel) and mobile monitors (2 units if breadth <30 m, 4 if ≥30 m, each ≥60 m³/hr) on container-deck ships built from 2016, with technical detail set in the FSS Code and MSC.1/Circ.1472.
60-Second AnswerThese are mandated under SOLAS II-2/10.7.3 for ships carrying containers on/above the weather deck (built from 1 Jan 2016). The FSS Code sets performance benchmarks: the lance pierces 2 mm steel to mist-flood a container without opening doors; monitors are carried at 2 units under 30 m breadth or 4 units at 30 m and above, each discharging at least 60 m³/hr to reach the highest tier while crew retreat to a safe distance.

Regulatory Context

Carriage is mandated by SOLAS Regulation II-2/10.7.3 for ships designed to carry containers on or above the weather deck (built on/after 1 Jan 2016); technical performance is set by the FSS Code and MSC.1/Circ.1472.

Functional Requirements

  • Water mist lance: piercing tip capable of penetrating 2 mm steel container skin; delivers fine high-velocity mist into the interior without opening doors.
  • Mobile monitors: breadth <30 m — minimum 2 units; breadth ≥30 m (typical Maersk main-line size) — minimum 4 units. Each must give continuous unattended discharge of at least 60 m³/hr reaching the highest deck tier.

Method of Use

  • Lance: mallet/slide-hammer punches the tip through the container skin, then the fire-main valve floods the interior.
  • Monitor: positioned on deck/lashing bridge with stabiliser legs, connected via dual-feed hose, aimed and locked so the crew can retreat while cooling continues.

Key Numbers

Lance pierces 2 mm steel. Monitors: 2 (breadth <30 m) / 4 (breadth ≥30 m), each ≥60 m³/hr.
Regulatory References
SOLAS II-2, Reg. 10.7.3Carriage requirement for lance/mobile monitors on container-deck ships
FSS CodePerformance, testing and construction benchmarks
MSC.1/Circ.1472Design, performance, testing and approval of mobile water monitors
CE Oral Tip: Nair often asks whether a monitor can connect to any standard hydrant. Confirm yes, but note both main and auxiliary fire pumps must run in parallel to hold required pressure across the highest lashing bridges when multiple monitors are open.
CE Relevance

Running 4 monitors simultaneously draws roughly 240 m³/hr; verify main fire pumps sustain this alongside standard hydrants and that sea suctions are clear of marine growth to avoid cavitation.

Trap Questions

"Can any hydrant feed a mobile monitor?" — Yes, but line pressure must be maintained per FSS Code limits; run pumps in parallel for multi-monitor operation.

Common CE Failures
  • Confusing the 2/4-unit breadth threshold (30 m) with hose length or other unrelated SOLAS figures
  • Unable to state the 2 mm piercing capability of the lance tip
Numbers & Regulations to Memorise
  • 2 units: breadth <30 m
  • 4 units: breadth ≥30 m
  • 60 m³/hr: minimum monitor discharge
  • 2 mm: lance piercing capability
Casualty Link

Yantian Express (2019): demonstrated the value of unmanned mobile monitors when high-tier heat radiation makes manual hose handling impossible.

Examiner Chain (Kochi MMD)

Nair: where is it mentioned → functional specification → carriage threshold by breadth → deployment method.

On My Vessel (Maersk Container)

Lances and monitors are stored in marked deck lockers; routine inspection lubricates piercing tips and checks monitor swivel joints for rapid deployment readiness.

What are the ventilation requirements for a totally enclosed lifeboat?
LSA Code|Lifeboats
15-Second AnswerUnder IMO Resolution MSC.535(107) — new LSA Code paras 4.6.6/4.6.7 — every totally enclosed lifeboat must provide ventilation of at least 5 m³/h per person for the permitted complement, sustained for 24 hours, operable from inside. Powered or passive; if engine-driven, sufficient fuel per 4.4.6.8 and the source can't be the radio battery. Mandatory from 1 Jan 2026, applies to lifeboats installed on/after 1 Jan 2029.
60-Second AnswerMSC.535(107), adopted at MSC 107 (June 2023), inserts new paragraphs 4.6.6 and 4.6.7 into LSA Code Chapter IV following the 2013 MOL Comfort casualty, where survivors in a totally enclosed lifeboat suffered dehydration/heat stress from inadequate air exchange. The rule requires a ventilation rate of at least 5 m³/h per person, for the number of persons the lifeboat is certified to carry, maintained for not less than 24 hours, operable from inside the lifeboat, and arranged to avoid stratification or unventilated pockets. Ventilation may be powered or passive. If powered, the source must not be the radio batteries, and if the lifeboat engine drives it, sufficient fuel must be carried per para 4.4.6.8 (the standard 24h engine-endurance provision). Consequential amendments: MSC.81(70) test procedures updated, MSC.559(108) adds the ventilation system to the annual thorough examination under MSC.402(96). Enters into force internationally 1 Jan 2026; applies to totally enclosed lifeboats installed on ships contracted/keel-laid on or after 1 Jan 2029.

Regulatory Context

Governed by IMO Resolution MSC.535(107) (adopted 8 June 2023, MSC 107), which inserts new paragraphs 4.6.6 and 4.6.7 into LSA Code Chapter IV, Section 4.6 (Totally Enclosed Lifeboats). The amendment was driven by the MOL Comfort (2013) casualty, where crew who abandoned ship in a totally enclosed lifeboat suffered dehydration and heat stress due to inadequate ventilation while sealed inside.

Core Rules (LSA Code 4.6.6)

  • 4.6.6.1 — Ventilation rate: at least 5 m³/h per person, for the number of persons the lifeboat is permitted to accommodate, sustained for not less than 24 hours. Must be operable from inside the lifeboat and arranged so the boat ventilates without stratification or unventilated pockets.
  • 4.6.6.2 — Power source: ventilation means may be powered or passive. If powered, the source shall not be the radio batteries (para 4.4.6.11); if dependent on the lifeboat engine, sufficient fuel must be provided to comply with para 4.4.6.8 (standard engine fuel-endurance requirement, i.e. continuous running for 24h).
  • 4.6.7 — Closing arrangements: ventilation openings need means of closing, operable from inside, positioned to minimise water ingress.

Scope & Timeline

Amendment enters into force internationally on 1 January 2026. It applies to totally enclosed lifeboats installed on ships whose building contract is dated (or keel laid, if no contract) on or after 1 January 2029 — new-build application only, not retrofit.

Consequential Amendments

Test procedures in MSC.81(70) (Revised Recommendations on Testing of LSA) updated to include ventilation performance testing with hatches closed. MSC.559(108) amends MSC.402(96) to add ventilation system condition/operation checks to the annual thorough examination of lifeboats.

Key Numbers

Ventilation rate: 5 m³/h per person. Duration: 24 hours minimum. Resolution: MSC.535(107), adopted 8 June 2023. In force: 1 Jan 2026. Application: lifeboats installed on ships contracted on/after 1 Jan 2029.
Regulatory References

LSA Code 4.4.6.8Engine fuel-endurance provision referenced for engine-driven ventilation
MSC.81(70)Revised Recommendations on Testing of LSA — ventilation test procedure amendments
MSC.559(108) / MSC.402(96)Adds ventilation system check to annual thorough examination of lifeboats
SOLAS Reg. III/20Maintenance and periodic inspection of survival craft
CE Oral Tip: Simon expects the exact figure and resolution number together: "5 cubic metres per hour per person, for 24 hours, under MSC.535(107)." Follow immediately with the timeline (force 1 Jan 2026, applies to lifeboats installed 1 Jan 2029 onward) and the MOL Comfort trigger — examiners use this as a chain question testing whether you know the regulation is prospective, not retrofit.
CE Relevance

As Chief Engineer verify, at annual thorough examination (post-MSC.559(108)), that the ventilation system's condition and operation are checked and logged alongside the standard structure/engine/release-gear items on the certificate.

Trap Questions

"Does this apply to your current vessel's lifeboats?" — Only if installed on a ship contracted/keel-laid on or after 1 Jan 2029; existing lifeboats on in-service tonnage are not retrofitted under this amendment. "Is mechanical ventilation mandatory?" — No; passive ventilation is acceptable provided it still achieves 5 m³/h per person for 24 hours.

Common CE Failures
  • Quoting the old pre-amendment 4.6/4.7/4.8 text instead of the new 4.6.6/4.6.7 paragraphs
  • Forgetting the 5 m³/h per person figure or confusing it with the 5,000 ppm CO2 threshold used for partially enclosed lifeboats (a separate, still-draft SSE proposal)
  • Missing that the application date (1 Jan 2029, new-build) is different from the force date (1 Jan 2026)
Numbers & Regulations to Memorise
  • 5 m³/h per person: minimum ventilation rate
  • 24 hours: minimum sustained duration
  • MSC.535(107): adopted 8 June 2023, in force 1 Jan 2026
  • 1 Jan 2029: application date for newly installed totally enclosed lifeboats
Casualty Link

Triggered by the MOL Comfort break-up and sinking (2013, Indian Ocean): survivors who abandoned ship in a totally enclosed lifeboat suffered dehydration and heat-related illness due to inadequate air exchange in the sealed enclosure, prompting IMO work from SSE 5 (2018) through to adoption at MSC 107 (2023).

Examiner Chain (Kochi MMD)

Simon: resolution number → ventilation rate/duration figures → powered-vs-passive and fuel-source rule → application date → MOL Comfort background.

On My Vessel (Maersk Container)

Present fleet lifeboats predate the 2029 application cut-off, so this is a "next-generation newbuild" requirement — PMS still tracks general ventilation flap/seal condition, and this will move into the annual thorough exam checklist once MSC.559(108) implementation guidance is issued by class.
