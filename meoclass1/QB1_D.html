What was the GRT and DWT of your last vessel?
Tonnage Senthil (Int)
⏱ 15-Second Answer "My last vessel, a Maersk container ship, had a Gross Tonnage (GT) of approximately 95,000 and a Deadweight Tonnage (DWT) of 110,000 tonnes. GT is a dimensionless number representing the ship's total enclosed volume, whereas DWT is the actual physical weight in tonnes the ship can carry when loaded to her summer marks."
🎯 60-Second Answer "Sir, on my last vessel, the GT was [insert exact number] and DWT was [insert exact number]. GT is a mathematical, dimensionless index derived from the total moulded volume of all enclosed spaces under the Tonnage 1969 Convention. It dictates the applicability of MARPOL and SOLAS regulations, manning scales, and port dues. DWT is a measure of the ship's physical weight-carrying capacity in metric tonnes — calculated as summer load line displacement minus the lightship weight. As Chief Engineer, I monitor GT closely because it determines which statutory thresholds apply to my engine room, such as MARPOL Annex I OWS capacity requirements."

Gross and Net Tonnage (TONNAGE 69)

Under the International Convention on Tonnage Measurement of Ships, 1969 (TONNAGE 69), the obsolete terms GRT (Gross Register Tonnage) and NRT (Net Register Tonnage) were replaced by GT and NT. [owaysonline]

  • Gross Tonnage (GT): A dimensionless index indicating the overall size of the vessel based on the moulded volume of all enclosed spaces. The formula is GT = K₁V. [treaties.un]
  • Net Tonnage (NT): A dimensionless index indicating the vessel's earning capacity based on the moulded volume of cargo spaces. The convention dictates that NT cannot be less than 30% of the GT. [imo]

Deadweight Tonnage (DWT)

Deadweight is the actual weight a ship can carry in metric tonnes. It includes cargo, fuel, lubricating oil, fresh water, ballast water, provisions, passengers, and crew.

DWT = Displacement at Summer Load Line − Lightship Displacement

Why Vessel Types Differ

Container ships have large enclosed cargo holds and deckhouses, giving them a very high GT relative to their DWT. Conversely, bulk carriers carry high-density cargo, resulting in a DWT that is numerically much higher than their GT. [owaysonline]

CE Oral Tip (Senthil / Simon): Kochi MMD checks your vessel familiarity immediately. Have the exact GT, NT, DWT, Engine BHP, and Lightship weight of your last two vessels memorized. Do not say "tons" when stating your GT.
🔧 CE Relevance

While the Master handles the commercial aspect of DWT (cargo loading), the CE uses GT to determine statutory compliance. GT thresholds dictate whether the vessel requires an IOPP certificate (≥ 400 GT), an IAPP certificate (≥ 400 GT), or compliance with the ISM Code (≥ 500 GT).

⚠ Trap Questions

"What are the units for Gross Tonnage?" — GT has no physical units; it is a strictly dimensionless mathematical index number. [owaysonline]

"Why did the industry move from GRT to GT?" — GRT used a system of exemptions that allowed ship designers to exploit loopholes (e.g., leaving shelter decks "open" to avoid tonnage taxes). GT provides a universal, mathematically rigorous formula without exemptions.

✗ Common CE Failures
  • Failing to recall own ship's particulars accurately — examiners use this as an instant credibility check
  • Confusing GT (dimensionless index) with GRT (100 cubic feet of volume), or giving GT in "tonnes"
🔢 Numbers & Regulations to Memorise
  • TONNAGE 69: Entry into force 18 July 1982
  • Formula: GT = K₁V (V = total enclosed volume in m³, K₁ = 0.2 + 0.02 log₁₀V)
  • NT Limit: NT ≥ 0.30 × GT
⚓ Casualty Link

Not directly casualty-driven — TONNAGE 69 arose from the need for a uniform international measurement system rather than a specific incident, replacing conflicting national tonnage rules.

Lifting appliance latest amendments — what changed and how were they previously checked?
SOLAS Ref Paul (Ext)
⏱ 15-Second Answer "SOLAS Chapter II-1 Regulation 3-13 mandates that all shipboard lifting appliances undergo mandatory statutory survey, including annual inspections and 5-yearly proof load tests witnessed by Class. Previously, lifting appliances were not covered by SOLAS and were only checked under ILO Convention 152 and individual Class rules."
🎯 60-Second Answer "Sir, effective 1 January 2026, SOLAS II-1/3-13 brought shipboard lifting appliances under statutory regulation to prevent severe crane accidents. The amendment requires design approval, permanent Safe Working Load (SWL) marking, and a mandatory 'Register of Ship's Lifting Appliances.' Equipment must undergo an annual thorough examination and a 5-yearly proof load test — typically to 1.25 times the SWL — witnessed by a Class surveyor. Before this, compliance was fragmented, relying primarily on ILO Convention C152 (which focused on dockworkers) and various classification society guidelines, leaving non-cargo engine room cranes with inconsistent oversight."

The SOLAS Amendment (Chapter II-1 Reg 3-13)

The new regulation applies to all permanently installed lifting appliances and anchor handling winches on ships. Introduced to combat the high rate of catastrophic crane failures, it shifts lifting appliances from an operational concern to a statutory safety requirement. [marinetegrity]

  • New Appliances: Must be designed, installed, and tested in accordance with Class rules before first use. [bureauveritas]
  • Existing Appliances (Pre-2026): Must comply with the examination, load testing, and marking requirements no later than the first SAFCON renewal survey on or after 1 January 2026.

Survey and Register Requirements

Under the guidelines of MSC.1/Circ.1663, lifting appliances require:

  • Annual Thorough Examination: A detailed visual check of structural integrity, wire ropes, and safety limits (limit switches, brakes). [eagle]
  • 5-Yearly Proof Load Test: A dynamic test exceeding the SWL (usually 1.25× SWL) witnessed by a recognized Class surveyor.
  • Record Keeping: The CE must maintain the mandatory Register of Ship's Lifting Appliances and Cargo Handling Gear — all loose gear must be certified, proof-tested, and logged. [bureauveritas]
⚠ Exam Trap Does the new SOLAS rule apply to a small 500 kg SWL engine room monorail? — Appliances with an SWL below 1,000 kg are exempt from strict design approval, but must still be marked with their SWL and incorporated into the SMS.

CE Oral Tip (Paul, Ext): Paul frequently asks about new amendments. Stress the phrase "moved from ILO guidance to SOLAS mandatory statutory survey." Emphasize that your primary duty is ensuring SWL is marked on ALL lifting gear and the Register is up to date.
🔧 CE Relevance

The CE integrates these SOLAS requirements into the Planned Maintenance System (PMS), tracking expiration dates of 5-year load tests for the engine room crane, provision cranes, and monorails. If a crane fails inspection or its certificate expires, it must be permanently marked "Not to be used" and logged in the Register.

⚠ Trap Questions

"If your engine room crane wire parts, can you replace it and lift loads immediately?" — You can replace it with an identical, certified wire rope; log the new certificate and perform a functional test. A full 5-yearly proof load test is only triggered by major structural repairs, not routine wire replacement.

✗ Common CE Failures
  • Failing to articulate the difference between the "old" ILO 152 regime and the "new" SOLAS statutory regime
  • Not knowing why IMO stepped in — ILO rules were not strictly enforced by all flag states for non-cargo machinery space cranes
🔢 Numbers & Regulations to Memorise
  • SOLAS II-1/3-13: Entry into force 1 Jan 2026
  • MSC.1/Circ.1663: Guidelines for lifting appliances
  • Proof Load: Generally 1.25 × SWL for standard capacities
⚓ Casualty Link

The Thorco Basilisk (2022) crane wire failure and the Sallie Knutsen crane fatality both highlighted deficiencies in shipboard crane maintenance and inspection regimes, driving IMO to adopt the SOLAS II-1/3-13 amendments. [iomshipregistry]

Lifting appliance survey — how is it conducted and what are the requirements?
Survey Paul (Ext)
⏱ 15-Second Answer "A lifting appliance survey under SOLAS II-1/3-13 consists of an annual thorough examination and a 5-yearly proof load test witnessed by a Class surveyor. The CE must ensure all appliances are marked with their Safe Working Load (SWL) and maintain the statutory 'Register of Ship's Lifting Appliances and Cargo Handling Gear' onboard."
🎯 60-Second Answer "Sir, under SOLAS II-1 Reg 3-13 and MSC.1/Circ.1663, lifting appliances require two main survey interventions. First is the annual thorough examination — a detailed visual and operational check of the crane's structure, wire ropes, hooks, sheaves, and limit switches. Second is the 5-yearly proof load test, typically at 1.25 times the SWL, witnessed and certified by a Class surveyor acting as the flag state's Recognized Organization. As CE, my responsibility is to align these surveys with the PMS, ensure SWL is permanently marked on all equipment, and keep the mandatory Lifting Appliance Register updated with valid certificates for the crane and all associated loose gear."

Annual Thorough Examination

According to MSC.1/Circ.1663, the annual thorough examination must be conducted by a competent person (often authorized shore-based personnel or a Class surveyor, depending on flag rules). This examination verifies: [dnv]

  • The structural integrity of the crane jib, slewing ring, and foundation
  • The condition of wire ropes, hooks, and sheaves against discard criteria
  • The correct operation of safety devices — limit switches, slack wire switches, overload alarms

5-Yearly Proof Load Test

  • The Load: For a typical engine room or provision crane, the test load is 1.25× the SWL
  • The Procedure: Load (often certified water bags or calibrated shore weights) is lifted, slewed, and luffed through the crane's entire operational range
  • Certification: Witnessed by a Class surveyor who issues a Certificate of Test and Thorough Examination

Documentation & Registers

The CE maintains the Register of Ship's Lifting Appliances and Cargo Handling Gear. Any loose gear (shackles, chain blocks) must also be proof-tested, marked with its own SWL, and have its certificate logged.

Key Numbers

Annual: thorough examination — 5-yearly: proof load test — Proof load: usually SWL × 1.25

CE Oral Tip (Paul, Ext): Paul expects a step-by-step process walkthrough. Do not just say "we load test it." Explicitly mention checking wire ropes, safety limits, and loose gear before the load is applied, and emphasize that the Class surveyor must witness the 5-year test.
🔧 CE Relevance

The CE is the primary custodian of engine room and provision crane records. If a crane's load test certificate expires, it cannot legally be used — the CE must permanently mark it "Not to be used," secure it, and log the out-of-service status in the Register.

⚠ Trap Questions

"If a hydraulic cylinder for luffing is replaced during a voyage, is a load test required?" — Yes; replacing a major load-bearing component constitutes a major repair requiring an occasional thorough examination and load test witnessed by a surveyor.

"Can the Chief Engineer perform the 5-yearly load test?" — No. It must be witnessed and certified by a Class surveyor acting as the RO.

✗ Common CE Failures
  • Forgetting to mention "loose gear" — surveyors will fail a crane if shackles or slings lack valid, traceable certificates in the Register, even if the crane passed the 5-year test
🔢 Numbers & Regulations to Memorise
  • Frequency: 12 months (annual), 5 years (proof load)
  • Proof Load: SWL × 1.25 (refer to Class rules for heavy lift cranes >50t)
  • Guidelines: MSC.1/Circ.1663
⚓ Casualty Link

In the Lodz 2 incident, catastrophic failure of the crane slew ring resulted in the crane collapsing onto the deck. Proper execution of the annual thorough examination — specifically grease analysis and slew bearing clearance measurements — is designed to prevent this exact failure mode.

Loadline survey — which documents are required?
Load Line Paul (Ext)
⏱ 15-Second Answer "For a Load Line survey, the surveyor requires the International Load Line Certificate (ILLC), the 'Record of Conditions of Assignment,' the Approved Intact Stability Booklet, the Damage Stability Booklet, and the Loading Manual. The survey ensures the vessel's watertight integrity and freeboard marks remain unchanged."
🎯 60-Second Answer "Sir, the core documents required for a Load Line survey are the International Load Line Certificate (ILLC) and the 'Record of Conditions of Assignment of Load Lines.' The surveyor uses the Record of Conditions of Assignment as a master checklist to verify that all structural arrangements — hatch covers, watertight doors, ventilators, air pipes — have not been altered since the freeboard was originally assigned. Additionally, the surveyor reviews the Approved Intact Stability Booklet, the Damage Stability Booklet (SOLAS II-1 Reg 25), the Trim and Loading Manual, and the AFS Certificate. Once documents are verified, physical checks of the deck, hull openings, freeing ports, and painted draft/freeboard marks are carried out."

Core Statutory Documents

The Load Line survey verifies that the ship's assigned freeboard has not been compromised by structural changes or degradation. [classnk]

  • International Load Line Certificate (ILLC): Valid for 5 years, subject to annual endorsement
  • Record of Conditions of Assignment: The most critical technical document — details exact specifications, sill heights, and closing arrangements for all doors, hatches, ventilators, air pipes, and scuppers on the freeboard deck. [sdir]

Stability and Operational Manuals

  • Approved Intact Stability Booklet
  • Damage Stability Booklet (mandatory for cargo ships under SOLAS II-1)
  • Loading / Ballasting Manual (or Trim and Stability Booklet)
  • Anti-Fouling System (AFS) Certificate (often checked concurrently)

Physical Survey Focus

After document review, the surveyor cross-references the Record of Conditions of Assignment against the physical deck:

  • Freeboard marks — correct positioning and visibility
  • Condition of weather-tight hatch covers and securing devices
  • Engine room skylights, fiddley openings, machinery space ventilators
  • Functionality of non-return valves on overboard discharges
  • Clear freeing ports for rapid deck drainage
⚠ Exam Trap If your ship uses two load line certificates, only one ILLC can be in use at a time. Active freeboard marks are painted in; marks for the unused certificate must be painted out or obscured, and the change logged in the Official Log Book.

CE Oral Tip (Paul, Ext): Paul explicitly fishes for "Record of Conditions of Assignment." Anchor your answer around this document — the ILLC is just a piece of paper; the Record is the actual engineering blueprint the surveyor uses to inspect the ship.
🔧 CE Relevance

The CE must ensure all engine room envelope openings — air pipe heads with internal floats, funnel louvers, skylights, and overboard discharge non-return valves — are well maintained. A corroded or stuck-open vent head float fails the Conditions of Assignment and the Load Line certificate cannot be endorsed.

⚠ Trap Questions

"Where do you find the exact required height of the engine room ventilators above the freeboard deck?" — In the Record of Conditions of Assignment of Load Lines.

✗ Common CE Failures
  • Mentioning the ILLC but not knowing the Record of Conditions of Assignment — considered an immediate red flag by examiners
🔢 Numbers & Regulations to Memorise
  • ILLC Validity: 5 years
  • Annual Endorsement Window: ±3 months from the anniversary date
  • Regulations: International Convention on Load Lines 1966 / 1988 Protocol
⚓ Casualty Link

The loss of the MV Derbyshire (1980) heavily influenced modern Load Line regulations on hatch cover strength and air pipe closing arrangements. Water ingress through unprotected forward ventilators during a typhoon led to catastrophic loss of buoyancy — the reason surveyors rigorously check these items against the Conditions of Assignment.

We replaced them prior to the surveyor's arrival to ensure compliance with the Record of Conditions of Assignment, guaranteeing the ILLC endorsement.
