What is a FONAR (Fuel Oil Non-Availability Report) and when must it be filed?
marpolghg
15-Second Answer: A FONAR is a formal report to the Flag Administration and destination Port State when a vessel cannot obtain compliant fuel oil despite documented good-faith efforts. It is evidence of genuine non-availability, not a waiver from MARPOL Annex VI Regulation 18 compliance.
60-Second Answer: Under MARPOL Annex VI Regulation 18.2, a ship need not deviate from its voyage or be unduly delayed to obtain compliant fuel — but must document every commercial effort to procure it. The FONAR must be sent to the Flag State and destination Port State as soon as non-availability is determined, well before entering the destination port's waters — never filed only on arrival. Port State Control cross-checks the FONAR against the Oil Record Book, Bunker Delivery Notes, and correspondence trail; if the claim is genuine, the ship is generally allowed to proceed, but may face mandatory de-bunkering or compulsory bunkering of compliant fuel at that port at the operator's cost.

Legal Nature

MARPOL Annex VI Reg. 18.2 does not require a ship to deviate or be unduly delayed to source compliant fuel. A FONAR is the formal disclosure mechanism for this scenario — a reporting tool, not a pollution exemption.

Evidence Required Before Filing

  • Documented attempts to source compliant fuel from alternative local suppliers/anchorages along the route.
  • Evaluation of whether sourcing at an intermediate port was operationally feasible.
  • Retained email trails, charterer instructions, and supplier rejections showing timely requests for compliant fuel.

FONAR Contents

  • Ship identification (IMO number, Flag, GRT) and itinerary.
  • Bunkering ports where compliant fuel was unavailable, and supplier contact details.
  • Steps taken to find alternatives.
  • Specifics of the non-compliant fuel to be used (quantity, sulphur content).
  • Action plan to restore compliance (e.g. de-bunkering or burn-off at the next compliant port).

PSC Scrutiny on Arrival

PSC checks the Oil Record Book Part II entries and Bunker Delivery Notes against the FONAR claim. If the vessel bunkered high-sulphur fuel without an operating scrubber, PSC may order complete de-bunkering or immediate compliant bunkering at the operator's cost.

Chief Engineer Relevance

On a Maersk container vessel, bunker procurement runs through a corporate bunker desk, but operational shortages still occur. Entering an ECA or port with non-compliant fuel and no active EGCS/scrubber, and no valid FONAR on file, is a serious MARPOL breach risking company fines, detention, and potential CoC scrutiny for the Chief Engineer.

Never say the FONAR is filed after arrival or during bunkering of non-compliant fuel — it must be sent immediately when non-availability is determined, before entering the destination port's territorial waters. Saying otherwise reads as a planning failure.
Deep-Dive: Traps, Failures, Numbers, Casualty, On My Vessel
Trap Questions: "Charterer wants cheaper non-compliant fuel — can you file a FONAR?" — No; commercial preference is never valid grounds, only genuine physical unavailability.
Common CE Failures: Showing only one supplier rejection email instead of a full trail across alternative local suppliers — PSC will reject a thin FONAR.
Numbers to Memorise: 0.50% m/m — global sulphur cap outside ECAs; 0.10% m/m — inside ECAs; MARPOL requires the report "immediately upon becoming aware" of non-availability.
Casualty Link: Several container vessels have faced prolonged, costly detentions over poorly documented FONAR filings — a commercial/compliance casualty rather than a physical one.
On My Vessel: On Maersk vessels the CE coordinates with the Global Vessel Performance Centre and technical superintendent so Class and Flag are copied into a properly formatted FONAR backed by lab test data or supplier declarations.

What is the 'Dark Fleet' (Shadow Fleet) and what safety/environmental risks does it pose?
marpolsolas
15-Second Answer: The Dark Fleet refers to ageing, poorly maintained vessels operating outside mainstream oversight to evade sanctions — masking position via AIS manipulation, using flags of convenience, carrying unverified insurance, and conducting high-risk ship-to-ship transfers.
60-Second Answer: These vessels routinely operate past normal service life without proper Class oversight, disable or spoof AIS to hide port calls, frequently change name/ownership under low-tier open registries, and lack legitimate International Group P&I cover — meaning a collision or spill leaves no credible party able to fund the clean-up. IMO Resolution A.1192(33) (adopted 6 December 2023) urges flag, port, and coastal States, plus all stakeholders, to take coordinated action against these illegal operations, including verifying insurance and monitoring suspicious AIS history.

Operational Characteristics

  • AIS manipulation/spoofing: transponders switched off or spoofed to disguise sanctioned port calls.
  • Flag hopping, opaque ownership: repeated renaming and re-registration under low-enforcement open registries.
  • Uninsured/under-insured risk: lack of International Group of P&I Clubs cover, relying on unverifiable alternative arrangements that cannot fund major spill liabilities.
  • High-risk ship-to-ship transfers: often conducted mid-ocean without proper vapour balancing or containment, contrary to MARPOL Annex I Ch. 8.

Safety and Environmental Risk

Many are older tankers lacking rigorous fatigue analysis or UTG-based structural monitoring, prone to hull failure; substandard machinery maintenance increases blackout/propulsion-loss risk near compliant traffic.

Regulatory Response

IMO Resolution A.1192(33) (adopted 6 December 2023, agenda item 13) urges Member States and stakeholders to prevent illegal dark/shadow fleet operations — including scrutiny of STS transfer compliance with SOLAS V/19–19-1 and MARPOL Annex I Ch. 8, and encourages flag/port State verification of insurance and AIS integrity.

Chief Engineer Relevance

Navigating dense lanes, a Maersk CE should be aware that dark-fleet vessels may present false or absent AIS data on ECDIS while physically present on radar — machinery reliability (steering gear, main engine emergency manoeuvring) must be kept at peak readiness since avoiding an unresponsive, unguided vessel may require instant propulsion response.

CE Oral Tip: Simon is testing awareness of current geopolitical/regulatory risk alongside classic navigation safety. Anchor the answer in safety-of-navigation and environmental liability — a collision with an uninsured dark-fleet vessel leaves your company exposed to uncompensated clean-up costs.
Deep-Dive: Traps, Failures, Numbers, Casualty, On My Vessel
Trap Questions: "A genuine AIS malfunction — does that make my vessel 'dark fleet'?" — No, provided it's promptly logged in the GMDSS/Deck Log and reported to Flag/coastal authorities; dark fleet status requires a deliberate, systematic pattern of deception, not an isolated fault.
Common CE Failures: Assuming every radar contact will correlate with an AIS/ECDIS target — dark fleet vessels may broadcast false MMSI or no signal at all, requiring reliance on raw radar and visual lookout.
Numbers to Memorise: A.1192(33) adopted 6 December 2023; the International Group of P&I Clubs covers the large majority of legitimate ocean-going tonnage — dark fleet vessels sit outside it.
Casualty Link: The 2023 tanker Pablo explosion off Malaysia — fire, fatalities, an oil slick, and no clearly liable party due to opaque shell-company ownership — illustrates the uncompensated-risk problem directly.
On My Vessel: Bridge/engine coordination on Maersk vessels emphasises verifying suspicious contacts by radar and lookout, not AIS alone, and keeping emergency manoeuvring systems (steering gear, engine telegraph response) fully tested.

Source confidence: High — IMO Resolution A.1192(33), adopted 6 December 2023, is confirmed and current as of mid-2026; verify no superseding resolution has been adopted at a more recent Assembly session before oral.

Describe the ship recycling procedure in India under the Recycling of Ships Act, 2019.
marpolism
15-Second Answer: Indian ship recycling is governed by the Recycling of Ships Act, 2019, which gives domestic legal effect to the Hong Kong International Convention. It requires an approved Inventory of Hazardous Materials (IHM), a Ready for Recycling Certificate, and dismantling only at a DG Shipping-authorised, HKC-compliant green recycling facility (principally Alang).
60-Second Answer: Before arrival, the ship must hold a comprehensive, Class-verified IHM (Parts I–III covering asbestos, PCBs, ozone-depleting substances, and heavy metals) and a Ship Recycling Plan agreed with the receiving yard. At the recycling anchorage, Port Health Officers, Customs, and the Gujarat Maritime Board or equivalent authority inspect the vessel; once the Ready for Recycling Certificate and SRP are confirmed against the vessel's actual condition, beaching or dock placement is authorised for contained, sequential dismantling with hazardous materials removed before major structural cutting begins.

Statutory Framework

  • Recycling of Ships Act, 2019 (India): enacted by the Ministry of Ports, Shipping and Waterways, giving effect to the Hong Kong International Convention (HKC), which entered into force globally in mid-2025.
  • DG Shipping: acts as the National Authority auditing recycling facilities and issuing statutory clearances.

Pre-Recycling Phase (Engine Department Duties)

  • Compile and verify IHM Parts I, II, III — CE ensures onboard hazardous materials (refrigerant, asbestos insulation, anti-fouling coatings) are accurately mapped.
  • Cargo, bunker, and slop tanks cleaned, gas-freed, and certified safe for hot work.
  • Ready for Recycling Certificate issued by the Flag Administration/RO after a final survey confirming the IHM and SRP match.

Arrival and Clearance Sequence

  • Anchorage inspection by Port Health Officer, Customs, and pollution control authorities.
  • De-bunkering control — remaining fuel/lube oil inventoried; bilge/sludge quantities reconciled with the Oil Record Book Part I.
  • Ship Recycling Plan reviewed against the vessel's actual hull structure before beaching/docking permission is granted.

Dismantling

Sequential cutting per the approved SRP, hazardous materials removed first; asbestos and toxic waste go to authorised Common Hazardous Waste Treatment, Storage and Disposal Facilities.

Chief Engineer Relevance

Maersk operates its own green-recycling standard ahead of statutory minimums. The CE's continuous, accurate maintenance of IHM Part I throughout the vessel's operating life — logging every dry-dock modification or equipment change — is what allows the final recycling declaration to be valid; failure here causes regulatory delay, penalties, and reputational exposure.

CE Oral Tip: Simon's stock question: "What's the primary engine-department document required before a ship goes for scrap?" Lead immediately with the IHM and the Gas-Free Certificate for engine room tanks/pump rooms/double bottoms, then note that unregulated open beaching without contained aprons is now illegal under India's HKC accession.
Deep-Dive: Traps, Failures, Numbers, Casualty, On My Vessel
Trap Questions: "Can residual sludge/bilge water be left for the yard to process?" — Operational waste must go to shore reception facilities before final delivery unless explicitly agreed in the SRP under certified slop-reception conditions; undeclared quantities are a major violation.
Common CE Failures: Letting the IHM lapse during active service — fitting cheaper components with unmapped heavy-metal alloys or banned sealants breaks the continuous verification chain.
Numbers to Memorise: 5 years — maximum validity of the International Certificate on IHM; IHM has 3 Parts — I (structure/equipment, maintained during operation), II (operationally generated waste), III (stores, compiled pre-recycling).
Casualty Link: Historical toxic exposures and fatal fires at Alang from trapped hydrocarbon gases during cutting drove the 2019 Act's ban on uncertified hot work.
On My Vessel: IHM documentation is updated digitally in the Maersk asset-management database whenever new machinery is fitted, keeping the vessel profile compliant for eventual green decommissioning at an approved Indian facility.

Source confidence: High — cross-checked against India's Recycling of Ships Act, 2019 and the Hong Kong Convention's mid-2025 global entry into force.

Ship recycling cross-questions: what practical, safety-critical checks will an examiner probe beyond the standard procedure?
marpolism
15-Second Answer: Cross-questioning on ship recycling moves from textbook procedure into practical defensive engineering: verifying hidden hazardous materials beyond the IHM, understanding why a 'gas-free' tank can regenerate a dangerous atmosphere later, and demonstrating full piping/refrigerant/waste decontamination before handover.
60-Second Answer: Examiners probe whether a candidate merely relies on a third-party IHM report or actively audits the vessel's own modification history — checking exhaust insulation, gaskets, and old brake linings for undeclared asbestos. They also test whether the candidate understands hydrocarbon regeneration: sludge disturbance or VOC-trapping scale on tank surfaces can make a previously gas-freed tank unsafe again once yard cutting introduces heat. A strong answer sets out the full engineering sequence — piping decontamination, refrigerant recovery reconciliation, and ORB-vs-physical-sounding consistency — before the vessel is handed over.

Core Cross-Question Targets

  • Hidden asbestos: the CE must show they checked historic dry-dock/refit files, exhaust insulation pads, gaskets, and old winch brake linings — not just trusted the IHM as issued.
  • Hydrocarbon regeneration: a tank certified gas-free at anchorage can become dangerous again at the yard due to sludge disturbance, VOC-trapping scale, or unpurged interconnected piping.

Operational Safeguards Before Handover

  • Piping decontamination: fuel transfer lines, sludge lines, purifier headers, and boiler fuel rings flushed with water, blown through with air, and physically blanked off — not just the main tanks.
  • Refrigerant/ODS management: freon recovered into certified shore cylinders with recovery weights matched exactly to logbook records to avoid allegations of illegal venting.
  • Bilge/sludge reconciliation: ORB Part I entries must match physical soundings taken by surveyors on arrival, or the vessel faces detention and the CE faces direct questioning over unrecorded discharges.

Chief Engineer Relevance

As CE approaching a vessel's end of life, the responsibility is to anchor answers in continuous IHM Part I upkeep. Signing off a recycling declaration while unaware of structural modifications or unmapped hazardous materials introduced during the vessel's life exposes the individual to statutory liability under the Recycling of Ships Act, 2019, and can void the vessel's green recycling status.

CE Oral Tip: Simon's scenario trap: "IHM says zero asbestos, but yard surveyors find asbestos gaskets in an auxiliary sea-chest valve during cutting — what do you do, who's responsible?" State clearly: as CE you are responsible for operational IHM maintenance during your tenure; suspend hot work in that zone immediately under the Ship Recycling Plan, notify the National Authority, revise the IHM, and bring in specialised hazardous-waste handlers.
Deep-Dive: Traps, Failures, Numbers, Casualty, On My Vessel
Trap Questions: "Can a standard portable gas meter permanently certify a tank safe for the entire recycling process?" — No; it's a snapshot only. Structural cutting generates localised heat that can re-volatilise residues trapped behind frames/stringers, so continuous monitoring is required throughout.
Common CE Failures: Leaving a single fuel-line cross-over valve unblanked between the engine room and deck filling line, allowing residual oil migration as trim/heel changes during cutting.
Numbers to Memorise: Under 1% LFL — maximum hydrocarbon concentration for initial hot-work entry approval; 21% Vol — required oxygen level; toxic gases (H₂S, CO) — target as close to 0 ppm as achievable before any structural breach.
Casualty Link: An explosion during recycling caused by an unflushed, unisolated deep fuel-oil transfer line — ignited by yard cutting torches — demonstrates that tank cleaning alone is never sufficient without total line isolation.
On My Vessel: Maersk maintains an Equipment HazMat Register synchronised with the PMS so that undocumented modifications cannot occur, keeping the final Ready-for-Recycling Certificate consistent with the vessel's true physical condition down to individual valve packings.
