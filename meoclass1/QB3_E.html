🛢 Air Pollution & Sulphur

Fuel sulphur limit — can you go beyond 0.5% S? How do you come to know the applicable sulphur limit?
MARPOL Annex VI

Governing Requirement

MARPOL Annex VI Regulation 14 sets the sulphur content limits for fuel oil used on board ships. The present global cap is 0.50% m/m, while the SOx ECA limit is 0.10% m/m.

Can a Ship Use Fuel Above 0.50% Sulphur?

Yes, but not as a routine choice. A ship may use higher-sulphur fuel only if fitted with an approved equivalent compliance method — typically an Exhaust Gas Cleaning System (EGCS) — achieving emissions equivalent to compliant fuel. MARPOL also recognises limited exceptions connected with safety of the ship, saving life at sea, or damage to the ship or its equipment.

What the Examiner Wants to Hear

The key distinction is between fuel sulphur content and equivalent SOx emission compliance. If the ship has no approved equivalent arrangement, burning fuel above 0.50% outside an ECA, or above 0.10% inside a SOx ECA, is non-compliance. From 1 March 2020, a non-scrubber ship generally cannot even carry fuel above 0.50% for use on board — this carriage ban catches many candidates out.

How I Know the Applicable Limit on Board

  • Trading area: global waters vs SOx ECA — the legal limit changes accordingly
  • Ship compliance mode: low-sulphur fuel or approved EGCS equivalent arrangement
  • Fuel documents: Bunker Delivery Note and retained fuel sample under Regulation 18
  • Company/port instructions: voyage orders, passage planning inputs, local restrictions

Chief Engineer Practical Application

On a container vessel this is a fuel-management job, not just a legal statement — correct bunker ordering, segregation between grades, purifier/heater settings suited to the fuel in use, and clean documentation for PSC or flag inspection. Any mismatch between voyage area, BDN, engine-room practice, and records is where candidates — and ships — get exposed.

15-Second AnswerUnder MARPOL Annex VI Regulation 14, the normal global sulphur limit for fuel oil used on board is 0.50% m/m, and inside SOx Emission Control Areas the limit is 0.10% m/m. A ship can go beyond 0.50% only when using an approved equivalent arrangement such as an EGCS/scrubber, or where the narrow MARPOL safety-or-damage exceptions apply.
60-Second AnswerFrom 1 January 2020, ships trading outside ECAs must use fuel not exceeding 0.50% sulphur, and from 1 March 2020 ships not fitted with scrubbers are also prohibited from carrying fuel oil above 0.50% for use on board. My first check is the ship's trading area — global waters, SOx ECA waters at 0.10%, or a ship under an approved equivalent arrangement. Then I confirm the fuel's declared sulphur content from the Bunker Delivery Note and retained sample records required under Regulation 18. As CE, I must plan bunkering, segregation, changeover, and records so the vessel never arrives in a controlled area on the wrong fuel.
⚠ Examiner TrapDo not answer, "Yes, outside ECA we can burn anything up to the charterer's requirement." Outside an ECA the ship is still bound by the 0.50% global cap unless using an approved equivalent arrangement. A second trap is forgetting the carriage ban from 1 March 2020.

Numbers to Memorise

Global sulphur limit 0.50% m/m · SOx ECA limit 0.10% m/m · Global cap effective 1 Jan 2020 · Carriage ban (non-scrubber ships) 1 Mar 2020 · BDN retention >3 years; retained sample >12 months.
⚓ Enforcement LinkThis topic is less about a classic machinery casualty and more about detention, enforcement exposure, and reputational damage after non-compliant bunkering or poor fuel-switch planning — a common starting point for PSC and engine-room management follow-up questions.

EGCS (Exhaust Gas Cleaning System / scrubber) working principle — explain.
MARPOL Annex VI

Regulatory Basis

EGCS is recognised as an equivalent compliance route to meet the sulphur requirements of MARPOL Annex VI instead of relying only on low-sulphur fuel. When the examiner asks for "working principle," answer both the engineering principle and the legal purpose.

Engineering Working Principle

Exhaust gas from the engine or boiler enters the scrubber tower, where washwater is sprayed to maximise gas-liquid contact. Sulphur dioxide in the exhaust dissolves into the washwater and is chemically neutralised; soot and fine particulates are also reduced to some extent. The cleaned gas then passes through mist-elimination stages before release to atmosphere.

Types of EGCS

  • Open-loop: uses natural seawater alkalinity; washwater is treated/monitored then discharged overboard subject to limits
  • Closed-loop: recirculated freshwater, normally with alkali addition; only a controlled bleed-off stream is treated, retained, or discharged as permitted
  • Hybrid: can switch between open-loop and closed-loop depending on sea area or port restrictions

Compliance Monitoring

The 2021 EGCS Guidelines, adopted by Resolution MEPC.340(77), govern performance and monitoring expectations. Washwater discharge criteria include pH, PAH, and turbidity limits: pH at discharge of at least 6.5 (or a maximum differential of 2.0 pH units), PAH not more than 50 µg/L above inlet background (normalised to 45 t/MWh), and turbidity not more than 25 FNU above inlet background.

Documentation and Survey Side

After installation, the EGCS is surveyed to confirm it is installed in accordance with the ETM-A (EGCS Technical Manual) and carries the relevant compliance documentation. The CE must understand not only pumps and nozzles, but also the approval basis, monitoring records, malfunction handling, and survey evidence.

Operational CE Points

Live risks are pump failure, nozzle blockage, sensor drift, scaling, sludge handling, alkali handling in closed-loop mode, and alarm response. If the scrubber fails, the ship's compliance mode changes immediately from "equivalent arrangement" to "fuel sulphur dependent" — the CE must know the fallback procedure at once.

15-Second AnswerAn EGCS is an approved equivalent compliance method under MARPOL Annex VI that allows a ship to use higher-sulphur fuel while achieving SOx emissions equivalent to compliant fuel. Its basic principle: exhaust gas contacts alkaline washwater so SOx is absorbed and neutralised, with discharge and monitoring controlled under the 2021 EGCS Guidelines.
60-Second AnswerA scrubber does not make the fuel low-sulphur; it cleans the exhaust after combustion so the ship complies by emissions equivalence rather than fuel specification. Exhaust enters a tower, washwater is sprayed through it, SOx dissolves into the water phase and is neutralised, and some particulate matter is also removed. Open-loop uses seawater alkalinity, closed-loop recirculates freshwater with alkali dosing and controlled bleed-off, hybrid can operate in both modes. IMO's 2021 EGCS Guidelines set monitoring and discharge criteria — pH, PAH and turbidity.
⚠ Examiner TrapQ: Does a scrubber reduce fuel sulphur content? A: No — it does not change the fuel; it reduces SOx emissions after combustion so the ship can comply by an approved equivalent method. Second trap: name all three monitoring parameters (pH, PAH, turbidity), not just one.

Numbers to Memorise

2021 EGCS Guidelines: Resolution MEPC.340(77) · pH ≥6.5 at discharge (or ≤2.0 unit differential) · PAH ≤50 µg/L above background, normalised to 45 t/MWh · Turbidity ≤25 FNU/NTU above inlet background · Malfunction reporting trigger: >1 hour or repetitive.
⚓ Compliance-Risk LinkThe strongest oral link here is not a classic machinery casualty but a compliance breakdown — sensor failure, pump failure, or washwater-quality failure can instantly turn into a PSC and voyage-risk problem, with possible off-hire or detention exposure.

What is a Special Area under MARPOL? What is a PSSA (Particularly Sensitive Sea Area)?
MARPOL Special Area

MARPOL Special Area — Definition

IMO's maintained list on Special Areas states that these are sea areas where, for recognised technical reasons in relation to their oceanographical and ecological condition and the particular character of their traffic, special mandatory methods for the prevention of sea pollution are required. Give this definition first in the oral.

Where the Special Area Concept Applies

The relevant IMO circular identifies Special Areas under MARPOL Annexes I, IV and V. When a ship enters one of those areas, the normal discharge rules become stricter for the pollutant covered by that Annex.

PSSA — Definition

A Particularly Sensitive Sea Area is an area needing special protection through action by IMO because of its ecological, socio-economic, or scientific significance and its vulnerability to damage by international maritime activities. The Revised PSSA Guidelines in Resolution A.982(24) require significance, vulnerability, and suitable protective measures.

How a PSSA Is Protected

A PSSA is protected by Associated Protective Measures such as ships' routeing systems, including an Area To Be Avoided (ATBA) — or reporting/routing/traffic-separation requirements. Compliance depends on the specific measure adopted for that area — a PSSA is not automatically a total "no-go" zone.

Main Difference

A Special Area is mainly a MARPOL discharge-control concept tied to a specific pollution Annex. A PSSA is a broader sensitivity-based IMO designation that triggers special protection through associated shipping measures. In oral form: Special Area = stricter pollution rules; PSSA = sensitive area plus IMO protective measures.

Chief Engineer's Practical Angle

Entry into Special Areas or PSSAs changes operational discipline — discharge planning, tank retention strategy, sewage or garbage handling, sludge landing arrangements, and bridge-engine coordination all become more sensitive. This is especially important on fixed-schedule liner routes where port rotation can push the ship through multiple regulated environmental zones in one voyage.

15-Second AnswerA MARPOL Special Area is a sea area where, because of recognised technical reasons relating to its oceanographical and ecological condition and the particular character of its traffic, special mandatory methods for the prevention of sea pollution are required. A PSSA is broader — an area needing special protection through IMO action because of recognised ecological, socio-economic, or scientific significance and vulnerability to damage from international shipping activities.
60-Second AnswerA Special Area is a MARPOL concept tied to stricter discharge controls under specific Annexes (I, IV, V), while a PSSA is an IMO protective designation based on sensitivity and vulnerability to shipping impacts. Special Areas impose stricter pollution-prevention rules for a particular Annex, whereas a PSSA is protected through associated measures such as routeing systems, reporting systems, or areas to be avoided. A PSSA is not automatically a total "no-go" zone — you must comply with the protective measures adopted for that particular area.
⚠ Examiner TrapDo not say a PSSA and a Special Area are the same thing. A Special Area is a MARPOL annex-based stricter discharge regime; a PSSA is a wider IMO protection concept based on sensitivity and vulnerability to shipping damage. Also, do not say every PSSA is fully prohibited to navigation.

Numbers to Memorise

PSSA guideline resolution: A.982(24) · IMO Special Areas list circular: MEPC.1/Circ.778/Rev.5 · Special Area applies under Annexes I, IV, V · Key PSSA words: ecological, socio-economic, scientific significance, vulnerable to damage.
⚓ Risk LinkIn environmentally sensitive waters, even a minor grounding, overflow, or unlawful discharge has disproportionate legal and ecological consequences — use this to show why IMO created different layers of protection.
