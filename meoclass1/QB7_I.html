1
What is the present technology used onboard to reduce methane slip?
GHGMachinery

15-Second Answer

Present shipboard technology tackles methane slip via a two-pronged strategy: in-cylinder combustion optimisation and exhaust gas aftertreatment. Low-pressure Otto-cycle engines use Variable Valve Timing (VVT)/Miller cycling, micro-pilot injection, and High-Pressure EGR to minimise unburnt gas trapped in cylinder crevices. Exhaust is then routed through a Methane Oxidation Catalyst (MOC) using a Palladium-Platinum (Pd-Pt) substrate to oxidise residual CH4 into CO2 and H2O. Real-world conversion is highly load-dependent — near-100% at low load, dropping to 50-60% at high load — not a flat headline figure.

60-Second Answer

Methane slip is governed primarily by combustion cycle: high-pressure direct injection engines (e.g. MAN ME-GI) have inherently negligible slip (0.2-0.4 g/kWh) because combustion follows the Diesel cycle, avoiding pre-ignition. Low-pressure Otto-cycle systems (4-stroke dual-fuel, and formerly MAN's ME-GA) suffer from crevice trapping and flame quenching, giving baseline slip of 2.0-3.0 g/kWh unmitigated.

Mitigation combines in-cylinder modification — VVT/Miller cycling and precise micro-pilot fuel injection to ensure complete flame propagation — with High-Pressure EGR, which raises the exhaust thermal baseline and suppresses knock. The resulting elevated exhaust temperature supports the downstream Methane Oxidation Catalyst, which uses a Pd-Pt noble-metal catalyst to crack the C-H bond of unburnt methane, typically becoming active in the 300-500°C range.

In-Cylinder Thermodynamic Modifications

The root cause of methane slip in low-pressure dual-fuel (LPDF) engines operating on the Otto cycle is the trapping of the air-gas mixture within cylinder crevices (piston ring land, indicator valve ports, cylinder head recesses) where the flame cannot propagate due to localised wall quenching.

  • Variable Valve Timing (VVT) & Miller Cycling: Early closing of intake valves precisely controls in-cylinder charge temperature and pressure, preventing unburnt gas from short-circuiting into the exhaust manifold during scavenging.
  • Micro-Pilot Fuel Injection: Common-rail electronic injectors deliver multi-stage pilot injections of MGO (~0.5-1% of total energy input), creating multiple ignition nuclei for stable flame propagation across lean air-fuel mixtures.
  • High-Pressure EGR: Routes cooled, filtered exhaust gas back into the charge air receiver, replacing excess oxygen with triatomic CO2/H2O molecules that raise combustion and exhaust temperature.

Post-Combustion Aftertreatment: Methane Oxidation Catalyst (MOC)

Because methane is a highly stable symmetrical molecule with a C-H bond dissociation energy of approximately 439 kJ/mol, standard oxidation catalysts are ineffective at low temperature. Dedicated MOC units use a honeycomb washcoat impregnated with Palladium (Pd) and Platinum (Pt) active sites on an Alumina or Zirconia support, typically becoming active in the 300-500°C range.

CH4 + 2O2 --Pd-Pt catalyst--> CO2 + 2H2O

Thermal Regeneration & Sulfur Poisoning

Palladium-based MOCs are highly sensitive to sulfur poisoning — SOx from cylinder lubricating oil combustion binds to active Pd sites forming Palladium Sulfate (PdSO4), destroying catalytic activity. Thermal regeneration above 500°C desorbs sulfates, but exceeding ~550°C risks thermal sintering — permanent coalescing of the noble-metal nanoparticles, destroying active surface area.

Deep-Dive: Trap Questions

Q: "Is the 98% methane abatement figure commonly quoted for MOC systems accurate?"
A: No, Sir. The authoritative peer-reviewed reference is Lehtoranta et al. (2021, VTT/JMSE), which found MOC decreases methane emission by 70-80% at 550°C exhaust temperature, declining over time from sulfur poisoning and recoverable via daily regeneration. Actual marine exhaust testing also shows conversion is load-dependent — near-100% at low load, 50-60% at high load. A flat 98% figure is not supported by published data.

Q: "Can a Palladium MOC be used indefinitely without maintenance?"
A: No, Sir. Palladium sites are progressively poisoned by SOx from cylinder lubricating oil, requiring periodic thermal regeneration above 500°C. However, exceeding approximately 550°C risks permanently sintering the catalyst, so regeneration must be tightly controlled.

What is the overview of the evolution of the MAN B&W ME-GI and ME-GA engine families to the present day, and what is the next prototype under consideration?
GHGMachinery

15-Second Answer

MAN B&W's dual-fuel lineage began with the ME-GI (high-pressure, ~300 bar, Diesel-cycle LNG injection — negligible methane slip). To cut shipyard CAPEX, MAN introduced the ME-GA (low-pressure, ~15-16 bar, Otto-cycle) in 2021 — but MAN discontinued the ME-GA in November 2024, citing incoming IMO methane-slip regulation (expected 2027) that would have required costly redesign. MAN ES now offers only the ME-GI for LNG. Parallel liquid-fuel variants — ME-LGIP (LPG) and ME-LGIM (methanol, now the dominant container-newbuild choice) — remain in production. The current next-generation prototype under scale-up is the ME-LGIA (liquid ammonia), which passed Type Approval Tests in mid-2024.

60-Second Answer

The ME-GI uses late-cycle direct injection of LNG at ~300 bar near TDC, following the Diesel cycle — this avoids knock and gives ultra-low methane slip (0.2-0.4 g/kWh), but requires an expensive high-pressure Fuel Gas Supply System. To lower the CAPEX barrier for LNG carriers, MAN launched the ME-GA in 2021, injecting gas at low pressure (~15-16 bar) on the Otto cycle, relying on High-Pressure EGR to control knock and NOx. Over 260 ME-GA engines were ordered, with 46 already in service.

However, MAN Energy Solutions discontinued the ME-GA engine line in November 2024 — just three years after launch — after concluding that upcoming IMO methane-slip regulations (expected to enter force around 2027), combined with EU FuelEU Maritime (2025) and EU ETS methane pricing (2026), would require significant technical redesign investment that was not commercially justified. MAN ES now offers only the high-pressure ME-GI for new LNG dual-fuel orders; existing in-service ME-GA units continue operating. In parallel, MAN adapted the ME platform for liquid alternative fuels: ME-LGIP (LPG, ~50-60 bar) and ME-LGIM (methanol, ~80 bar via Fuel Booster Injection Valves), the latter now the leading choice for container newbuilds including Maersk's methanol-fuelled fleet. The current frontier prototype is the ME-LGIA (ammonia), which completed Type Approval Tests in mid-2024 and is undergoing multi-cylinder scale-up and field verification, with the central technical challenge being nitrous oxide (N2O) slip from incomplete ammonia combustion.

ME-GI — High-Pressure Diesel-Cycle LNG (established, still offered)

Late-cycle direct injection of LNG at approximately 300 bar near TDC. Because combustion follows the Diesel cycle, it avoids pre-ignition/knock and achieves ultra-low methane slip (0.2-0.4 g/kWh), at the cost of an expensive high-pressure Fuel Gas Supply System.

ME-GA — Low-Pressure Otto-Cycle LNG (DISCONTINUED November 2024)

This simplified the Fuel Gas Supply System dramatically but introduced knock/pre-ignition vulnerability, mitigated with mandatory High-Pressure EGR, which reduced methane slip by a reported 30-50% versus early Otto-cycle designs without EGR.

Expansion into Liquid Alternative Fuels — ME-LGIP & ME-LGIM

  • ME-LGIP (LPG): Developed for LPG carriers, injecting liquid petroleum gas at approximately 50-60 bar, using the cargo itself as fuel.
  • ME-LGIM (Methanol): Injects liquid methanol at ~80 bar via dedicated Fuel Booster Injection Valves (FBIV). Because methanol is liquid at ambient temperature, it avoids cryogenic storage complexity — this is why it has become the dominant alternative-fuel standard for container newbuilds, including Maersk A/S's methanol dual-fuel fleet.

The Next Prototype — ME-LGIA (Ammonia)

The ME-LGIA is MAN's zero-carbon frontier engine, using high-pressure liquid direct injection (600-700 bar) of ammonia late in the compression stroke, combined with a pilot fuel fraction (~10-15% energy share) because ammonia has a high auto-ignition temperature (~651°C) and narrow flammability limits. It completed Type Approval Tests in mid-2024 and is undergoing multi-cylinder scale-up and field verification through 2025/2026.

Note: ME-LGIA remains a prototype under scale-up, not yet in commercial fleet service — do not state it as "in service" in the oral exam.

The N2O Slip Trap: Incomplete or low-temperature ammonia combustion yields nitrous oxide (N2O), with a global warming potential roughly 265-298 times that of CO2. Uncontrolled N2O slip can fully negate ammonia's carbon-free advantage, requiring dedicated De-N2O SCR catalyst systems.

Compare methane slip control between the MAN ME-GA (Otto-cycle, two-stroke) engine and low-pressure Otto-cycle 4-stroke dual-fuel engines.
GHGMachinery

15-Second Answer

The (now-discontinued, legacy-fleet) two-stroke ME-GA used uniflow scavenging with gas admission mid-stroke and relied on High-Pressure EGR to suppress knock and reduce methane slip. Four-stroke low-pressure Otto engines (e.g. Wärtsilä, WinGD X-DF) suffer slip mainly from piston ring crevice trapping and valve-overlap blow-through, mitigated with Variable Valve Timing (VVT)/Miller cycling. WinGD's second-generation X-DF2.0 uses iCER (low-pressure cooled EGR) to cut slip by up to 50%, reaching 1.0-1.2 g/kWh versus 2.0-2.5 g/kWh on first-generation units.

60-Second Answer

Methane slip control depends heavily on combustion chamber geometry and scavenging design. The two-stroke ME-GA admitted gas via valves in the cylinder liner wall at 15-16 bar early in the compression stroke, using a uniflow-scavenged layout with a single overhead exhaust valve. This kept the gas-air mixture concentrated centrally, away from cold liner walls. It controlled knock and slip primarily via High-Pressure EGR.

Four-stroke low-pressure Otto-cycle engines (Wärtsilä 46DF/31DF, WinGD X-DF) inject gas into the intake port at lower pressure (4-8 bar), making the charge susceptible to escaping during valve overlap. Slip also originates from piston ring crevice volumes where flame quenches against cold cylinder walls. Mitigation relies on VVT/Miller cycling to shorten intake duration and lower compression temperature. WinGD's X-DF2.0 platform introduced iCER (Intelligent Control by Exhaust Recycling — cooled low-pressure EGR), cutting fuel consumption and methane slip by up to 50%, bringing slip down to approximately 1.0-1.2 g/kWh versus 2.0-2.5 g/kWh on first-generation units — a very similar approach and outcome to what MAN achieved on the ME-GA using high-pressure EGR.

Context correction: MAN discontinued the ME-GA engine line in November 2024. This comparison remains valid for understanding the engineering principles and for the existing ME-GA fleet (46+ units in service), but the ME-GA should not be described as a current production option — see Q2.

Gas Admission Location and Timing

  • ME-GA (two-stroke): Gas admission valves in the cylinder liner wall, gas admitted at 15-16 bar early in compression after the piston covers the ports — homogeneous mixture concentrated toward the combustion space centre.
  • 4-stroke dual-fuel: Gas injected into the intake port channel upstream of the inlet valve at 4-8 bar, entering with intake air — vulnerable to escaping during valve overlap when both inlet and exhaust valves are briefly open together.

Scavenging Architecture and Flame Quenching

The two-stroke ME-GA's uniflow scavenging (single overhead exhaust valve, piston-controlled ports) confines the charge dynamically, reducing wall-quenching losses. Four-stroke loop/cross-scavenging designs lose unburnt methane in two distinct zones: piston ring pack crevice volumes (flame cannot propagate due to thermal quenching) and the valve overlap period (direct charge escape to exhaust).

Aftertreatment Strategy Comparison

  • Two-stroke (ME-GA): High-Pressure EGR, reported to cut methane slip 30-50% versus non-EGR first-generation Otto-cycle engines.
  • Four-stroke (WinGD X-DF2.0): iCER — cooled, low-pressure EGR — achieving similar slip reductions, bringing slip to ~1.0-1.2 g/kWh from ~2.0-2.5 g/kWh on first-generation platforms.

What safety systems are unique to ammonia-fuelled two-stroke engines (MAN ME-LGIA), and what is the N2O slip risk associated with ammonia combustion?
GHGMachinery

15-Second Answer

The ME-LGIA prototype uses high-pressure liquid direct injection (600-700 bar) with a 10-15% pilot fuel fraction to stabilise ammonia's slow flame propagation. Because ammonia (NH3) is toxic and corrosive, the engine incorporates an automated Ammonia Catch System (ACS) to purge and neutralise vapour during fuel-changeover or emergency shutdown. Incomplete combustion risks N2O slip — a greenhouse gas with GWP ~265-298x CO2 — requiring dedicated De-N2O SCR aftertreatment.

60-Second Answer

Ammonia's high auto-ignition temperature (~651°C) and narrow flammability limits mean the ME-LGIA cannot rely on compression ignition alone — it requires a substantial pilot fuel fraction (10-15% energy share, typically MGO) injected alongside high-pressure liquid ammonia (600-700 bar) late in the compression stroke to stabilise the flame front.

Because ammonia is highly toxic (IDLH ~300 ppm) and corrosive to certain metals, the fuel system uses double-walled piping, nitrogen-purged interspaces, and an automated Ammonia Catch System (ACS) that displaces liquid ammonia out of the injectors into a dedicated catch tank or water-baffled expansion tank during fuel changeover or emergency shutdown — an instant fuel-oil trip is not possible; the changeover follows a choreographed automated sequence to prevent toxic release into the engine room. On the emissions side, incomplete or low-temperature combustion generates N2O, with a GWP of roughly 265-298 times CO2 over 100 years — uncontrolled N2O slip can fully offset ammonia's zero-carbon combustion advantage, making dedicated high-temperature De-N2O SCR catalyst systems (using vanadium-titanium elements) a mandatory design feature.

High-Pressure Injection and Pilot Fuel Requirement

Liquid ammonia is injected directly into the cylinder at 600-700 bar late in the compression stroke. Due to ammonia's high auto-ignition temperature and slow laminar burning velocity, a pilot fuel fraction of approximately 10-15% of energy input (early-version figure) is required to stabilise the flame front and ensure complete combustion.

Toxicity and Emergency Safety Systems

  • Ammonia Catch System (ACS): An automated system that purges and neutralises toxic ammonia vapour from fuel lines during changeover or emergency shutdown, preventing toxic release into the engine room.
  • Double-walled piping and nitrogen purging: Standard IGF Code-driven design features for toxic, low-flashpoint fuel systems, isolating ammonia from occupied spaces.
  • No instant fuel-oil trip: Unlike conventional fuel changeover, an emergency shift away from ammonia requires a choreographed automated nitrogen purge sequence to safely displace liquid ammonia from the high-pressure rails before switching to conventional injectors.

The N2O Emissions Trap

Ammonia combustion produces zero CO2, but incomplete combustion — particularly at low load or during transients when flame temperature drops — generates Nitrous Oxide (N2O), with a global warming potential of approximately 265-298 times that of CO2 over 100 years. Even minor N2O slip can negate the entire carbon-free benefit under Well-to-Wake accounting (FuelEU Maritime, EU ETS).

How does the FuelEU Maritime GHG intensity reduction trajectory influence main engine and fuel technology selection onboard?
GHG

15-Second Answer

FuelEU Maritime (Regulation (EU) 2023/1805) sets a step-down Well-to-Wake GHG intensity trajectory against a 2020 baseline of 91.16 gCO2eq/MJ: 2% reduction from 2025, 6% from 2030, 14.5% from 2035, 31% from 2040, 62% from 2045, and 80% from 2050. Because it counts methane and N2O on a lifecycle basis, it directly penalises methane-slip-prone engines and rewards methanol (ME-LGIM) and, longer-term, ammonia (ME-LGIA) platforms.

60-Second Answer

FuelEU Maritime tracks the full lifecycle (Well-to-Wake) GHG intensity of energy used onboard, not just stack emissions. Against the 2020 fleet baseline of 91.16 gCO2eq/MJ, the mandated reduction steps down every five years: 2% (2025), 6% (2030), 14.5% (2035), 31% (2040), 62% (2045), 80% (2050). Vessels exceeding the intensity limit face financial penalties; over-performing vessels can bank, borrow, or pool compliance surpluses.

This structure directly shapes engine and fuel choice. Because CH4 and N2O both carry high global warming potential multipliers, low-pressure Otto-cycle engines with unmitigated methane slip are penalised through default Tank-to-Wake emission factors, while liquid alternative fuels like green methanol (ME-LGIM) — which avoid methane slip entirely — gain a structural compliance advantage. Ammonia (ME-LGIA) offers a long-term zero-carbon pathway but only once N2O slip is controlled and the supply chain shifts to green production. From 2030, container and passenger ships must also connect to shore power at major EU ports (extending to all EU ports with shore power from 2035), further reducing at-berth GHG intensity.

Well-to-Wake Lifecycle Accounting

Unlike frameworks measuring only stack emissions, FuelEU Maritime tracks emissions from fuel production and distribution (Well-to-Tank) through combustion (Tank-to-Wake), and explicitly includes CH4 and N2O, not just CO2.

Penalty Structure for Non-Compliance

If a vessel does not close its compliance deficit via banking, borrowing, or pooling, the fixed financial penalty is €2,400 per tonne VLSFO-equivalent of non-compliant energy. For a ship with a compliance deficit in two or more consecutive reporting periods, the penalty is multiplied by 1 + (n-1)/10 — producing escalating multipliers of roughly +10%, +20%, +30% for the 2nd, 3rd, 4th consecutive non-compliant year. The Document of Compliance is only issued once the penalty is paid.

Impact on Engine and Fuel Selection

  • Methane Slip Penalisation: Low-pressure Otto-cycle engines are assigned a default Tank-to-Wake methane emission factor that structurally penalises their compliance score.
  • Methanol Dominance: Green methanol (ME-LGIM) avoids methane slip entirely and remains liquid at ambient temperature, making it the preferred current compliance pathway for container newbuilds.
  • Ammonia Preparedness: For post-2045 compliance, zero-carbon ammonia (ME-LGIA) becomes critical — but only if N2O slip is controlled and the fuel is produced via green pathways (grey ammonia retains a high Well-to-Tank score).

GWP Multipliers Driving Aftertreatment Investment

Methane carries a GWP factor of approximately 28-30x CO2 (AR5, 100-year), and N2O approximately 265-298x. These multipliers mean even small slip rates materially affect calculated carbon intensity, driving investment in EGR, MOC, and SCR/De-N2O systems.

What is the role of High-Pressure Exhaust Gas Recirculation (HP-EGR) in knock control and emissions management on Otto-cycle dual-fuel engines?
MachineryGHG

15-Second Answer

HP-EGR routes hot exhaust gas from before the turbocharger back into the scavenge air stream on low-pressure Otto-cycle engines. The triatomic CO2/H2O molecules increase the charge's thermal mass and reduce excess oxygen concentration, lowering peak combustion temperature to suppress knock and pre-ignition, while simultaneously suppressing thermal NOx formation to help meet MARPOL Annex VI Tier III limits.

60-Second Answer

On low-pressure Otto-cycle dual-fuel engines, gas is admitted early in the compression stroke, making the charge vulnerable to knocking (uncontrolled auto-ignition of end-gas) and pre-ignition from hot spots or oil droplets. HP-EGR redirects a controlled volume of exhaust gas from before the turbine inlet into the scavenge air receiver.

The recirculated exhaust contains CO2 and H2O — triatomic molecules with higher molar heat capacity than diatomic O2/N2 — which increases the charge's thermal mass, slows chemical reaction rates, and dampens the heat release profile. This extends the ignition delay period, preventing the erratic pressure waves that cause knock. Reducing oxygen concentration also limits the thermal NOx formation pathway, helping the engine meet Tier III ECA limits, in some designs without secondary SCR aftertreatment.

Thermodynamic Principles of Knock Suppression

Engine knock occurs when unburnt end-gas ahead of the flame front is exposed to high temperature/pressure for too long and auto-ignites prematurely.

  • Thermal Mass Modification: CO2 and H2O absorb more thermal energy per degree of temperature rise than standard air, reducing peak localised cylinder temperatures.
  • Chemical Kinetics Retardation: Diluting intake air with inert exhaust components reduces reactive oxygen concentration, extending pre-flame reaction time.
  • Ignition Delay Extension: The pilot-fuel-initiated flame front can travel across the chamber before remaining end-gases can detonate prematurely.

System Architecture

The loop draws exhaust gas before the turbine inlet, cools/scrubs it, and blows it into the scavenge receiver via an EGR blower, maintaining positive pressure differential across the turbocharging system.

NOx Co-Benefit

By reducing oxygen concentration and peak flame temperature, HP-EGR suppresses the thermal-NOx formation pathway, supporting Tier III ECA compliance alongside knock control — a genuine dual-purpose system, not solely an emissions add-on.

What are Wind-Assisted Propulsion Systems (WAPS) and what is their current adoption status on merchant ships?
GHG

15-Second Answer

WAPS use mechanical wind technologies — Flettner rotor sails (Magnus effect), rigid suction wings, and towing kites — to generate auxiliary thrust. Documented fuel/emissions savings are route- and speed-dependent, commonly cited in the roughly 5-20% range. Adoption is growing on tankers and bulk carriers with open deck space, driven by CII rating improvement and EU ETS cost avoidance; container ship retrofits require careful design around crane operations and cargo layout.

60-Second Answer

WAPS reduce main engine load using wind energy. Flettner rotors are motor-driven vertical cylinders that exploit the Magnus effect — spinning cylinders create a pressure differential in an airstream, generating forward thrust. Rigid suction wings use internal fans to control boundary-layer separation for high lift from a compact footprint. Towing kites fly at altitude (commonly cited around 100-300m) to capture stronger, steadier winds.

Adoption is driven by the IMO Carbon Intensity Indicator and FuelEU Maritime — reducing fuel burn while maintaining speed directly improves CII rating and lowers EU ETS carbon costs. Installation is comparatively straightforward on tankers and bulk carriers with open deck space; retrofitting container vessels is more complex, requiring designs (e.g. tilting foundations) that avoid interfering with cargo crane operations, container stack sightlines, and bridge visibility requirements under SOLAS Chapter V Regulation 22.

Aerodynamic Mechanisms

  • Flettner Rotor Sails: Motor-driven spinning cylinders; airflow speeds up on one side and slows on the other, creating a pressure differential (Magnus Effect) generating forward thrust.
  • Rigid Suction Wings: Vertical aerofoils with internal fans drawing air through surface slots to control boundary-layer flow, generating high lift from a compact footprint.
  • Towing Kites: Automated foils flown ahead of the vessel at altitude to access stronger, more consistent winds away from surface friction.

Fleet Adoption Drivers

  • CII Rating Optimisation: Reduced fuel consumption directly improves the Carbon Intensity Indicator rating, potentially moving a vessel from a failing band toward a compliant one.
  • EU ETS Cost Avoidance: Lower fuel burn reduces the carbon allowances an operator must purchase.
Verify before oral: Specific percentage savings figures for WAPS vary significantly by source, route, vessel type, and installation — treat any single percentage (including ranges quoted here) as indicative, not a fixed guaranteed figure, and cite the specific technology/vessel/route context if asked for a number.

Container Vessel Integration Challenges

  • Deck Space and Cargo Constraints: Units must avoid interfering with container cell guides or shore gantry cranes; some designs use tilting/folding foundations.
  • Stability Impact: Wind side-forces introduce additional heeling moments — GM management becomes relevant when large WAPS units are fitted.
  • Bridge Visibility: Height/placement must comply with SOLAS Chapter V Regulation 22 forward visibility requirements.

What is Onboard Carbon Capture (OCC) technology and what is its current state of deployment on merchant vessels?
GHG

15-Second Answer

OCC captures CO2 from engine exhaust using amine-based chemical absorption, then compresses/liquefies it for storage in cryogenic tanks. It remains a pilot/Approval-in-Principle (AIP) stage technology on select vessels, carrying a significant energy penalty (commonly cited in the 15-30% range) from solvent regeneration and liquefaction — a bridge technology for EU ETS and FuelEU compliance, not yet mainstream.

60-Second Answer

OCC is a post-combustion exhaust treatment: exhaust gas passes through an amine solvent absorption column, where the amine chemically binds CO2 (forming carbamates) at lower temperature. The CO2-rich solvent is then heated in a stripper column (commonly cited around 120-140°C using auxiliary steam) to reverse the reaction and release high-purity CO2, regenerating the solvent for reuse.

Released CO2 is compressed, dehydrated (to prevent hydrate/ice formation), and liquefied cryogenically for storage — commonly cited around -35°C to -50°C and 7-20 bar, figures that vary by system design. The major operational hurdle is the energy penalty: steam for solvent regeneration plus electrical load for cryogenic compression can meaningfully increase fuel consumption, with industry figures commonly cited in the 15-30% range depending on capture rate and vessel integration. Despite this, classification societies have issued Approvals in Principle for maritime OCC, and container/bulk operators are running active shipboard pilots to manage EU ETS and step-down FuelEU liabilities.

Post-Combustion Chemical Absorption

  • Amine Scrubbing Loop: Exhaust gas contacts a liquid amine solvent in a counter-current absorption tower; the solvent reacts exothermically with CO2 to form stable carbamates.
  • Thermal Stripping: The rich solvent is heated (commonly cited ~120-140°C, using auxiliary boiler steam) in a stripper column, reversing the reaction to release high-purity CO2 and regenerate the solvent.

Liquefaction and Storage

CO2 is compressed, dehydrated to prevent hydrate/ice formation in downstream piping, and cryogenically liquefied for storage in dedicated tanks.

Verify before oral: Specific temperature/pressure storage figures and the energy-penalty percentage vary meaningfully between vendor designs and are still evolving as pilot data accumulates — quote them as indicative industry figures, not settled engineering constants, and be ready to explain the mechanism even if exact numbers are challenged.

Deployment Status

OCC remains at the pilot and Approval-in-Principle (AIP) stage. Classification societies have issued AIPs for maritime OCC systems, and container/bulk operators are running active shipboard trials, but it is not yet standard commercial equipment — driven primarily by the need to manage EU ETS carbon allowance costs and step-down FuelEU Maritime compliance.

Key Failure Mode — Heat Stable Salt (HSS) Formation

If upstream SOx scrubbing is inadequate, SOx entering the amine loop forms Heat Stable Salts that cannot be thermally regenerated, progressively degrading solvent capacity and requiring costly solvent replacement.

Deep-Dive: Trap Questions

Q: "What happens if the dehydration unit fails and moisture enters the liquefied CO2 storage tanks?"
A: Sir, moisture reacts with concentrated CO2 to form carbonic acid, creating a corrosive environment attacking internal steel surfaces of piping and storage tanks. Water vapour can also freeze into ice crystals in refrigeration expansion valves and downstream piping, causing blockages that risk overpressure events and system shutdowns.

Q: "Does OCC eliminate the need for other emissions controls?"
A: No, Sir. OCC targets CO2 specifically; it does not replace SOx scrubbing, NOx Tier III controls, or methane slip mitigation — and in fact depends on effective upstream SOx removal to protect the amine solvent from Heat Stable Salt formation.

What is shore power (cold ironing) and how does its uptake affect a vessel's CII and emissions compliance?
GHG

15-Second Answer

Shore power (cold ironing / Alternative Maritime Power) lets a vessel shut down auxiliary diesel generators at berth by connecting to the shore grid, standardised under IEC/ISO/IEEE 80005-1 at 6.6 kV or 11 kV. Eliminating at-berth fuel burn improves the vessel's CII rating. Under FuelEU Maritime, container and passenger ships must connect at major EU (TEN-T) ports from 2030, extending to all EU ports with shore power facility from 2035.

60-Second Answer

Alternative Maritime Power modifies the ship's electrical distribution to accept external power while moored. Shipboard hardware includes a motorised cable management reel, high-voltage plugs, an isolation transformer (to separate shore and ship grounding), and interlocking switchgear, all conforming to IEC/ISO/IEEE 80005-1. Because shore grids vary (50Hz vs 60Hz, different voltage standards), frequency conversion and step-down transformation to the ship's typical 440V distribution is required.

CII calculations account for all fuel consumed across transit and port stays, so eliminating auxiliary engine fuel burn during port calls directly lowers annual carbon intensity. Under FuelEU Maritime, container and passenger ships must connect to shore power (or a zero-emission alternative) at major EU TEN-T ports from 2030 when berthed over two hours, with the requirement extending to all EU ports with a shore power facility from 2035. Non-connection at a port where OPS is mandatory and available results in financial penalties.

Technical Equipment and Standards

  • IEC/ISO/IEEE 80005-1: The governing international standard for high-voltage shore connection systems.
  • Cable Management Reel: Motorised winch deploying/retrieving heavy high-voltage flexible cables.
  • Isolation Transformer: Separates the ship's electrical ground network from the shore grid, preventing stray galvanic currents from accelerating hull corrosion.
  • Interlocking Switchgear: High-voltage breakers with mechanical/electrical interlocks preventing accidental contact with energised circuits.

Frequency and Voltage Synchronisation

Port grids operate at 50Hz (common in Europe/Asia) or 60Hz (common in the Americas). Where the ship's grid frequency doesn't match the port supply, power must route through an onboard solid-state frequency converter before reaching the main switchboard.

Seamless Power Transfer Sequence

  • Parallel Synchronisation: The CE coordinates with the bridge and shore technicians; the incoming shore breaker is synchronised with the running auxiliary generator using a synchroscope, matching voltage, frequency, and phase angle.
  • Load Shedding: Once breakers are paralleled, load is shifted from the auxiliary diesel to the shore grid by adjusting governor speed control.
  • Generator Disconnection: When generator load approaches zero, its breaker opens and the diesel completes a standard cool-down before stopping.

CII and FuelEU Compliance Impact

  • CII: Eliminating at-berth auxiliary fuel burn lowers total annual carbon emissions, directly improving the numerator of the CII calculation.
  • FuelEU At-Berth Mandate: Container and passenger ships must use OPS at major EU TEN-T ports from 2030 (stays over two hours), extending to all EU ports with the facility from 2035.
  • Local Environmental Zones: US West Coast (CARB) and Chinese Emission Control Area rules independently mandate/incentivise shore connection with their own penalty structures.

Deep-Dive: Trap Questions

Q: "Does connecting to shore power completely eliminate a vessel's GHG footprint under FuelEU's Well-to-Wake accounting?"
A: No, Sir. Shore power cuts localised Tank-to-Wake stack emissions to zero at berth, but FuelEU evaluates emissions Well-to-Wake — if the port grid relies on fossil generation, the corresponding Well-to-Tank emission factor still counts toward the vessel's compliance calculation. Only grid power certified from renewable sources achieves a true zero-emission score.

Q: "Why can't the high-voltage shore cable connect directly to the ship's 440V switchboard without a transformer?"
A: Sir, a direct connection is impossible and dangerous for two reasons: shore power is supplied at 6.6kV or 11kV to minimise cable thickness and transmission losses, which would destroy a standard 440V switchboard; and the isolation transformer separates the shore grid's neutral grounding from the ship's insulated hull ground, preventing stray currents that cause galvanic corrosion and protecting equipment from shore-side ground faults.
