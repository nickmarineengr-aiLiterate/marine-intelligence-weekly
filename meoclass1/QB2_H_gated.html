Q1
What do the FSS Code and FTP Code contain?
firesolasExaminer: Nair
15-Second AnswerThe FSS Code is the mandatory 15-chapter engineering-specification companion to SOLAS Ch II-2, covering every fixed fire-fighting/detection system from portable extinguishers to inert gas systems. The FTP Code specifies the laboratory test methods and type-approval procedures used to certify the materials/systems SOLAS II-2 and the FSS Code require.
60-Second AnswerThe FSS Code (Res MSC.98(73)) was adopted in December 2000 to provide engineering standards for fire safety systems required by SOLAS Ch II-2, made mandatory and in force 1 July 2002. It applies to ships built after that date, in 15 chapters: General; International Shore Connections; Personnel Protection; Fire Extinguishers; Fixed Gas Fire-Extinguishing Systems; Fixed Foam Systems; Fixed Pressure Water-Spraying/Water Mist Systems; Automatic Sprinkler/Detection/Alarm Systems; Fixed Fire Detection and Alarm Systems; Sample Extraction Smoke Detection; Low Location Lighting; Fixed Emergency Fire Pumps; Means of Escape; Fixed Deck Foam Systems (tankers); Inert Gas Systems (tankers). The FTP Code (2010, Res MSC.307(88), in force 1 July 2012) is the companion instrument defining laboratory test methods, type-approval procedures, and pass/fail criteria — e.g. the standard fire test defining A-60/A-30/A-15/A-0 class divisions — for materials referenced in SOLAS II-2 and the FSS Code.

FSS Code — Purpose and Structure

The International Code for Fire Safety Systems, Res MSC.98(73), gives the engineering specification for fire safety systems SOLAS Ch II-2 requires in general terms. In force 1 July 2002, mandatory for ships built after that date.

FSS Code — 15 Chapters

  1. General (definitions)
  2. International Shore Connections
  3. Personnel Protection
  4. Fire Extinguishers
  5. Fixed Gas Fire-Extinguishing Systems
  6. Fixed Foam Fire-Extinguishing Systems
  7. Fixed Pressure Water-Spraying / Water Mist Systems
  8. Automatic Sprinkler, Fire Detection & Alarm Systems
  9. Fixed Fire Detection & Fire Alarm Systems
  10. Sample Extraction Smoke Detection Systems
  11. Low Location Lighting Systems
  12. Fixed Emergency Fire Pumps
  13. Arrangement of Means of Escape
  14. Fixed Deck Foam Systems (tankers)
  15. Inert Gas Systems (tankers)

FTP Code — Purpose

The International Code for Application of Fire Test Procedures (2010, Res MSC.307(88), in force 1 July 2012) specifies standardised laboratory fire-test methods and type-approval procedures for materials/equipment referenced by SOLAS II-2 and the FSS Code — e.g. the standard fire test defining A-60/A-30/A-15/A-0 divisions by time-to-temperature-rise, and approval for low-flame-spread surfaces, fire doors/dampers.

Standard fire test — A-60 division pass criteria: tested against the standard time-temperature curve (furnace reaching approximately 945°C at 60 minutes) for 60 minutes' duration. On the unexposed side, the average temperature rise must not exceed 140°C (250°F) above ambient, and no single point (including any joint) may rise more than 180°C (325°F) above ambient. The same dual-limit logic (average + single-point) applies to A-30/A-15/A-0 at their respective durations.

Relationship

SOLAS II-2 sets the goal ("this space shall have fire detection"); the FSS Code sets the engineering specification for how the system is built/installed; the FTP Code sets how the materials/components used are tested and type-approved before fitting.

Key Numbers / Regs

  • FSS Code: MSC.98(73), in force 1 Jul 2002, 15 chapters
  • FTP Code: MSC.307(88) (2010 ed.), in force 1 Jul 2012
  • Class A-60: average ≤140°C (250°F) / single point ≤180°C (325°F) temp rise, unexposed side, 60 min
  • FSS Ch 15 = Inert Gas Systems (tankers only)

Deep-Dive — CE Relevance, Traps, Failures, Numbers, Casualty, Examiner Chain, On My Vessel
Chief Engineer Relevance

As CE you're the person most likely asked which FSS chapter governs a specific PMS item (Ch 8/9 fire detection panel, Ch 12 emergency fire pump) — knowing the chapter number demonstrates regulatory grounding, not just equipment familiarity.

Trap Questions

Q: 'Which FSS chapters don't apply to your container vessel?' A: Ch 14 (deck foam) and Ch 15 (inert gas) — both tanker-specific.
Q: 'Does FTP test the system or the material?' A: Primarily materials/components — it's a test/type-approval standard, not a system-design spec (that's the FSS Code's role).

Common CE Failures

Confusing FSS (system spec) with FTP (material test/approval) as the same thing; not knowing either resolution number; missing that Ch 14–15 don't apply to container ships.

Numbers to Memorise

MSC.98(73) = FSS Code; MSC.307(88) = 2010 FTP Code; 1 Jul 2002 / 1 Jul 2012 in-force dates; 15 = FSS chapters; A-60 = average ≤140°C / single point ≤180°C, 60 min.

Casualty Link

The 1996 SOLAS fire safety amendments (predecessor framework) followed passenger/cargo ship fires in the early 1990s, including the Scandinavian Star fire (1990) — a driver for the structured code-based approach.

FSS Code systems directly fitted and maintained on my vessel (Ch 1–13 apply; Ch 14 deck foam and Ch 15 inert gas are tanker-specific and do not apply): fixed CO2 system (Ch 5) as the primary extinguishing system for the engine room, sized to deliver a CO2 quantity equivalent to 35% of the gross volume of the largest engine room space (40% where boilers are fitted), dischargeable within 2 minutes for the first 85% of the required quantity; a fixed CO2 flooding system also protects the cargo holds per SOLAS II-2/Reg 10.7 and FSS Code Ch 5, sharing the same central CO2 room/bottle bank as the engine room system via separate distribution manifolds; fixed fire detection and alarm system (Ch 9) covering accommodation, machinery spaces, and cargo hold smoke sampling; and a fixed emergency fire pump (Ch 12) located independently of the main engine room. For container fires specifically: SOLAS II-2/10.7.3 (ships built on/after 1 January 2016 designed to carry containers on/above the weather deck) mandates at least one water mist lance onboard, capable of penetrating a container wall to inject water mist directly into a burning stack — my vessel carries the branded 'Hydropen' system, which satisfies this statutory requirement directly, not merely as a supplementary tool. Ships built before 2016 are recommended, though not mandated, to carry the same equipment per flag administration guidance.

Q2
Difference between water lance and 'hydropen' for internal container firefighting, and use of water monitors.
fss code ch 10deck cargo firecontainer firefightingsolas ii-2/10Examiner: Simon
15-Second Answer

The conventional water lance is a manually driven, physical probe used to pierce a container wall to discharge water inside, requiring high physical effort and proximity to the fire. In contrast, the HydroPen system is an automated, water-pressure-driven drilling and spraying unit that hooks onto the container corner casting, ensuring safer, hands-free penetration and optimal mist distribution. On vessels with high-cube stacks, mobile water monitors are deployed on deck to provide continuous boundary cooling and suppress external thermal radiation.

60-Second Answer

Under SOLAS Regulation II-2/10.7.3 (amended by MSC.365(93) for vessels built after January 1, 2016, designed to carry containers on deck), ships must carry specific equipment for internal container firefighting. The traditional water lance requires crew members to physically hammer or force a hardened nozzle through the container outer skin, which is structurally hazardous and highly labor-intensive under intense heat. The HydroPen system—complying with these modern mandates—utilizes the fire main’s hydraulic pressure to automatically drill a dual-action rotor nozzle through the container steel, transitioning seamlessly from drilling to a 360-degree extinguishing mist without requiring continuous manual assistance. Additionally, SOLAS requires at least two mobile water monitors for vessels with a beam over 30 meters to ensure sustained, high-volume external cooling of adjacent stacks.

Governing Instrument

  • SOLAS Regulation II-2/10.7.3: Mandates specialized firefighting equipment for ships designed to carry containers on or above the weather deck (effective for constructs post-1 January 2016).
  • ISO 1496-1: Standards governing container structural integrity, which firefighting tools must penetrate.

Definition or Core Concept

  • Water Lance: A traditional manual penetration tool featuring a hardened steel piercing tip, an impact surface, and a perforated lance body connected to a standard fire hose. It relies entirely on kinetic energy (sledgehammer blows) to breach the container skin.
  • HydroPen System: An advanced, semi-automatic container firefighting system. It is mounted onto the container's corner castings or side pockets using a telescopic pole and uses the pressure of the delivered fire main water (typically 5–7 bar) to drive an internal turbine. This turbine drills a hole through the steel plate and automatically switches to an internal mist-spray pattern once penetration is complete.
  • Mobile Water Monitors: Portable, high-capacity water monitors (minimum capacity 60 m³/h or 1,000 l/min) pinned to the deck to provide continuous, unmanned water screens or boundary cooling over elevated container tiers.

Key Requirements or Procedure

  • Deploying a Manual Water Lance: 1. Identify the container boundary hotspot using a thermal imaging camera.
    1. Position crew on adjacent lashing bridges with full PPE/SCBA.
    2. Physically drive the lance through the upper section of the container side panel using an engineering hammer.
    3. Charge the line to flood the interior.
  • Deploying the HydroPen System:
    1. Attach the HydroPen unit to the rigging pole and align it with the targeted container's side wall or door.
    2. Lift and secure the unit using the container's structural lips/castings.
    3. Open the fire main hydrants. The water pressure activates the internal drill bit, breaching the container wall within seconds.
    4. The unit locks in place, shifting automatically from drilling mode to a highly atomized extinguishing spray.
  • Deploying Mobile Water Monitors:
    1. Position the monitor on the deck plating or lashing bridge walkways facing the affected bay.
    2. Secure the foundation using integrated twistlocks or ratchet straps to withstand the nozzle reaction force.
    3. Connect dual supply lines from separate fire main hydrants to ensure adequate volumetric flow.
    4. Adjust elevation and oscillation to cover the upper tiers, allowing crew to retreat to a safe distance.

Any Distinguishing Trap Point

  • The Lashing Bridge Clearance Trap: The manual water lance requires significant swinging room and physical leverage. In narrow lashing bridges or high-wind environments, it is incredibly difficult and dangerous to use effectively.
  • Pressure Drops: Both the HydroPen and mobile water monitors are highly sensitive to fire main pressure drops. If the emergency fire pump is running alone or if too many hoses are cracked simultaneously, the HydroPen may fail to drill, and the monitors will short-fall, losing their vertical reach on high-cube stacks.

Running a mobile water monitor simultaneously with multiple fire hoses can deplete the fire main pressure below the 4.0 bar minimum required at the highest hydrant. Always ensure the main fire pumps are running in parallel or the emergency fire pump is fully isolated to supply designated lines if structural boundaries are breached.

Key Numbers / Regs

  • SOLAS Reg II-2/10.7.3: Mandates container firefighting equipment for ships carrying containers on deck.
  • 60 m³/h (1,000 Liters/min): Minimum required discharge capacity for each mobile water monitor under SOLAS.
  • 4.0 Bar: Minimum operational pressure required at the furthest deck hydrant on a large container vessel during multi-line operations.
