MARPOL Annex V latest amendments?
MARPOLSURVEY
15-Second AnswerRecent MARPOL Annex V amendments introduce stricter controls on garbage management, requiring a Garbage Management Plan for ships $\ge$ 100 GT and a Garbage Record Book for ships $\ge$ 400 GT. It establishes a total ban on plastic discharges anywhere at sea and designates strict zero-discharge Special Areas.
60-Second AnswerMARPOL Annex V has been progressively tightened to eliminate marine plastic pollution and enhance garbage tracking. Under current regulations—reinforced by resolutions like MEPC.277(70) and subsequent revisions—plastics are completely banned from sea disposal globally. The compliance framework lowers operational thresholds, mandating a Garbage Management Plan for ships $\ge$ 100 GT and a Garbage Record Book (Parts I and II) for all ships $\ge$ 400 GT and passenger vessels. In strict Special Areas—such as the Baltic, North, Red, and Mediterranean Seas—discharges of all garbage, including cargo residues and food waste, are subject to zero-discharge limits or strict distance parameters.

Core Regulatory Shifts and Classifications

The focus of modern MARPOL Annex V amendments is to enforce rigorous record-keeping and eliminate synthetic discharges:

  • Structural Categorization: Garbage is classified into strict categories (Categories A through K), splitting operational plastics, food wastes, domestic wastes, cooking oil, incinerator ashes, and cargo residues.
  • Absolute Plastic Prohibition: Plastics of any form—including synthetic ropes, fishing nets, and plastic garbage bags—are subject to a permanent, global ban and can never be discharged into the sea under any circumstances.
  • E-Garbage Record Book (E-GRB): Electronic logging of garbage operations is fully harmonized, allowing entries to be managed via certified digital software frameworks with secure electronic signatures.

Tonnage Thresholds & Mandatory Shipboard Documents

Compliance requirements are strictly tied to gross tonnage (GT) criteria:

Document / Equipment RequirementTonnage Threshold (GT)Operational Scope
Garbage Placards\ge 12\text{ m} (Length Overall)Must display clear discharge layout rules to crew and passengers.
Garbage Management Plan (GMP)\ge 100\text{ GT} and passenger ships Written procedures for minimizing, collecting, storing, processing, and discharging garbage.
Garbage Record Book (GRB)\ge 400\text{ GT} and passenger ships Split into Part I (All crews/spaces) and Part II (Cargo residues for bulk carriers).

Special Area Regulations

Within designated MARPOL Annex V Special Areas (including the Baltic Sea, North Sea, Mediterranean Sea, Red Sea, Black Sea, Gulf Area, and Antarctic Area), discharge rules are extremely strict:

  • General Garbage: Zero discharge permitted for all domestic, operational, and structural wastes.
  • Food Waste: Can only be discharged if comminuted/ground ( \le 25\text{ mm} screen) and the vessel is not less than 12 nautical miles from the nearest land or ice shelf.
  • Cargo Residues: Discharge of cargo hold washing water containing residues is prohibited unless the substances are verified as non-Harmful to the Marine Environment (HME) and no port reception facilities are available at both departure and destination ports.

Trap Questions

MARPOL Annex V makes no exception for "bio-degradable" or "eco-friendly" plastics. All synthetic materials are strictly banned from sea disposal everywhere on Earth.

Oil tanker amendment package?
MARPOLSOLASSTCWMLC
15-Second AnswerRecent oil tanker amendments enforce stricter structural and survey compliance, including SOLAS Chapter II-2 (MSC.520(106)) for flashpoint safety and MARPOL Annex VI for EEXI/CII carbon controls. They tie directly into STCW for specialized tanker endorsements and MLC 2006 for mandatory financial security certificates.
60-Second AnswerModern oil tanker operations are governed by a complex matrix of updated international instruments. Under SOLAS (MSC.520(106)), strict pre-bunkering flashpoint declarations are mandatory to mitigate ignition risks. Environmental controls are stepped up under MARPOL Annex VI, enforcing strict EEXI and Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) operational ratings for tankers. On the human element side, STCW mandates specialized, tiered tanker training endorsements for officers, while the MLC 2006 financial security amendments require valid certificates covering shipowner liability for repatriation and abandonment across all trading tankers.

Multi-Convention Regulatory Matrix for Tankers

Operating an oil tanker demands compliance across environmental, structural, and human element frameworks:

1. MARPOL Environmental & Carbon Controls

  • MARPOL Annex I & ESP Code: Tankers are subject to the Enhanced Survey Programme (ESP Code), requiring intensive, close-up structural thickness measurements of tank boundaries, transverse bulkheads, and deck plating to monitor accelerated localized corrosion.
  • MARPOL Annex VI (EEXI & CII): Effective for tankers, vessels must calculate their attained Energy Efficiency Existing Ship Index (EEXI) and maintain an active Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) operational profile. Tankers must log their annual fuel consumption to receive an operational carbon rating from A to E, with a Corrective Action Plan required for poorly performing vessels.

2. SOLAS Fire and Safety Amendments

  • Flashpoint Enforcement (Resolution MSC.520(106)): Enforces pre-bunkering supplier safety declarations and explicit flashpoint records on the Bunker Delivery Note (BDN) to eliminate volatile fuel vapors near product storage areas.
  • Fixed Gas Detection: Mandates fixed hydrocarbon gas detection systems in the pump rooms and double-hull ballast spaces of oil tankers to provide early detection of cargo boundary leakage.

3. STCW Training Framework

  • Tiered Endorsements: Mandates a two-tiered certification structure for cargo handling teams:
  1. Basic Training for Oil and Chemical Tanker Cargo Operations (Section A-V/1-1-1).
  1. Advanced Training for Oil Tanker Cargo Operations (Section A-V/1-1-2) for Masters, Chief Engineers, Mates, and Second Engineers who have direct responsibility for loading, discharging, and care of cargo in transit.

4. MLC 2006 Seafarer Protections

  • Financial Security Amendments: Tankers must carry valid insurance or financial security certificates under Title 2 and Title 4 of the MLC. This ensures that if a company suffers financial distress or abandons a vessel, seafarers are guaranteed up to 4 months of outstanding wages, repatriation costs, and essential provisions.

Hong Kong Convention — IHM three parts and CE role?
MARPOLSURVEYIACS
15-Second AnswerThe Hong Kong Convention (HKC) ensures that ships are recycled safely without endangering human health or the environment. Flag states must mandate, verify, and certify an Inventory of Hazardous Materials (IHM) for all vessels $\ge$ 500 GT, tracking onboard hazardous materials from construction to demolition.
60-Second AnswerThe Hong Kong International Convention for the Safe and Environmentally Sound Recycling of Ships entered into force on 26 June 2025. Its primary purpose is to ensure that ship recycling does not pose unnecessary risks to human health or the environment. Under this framework, Flag State duties include mandating and certifying an Inventory of Hazardous Materials (IHM) for all ships $\ge$ 500 GT, issuing a Statement of Compliance, and verifying the Ship Recycling Plan before issuing a Ready for Recycling certificate. The IHM is split into three parts: Part I tracks materials in the ship's structure and equipment, Part II logs operational waste, and Part III lists stores and consumables compiled immediately prior to recycling.

Core Purpose of the Convention

The Hong Kong Convention (HKC) 2009 aims to facilitate safe and environmentally sound ship recycling practices globally. It addresses concerns around occupational safety at recycling yards, the management of hazardous materials onboard, and the prevention of marine pollution during dismantling.

Flag State Duties and Enforcement

Flag States carry out regulatory oversight through a structured certification process:

  • IHM Verification: Ensure every vessel flying its flag maintains an accurate, updated Inventory of Hazardous Materials and issue an International Certificate on Inventory of Hazardous Materials (or Statement of Compliance).
  • Recycling Authorization: Prior to recycling, the Flag State must review the yard-specific Ship Recycling Plan and issue a Ready for Recycling (RFR) Certificate.
  • Facility Control: Ensure that ships flying its flag are recycled exclusively at authorized Ship Recycling Facilities (SRFs) that maintain strict environmental and safety controls.

Structure of the Inventory of Hazardous Materials (IHM)

The IHM is a living document that stays with the vessel throughout its life cycle, identifying the location and approximate quantities of hazardous substances (such as asbestos, PCBs, ozone-depleting substances, and heavy metals):

IHM PartContent & ScopeResponsibility & Timing
Part IHazardous materials utilized within the ship’s structure, machinery, and equipment (e.g., insulation, paints). Compiled at delivery by the shipyard; updated by the Chief Engineer during operations when equipment is modified or replaced.
Part IIOperationally generated wastes present onboard. Compiled by the ship's crew immediately prior to the vessel being taken out of service for recycling.
Part IIIStores and consumables present onboard. Logged concurrently with Part II at the end of the vessel's operational life.

CE Relevance, Traps, Failures & Exam Strategy
Trap Questions

Examiner Rajappan asks: "Can you fill out Part II and Part III of the IHM as part of your normal monthly routine?" Answer: No, sir. Part I is the only operational section maintained during the ship’s service life. Part II (operational waste) and Part III (stores/consumables) are strictly completed at the end of the ship's life cycle when the vessel is preparing to go to the authorized recycling facility.

Common CE Failures

Candidates frequently forget that the Hong Kong Convention is now fully in force (as of 26 June 2025) and mistakenly refer to it as an unratified or voluntary text.

GESAMP — role and MARPOL Annex II link?
MARPOLSURVEY
15-Second AnswerGESAMP is a joint UN advisory body of independent scientific experts that evaluates marine pollution hazards. It provides the scientific assessments used by the IMO to classify hazardous chemicals under MARPOL Annex II and evaluate ballast water treatment systems.
60-Second AnswerGESAMP stands for the Joint Group of Experts on the Scientific Aspects of Marine Environmental Protection. Founded in 1969, it is an independent UN advisory body sponsored by multiple organizations, including the IMO, FAO, and UNEP. GESAMP does not pass laws itself; instead, it provides the technical and scientific foundation for regulatory decisions made by the MEPC. Its core maritime work includes establishing the GESAMP Hazard Profiles to classify noxious liquid substances into Categories X, Y, or Z under MARPOL Annex II, evaluating anti-fouling system impacts, and auditing active substances in ballast water treatment technologies.

Definition and Institutional Role

GESAMP is a multidisciplinary UN advisory body composed of independent scientists who provide specialized expertise on marine environmental health. It serves as a scientific resource for UN sponsoring agencies, ensuring that international maritime pollution legislation is backed by peer-reviewed research.

Primary Regulatory Work in the Maritime Industry

GESAMP's scientific evaluations directly influence the structure of several key IMO environmental conventions:

  1. MARPOL Annex II (Noxious Liquid Substances in Bulk): The GESAMP/EHS Working Group maintains a comprehensive composite list evaluating the environmental, toxicological, and safety hazards of bulk chemical cargoes. These scientific "GESAMP Hazard Profiles" are used to categorize substances into Categories X, Y, or Z, dictating their permissible discharge controls and stripping requirements.
  1. Ballast Water Management (BWM) Convention: The GESAMP-Ballast Water Working Group reviews and evaluates the safety and environmental impacts of ballast water treatment systems that utilize "Active Substances" (such as ozone, chlorine, or peracetic acid), ensuring they do not harm the local marine environment when discharged.
  1. Anti-Forming Systems (AFS Convention): Evaluates the ecotoxicological profiles of biocides used in hull coatings to assist the IMO in banning harmful compounds, similar to the historical ban on tributyltin (TBT).
  2. Marine Litter and Plastics (MARPOL Annex V): Conducts global studies on microplastics and sea-based sources of litter to provide the data that supports tighter garbage discharge standards.

Trap Questions

It does not possess regulatory or enforcement authority. It provides the scientific hazard profiles and technical data that the IMO's MEPC uses to draft and enforce mandatory MARPOL regulations.

Common CE Failures

Candidates frequently do not know the full name of GESAMP or confuse it with a specific MARPOL annex, failing to identify it as a joint UN scientific body.

Environmentally Sound Management (ESM) in shipping?
MARPOLISM
15-Second AnswerEnvironmentally Sound Management (ESM) means taking all practicable shipboard steps to minimize, manage, and dispose of hazardous waste safely to protect human health and the environment. It integrates compliance across the Basel Convention, Hong Kong Convention, and all MARPOL Annexes under a single operational standard.
60-Second AnswerEnvironmentally Sound Management, or ESM, is a structural concept originally derived from Article 2(8) of the Basel Convention. In the maritime regulatory framework, it represents a commitment to managing all shipboard generated wastes, hazardous materials, and operational systems in a manner that protects human health and prevents marine pollution. For a Chief Engineer, ESM is implemented practically through strict adherence to the Hong Kong Convention for certified ship recycling, tracking structural and operational hazardous items via the IHM, and managing operational discharges—including ODS recovery under MARPOL Annex VI, sewage treatment under Annex IV, and garbage segregation under Annex V.

Regulatory Definition and Origin

  • Basel Convention Foundation: The term "Environmentally Sound Management" (ESM) originates from Article 2(8) of the Basel Convention on the Control of Transboundary Movements of Hazardous Wastes and their Disposal.
  • Core Definition: It means taking all practicable steps to ensure that hazardous wastes or other wastes are managed in a manner which will protect human health and the environment against the adverse effects which may result from such wastes.
  • Application to International Shipping: In a maritime context, ESM serves as an overarching compliance standard, linking global shore-side waste management treaties with shipboard operational codes and conventions.

Practical Shipboard Implementation by the Chief Engineer

Executing ESM onboard requires the engineering team to manage multiple overlapping regulatory areas systematically:

  1. Ship Recycling and the Living IHM: Ensuring the ship is recycled only at authorized, ESM-certified facilities in compliance with the Hong Kong Convention. The Chief Engineer must continuously maintain and update the Inventory of Hazardous Materials (IHM) Part I during operations to log structural changes.
  1. Ozone-Depleting Substances (ODS) Controls: Strict adherence to MARPOL Annex VI Regulation 12, ensuring that any ODS gases (such as Halon or HCFCs) are captured and recovered during maintenance using dedicated reclamation units, rather than being vented to the atmosphere.
  1. Sewage and Graywater Management: Maintaining the type-approved sewage treatment plant or comminuting system in strict accordance with MARPOL Annex IV, preventing illegal discharges in restricted or coastal waters.
  1. Rigid Garbage Segregation: Executing the ship's Garbage Management Plan under MARPOL Annex V, including the sorting of domestic and operational wastes, logging ash from incinerators, and securing port reception facility receipts.
  1. Bilge and Sludge Accountability: Operating the Oil Water Separator (OWS) and logging all sludge generation and oil transfers in the Oil Record Book (ORB) Part I, ensuring no hazardous oily waste bypasses the certified processing line.

CE Relevance, Traps, Failures & Exam Strategy
Trap Questions

Examiner Nair asks: "If a local port authority allows you to dispose of plastic waste into their shore skips without giving you a formal receipt, does this meet the criteria for Environmentally Sound Management?" Answer: No, sir. Under ESM and MARPOL Annex V, a closed loop of accountability must be maintained. Disposing of waste without obtaining a verified Port Reception Facility (PRF) receipt breaks the tracking chain, making it impossible to prove that the hazardous material was transferred to a facility equipped to process it safely.

Common CE Failures

Candidates often limit their description of ESM to basic MARPOL bilge water separation, completely missing its conceptual link to the Basel Convention and the Hong Kong Convention's lifecycle approach to structural hazardous waste management.

In the maritime sector, these rules ensure that highly toxic residues from cargo tanks and older hull insulation are accounted for and disposed of safely.
