15-Second AnswerThe latest SOLAS Chapter II-2 amendments (primarily MSC.520(106) and ⚠CORRECTED: MSC.532(107), entering into force on 1 January 2026) enforce strict fuel oil flashpoint compliance—requiring a supplier declaration and BDN flashpoint notation prior to bunkering—and establish a global ban on firefighting foams containing PFOS.
60-Second AnswerThe recent amendments to SOLAS Chapter II-2 represent a major regulatory shift in operational fuel safety and environmental compliance. Under Resolution MSC.520(106), effective 1 January 2026, strict controls are placed on fuel oil supply to prevent flashpoints below 60°C. Suppliers must now provide a signed declaration of flashpoint conformity prior to bunkering, and the Bunker Delivery Note (BDN) must explicitly state the flashpoint or confirm it is at or above 70°C. Concurrently, Resolution ⚠CORRECTED: MSC.532(107) enforces a complete ban on the use and carriage of firefighting foams containing Perfluorooctane Sulfonic Acid (PFOS), mandating their complete removal and safe shore disposal by the first safety equipment survey on or after 1 January 2026.
Core Technical Changes & Regulatory Framework

The recent updates to SOLAS Chapter II-2 introduce mandatory safety layers targeting fuel supply integrity and toxic firefighting agents:

1. Oil Fuel Safety Standards (Resolution MSC.520(106))

To combat the persistent safety risks of low-flashpoint fuel oils being supplied to commercial vessels, the amendments introduce binding pre-bunkering and documentation loops:

  • Pre-Bunkering Declaration: Prior to starting any bunkering operation, the fuel supplier's representative must deliver a signed and certified declaration to the ship confirming that the fuel oil complies with the mandatory minimum flashpoint requirement (not less than 60°C) and specifying the test method utilized.
  • Bunker Delivery Note (BDN) Notation: The BDN must include either the actual measured flashpoint value or a formal statement certifying that the flashpoint has been measured at or above 70°C.
  • Administrative Reporting Track: Governments are required to report all "confirmed cases" where suppliers deliver fuel with a flashpoint below 60°C to the IMO GISIS database, triggering formal enforcement actions against the bunker supplier.
2. Prohibition of PFOS-Based Firefighting Media (Resolution ⚠CORRECTED: MSC.532(107))

Recognizing the persistent environmental and toxic health risks of fluorinated compounds, the amendment completely outlaws foam concentrates containing Perfluorooctane Sulfonic Acid (PFOS):

  • New Ships: Ships delivered on or after 1 January 2026 are prohibited from using or storing any firefighting media containing PFOS concentrations above 10 mg/kg (0.001% by weight).
  • Existing Ships: Vessels constructed before 1 January 2026 must completely remove and flush out any PFOS-containing foam media no later than the first scheduled Safety Equipment (SEQ) Survey following 1 January 2026.
  • Disposal Track: All contaminated foam media removed from the vessel cannot be discharged at sea and must be delivered exclusively to approved shore-based reception facilities, with the operation logged in the Official Log Book (OLB).

Generic terms like "compliant" or "safe" are non-conformities.
  • Common CE Failures: Candidates often state that the PFOS ban applies only to new buildings, completely missing the retroactive mandate requiring existing ships to replace their foam charges by the first scheduled safety equipment survey after 1 January 2026.

FIRESOLASISM
15-Second AnswerFighting an undeclared Dangerous Goods (DG) container fire requires assuming a worst-case scenario. Treat it as an explosive or toxic substance, utilize maximum boundary cooling from a safe distance, deploy protective gear, and reference the IMDG Emergency Schedule (EmS) guidelines to guide response tactics.
60-Second AnswerWhen an undeclared dangerous goods cargo fire breaks out, the absence of a verified UN number means we cannot immediately consult the standard IMDG documentation. My approach as Chief Engineer is to assume a worst-case scenario—treating the space as containing Class 1 explosives or toxic chemicals. I will direct the fire party to establish defensive boundaries using full structural firefighting sets with breathing apparatus, avoid directing water directly inside the unknown container to prevent a violent chemical reaction, and initiate massive boundary cooling on adjacent bays. Simultaneously, I will have the cargo manifest audited for adjacent container details and coordinate with the Master to contact chemical emergency centers like CHEMTREC.
Operational and Tactical Chief Engineer Response

Undeclared dangerous goods represent a severe shipboard hazard because the chemical properties, reactivity, and toxic products of the burning cargo are completely unknown. The response protocol must be systematic and defensive:

1. Immediate Safety and Isolation Phase
  • General Alarm Activation: Sound the general alarm, muster the crew to emergency stations, and ensure the vessel is maneuvered to position the smoke clear of crew muster points.
  • Ventilation Isolation: Immediately trip all cargo hold mechanical ventilation fans and secure fire dampers to starve the space of oxygen and prevent toxic gas migration.
  • Personnel Protection: Mandate that all firefighting team members wear full protective clothing and positive-pressure Self-Contained Breathing Apparatus (SCBA) to guard against unknown toxic vapors.
2. Firefighting Tactics (Defensive vs. Aggressive)
  • Worst-Case Assumption: Treat the container as containing Class 1 explosives or highly reactive water-activated chemicals.
  • Massive Boundary Cooling: Deploy fixed or portable water monitors to cool the surrounding container tiers and deck structures. Do not direct a direct water stream into the unknown container door until its compatibility is evaluated.
  • No Door Penetration: Do not open the container doors or punch a borehole for a water mist lance blindly, as introducing oxygen can trigger a backdraft or explosion.
3. Information Gathering and Logistics
  • Stowage Position Analysis: Cross-check the bay, row, and tier position on the cargo stowage plan. Review the documentation of adjacent containers; if nearby containers hold compatible or reactive goods, it can provide clues about the cargo.
  • External Assistance: Direct the bridge to contact 24-hour chemical emergency networks such as CHEMTREC (+1-703-527-3887) or regional maritime rescue coordination centers (MRCC) to leverage ashore expert chemical advice.

Deep-Dive Analysis
  • Trap Questions: Examiner Nair asks: "The fire team wants to flood the hold immediately with CO2. Do you authorize this for an unknown cargo fire?" Answer: No, not blindly. If the undeclared cargo contains self-sustaining oxidizing agents (such as certain Class 5.1 chemicals) or reactive metals (Class 4.3), CO2 can be completely ineffective or can break down chemically, worsening the reaction. Defensive boundary cooling and identifying the cargo must take priority.
  • Common CE Failures: Candidates frequently give a textbook answer for a declared cargo fire, stating they would look up the UN number in the IMDG index. They fail to address the core problem: the cargo is undeclared, meaning there is no UN number available.
  • Numbers / Regs:
  • IMDG Code Part 3: Governs dangerous goods lists, special provisions, and exceptions.
  • SOLAS Regulation II-2/10: Prescribes fixed and portable firefighting arrangements for cargo spaces.
  • Casualty Link: The catastrophic explosion aboard the MV Flaminia was intensified by undeclared or improperly documented chemical containers. The initial firefighting attempts lacked specific chemical data, leading to a massive explosion that breached the cargo holds and resulted in fatalities.

Simon focuses on the dynamic stability changes if a cargo hold accumulates tons of free-surface water from continuous boundary cooling.

We deploy portable water monitors configured to operate unmanned on deck, allowing the crew to maintain continuous boundary cooling from a safe distance upwind, protecting them from unexpected flashovers or toxic gas releases.

15-Second AnswerFor a container fire with unknown cargo, sound the general alarm, muster the crew, and isolate cargo hold ventilation. Deploy unmanned portable water monitors from an upwind position for heavy boundary cooling, and do not open the container doors or inject water blindly to avoid triggering a backdraft or violent chemical reaction.
60-Second AnswerManaging a container fire with unknown cargo demands a systematic, risk-aware response. First, I will sound the general alarm, muster the crew to fire stations, and immediately isolate the mechanical ventilation and secure the fire dampers of the affected zone. The fire team must don full structural firefighting outfits with positive-pressure breathing apparatus. For an on-deck fire, we will deploy portable water monitors from an upwind position to carry out boundary cooling on adjacent tiers. Crucially, we must not open the container doors or inject water blindly, as this poses an explosion or backdraft risk. We will then utilize the vessel's cargo manifest and stowage plan to cross-reference adjacent containers and contact MRCC for external chemical advice.
Systematic Firefighting Approach

When the cargo within a burning container is completely unidentified (due to missing, unreadable, or lost documentation), the Chief Engineer must coordinate with the Master to execute a highly disciplined, defensive tactical plan:

1. Command, Control, and Isolation
  • Alert System: Sound the general emergency alarm and muster all crew members to their designated fire stations.
  • Vessel Maneuvering: Direct the bridge to alter course if necessary to keep the engine room casing, accommodation spaces, and the responding fire party upwind of the toxic smoke plume.
  • Ventilation Control: Instantly trip all mechanical ventilation fans servicing the cargo holds and completely secure the mechanical fire dampers to prevent oxygen replenishment and contain toxic fumes.
2. On-Deck Tactical Execution
  • Visual Assessment: Evaluate the container's position (bay, row, tier) and note visible indicators such as smoke color, acrid or chemical odors, or structural warping of the container walls.
  • Unmanned Boundary Cooling: Rig and deploy high-capacity portable water monitors on deck to provide continuous cooling to adjacent container bays and tiers. The fire party must retreat to a safe distance once monitors are secured in place to minimize exposure to unexpected structural failure or explosion.
  • Door Containment: Do NOT attempt to crack open the container doors. Introducing atmospheric oxygen into a starved, smoldering container can trigger a catastrophic backdraft or thermal explosion.
  • Hold Flooding Limitations: If the container is located inside a cargo hold, realize that fixed CO_2 or inert gas flooding is generally ineffective for fires sealed deep within individual containers; focus instead on cooling the surrounding steel structure.
3. Data Triangulation and External Liaison
  • Stowage Analysis: Map the container's location against the ship's cargo manifest. Even if the target container's details are missing, analyzing the cargo profiles of adjacent containers can help identify nearby hazards (such as aerosols, oxidizers, or flammables).
  • Shore-Side Assistance: Coordinate with the Master to contact the Maritime Rescue Coordination Centre (MRCC) and corporate technical channels to request immediate hazardous chemical advice based on the manifest location.
  • Abandonment Preparedness: If the container exhibits signs of rapid, uncontrolled exothermic reactions or explosive cracking, shift the crew entirely to a defensive boundary cooling posture and prepare the lifeboats.

Extra Sub-Block: Trap Warning

CRITICAL EXAMINER TRAP: Nair will ask: "If you see smoke coming out of an unknown container, shouldn't you immediately use an axe to smash a hole in the side panel and stick a standard fire hose nozzle inside?" Answer: Absolutely not. Smashing a hole introduces a sudden rush of oxygen, which can cause a violent backdraft. Furthermore, if the unknown cargo consists of water-reactive chemicals (such as Class 4.3 dangerous goods), injecting high-velocity water can cause an explosion. A specialized water mist lance must only be used through a drilled borehole once a reasonable assessment is complete.

CE Relevance

For engineering officers, a container fire means managing the ship's fire main pressure. The Second Engineer must immediately start the main and emergency fire pumps in parallel to ensure maximum hydrostatic pressure is maintained at the deck manifolds while portable water monitors and boundary hoses are running simultaneously.

Deep-Dive Analysis
  • Trap Questions: Examiner Nair asks: "You are running three fire hoses and two portable monitors on deck. The fire pump pressure drops. What are your actions?" Answer: I will immediately start the emergency fire pump to boost fire main pressure, check for open overboard scour valves or internal engine room leaks, and isolate non-essential sections of the fire main deck line to prioritize the lines feeding the boundary cooling operations.
  • Common CE Failures: Candidates often state they would simply "open the container and extinguish the fire with water," demonstrating a lack of understanding of container backdrafts and chemical water-reactivity hazards.

FIRESOLAS
15-Second AnswerA water mist lance is a specialized tool that penetrates a container's wall to discharge a cooling mist inside. Portable water monitors are deck-mounted devices that provide high-capacity boundary cooling. Both are mandated by SOLAS Regulation II-2/10.7.3.1 for container ships to manage deck cargo fires.
60-Second AnswerFormally mandated under SOLAS Chapter II-2, Regulation 10.7.3.1, container ships must carry at least one water mist lance and a minimum of two portable water monitors. The water mist lance consists of a protective nozzle tube inserted through a small borehole drilled by the crew into the container casing, allowing internal fire suppression without introducing excessive oxygen. The portable water monitors are high-capacity units designed for mounting on deck. They must feature a minimum flow rate of 400 L/min per monitor (totaling 800 L/min combined) and be capable of reaching any section of the cargo deck to provide boundary cooling.
Engineering Construction and Functional Requirements
1. Water Mist Lance
  • Design and Operation: Consists of a long, heavy-duty stainless steel piercing tube or lance fitted with a specialized misting nozzle. The fire team uses a portable drill to create a small borehole through the container door or side panel, inserts the lance, and connects it to the fire main.
  • Extinguishing Principle: Discharges a high-pressure fine water mist directly inside the sealed container. This design suppresses Class A fires (wood, paper, textiles) by cooling and displacing oxygen with water vapor, while preventing a backdraft since the container doors remain closed.
  • Limitations: Not suitable for containers carrying water-reactive chemicals, calcium hypochlorite, or explosive compounds where adding water can worsen the reaction.

2. Portable Water Monitor
  • Design and Operation: High-capacity, stable, deck-mounted water monitors equipped with quick-coupling attachments for the fire main. They are designed to operate unmanned once positioned and secured on deck.
  • Flow Rate and Performance Requirements: Under SOLAS II-2/10.7.3.1, each monitor must discharge at least 400 L/min (24 m³/h) at the design pressure. The ship must carry at least two monitors, providing a combined flow of 800 L/min.
  • Tactical Reach: Must be capable of being carried by the crew, rigged quickly on any part of the open cargo deck, and have a jet reach capable of cooling the highest tiers of containers carried onboard.

Deep-Dive Analysis
  • Trap Questions: Examiner Nair asks: "Where exactly in the FSS Code do you find the detailed layout for the water mist lance?" Answer: The technical specifications for the water mist lance and portable monitors are outlined within SOLAS Chapter II-2, Regulation 10.7.3 and associated MSC amendments (such as MSC.520(106)), rather than as a standalone chapter in the old FSS Code.
  • Common CE Failures: Candidates frequently state that any standard fire hose nozzle can serve as a water monitor, failing to recognize that SOLAS mandates dedicated, self-supporting portable monitor units with specific flow controls and structural stability brackets.
  • Numbers / Regs:
  • SOLAS Reg II-2/10.7.3.1: Establishes carriage rules for ships designed to carry containers on or above the weather deck.
  • Resolution MSC.496(105) & MSC.520(106): Updates fire protection requirements for modern container vessels.
  • Casualty Link: Incidents of large-scale container fires on deck showed that standard hand-held fire hoses were insufficient to reach upper container tiers. This prompted the IMO to introduce mandatory requirements for high-capacity portable monitors to allow continuous boundary cooling without keeping crew members in high-risk zones.
  • Examiner Chain: Nair arrow Simon arrow Senthil. Nair tests the specific flow rates, drilling protocols, and construction requirements. Simon asks about the impact on the vessel's bilge system if water from the lance drains into the cargo holds. Senthil checks the maintenance records for the portable drills and safety equipment logs.
  • On My Vessel (Maersk A/S Container Fleet): On our Maersk container vessels, our water mist lances and portable water monitors are stowed in dedicated, marked safety lockers near the cargo decks. The portable air-driven or battery drills used for creating the container boreholes are checked monthly as part of our scheduled maintenance program to ensure they are fully charged and ready for emergency deployment.
