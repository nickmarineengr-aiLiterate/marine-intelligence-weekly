1
Is the Chief Engineer a leader? Elaborate.
ISM

15-Second Answer

Yes — the CE is a leader by function under ISM/STCW, not just by rank: technical authority, safety-culture ownership, mentoring, and resource management all fall under leadership.

60-Second Answer

The Chief Engineer is the designated head of department under the ISM Code's responsibility-and-authority structure, and STCW Table A-III/2 formally examines leadership and managerial skills at management level since the Manila Amendments of 2010. CE leadership has four dimensions: technical (final authority on machinery decisions and PMS priorities), safety (setting the tone for near-miss reporting and PTW discipline), people (mentoring juniors, managing fatigue and multicultural crew dynamics), and resource/administrative (budgets, dry-dock planning, superintendent liaison). The key distinction from pure management is that leadership is about people and influence — getting the department to follow standards even unsupervised — while management is about processes, schedules and budgets.

Yes — by function, not just rank

The Chief Engineer is the designated head of the engine department under the ISM Code's Master/Company defined responsibility and authority structure (ISM Code, Element 5/6). Leadership here is functional: technical authority, safety culture ownership, and team development — not just hierarchical seniority.

Dimensions of CE leadership

  • Technical leadership: final authority on machinery decisions, PMS compliance, and defect prioritisation.
  • Safety leadership: sets the tone for near-miss reporting, PTW discipline, and STOP-work authority within the engine team — modelling the behaviour ISM requires of all levels.
  • People leadership: mentoring juniors (2/E, 3/E, cadets), managing watchkeeper fatigue/STCW rest hours, conflict resolution in a multicultural crew.
  • Resource/administrative leadership: budget for spares, planning dry-dock work lists, liaising with superintendent — a management function under the Human Element (STCW Manila Amendments leadership/managerial competence tables).

Distinguishing leadership from management

Management is about processes (PMS, budgets, schedules); leadership is about people and influence — getting the department to follow standards even when unsupervised. STCW Table A-III/2 explicitly examines both.

Deep-Dive: CE Relevance

Daily leadership shows up in toolbox talks before PTW jobs, delegating jobs matched to competence, and being visible during high-risk evolutions (bunkering, entry into enclosed space).

Deep-Dive: Trap Questions

“What's the difference between a leader and a manager?” follow-up — have a crisp two-line distinction ready (people/influence vs. process/resources).

Deep-Dive: Common CE Failures

Answering only in terms of authority ('I give orders') without covering the human/mentoring dimension examiners are probing for.

Deep-Dive: Numbers to Memorise

STCW Manila Amendments (2010) formally added leadership and teamwork as assessable competences at management level — cite the year.

Deep-Dive: Casualty Link

Poor engine-room leadership/communication culture is repeatedly cited as a contributing factor in MAIB/investigative reports on engine room fires and flooding incidents (crew reluctant to report defects upward).

Deep-Dive: On My Vessel (Maersk A/S, container)

On a Maersk container ship with a multinational crew, CE leadership includes bridging language/cultural gaps during critical manoeuvring stand-by and ensuring juniors feel safe raising defects.

2
You join a ship as Chief Engineer and the main engine type is unfamiliar to you. What actions will you take?
MACHINERYISM

15-Second Answer

Structured familiarisation first: extended handover, review manuals/PMS history, complete the SMS familiarisation checklist, shadow the 2/E, and escalate to the Superintendent if the gap is significant — never fake confidence on sign-off.

60-Second Answer

Joining with an unfamiliar main engine type demands a structured, documented approach rather than learning on the fly. I'd start with an extended handover from the outgoing CE covering running hours, open defects, and recent overhaul history, then review the manufacturer's manual, PMS job history and trend data to understand this engine's normal baseline. Most SMS mandates a signed familiarisation checklist for new equipment types under ISM 6.3–6.5 and STCW Regulation I/14 — I'd complete that properly, shadow the 2/E and senior ratings during early watches and manoeuvring stand-bys, and request Superintendent or OEM support if the unfamiliarity gap is significant, such as a first exposure to a new electronic control system. I would not sign off on independent readiness decisions until genuinely confident, and I'd be professionally honest with the Master about any residual gap.

Immediate priority — do not operate blind

Safety of navigation/propulsion cannot depend on the CE's personal unfamiliarity; the priority is structured self-briefing before assuming independent operational control.

Sequenced actions

  1. Handover: extended, structured handover with outgoing CE — running hours, open defects, recent overhauls, class/PSC open items, critical spares status.
  2. Documentation review: manufacturer's instruction manual, PMS job history, engine log abstracts, recent trend data (exhaust temps, scavenge pressure, cylinder pressures) to understand this specific engine's baseline "normal".
  3. Familiarisation per ISM/company SMS: most SMS mandates a documented familiarisation checklist for a new engine type — complete and sign it; do not skip even under schedule pressure.
  4. Shadow the 2/E and senior ratings: who already know the plant, for the first watches/manoeuvring stand-bys.
  5. Escalate to Superintendent if needed: request manufacturer service engineer support or an onboard familiarisation period if the gap is significant (e.g., first exposure to a new engine family/electronic control system).
  6. Do not delegate final sign-off decisions (e.g., readiness for sea) until genuinely confident — professional honesty with Master/Company over any residual gap.

Deep-Dive: CE Relevance

CE must document familiarisation formally (signed checklist retained onboard) — this is a routine PSC/Vetting inspection item (e.g., SIRE/CDI).

Deep-Dive: Trap Questions

“What if you're unfamiliar and the ship must sail immediately?” — answer: rely on 2/E's plant knowledge, intensify monitoring, request superintendent/OEM support en route, and formally flag the gap — never conceal it.

Deep-Dive: Common CE Failures

Claiming 'I would just study on my own' without referencing the company's formal familiarisation procedure — misses the ISM Code compliance angle examiners want.

Deep-Dive: Numbers to Memorise

STCW A-I/14 requires documented familiarisation 'as soon as possible' — no fixed day-count in the Convention text itself, but company SMS often specifies a window (commonly within the first 24–48 hours for critical machinery) — verify against your own vessel's SMS.

Deep-Dive: Casualty Link

Several engine-room incident investigations (e.g., blackout/loss of propulsion cases) have cited inadequate familiarisation of newly joined engineers with unfamiliar automation/control systems as a contributing factor.

Deep-Dive: On My Vessel (Maersk A/S, container)

Maersk's SMS carries a structured joining familiarisation checklist per rank; for main engine electronic control systems (e.g., ME-type engines), OEM computer-based training modules are typically mandatory before independent watchkeeping sign-off.
