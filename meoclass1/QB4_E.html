MLC 2006 titles — Regulations, Standards, Guidelines; differences and similarities with RPSL; which came first?
MLC
15-Second AnswerStrictly, MLC 2006 is built as Articles, Regulations, and a Code, and the Code is split into Part A mandatory Standards and Part B non-mandatory Guidelines. The Convention is organised under five Titles, while RPSL in India is the national recruitment and placement licensing regime for crewing agencies. In practical chronology, the Indian RPS framework predates MLC’s international entry into force on 20 August 2013.
60-Second AnswerMLC 2006 is the ILO’s seafarer labour convention, and its legal architecture is: Articles at the top, Regulations stating the core obligations, and the Code giving the implementation detail through Part A mandatory Standards and Part B recommended Guidelines. The five Titles are: Title 1 Minimum requirements for seafarers to work on a ship, Title 2 Conditions of employment, Title 3 Accommodation, recreational facilities, food and catering, Title 4 Health protection, medical care, welfare and social security protection, and Title 5 Compliance and enforcement. RPSL means Recruitment and Placement Service License in India, issued by DG Shipping to agencies recruiting or placing seafarers for Indian or foreign shipowners. The similarity is that both protect seafarers from abusive or non-compliant recruitment practices, but the difference is that MLC is an international convention covering the full seafarer welfare framework, whereas RPSL is a national Indian licensing and control mechanism focused mainly on recruitment and placement services. India ratified MLC on 9 October 2015, and ships covered by MLC use the Maritime Labour Certificate together with DMLC Part I and Part II to demonstrate compliance.

Structure of MLC

In oral exams, say the structure correctly as: “MLC consists of Articles, Regulations, and the Code; Code Part A is mandatory and Code Part B is recommendatory.”

If the examiner asks about “Regulations, Standards, Guidelines,” that is shorthand for Regulation level obligation, Part A Standards, and Part B Guidelines.

The five Titles are the exam backbone and should be answered in order from Title 1 to Title 5.

Five Titles

Title 1: Minimum requirements for seafarers to work on a ship.

Title 2: Conditions of employment.

Title 3: Accommodation, recreational facilities, food and catering.

Title 4: Health protection, medical care, welfare and social security protection.

Title 5: Compliance and enforcement.

Regulations vs Standards vs Guidelines

A Regulation gives the principle or legal duty at high level.

A Standard means the mandatory detail in Code Part A.

A Guideline means the non-mandatory but recommended method in Code Part B.

A clean oral example is: Regulation 2.1 creates the obligation for seafarers’ employment agreements, Part A gives the mandatory content, and Part B gives recommended good practice.

MLC and RPSL

RPSL stands for Recruitment and Placement Service License, and DG Shipping describes it as the licence framework authorising agencies to recruit seafarers for Indian or foreign shipowners.

The Indian RPS framework is associated with the Merchant Shipping (Recruitment and Placement of Seafarers) Rules, 2005, so as a domestic regime it predates MLC’s global entry into force in 2013.

MLC covers the entire labour ecosystem onboard, but RPSL is much narrower and concentrates on how crewing agencies recruit, document, and place seafarers.

Similarities and differences

Similarity: both aim to protect seafarers from exploitation and poor recruitment practice.

Difference 1: MLC is an international ILO convention, while RPSL is an Indian national licensing system.

Difference 2: MLC applies at shipboard welfare and compliance level, while RPSL applies at crewing-agency approval and oversight level.

Difference 3: MLC compliance is evidenced onboard by an MLC Certificate and DMLC, whereas RPSL compliance is evidenced by a valid agency licence.

India link

India ratified MLC on 9 October 2015.

For ships of 500 GT and above engaged on international voyages, compliance is demonstrated through a Maritime Labour Certificate and DMLC Part I and Part II.

Extra: Trap Warning

The common trap is to say “MLC has only Regulations, Standards and Guidelines” and forget the Articles and the Code.

A second trap is to describe RPSL as if it were equivalent to the full MLC regime; it is not, because RPSL is only the Indian recruitment and placement licensing mechanism.

CE Relevance

As Chief Engineer, you are not dealing with RPSL as a crewing office matter alone, because poor recruitment documentation can become an onboard MLC deficiency during inspection.

Your practical interface is through SEA verification, hours of rest discipline, accommodation defects, food and catering complaints, and documentary readiness of the MLC Certificate with DMLC Part I and II.

In engine department terms, Title 4 and Title 5 matter heavily because welfare, medical care, occupational safety, and inspection readiness often surface first through machinery-space workload, fatigue, and maintenance planning.

Trap Questions

“What is the exact legal structure of MLC?” — “Articles, Regulations, and the Code; the Code has Part A mandatory Standards and Part B non-mandatory Guidelines.”

“Is RPSL same as MLC Regulation 1.4?” — “No; RPSL is India’s national licensing mechanism, while MLC Regulation 1.4 is the international obligation dealing with recruitment and placement.”

“Which came first?” — “RPS in Indian domestic practice predates MLC’s international entry into force of 20 August 2013.”

Common CE Failures

Candidates often mix up Regulation and Part A Standard and then cannot explain the legal hierarchy.

Many answer only Title 1 to Title 4 and forget Title 5 Compliance and enforcement, which is exactly where PSC and certification sit.

Some say “RPSL means Recruitment and Placement of Seafarers” instead of the currently used DG Shipping term “Recruitment and Placement Service License.”

Numbers / Regs

India ratified MLC on 9 October 2015.

MLC is arranged under 5 Titles.

Ships of 500 GT and above on international voyages carry the Maritime Labour Certificate and DMLC.

Maritime Labour Certificate full-term validity is up to 5 years, with an intermediate inspection between the second and third anniversary dates.

Casualty Link

MLC is not a collision or machinery casualty convention, but weak MLC compliance often sits in the background of fatigue, poor recruitment quality, excessive workload, and weak welfare controls that degrade safe engineering performance.

In oral delivery, connect this by saying that substandard labour management becomes an operational risk multiplier in engine-room emergencies.

Examiner Chain

Simon’s pattern in the source notes is to start with the structure, then move to Title 1 to 5, then ask the difference between Regulation, Standard A, and Guideline B, and then test India-specific linkage through RPSL and DMLC.

A strong flow is: “structure → titles → one example → India ratification → RPSL difference.”

On My Vessel

On a Maersk container ship, your spoken application should be that MLC is visible not in abstract law but in SEA checks, overtime control during port rotations, accommodation defects, galley complaints, medical access, and inspection readiness of MLC documents.

If crewing has engaged personnel through an Indian agency, you should understand that the recruitment side is linked to the national RPSL regime, but onboard compliance is still judged against MLC standards and DMLC procedures.

MLC insurance covers — what is covered under MLC financial security requirements?
MLC
15-Second AnswerUnder MLC financial security, two big protections are examined: repatriation security and shipowners’ liability security. The cover includes repatriation costs, essential support if the company fails, outstanding wages up to four months under the repatriation side, and contractual compensation for death or long-term disability under the liability side. The ship must carry valid evidence of this financial security, and PSC can inspect it.
60-Second AnswerMLC financial security is usually answered under two heads: Standard A2.5.2 for repatriation and Standard A4.2 for shipowners’ liability. Repatriation security covers the seafarer if the shipowner fails to repatriate him and also covers associated entitlements including outstanding wages up to four months. Shipowners’ liability security covers compensation relating to death, long-term disability, injury, and related medical consequences as required under the convention and contract. The evidence may be an insurance certificate, bank guarantee, or P&I-backed security document, and it must be available onboard and normally posted in a visible place for seafarers. The 2014 MLC amendments made these financial security provisions mandatory, and the source notes place their entry into force at 18 January 2017.

Legal heads of cover

Standard A2.5.2 deals with financial security for repatriation.

Standard A4.2 deals with shipowners’ liability, including the security backing claims such as death, disability, and medical consequences.

In oral delivery, always separate the answer into these two heads before explaining the cover items.

What repatriation cover includes

Repatriation cover responds when the shipowner fails in its duty to return the seafarer home.

The source notes also identify outstanding wages up to four months under this financial security head.

Practically, this is the anti-abandonment side of MLC financial protection.

What shipowners’ liability cover includes

The liability side covers contractual compensation for death and long-term disability.

It also extends to injury and related medical expense/liability consequences identified under the shipowners’ liability framework.

If a CBA provides higher benefits, that may sit above the minimum convention floor.

Documentary evidence onboard

The ship should carry a Certificate of Financial Security or equivalent evidence such as insurance or guarantee documentation.

The source notes specifically mention that the certificate must identify the insurer or security provider, the vessel details, and the scope of cover.

A strong exam point is that the seafarer can claim directly against the financial security provider if the company fails.

Inspection and detention risk

The notes treat these as inspectable MLC items and state that the 2014 amendment expanded the DMLC inspectable list to 16 items, adding financial security items 15 and 16.

If the certificate has lapsed or is missing, the ship is exposed to MLC deficiency action and possible detention risk.

CE action

As Chief Engineer, your role is not to “own” the insurance contract, but you must ensure the certificate is present onboard, valid, posted, and readily produced during inspection.

You should also know the escalation route if a crew member raises wage, injury, repatriation, or abandonment concerns.

Extra: Key Forms

MLC financial security evidence may appear as an insurer’s certificate, a bank guarantee, or a P&I-backed document.

In oral exams, call it “evidence of financial security” first, then mention the document form.

CE Relevance

For the engine department, the issue becomes real after serious injury, long illness, hospitalisation, disability, or abandonment-type disputes, because the CE is often the first senior officer documenting the facts, witness statements, log extracts, and medical sequence.

A weak paper trail after an engine-room injury can compromise both the crew claim pathway and the ship’s inspection posture.

So your CE application is: verify the certificate, know where it is posted, know who to notify, and document every injury or incapacity event cleanly.

Trap Questions

“What exactly is covered under MLC insurance?” — “Two heads: repatriation under A2.5.2 and shipowners’ liability under A4.2.”

“How much wage cover should you remember?” — “Outstanding wages up to four months under the repatriation security.”

“Can the seafarer only claim through the company?” — “No, the notes state a direct claim route against the financial security provider.”

“Where will PSC see it?” — “Onboard certificate or equivalent evidence, posted and available for inspection.”

Common CE Failures

Candidates often answer only “death compensation” and forget repatriation security entirely.

Many mix up P&I with hull and machinery and cannot explain that MLC crew liability protections sit under the liability/financial security side rather than ordinary machinery damage insurance.

Some remember the certificate exists but forget the killer number: four months outstanding wages.

Numbers / Regs

Standard A4.2 — shipowners’ liability.

2014 MLC amendments introduced the financial security framework reflected in the notes.

Entry into force in the notes: 18 January 2017.

Four months outstanding wages should be memorised.

DMLC inspectable items total 16, with items 15 and 16 linked to financial security in the notes.

Casualty Link

The real-world link is abandonment, unpaid wages, serious crew injury, or fatal accident after which the seafarer or next of kin need a solvent financial security mechanism instead of relying on a failed operator.

In CE terms, a machinery-space injury becomes both a safety case and a labour-rights/financial-security case within minutes.

Examiner Chain

Simon’s pattern in the source notes is usually: “What is covered?” then “Which standards?” then “How many months wages?” then “What happens if the certificate lapses?”

Answer best in this order: “two heads → cover items → documentary proof → PSC consequence.”

On My Vessel

On a Maersk container ship, you should know exactly where the MLC financial security certificates are filed, who posts them in the crew common area, and how to produce them instantly during PSC or flag inspection.

In a real injury case in the engine room, your practical duty is to preserve evidence, record the event accurately, support medical evacuation logistics, and ensure the crew member’s contractual rights are not lost through poor documentation.

Young seafarer under MLC — definition, MLC requirements, how will you handle a young seafarer onboard?
MLC
15-Second AnswerA young seafarer is a seafarer under 18, and in practical terms this means a 16- or 17-year-old seafarer. MLC controls minimum age, restricts night work, and prevents hazardous work for young seafarers. As CE, I would use formal risk assessment, close supervision, a buddy system, and a documented training plan before assigning any engine-room task.
60-Second AnswerUnder the source notes, the absolute minimum age is 16, but hazardous work requires a minimum age of 18. Night work for young seafarers is prohibited in the 21:00 to 05:00 window except for training-related or permitted exceptions referred to in the notes. DG Shipping material also highlights the importance of risk assessment, job hazard analysis, and education of young seafarers, with reference to MS Notice 16 of 2016. As Chief Engineer, I would not allow enclosed-space entry alone, hot work, work at height, or other hazardous engine-room jobs without competence check, supervision, and documented control measures.

Definition

The working exam definition is: young seafarer = under 18 years of age.

A supporting administrative example in the retrieved material describes a young seafarer as a 16- or 17-year-old seafarer.

Core MLC requirements

Minimum age to work at sea is 16 in the source notes.

Minimum age for hazardous work is 18 in the source notes.

Night work for a young seafarer is restricted, and the note set gives the memorisation band as 21:00 to 05:00.

The notes also emphasise health assessment and tighter supervision for young persons.

What counts as hazardous work

The source notes identify typical hazardous activities as high-voltage work, enclosed-space entry, work at height, heavy lifting, and fire-fighting duties.

DG Shipping training material separately stresses the need for risk assessment and job hazard analysis for young seafarers.

So in oral delivery, hazardous work should be explained not as a vague label but as a task-based control issue.

CE handling plan

First, verify age, medical fitness, rank, and whether the company has issued any young-seafarer restrictions or familiarisation requirements.

Second, assign a mentor and operate a buddy system for all workshop, machinery-space, and deck transfer tasks.

Third, do a specific risk assessment/JHA before assigning hot work, lifting, chemical handling, work aloft, or confined-space related activity.

Fourth, maintain a training record and progress plan rather than using the young seafarer as extra labour during peak maintenance.

Fifth, if the trainee is struggling physically, mentally, or operationally, escalate early to the Master and company instead of waiting for a near miss.

Hours of work and rest link

The notes connect this answer to MLC hours of work/rest and STCW watchkeeping controls, meaning fatigue management is part of young-seafarer protection.

In practice, this matters in engine departments because port turnaround and UMS alarms can push trainees into unsafe fatigue unless the CE actively controls workload.

Documentation onboard

A strong exam answer mentions age proof, medical fitness, training/familiarisation records, and risk-assessment evidence for restricted tasks.

If the vessel has young seafarers, the company’s procedures should reflect how hazardous work and supervision are controlled.

Extra: Trap Warning

Do not answer only “young seafarer means under 18” and stop.

The examiner wants the management response: supervision, risk assessment, mentor, training record, and prohibited/high-risk tasks.

CE Relevance

This topic is very CE-heavy because the engine room contains exactly the kind of hazard set that young seafarer protections are designed for: rotating machinery, electrical risk, confined spaces, hot surfaces, chemicals, noise, lifting gear, and emergency duties.

A good Chief Engineer does not simply “keep the young person away,” but uses staged exposure, close supervision, and controlled competence-building so that training happens without normalising unsafe shortcuts.

That is the balance examiners want: welfare protection plus structured professional development.

Trap Questions

“What is the minimum age?” — “16 for service, but 18 for hazardous work, as per the source notes.”

“What is night work?” — “For memorisation, 21:00 to 05:00 is the prohibited band stated in the notes.”

“Can a young seafarer enter an enclosed space?” — “Not as a casual assignment; I would treat it as hazardous work requiring restriction, formal risk control, and competent supervision, and I would not permit solitary entry.”

“What is your CE action?” — “Mentor, buddy, JHA, training record, no unsafe task dumping, and early escalation.”

Common CE Failures

Candidates often recite age numbers but fail to convert them into an engine-room supervision plan.

Many forget to mention risk assessment/JHA, even though DG Shipping training material specifically highlights it for young seafarer control.

Another common failure is allowing the trainee into high-risk work informally because “he is only helping,” which is exactly the sort of weak control the examiner is probing.

Numbers / Regs

Young seafarer: under 18.

Minimum age for sea service in the notes: 16.

Minimum age for hazardous work in the notes: 18.

Night work memorisation band in the notes: 21:00 to 05:00.

DG Shipping training material: importance of risk assessment, job hazard analysis, and education of young seafarers, with reference to MS Notice 16 of 2016.

Casualty Link

The real-world risk is not one dramatic legal breach but the slow drift into unsafe assignments, fatigue, and poor supervision until the young seafarer is injured during enclosed-space work, lifting, hot work, or an emergency response.

In oral exams, say that young-seafarer control is a barrier against first-job overexposure in high-energy machinery spaces.

Examiner Chain

Simon’s note pattern is: definition first, then MLC requirement, then practical CE handling.

So the best sequence is: “definition → age limits → night work → hazardous work examples → my CE control plan.”

On My Vessel

On a Maersk container ship, I would induct the young seafarer first on workshop discipline, permit-to-work boundaries, PPE, alarm response, and machinery-space safe routes before any maintenance participation.

I would not place him casually into enclosed-space attendance, purifier strip-down under time pressure, boiler burner maintenance, electrical isolation checks, or emergency generator testing without direct competent supervision and prior task briefing.

His early months should be training-led, not overtime-led.

SOLAS latest amendments — key recent amendments and when they entered into force?
SOLAS
15-Second AnswerTwo critical recent SOLAS amendment packages apply to shipboard operations. MSC.496(105) entered into force on 1 January 2024, bringing major GMDSS modernisation to Chapter IV, including the removal of NBDP requirements and redefining Sea Area A3 around RMSS coverage. MSC.520(106) enters into force on 1 January 2026, targeting Chapter II-2 with new fire safety requirements including flashpoint declaration controls and container ship fire safety upgrades like water mist lances.
60-Second AnswerExaminers currently target two major packages: MSC.496(105) and MSC.520(106). Under MSC.496(105), which came into force on 1 January 2024, SOLAS Chapter IV (Radiocommunications) was comprehensively modernised; the term "Inmarsat" was replaced with "Recognized Mobile Satellite Service" (RMSS), Narrow Band Direct Printing (NBDP) was dropped, and Sea Area A3 was redefined to match the coverage of the specific RMSS installed onboard. Under MSC.520(106), coming into force on 1 January 2026, Chapter II-2 (Fire protection) is amended; key items include stricter control on the flashpoint of delivered oil fuel and enhanced fire safety for container ships requiring water mist lances and portable fire monitors of at least 400 L/min capacity. A third relevant update is the Chapter II-2 regulation on lifting appliances and anchor handling winches.

MSC.496(105) — GMDSS Modernisation (1 Jan 2024) This package completely overhauled SOLAS Chapter IV.

The outdated term “Inmarsat” was removed and replaced by the technology-neutral term Recognised Mobile Satellite Service (RMSS).

The requirement for Narrow Band Direct Printing (NBDP) for distress and safety communications was abolished.

Sea Area A3 was redefined; it is now defined by the coverage of the specific RMSS Earth Station installed onboard, meaning A3 is no longer a fixed geographic latitude band but depends on the ship's equipment.

For Sea Area A1, VHF-EPIRB is no longer accepted in lieu of a satellite EPIRB.

MSC.520(106) — Fire Safety and Flashpoint (1 Jan 2026) This package amends SOLAS Chapter II-2.

For engine departments, the crucial change is fuel safety: the amendments introduce strict requirements regarding the delivery of oil fuel, specifically demanding that the bunker delivery note states the flashpoint has been determined and meets SOLAS minimums.

For container ships, it introduces enhanced fire-fighting requirements: vessels must carry a water mist lance, and depending on size, portable fire monitors capable of at least 400 L/min, along with enhanced cargo hold fire detection and extra breathing apparatus.

Lifting Appliances

The source notes also point out recent SOLAS requirements concerning lifting appliances (cranes, engine room hoists) and anchor handling winches, bringing their design, testing, and maintenance strictly under SOLAS certification instead of just class rules.

Tacit Acceptance

When examiners ask how these amendments enter into force, the correct answer is the Tacit Acceptance procedure.

This means an adopted SOLAS amendment will enter into force on a specified date unless, before that date, objections are received from a specified number of contracting governments.

Extra: Key Numbers

The examiner wants to hear 400 L/min for the portable fire monitors.

The implementation dates 1 Jan 2024 and 1 Jan 2026 must be precise.

CE Relevance

As Chief Engineer, the 2026 flashpoint amendments directly impact your bunkering procedures; if the bunker barge cannot provide the required flashpoint declaration under MSC.520(106), you cannot legally load the fuel.

For the 2024 GMDSS changes, although operated by the deck officers, the CE must manage the power supply, backup batteries, and the integration of the GMDSS console into the emergency switchboard without worrying about obsolete NBDP equipment.

The lifting appliance rules mean your engine-room gantry crane now sits under strict statutory scrutiny, not just a routine PMS check.

Trap Questions

“What changed in 2024 for SOLAS?” — “Chapter IV GMDSS modernisation under MSC.496(105), dropping NBDP and redefining Sea Area A3.”

“What is the 2026 amendment for container ships?” — “MSC.520(106) under Chapter II-2, requiring water mist lances and portable monitors of 400 L/min.”

“Does the 2026 amendment only affect container ships?” — “No, it also tightens oil fuel flashpoint documentation for all ships.”

Common CE Failures

Candidates often confuse MARPOL Annex VI fuel amendments (like the 0.5% sulphur cap) with SOLAS Chapter II-2 fuel amendments (like the 2026 flashpoint rules).

When asked about GMDSS changes, candidates say “new radios” without knowing the exact technical shift: removal of NBDP and the switch to RMSS.

Numbers / Regs

1 January 2024: Entry into force for MSC.496(105) (GMDSS).

1 January 2026: Entry into force for MSC.520(106) (Fire Safety/Fuel).

Portable monitors: Minimum 400 L/min.

SOLAS Chapter IV: Radiocommunications.

SOLAS Chapter II-2: Fire protection, fire detection and fire extinction.

Casualty Link

MSC.520(106) was driven by devastating container ship fires (like the Maersk Honam) where traditional CO2 could not suppress deep-seated container fires, hence the water mist lance and portable monitors.

The flashpoint rules were accelerated by incidents of ships receiving off-spec fuel with dangerously low flashpoints, risking engine-room explosions.

Examiner Chain

Simon asks this question to test currency. His chain is: “What is new in SOLAS? → MSC number? → When does it apply? → What is the container ship requirement?”

If you answer flashpoint, he will ask how it affects bunkering.

On My Vessel

On a Maersk container ship, MSC.520(106) is a major hardware issue; the vessel either already has the mist lance and monitors or must be retrofitted by 2026.

As CE, you would verify that the fire main can deliver the required pressure to the new 400 L/min portable monitors at the furthest container tier.

ISM Code elements — list all 12 elements and explain the key ones.
ISM
15-Second AnswerThe ISM Code Part A consists of 12 elements ranging from General to Company Verification. The 12 elements are: 1. General, 2. Policy, 3. Company responsibilities, 4. DPA, 5. Master's authority, 6. Resources/personnel, 7. Shipboard operations, 8. Emergency preparedness, 9. Non-conformities, 10. Maintenance, 11. Documentation, and 12. Company verification. For a Chief Engineer, the core functional elements are 7 (Plans), 9 (NCRs), and 10 (Maintenance).
60-Second AnswerPart A of the ISM Code deals with Implementation and contains 12 elements. Element 1 provides General definitions; 2 requires a Safety and Environmental Policy; 3 outlines Company Responsibilities and Authority; 4 establishes the Designated Person Ashore; 5 defines the Master’s Responsibility and Overriding Authority; 6 covers Resources and Personnel; 7 requires Development of Plans for Shipboard Operations; 8 mandates Emergency Preparedness; 9 deals with Reports and Analysis of Non-Conformities; 10 governs Maintenance of Ship and Equipment; 11 controls Documentation; and 12 requires Company Verification, Review and Evaluation. A Chief Engineer practically executes the code through Element 10 via the PMS, Element 9 by reporting technical near-misses and NCRs, and Element 7 by following safe engineering procedures.

List of 12 Elements

General (Definitions and objectives).

Safety and environmental protection policy.

Company responsibilities and authority.

Designated person(s) (DPA).

Master’s responsibility and authority (Including overriding authority).

Resources and personnel.

Development of plans for shipboard operations.

Emergency preparedness.

Reports and analysis of non-conformities, accidents and hazardous occurrences.

Maintenance of the ship and equipment.

Documentation.

Company verification, review and evaluation (Internal audits and master's review).

Key Elements for the CE

Element 10 (Maintenance): This is the CE’s direct domain. The company must establish procedures to ensure the ship is maintained in conformity with regulations. It mandates inspections, reporting of non-conformities, and specific identification of "critical equipment" (equipment whose sudden failure creates a hazardous situation).

Element 9 (NCRs): The CE must report technical non-conformities, machinery accidents, and near misses so the company can perform root cause analysis and prevent recurrence.

Element 7 (Shipboard Operations): This covers the daily engineering procedures—permit to work, bunkering plans, and enclosed space entry.

Element 6 (Resources and Personnel): The CE ensures the engine room is appropriately manned with qualified seafarers and that familiarisation training is completed and documented.

The PDCA Cycle

The elements are not standalone; they form a continuous improvement loop based on the Plan-Do-Check-Act cycle.

Operations are planned (Element 7), executed, checked via audits and NCRs (Elements 9 and 12), and acted upon by updating policies and procedures (Elements 2 and 7).

Extra: Trap Warning

Examiners will ask you to recite the list. Do not mix the order.

Hull Character: Indicates the structural integrity of the ship. The Maltese Cross (✠) generally means the ship was built under the direct supervision of that specific classification society.

Machinery Character: Indicates that the propulsion and auxiliary machinery comply with class rules (e.g., LR's LMC means Lloyd's Machinery Certificate).

Service Notation: Explicitly states what the ship is built to do (e.g., Oil Tanker, Bulk Carrier, Container Ship). Operating outside this service notation invalidates the class certificate.

BWT: Indicates a class-approved Ballast Water Treatment system is installed.

DYNPOS: Dynamic Positioning (if applicable).

If the fire detection system in the engine room fails, the vessel no longer complies with AUT-0.

Process of ship registration in India — what certificates and who issues them?
SURVEY
15-Second AnswerShip registration in India is governed by the Merchant Shipping Act (updated to 2025) and requires an application to the MMD/Shipping Master. The core documents required include the Builder's Certificate for newbuilds or a Bill of Sale for second-hand ships, along with a Carving and Marking Note and a Survey Certificate. 60-Second AnswerUnder the MS Act, registering an Indian ship follows a strict sequence. First, the vessel is surveyed by the Indian Register of Shipping (IRS) or an approved class society, and tonnage is measured to issue a Tonnage Certificate. The official number, net tonnage, and port of registry are permanently marked on the hull, leading to the issuance of a Carving and Marking Note. The owner then submits an application to the Registrar of Ships containing the Builder's Certificate (or Bill of Sale), the Declaration of Ownership, the Tonnage Certificate, and a Deletion Certificate if transferring from a foreign flag. Upon verification, the vessel is entered into the Ship Register and the Registrar of Indian Ships (situated at the MMD office) issues the Certificate of Registry. It is crucial to distinguish between the issuing authorities: The Registrar issues the Certificate of Registry, the MMD issues statutory safety certificates, and IRS or Class issues the class and survey certificates.

The Legal Framework

Ship registration establishes the nationality of the ship, bringing it under the jurisdiction and protection of the Indian flag.

The governing law is Part III of the Merchant Shipping Act. With the transition to the Merchant Shipping Act 2025, registration has been modernised, making it mandatory for Indian-owned vessels (with minor coastal exemptions) to register to streamline offshore and special-purpose vessel administration.

Step-by-Step Process

Survey and Measurement: An approved Recognised Organisation (like IRS) measures the vessel to establish its gross and net tonnage.

Carving and Marking: The assigned official number and registered tonnage must be carved into the ship’s main beam, and the name and port of registry marked on the bow and stern. The surveyor verifies this and issues a Carving and Marking Note.

Application: The owner applies to the Registrar of Indian Ships with proof of ownership (Builder's Certificate for new ships, or Bill of Sale for existing ships).

Registry Entry: If the vessel is mortgaged, the deed of covenant is recorded.

Issuance: The Certificate of Registry is issued, formally granting the vessel the right to fly the Indian flag.

Provisional Registration

If an Indian ship is purchased abroad, it can be issued a Provisional Certificate of Registry by the local Indian consulate.

This allows the ship to sail to an Indian port, after which full registration must be completed.

Differentiation of Authorities

Registrar of Indian Ships: Issues the Certificate of Registry (ownership/nationality).

Mercantile Marine Department (MMD): Issues statutory certificates (e.g., Cargo Ship Safety Construction, Safety Equipment) after verifying SOLAS compliance.

IRS/Class Societies: Issue the Classification Certificate (structural/machinery compliance) and act as ROs for statutory surveys.

Extra: Key Forms

Builder's Certificate: Identifies the shipbuilder, dimensions, and the person on whose account the ship was built.

Bill of Sale: The legal document transferring ownership of an existing vessel.

CE Relevance

While the Master secures the Certificate of Registry, the CE manages the surveys and statutory certificates (Safety Construction, IOPP, IAPP) that the MMD and Class issue. If the ship loses its class certification due to an engine room defect, the statutory certificates are invalidated, effectively paralyzing the ship's legal standing to operate.

60-Second AnswerThe crew complement on any vessel is legally underpinned by the Safe Manning Document (SMD). The process begins with the company conducting a risk assessment based on the ship's size, main propulsion power, level of automation (e.g., UMS notation), trade area, and the requirement to maintain safe STCW watchkeeping and MLC rest hours. The company submits this proposed manning scale to the Flag State. Once approved, the Flag State issues the Safe Manning Document, which details the absolute minimum ranks and qualifications required onboard. The Regulatory Basis

SOLAS Chapter V Regulation 14: Mandates that every ship must be sufficiently and efficiently manned to ensure safety of life at sea, and must carry a Safe Manning Document.

STCW Code: Dictates the qualifications of the officers and ratings, ensuring that the manning level allows for safe navigational and engineering watches without violating fatigue limits.

MLC 2006 Title 2.7: Requires that ships have a sufficient number of seafarers onboard to ensure safe operations and to cope with peak workloads without compromising the crew's welfare or rest hours.

The Process of Determination

Company Proposal: The shipowner assesses the vessel's technical specifications. A fully automated UMS (Unattended Machinery Space) container ship requires fewer engineers on watch than a 30-year-old manually operated steamship. The company drafts a proposed manning level.

Flag State Review: The Flag State evaluates the proposal against IMO Principles of Minimum Safe Manning. They verify that the proposed crew can handle emergency fire responses, abandon ship protocols, and routine maintenance without fatigue.

Issuance: The Flag State issues the Safe Manning Document.

CBA Enhancements: Often, Collective Bargaining Agreements (CBAs) or union rules demand a higher crew complement than the absolute statutory minimum stated in the SMD.

Cyber security management on ships — key requirements and latest developments?
ISM
15-Second AnswerMaritime cyber risk management became mandatory under the ISM Code starting January 1, 2021, guided by IMO MSC-FAL.1/Circ.3. The framework requires companies to Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, and Recover from cyber threats. The most critical recent development is the enforcement of IACS Unified Requirements UR E26 and UR E27, which mandate strict cyber resilience in the design and integration of shipboard equipment for newbuilds contracted after July 2024.
60-Second AnswerCyber security in shipping operates on two levels: management and technical design. On the management side, IMO Resolution MSC.428(98) and MSC-FAL.1/Circ.3 required all companies to incorporate cyber risk management into their Safety Management Systems (ISM Code Element 7) no later than the first annual verification of the DOC after January 1, 2021. The operational approach uses the NIST framework: Identify vulnerable systems, Protect them, Detect anomalies, Respond to breaches, and Recover operations. On the technical side, the latest major development is the entry into force of IACS UR E26 (Cyber resilience of ships) and UR E27 (Cyber resilience of on-board systems) in July 2024. These enforce mandatory hardware and software security standards for operational technology (OT) like SCADA and PMS on new vessels.

The ISM Management Requirement (IMO)

Cyber security is no longer an IT department recommendation; it is a statutory ISM Code requirement.

Following IMO guidelines, the ship's SMS must explicitly address cyber risks.

A vessel can be detained by PSC if the auditor finds that the SMS does not contain procedures for managing cyber threats (e.g., unsecured USB usage in the engine room).

The Technical Design Requirement (IACS 2024)

While the IMO addressed the management side, IACS addressed the engineering side.

UR E26 ensures the ship as a whole is cyber resilient, establishing secure networks between the bridge, engine room, and administrative computers.

UR E27 applies to the equipment manufacturers. It ensures that critical systems (like main engine control systems or ballast water management panels) have built-in cyber resilience before they are even installed on the ship.

These URs became mandatory for newbuilds contracted from July 1, 2024.

OT vs IT

IT (Information Technology): The ship's administrative computers, crew internet, and email servers.

OT (Operational Technology): The engine room SCADA, Power Management System (PMS), Main Engine controllers, and ECDIS.

Cyber attackers target IT to steal data, but they target OT to cause physical damage or operational paralysis. The separation (air-gapping) of OT from IT is a primary protective measure.
