Form E — what is it and what does the examiner expect on this?
Certification

Governing Instrument

Form E is issued under the provisions of the International Convention for the Safety of Life at Sea (SOLAS), 1974, as amended, under the authority of the flag State government. It is the Record of Equipment permanently attached to the Cargo Ship Safety Equipment Certificate (SEC).

Key Requirements and Contents

  • Ship details: name, IMO number, port of registry
  • Life-Saving Appliances (LSA): total persons accommodated, lifeboats (number/capacity port & stbd), free-fall lifeboats, motor lifeboats, rescue boats
  • Personal LSA: lifebuoys, lifejackets, immersion suits, thermal protective aids
  • Navigational systems: ECDIS, VDR/S-VDR, radar transponders, AIS

Chief Engineer's Practical Application

While the Master traditionally holds the original certificate, the CE must maintain a copy for the Planned Maintenance System. When ordering replacement immersion suits, lifejackets, or updating the rescue boat's outboard motor, the CE must cross-reference Form E. If a liferaft is temporarily removed for servicing without immediate replacement, an exemption certificate or short-term SEC with an updated Form E provision must be issued by Class/Flag.

15-Second AnswerForm E is the Record of Equipment permanently attached to the Cargo Ship Safety Equipment Certificate. It details the exact inventory and specifications of the ship's life-saving appliances (LSA) and navigational equipment as mandated by SOLAS.
60-Second AnswerForm E is a mandatory statutory document supplementing the Cargo Ship Safety Equipment Certificate for cargo ships of 500 GT and upwards. It explicitly records the number and capacity of lifeboats, rescue boats, liferafts, lifejackets, immersion suits, and essential navigational systems onboard. The examiner expects you to know Form E must perfectly match the actual physical equipment onboard. As CE, any modification — changing a lifeboat capacity or replacing a rescue boat engine — requires Class approval and an immediate update to Form E; failure to do so is a guaranteed PSC detainable deficiency.
⚠ Examiner TrapQ: You replace a 15-person liferaft with a 20-person liferaft — total capacity is still more than required. Do you need to update Form E? A: Yes. Form E must reflect the exact equipment installed onboard. Any discrepancy between the certificate and physical inventory is a PSC deficiency. Common failure: confusing Form E (Safety Equipment) with Form C (Safety Construction) or Form R (Safety Radio).

Numbers to Memorise

Governing reg: SOLAS Ch I, Reg 12 (Issue of Certificates) · Form E validity: up to 5 years (matches the SEC).
⚓ Enforcement LinkNumerous PSC detentions occur globally under LSA-related deficiency codes because a vessel upgraded equipment but failed to call Class to amend Form E.

UR and UI — what do these acronyms stand for (Unified Requirements and Unified Interpretations)?
IACS Framework

Governing Principle

The International Association of Classification Societies (IACS) — currently 12 member societies, classing over 90% of world tonnage — develops both URs and UIs to eliminate substandard shipping and ensure a level playing field across societies and shipyards.

Unified Requirements (UR)

Technical resolutions on classification matters (hull strength, machinery, electrical installations). Once adopted, IACS members must integrate a UR into their own rules within a set implementation period. This is the baseline — a society can be stricter than a UR but never less strict.

Unified Interpretations (UI)

Deal with statutory requirements (IMO conventions), clarifying ambiguities where the text states "to the satisfaction of the Administration." Flag State rules always supersede IACS UIs — if a flag issues its own interpretation, the vessel follows the flag, not the IACS UI.

Chief Engineer's Practical Application

During machinery breakdowns, major repairs, or automation upgrades, Class surveyors evaluate the work against their society's rules, which are inherently based on IACS URs (e.g. UR M for machinery installations).

15-Second AnswerUR stands for Unified Requirements — minimum technical rules adopted by IACS that member classification societies must incorporate into their own rules. UI stands for Unified Interpretations — common understandings of vague statutory IMO regulations to ensure uniform global application.
60-Second AnswerIACS Unified Requirements establish the fundamental minimum technical standards for classification — for example UR M covers machinery installations. A society can make its rules stricter than a UR but never less strict. Unified Interpretations clarify ambiguities in IMO conventions like SOLAS or MARPOL. Unless a Flag State provides its own specific written interpretation, IACS UIs are applied by all Recognized Organizations acting on the flag's behalf — but the Flag State's own interpretation always takes precedence.
⚠ Examiner TrapQ: If a class society has a Unified Interpretation, but the Flag State disagrees, which do you follow? A: The Flag State always has ultimate authority — IACS UIs apply only in the absence of specific guidance from the Flag Administration. Common failure: mixing up the definitions — claiming URs apply to SOLAS and UIs apply to Class rules (it's the reverse: UR = Class/Technical; UI = IMO/Statutory/Legal).

Numbers to Memorise

IACS UR M (Machinery Installations) · IACS UR Z (Survey and Certification) · 12 IACS Member Societies (as of 2026 — Russia's RMRS withdrawn Mar 2022, Türk Loydu admitted Nov 2023, restoring the count to 12).
⚓ Casualty LinkThe sinking of several bulk carriers in the early 1990s led to strict Unified Requirements for longitudinal strength and water-ingress alarms (UR S series).

Principles of watchkeeping — explain.
STCW

Governing Instrument

STCW Convention 1978, as amended (Manila Amendments 2010), Chapter VIII (Watchkeeping), specifically Section A-VIII/2, Part 4-2.

Key Requirements — Watchkeeping Principles

  • Watch arrangements: CE determines watch composition based on operational conditions (UMS vs manned, heavy weather, restricted visibility)
  • Taking over the watch: relieving officers verify machinery status, logbook entries, and confirm no critical operations (e.g. fuel changeovers) are in a precarious stage
  • Performing the watch: continuous monitoring, proper log entries, immediate action in emergencies
  • Fitness for duty: no watchkeeper impaired by fatigue, alcohol, or drugs; minimum rest hours strictly enforced
  • Environmental protection: the watchkeeper must be fully aware of MARPOL and prevent pollution during their watch

Chief Engineer's Practical Application

The CE translates STCW requirements into reality through the "Chief Engineer's Standing Orders" and "Night Orders," ensuring the duty roster complies with the STCW minimum of 10 hours rest in any 24-hour period and 77 hours in any 7-day period.

15-Second AnswerUnder STCW Chapter VIII, the principles of watchkeeping require the Chief Engineer, in consultation with the Master, to ensure a safe and continuous engineering watch is maintained — safe operation of all machinery affecting ship safety, pollution prevention, and watchkeepers who are physically and mentally fit for duty.
60-Second AnswerSTCW Code Part 4-2 establishes the mandatory principles for an engineering watch. The CE must arrange watchkeeping that prevents fatigue and ensures environmental protection under MARPOL. Key principles: fitness for duty (strict rest hours), thorough familiarisation with machinery, explicit take-over procedures, and clear communication. For UMS, it dictates immediate response to alarms. The relieving engineer must never take over the watch if they believe the relieved officer is incapable of carrying out their duties.
⚠ Examiner TrapQ: During handover, a critical bilge high-level alarm sounds — who handles it? A: The relieved officer remains responsible for the watch until the situation is stabilised and handover is explicitly completed. Q: The relieving engineer smells of alcohol — your action? A: Refuse to hand over the watch, inform the CE immediately, do not leave the engine room. Common failure: forgetting "protection of the marine environment" as a core STCW watchkeeping principle.

Numbers to Memorise

Rest hours: minimum 10 hours in any 24-hour period (splittable into two periods, one at least 6 hours) · minimum 77 hours in any 7-day period · Manila 2010 amendments also introduced a maximum 0.05% BAC alcohol limit for watchkeepers.
⚓ Casualty LinkNumerous groundings and collisions traced to fatigue or improper handover protocols drove the Manila 2010 amendments to enforce stricter rest-hour monitoring and mandatory alcohol limits.

Taking over as CE: what documents do you check in relation to ISM?
ISM Takeover

Governing Principle

The ISM Code (SOLAS Chapter IX) requires every vessel to operate under an approved Safety Management System (SMS). The CE acts as the key management representative for the technical department, making SMS-compliance verification upon takeover a legal and operational necessity.

ISM Documentation Verification Checklist

  • Statutory certificates: validate the SMC (original onboard) and DOC (copy onboard); check intermediate/renewal audit dates; ensure the DOC covers the vessel's ship type
  • SMS manuals: check document control index — all engine room SMS manuals must be the latest revision
  • Audits and non-conformities: review latest internal/external ISM audit reports; check status of open NCs, Observations, or CAPA plans
  • Reporting systems: review Near-Miss reports, Defect Reports, and incident investigations specific to the engine room for the last 6 months
  • Familiarisation records: ensure ISM familiarisation checklists (including emergency duties) for all engine crew are fully signed off
  • Critical equipment list: verify SMS-designated "critical equipment" maintenance is current in the PMS with no overdue items

Chief Engineer's Practical Application

If open, major non-conformities or overdue critical machinery are found without a dispensation from Class/Flag, do not accept the handover blindly — record deficiencies in the official handover report, inform the Master, and notify the company's Designated Person Ashore (DPA) before signing the takeover document.

15-Second AnswerWhen taking over as Chief Engineer, I verify the validity of the ship's Safety Management Certificate (SMC) and the company's Document of Compliance (DOC) copy. Under the ISM Code, I confirm the SMS manuals match the current revision index and that mandatory records — master's review and defect reports — are up-to-date and closed out.
60-Second AnswerTaking over under the ISM framework requires ensuring the vessel holds a valid SMC and a DOC copy valid for the specific ship type. I review the SMS manuals to ensure the latest revisions, verify the status of non-conformities, corrective action reports, and the PMS defect list. I also check familiarisation checklists of newly joined engine room crew, ensuring completion prior to sailing, and verify the Chief Engineer's Standing Orders are actively acknowledged by the current engineering watchkeepers.
⚠ Examiner TrapQ: The DOC copy onboard expired a week ago, but the SMC is valid for another two years — can you sail? A: No. SMC validity is explicitly tied to DOC validity. If the DOC expires, the SMC is automatically invalidated and the vessel cannot legally sail. Common failure: mentioning the Oil Record Book or fuel soundings — these are MARPOL/operational checks, not ISM Code document checks. Keep the answer strictly focused on SMS, audits, NCs, and certificates.

Numbers to Memorise

SMC validity: 5 years (at least one intermediate audit between the 2nd and 3rd anniversary) · DOC validity: 5 years (subject to annual audits) · Governing reg: SOLAS Chapter IX.
⚓ Casualty LinkFollowing major tanker disasters, accident investigations revealed incoming CEs signed handover forms without verifying SMS defect-reporting logs, effectively inheriting hidden structural and machinery failures never reported to the DPA.

Cyber security — basic principles, cross questions.
Maritime Cyber Security

Governing Principle

Cyber security is enforced through the ISM Code (Paragraph 1.2.2), guided by IMO Resolution MSC.428(98) and MSC-FAL.1/Circ.3 (Guidelines on maritime cyber risk management). It addresses vulnerabilities in both IT (computers/networks) and OT (machinery control systems).

Basic Principles — The 5-Step Framework

  • Identify: define roles for cyber risk management; identify vulnerable systems (ECDIS, Main Engine Governor, Alarm Monitoring System)
  • Protect: establish "air gaps" separating shipboard OT networks from internet-connected IT networks; strict password controls; ban unverified USB drives
  • Detect: continuously monitor networks for unauthorized access or malware signatures
  • Respond: execute the SMS cyber-incident response plan to isolate infected systems
  • Recover: restore systems using secure, offline data backups

Chief Engineer's Practical Application

Enforce strict "USB hygiene." When a service engineer boards to update main engine software or service boiler automation, ensure their laptops/USB drives are scanned on a standalone, dedicated scanning PC before connecting to the ship's OT network.

Note: this is the foundational version of this topic — see QB4_E Q15 for full regulatory depth on maritime cyber risk management. Keep this answer shorter and principle-focused.

15-Second AnswerUnder IMO Resolution MSC.428(98), maritime cyber risk management is a mandatory, integral part of the ship's Safety Management System under the ISM Code. The basic principles focus on identifying cyber risks to both IT and OT systems, protecting them, and having a response plan for cyber incidents.
60-Second AnswerCyber security under the ISM Code is no longer optional. Resolution MSC.428(98) required companies to incorporate cyber risk management into their SMS by the first annual DOC verification after 1 January 2021. The basic principles follow a framework: Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover. For a Chief Engineer, protecting Operational Technology — main engine governors, AMS, power management systems — is the primary focus.
⚠ Examiner TrapA class surveyor hands you a USB drive with the latest Class rules to view on the Engine Control Room computer — what do you do? A: Do not plug it directly into the ECR computer, as it may be networked to the Alarm Monitoring System (OT). It must be scanned first on a designated IT-isolated machine per SMS protocols. Common failure: treating cyber security purely as an IT/deck problem — as CE, emphasise OT protection (Power Management System, Main Engine automation).

Numbers to Memorise

IMO Resolution MSC.428(98) · Effective date: first annual DOC verification/audit after 1 January 2021.
⚓ Casualty LinkThe 2017 NotPetya ransomware attack crippled Maersk's global IT infrastructure — while it primarily affected terminals and shore operations, it highlighted the devastating potential of malware spreading through maritime networks, accelerating IMO's cyber security mandates.

LRIT and AIS — difference, purpose, and basic cross questions.
Ship Tracking

Governing Instruments

AIS: SOLAS Chapter V, Regulation 19 (carriage requirements for shipborne navigational systems). LRIT: SOLAS Chapter V, Regulation 19-1 (Long-range identification and tracking of ships).

Key Differences

FeatureAISLRIT
Primary purposeCollision avoidance & coastal monitoringMaritime security & SAR
TransmissionVHF radio (open broadcast)Satellite (Inmarsat-C, secure)
RangeShort-range (~20–30 NM)Global (except polar gaps)
Data recipientAnyone with an AIS receiverAuthorized Flag/Port/Coastal States only
Update frequencyContinuous (seconds–minutes)Default every 6 hours (pollable)
Data sentName, IMO, position, course, speed, cargoShip identity, lat/long, date/time only

Chief Engineer's Practical Application

While navigation equipment falls under the Deck department, the CE is responsible for the power supply — including emergency power — to the GMDSS/Inmarsat-C equipment that LRIT utilises. During blackout scenarios, the UPS/battery backup for these systems must function flawlessly to maintain LRIT transmission.

15-Second AnswerAIS is an open, short-range VHF-based system for collision avoidance and coastal tracking. LRIT is a secure, satellite-based system mandated by SOLAS Chapter V, Reg 19-1, designed for global tracking and maritime security, reporting only to authorised flag and coastal states.
60-Second AnswerBoth are mandated under SOLAS Chapter V, but serve different primary purposes. AIS operates on VHF, broadcasting ship data openly to any vessel or shore station within 20–30 nautical miles, primarily for anti-collision and situational awareness — anyone with a receiver can see it. LRIT uses Inmarsat-C satellites to transmit data globally; its primary purpose is maritime security. LRIT data is encrypted, transmitting only the ship's identity, position, and timestamp directly to designated LRIT Data Centres accessible only by Flag, Port, and Coastal States, reported by default every 6 hours.
⚠ Examiner TrapQ: Can anyone track your ship using LRIT on websites like MarineTraffic? A: No — LRIT data is encrypted and strictly confidential, routed through National/Regional Data Centres; only authorised governments can access it. Websites use AIS data. Common failure: stating LRIT is governed by SOLAS Chapter XI-2 (Security) — while its purpose is security, the mandating regulation is SOLAS Chapter V, Reg 19-1.

Numbers to Memorise

SOLAS V/Reg 19 (AIS) · SOLAS V/Reg 19-1 (LRIT) · LRIT default reporting interval: 6 hours · LRIT applicability: cargo ships 300 GT and upwards.
⚓ Context LinkLRIT implementation was accelerated post-9/11 to let governments track vessels approaching their coastlines from thousands of miles away, before the ship came within AIS/VHF range.
