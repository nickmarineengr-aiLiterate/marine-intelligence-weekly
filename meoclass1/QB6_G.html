📋 QB6_G — Electrical, Automation & Machinery (New Engine Technology)

2 questions
1
New type of engine — what problems are commonly faced, how do you mitigate them, and what does the maker recommend to avoid these in future designs?
MACHINERY

15-Second Answer

New ultra-long-stroke electronically-controlled 2-stroke engines commonly suffer cold corrosion of liners, hydraulic servo/accumulator leaks, and control-sensor faults. Mitigation: thermal management (keep liner temp above acid dew point), tight hydraulic-oil cleanliness (ISO 4406), and dual-redundant sensors. Makers recommend cermet-coated rings, hardened liners and block-drilled hydraulic manifolds for future designs.

60-Second Answer

On engines like the MAN B&W ME-C or WinGD X/Flex series, common teething issues are cold corrosion in extended-stroke liners, high-pressure hydraulic leaks/accumulator failures in the fuel and exhaust valve actuation system, and electronic control sensor faults. Onboard mitigation follows class-approved service bulletins: adjusting cylinder oil BN and feed rate via smart lubrication algorithms, maintaining jacket-water/load-dependent cooling to keep liner temperature above the acid dew point, and keeping hydraulic oil at ISO 4406 15/12 (NAS Class 6) or better. For future generations, makers recommend structural stiffening of bedplate/A-frame, cermet-coated piston rings with laser-hardened liners, and block-drilled hydraulic manifolds with dual-redundant position encoders to remove flexible-hose and single-sensor weak points.

Governing framework

SOLAS Ch.II-1 Part C (machinery reliability), Class rules for novel-technology type approval, and MARPOL Annex VI Reg.13 (which bounds combustion tuning).

Common teething problems

  • Cold corrosion: low liner-wall temperature relative to high Pmax causes sulphuric-acid condensation.
  • Hydraulic system faults: pressure-pulsation fatigue in HP fuel/exhaust valve servo piping, accumulator diaphragm rupture.
  • Sensor/automation faults: cylinder-pressure sensor or angle-encoder drift causing false slowdowns.

Onboard mitigation

  • Load-dependent cooling to hold liner temperature above the acid dew point.
  • Cylinder-oil BN matched to fuel sulphur/BN via automated pulse lubrication — never simply "add more oil" (causes deposit build-up and scavenge fires).
  • Hydraulic oil kept at ISO 4406 15/12 (NAS Class 6) cleanliness for the HPS block.

Maker recommendations for future designs

  • Stiffened bedplate/A-frame geometry against crosshead vibration.
  • Cermet-coated rings and laser-hardened liners.
  • Block-drilled hydraulic manifolds and dual-redundant contactless encoders replacing external HP piping.

Trap point

Do not drift into emissions/alternative-fuel talk — this question is about mechanical/structural/automation teething issues, not GHG.

Deep-Dive: CE Relevance

Shift from reactive to predictive maintenance: trend hydraulic oil particle counts, liner wear via scuffing sensors/scavenge inspection, and track ECU software-update baselines to avoid off-hire.

Deep-Dive: Trap Questions

Q: "Why not just raise cylinder-oil feed rate to fix cold corrosion?" A: Excess oil causes deposit build-up, ring sticking and scavenge fires — the fix is correct BN matching and keeping liner temperature above the dew point, not blanket over-lubrication.

Deep-Dive: Common CE Failures

Jumping to emissions/alternative-fuel discussion instead of mechanical/structural/automation failure modes; failing to name a cleanliness standard (ISO 4406/NAS) for hydraulic oil.

Deep-Dive: Numbers to Memorise

ISO 4406 15/12 (NAS Class 6) hydraulic-oil cleanliness; liner/scavenge temperature typically held ~70–80°C to stay above the acid dew point.

Deep-Dive: Casualty Link

No single named casualty — this is a recognised OEM service-bulletin/reliability topic tracked through class technical circulars rather than a specific reported incident.

2
What are the latest developments in main engine technology (not GHG/emissions-related)?
MACHINERY

15-Second Answer

Modern 2-stroke development is camshaftless: a hydraulic power supply (200–300 bar) actuates electronic fuel-injection and exhaust valves (FIVA/ELVA), with per-cylinder pressure sensors feeding closed-loop auto-tuning, plus smart pulse lubrication and cermet ring/liner coatings — all mechanical, digital and tribological, not emissions-driven.

60-Second Answer

Leaving GHG/emissions aside, the main non-environmental development track for 2-stroke engines is mechatronic and digital. Camshaftless hydraulic actuation (HPS at roughly 200–300 bar) drives Fuel Injection Valves and exhaust valves (FIVA/ELVA), giving fully variable timing across the load range instead of a fixed mechanical cam profile. Piezoelectric pressure sensors on every cylinder cover feed an online closed-loop auto-tuning system that balances Pmax/Pcomp across units automatically. Tribologically, pulse lubrication systems meter cylinder oil precisely as the ring pack passes the quills (cutting oil consumption and cold-corrosion risk), and cermet-coated rings paired with laser-hardened liners target very long component life.

Governing framework

SOLAS Ch.II-1 Reg.31 (automation/UMS safety provisions) and Class design rules (e.g. DNV Pt.4 Ch.2) for the engine and its control loops; ISO 4406 for hydraulic fluid cleanliness.

Mechatronic/hydraulic actuation

Camshaft, mechanical fuel pumps and roller guides are eliminated; a Hydraulic Power Supply at ~200–300 bar actuates Fuel Injection Valves and exhaust valves (FIVA/ELVA), giving fully variable injection/exhaust timing over the whole load range.

Digitalisation / closed-loop tuning

Piezoelectric pressure sensors on every cylinder feed real-time pressure diagrams; the control system auto-adjusts injection to balance cylinder loads (Pmax/Pcomp) without manual fuel-rack adjustment.

Tribology and materials

Pulse lubrication meters cylinder oil at the exact moment the ring pack passes the injection quills, scaled to real-time load; cermet (ceramic-metallic) ring coatings improve scuffing resistance with modern liner geometries.

Trap point

The question explicitly excludes GHG topics — do not mention EEXI/CII/SEEMP, scrubbers, LNG/methanol/ammonia dual-fuel; stay on mechanical, structural, digital and tribological ground.

Deep-Dive: CE Relevance

The CE now acts as systems technician as much as mechanic — performance balancing is done through software analysis of proportional hydraulic actuators rather than manual fuel-rack adjustment, so understanding these developments is essential for accurate health assessment and superintendent communication.

Deep-Dive: Trap Questions

Q: "How does an electronic engine balance cylinders without manual fuel-rack adjustment?" A: Piezoelectric per-cylinder pressure sensors feed the ECS, which auto-adjusts FIVA/ELVA opening/closing profiles in real time to equalise load across units.

Deep-Dive: Common CE Failures

Straying into GHG/alternative-fuel territory despite the explicit exclusion; being unable to explain how a camshaftless system physically moves the injection/exhaust valves; ignoring the tribology/material side and only discussing software.

Deep-Dive: Numbers to Memorise

HPS operating pressure ~200–300 bar; hydraulic oil cleanliness target ISO 4406 15/12 (NAS Class 6) or better for servo-valve protection.

Deep-Dive: On My Vessel

Our main engine runs a common-rail-type hydraulic actuation network with real-time cylinder balancing and pulse lubrication that continuously micro-adjusts cylinder-oil feed against load, optimising both wear and consumption.
