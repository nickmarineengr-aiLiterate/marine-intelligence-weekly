FLL — what will the CE know from FLL data?
GHG
15-Second Answer15-Second Answer (Teal Practice-Block) Fuel Lifecycle Assessment (LCA) or FLL data provides the Chief Engineer with the full well-to-wake greenhouse gas emissions profile of a fuel. It ensures compliance with MARPOL Annex VI and the IMO Strategy by accounting for emissions during production and transport (Well-to-Tank) combined with onboard combustion (Tank-to-Wake).
60-Second Answer60-Second Answer (Teal Practice-Block) Under the modern IMO regulatory framework for decarbonisation, FLL data shifts our focus from simple onboard fuel consumption to the total environmental impact of the fuel lifecycle. For a Chief Engineer, this data is reflected in the Bunker Delivery Note (BDN) and the Fuel Lifecycle Label (FLL). It provides the exact carbon dioxide equivalent (CO2e) factors required for calculating the vessel’s annual Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) and ensuring compliance with FuelEU Maritime frameworks. Understanding FLL prevents the vessel from being penalized for using alternative fuels that might have low operational emissions but a high upstream production footprint.

Governing Instrument and Regulatory Principle

The governing framework is MARPOL Annex VI (Regulations 26, 27, and 28), heavily driven by the IMO LCA Guidelines (Resolution MEPC.376(80)). The fundamental working principle relies on calculating the total Carbon Dioxide Equivalent emissions using the formula:

Total GHG Factors = Well-to-Tank (WTT) + Tank-to-Wake (TTW)

Key Requirements & Working Principle

  • Well-to-Tank (WTT): Accounts for extraction, processing, refining, and logistics/distribution of the fuel before it reaches the ship's manifold.
  • Tank-to-Wake (TTW): Accounts for onboard storage, energy conversion, and combustion in main engines, auxiliary engines, and boilers.
  • GHG Scope: It tracks not just standard CO2, but also methane (CH4) slip and nitrous oxide (N2O) emissions, converting them into a unified metric (gCO2e/MJ).

Practical Application for the Chief Engineer

The CE utilizes FLL data to verify the documentation provided during bunkering. The statutory values are integrated into the ship's SEEMP Part III (Ship Energy Efficiency Management Plan) to accurately project and log the vessel’s operational carbon intensity, directly impacting its yearly CII rating (A to E).

Examiner Angle — Simon

Simon focuses strictly on whether you understand that "zero-emission at the funnel" does not mean zero-emission legally. He will ask you how a fuel like grey methanol or blue ammonia impacts your statutory paperwork if the upstream supply chain is dirty.

Chief Engineer Relevance

The CE must ensure that the FLL documentation/sustainability certificates match the bunker delivery parameters. Incorrect values entered into the data collection system will result in an artificially inflated or deflated CII calculation, leading to non-compliance during annual verification.

Key Numbers / Regs to Remember

  • Resolution MEPC.376(80): Guidelines on Life Cycle GHG Intensity of Marine Fuels.
  • 100-year Global Warming Potential (GWP100): Used to scale Methane (factor of 28) and Nitrous Oxide (factor of 265) relative to CO2.

Applies To

Container vessels / Maersk A/S fleet globally.

  • A: Because the WTT phase accounts for the carbon absorbed by the biomass feedstock during growth, resulting in a significantly lower net Well-to-Wake value compared to fossil fuels.

2
Can cargo be used as fuel? Which code governs this?
GHG
15-Second Answer15-Second Answer (Teal Practice-Block) Yes, cargo can be used as fuel, provided the arrangement complies with safety regulations. For liquefied gases and low-flashpoint fuels, this process is governed globally by the IGF Code and SOLAS Chapter II-1, or the IGC Code if the vessel is a dedicated gas carrier utilizing its boil-off gas.
60-Second Answer60-Second Answer (Teal Practice-Block) Under SOLAS Chapter II-2, using fuel with a flashpoint below 60°C is generally prohibited unless explicitly permitted. However, cargo can be used as fuel under strict regulatory conditions. For standard gas carriers, the IGC Code governs the use of boil-off cargo gas. For non-gas carriers—such as modern container ships using LNG, methanol, or ammonia as fuel—the arrangement is strictly governed by the IGF Code (International Code of Safety for Ships Using Gases or Other Low-flashpoint Fuels). The primary safety objective is to ensure that the risk level of these low-flashpoint fuels is equivalent to that of conventional marine fuel oils.

Governing Instrument and Regulatory Principle

The use of low-flashpoint cargo or alternative fuels is governed by SOLAS Regulation II-1/56 and II-1/57, which make the IGF Code (Resolution MSC.391(95)) mandatory for non-IGC vessels.

Key Requirements & Working Principle

  • Flashpoint Limitation Exceptions: Standard machinery space arrangements require a minimum fuel flashpoint of 60°C. The IGF code bypasses this by implementing specialized containment and delivery systems.
  • Dual-Walled Piping: All fuel delivery lines outside the storage tanks through machinery spaces must be double-walled (jacketed) with continuous inert gas purging or ventilation combined with gas detection.
  • Hazardous Area Classification: Strict zoning rules separate safe spaces from potential explosive atmospheres around fuel preparation rooms and bunkering stations.

Practical Application for the Chief Engineer

On modern vessels, the CE oversees the dual-fuel system configuration. This includes verifying the integrity of the fuel preparation room, managing boil-off gas pressure, and ensuring the emergency shutdown (ESD) system is fully functional and tested regularly.

Examiner Angle — Simon

Simon focuses on the core exception rule. He will test you on whether you know the baseline flashpoint limit (60°C) and the exact code required when a container ship utilizes alternative low-flashpoint fuels stored in its fuel/cargo tanks.

Chief Engineer Relevance

The CE must ensure daily verification of the gas detection systems, ventilation systems in double-walled pipes, and the operational readiness of the inert gas system providing the protective barrier around the fuel lines.

Key Numbers / Regs to Remember

  • 60°C: The fundamental minimum flashpoint threshold for conventional machinery spaces under SOLAS.
  • Resolution MSC.391(95): Adoption of the IGF Code.

Applies To

Dual-fuel container vessels / Maersk A/S fleet conversions and newbuilds.

3
Why is the IGF Code required?
GHG
15-Second Answer15-Second Answer (Teal Practice-Block) The IGF Code is required to provide a mandatory international standard for ships operating with gas or low-flashpoint fuels. It mitigates the inherent risks of explosion, fire, and toxicity associated with these fuels to ensure ship, crew, and environmental safety.
60-Second Answer60-Second Answer (Teal Practice-Block) The IGF Code is necessary because conventional maritime safety regulations under SOLAS are designed around high-flashpoint fuels (above 60°C) like standard heavy fuel oil and marine gas oil. As the industry transitions to alternative fuels to meet IMO GHG targets, ships are increasingly using low-flashpoint options like LNG, methanol, and ammonia. The IGF Code bridges this regulatory safety gap by providing mandatory criteria for the arrangement, installation, control, and monitoring of machinery, equipment, and spaces utilizing these volatile fuels, ensuring that their overall risk profile does not exceed that of a conventional vessel.

Governing Instrument and Regulatory Rationale

The code is made mandatory via SOLAS Chapter II-1, Part G. Its existence stems from the physical characteristics of modern alternative fuels which violate traditional safety assumptions of shipboard machinery spaces.

Key Risk Mitigation Principles

  • Explosion Control: Mitigates the high volatility and wide flammability limits of gaseous fuels through structural containment and continuous hazardous area ventilation.
  • Single Failure Principle: The code dictates that a single failure in any technical component or system must not lead to an unsafe situation or an immediate explosive atmosphere (hence double-walling and automatic ESD integration).
  • Toxicity and Cryogenic Protection: Addresses specific hazards such as cryogenic embrittlement of conventional ship hulls (from LNG at -162°C) or acute toxicity to the crew (from alternative fuels like ammonia).

Practical Application for the Chief Engineer

The IGF Code establishes the operational boundaries for maintenance, testing schedules, and safety drills. The CE is legally obligated to maintain the integrity of all certified systems, ensuring that interlocks, hazardous area boundary integrity, and automatic gas-trips are never bypassed or compromised.

Examiner Angle — Simon

Simon frames this question from a core safety philosophy perspective. He wants to see if you understand why the engineering designs have changed, focusing heavily on the "Single Failure Principle."

Chief Engineer Relevance

The code dictates that the CE must manage specialized safety equipment, maintain strict explosive-area tool control, and ensure that all bunkering operations follow the explicit risk-assessment framework mandated by the code.

Key Numbers / Regs to Remember

  • Part G (SOLAS Ch II-1): Ships using low-flashpoint fuels.
  • STCW Regulation V/3: Mandatory minimum requirements for the training and qualifications of masters, officers, and ratings on ships subject to the IGF Code.

Applies To

Container vessels / Maersk A/S fleet operating with low-flashpoint alternative fuels.

Common failure point: Candidates focus only on the environment (GHG reduction) and forget that the IGF Code is exclusively a safety standard designed to keep the ship from exploding or poisoning the crew.

Common failure: describing IGF Code content without explaining the regulatory gap it was created to close.
CE Relevance, Traps, Failures & Exam Strategy
CE Relevance

Ensures that all safety systems—such as water spray curtains, gas detection heads, and double-block-and-bleed valves—are regularly overhauled and fully certified.

Trap Questions
  • Q: Is the IGF Code designed to reduce the greenhouse gas emissions of a vessel?
  • A: No, sir. The IGF Code is purely a safety code. The reduction of GHG emissions is regulated by MARPOL Annex VI; the IGF Code simply ensures that when we use low-carbon, low-flashpoint fuels to satisfy MARPOL, we do so safely.
Common CE Failures

Failing to explain the concept of the "Single Failure Principle" when describing why double-walled pipes are required.

Numbers to Memorise

SOLAS Chapter II-1 Part G; STCW V/3.

Casualty Link

Historical land-based and early marine gas distribution accidents where a single pipe rupture caused catastrophic vapor cloud explosions.

Examiner Chain

4
Low flashpoint fuels — where are they used without requiring the IGF Code, and why is IGF not required there?
GHG
15-Second Answer15-Second Answer (Teal Practice-Block) Low-flashpoint fuels are used without requiring the IGF Code onboard dedicated gas carriers governed by the IGC Code, or when utilizing emergency diesel generators that comply with specific SOLAS Chapter II-2 exceptions. The IGF Code is not required in these cases because their construction, safety systems, and containment are already comprehensively covered by these alternative international instruments.
60-Second Answer60-Second Answer (Teal Practice-Block) The explicit purpose of the IGF Code is to regulate non-gas carriers using low-flashpoint fuels. Dedicated gas carriers carrying liquefied gases as cargo are exempted from the IGF Code when using their own cargo boil-off gas as fuel, because they are already strictly regulated under SOLAS Regulation II-2/4.2.1.1 and the IGC Code (International Gas Carrier Code). Additionally, under strict SOLAS Part B exceptions, emergency generators may use fuel with a flashpoint down to 43°C. The IGF Code is omitted here because these specific vessel configurations and limited emergency applications already possess an equivalent regulatory and safety framework optimized for their exact risk profiles.

Governing Instrument and Regulatory Exemptions

The baseline application rule is defined under SOLAS Chapter II-1, Regulation 56 (Part G). The IGF Code explicitly excludes ships covered by the IGC Code through specific regulatory phrasing that defines its scope as applying only to "ships to which part G of SOLAS chapter II-1 applies."

Key Requirements & Working Principle

  • The IGC Code Alternative: Dedicated LNG/LPG carriers route boil-off gas (BOG) from their cargo tanks directly to dual-fuel main engines or boilers. Because the entire ship is structurally designed around gas containment, hazard zoning, and cargo safety systems, applying the IGF Code would cause redundant, conflicting regulations.
  • Emergency Diesel Generator Exception: Under SOLAS Chapter II-2, Regulation 4.2.2.3, emergency generators are permitted to use fuels with a flashpoint not less than 43°C (such as specialized arctic-grade low-temperature marine gas oils).
  • Machinery Space Isolation: The safety principle relies on the fact that emergency generator rooms are structurally isolated from the main machinery spaces, minimizing the cross-contamination risk of volatile vapors.

Practical Application for the Chief Engineer

For a Chief Engineer, this distinction governs asset management and compliance pathways. On a standard container vessel, any transition to low-flashpoint fuel (like methanol) demands absolute adherence to IGF design and operational checklists. On a dedicated gas carrier, the safety infrastructure relies instead on the IGC cargo system controls.

Examiner Angle — Simon

Simon tests your understanding of the boundaries between international codes. He wants to see if you can identify the exact line where the IGF Code stops applying and where the IGC Code or baseline SOLAS exceptions take over.

Chief Engineer Relevance

The CE must ensure that if low-flashpoint fuels (43°C to 60°C) are utilized in emergency machinery under SOLAS exemptions, specific temperature monitoring and dedicated fuel lines are maintained without confusing them with the comprehensive gas-safe double-wall requirements of the IGF Code.

Key Numbers / Regs to Remember

  • 43°C: The absolute lower limit for fuel oil flashpoints in emergency generators under SOLAS exceptions.
  • 60°C: The baseline SOLAS flashpoint floor for standard machinery spaces.

Applies To

Container vessels / Fleet asset classes transitioning between dual-fuel and conventional setups.

5
UNFCCC — what is it and its relevance to shipping?
GHG
15-Second Answer15-Second Answer (Teal Practice-Block) The UNFCCC is the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change, the ultimate global treaty driving greenhouse gas reductions. While it sets overarching climate targets like the Paris Agreement, it defers the direct regulation of international maritime emissions to the IMO via MARPOL Annex VI.
60-Second Answer60-Second Answer (Teal Practice-Block) The UNFCCC establishes the global political and legal mandate for addressing climate change, famously producing the Kyoto Protocol and the Paris Agreement to limit global warming to well agriculture-and-industry targets. However, because international shipping operates across global borders, the UNFCCC explicitly delegates the responsibility of setting and enforcing maritime greenhouse gas reduction strategies to the International Maritime Organization (IMO). For a Chief Engineer, this means the high-level goals decided at UNFCCC COP summits directly dictate the stringency, timeline, and aggressive tightening of the IMO GHG Strategy, which manifests onboard as mandatory EEXI, CII, and FuelEU compliance metrics.

Governing Instrument and Institutional Hierarchy

The UNFCCC (established in 1992) acts as the parent global treaty framework. Under Article 2.2 of the Kyoto Protocol, and reinforced by the Paris Agreement (2015), the mandate to control greenhouse gases from international shipping was formally passed to the IMO's Marine Environment Protection Committee (MEPC).

Key Requirements & Working Principle

  • Global Carbon Budgets: The UNFCCC sets the absolute timeline for global net-zero targets. The IMO translates these into marine-specific metrics to prevent carbon leakage across international waters.
  • The Common But Differentiated Responsibilities (CBDR) Dilemma: A core UNFCCC principle is CBDR (developed nations move faster). However, the IMO operates on the principle of No More Favourable Treatment (NMFT), meaning maritime regulations apply equally to all vessels regardless of flag.
  • Regulatory Transmission: The political pressure from UNFCCC COP meetings forces the IMO to continuously accelerate its decarbonisation strategy targets (e.g., reaching net-zero GHG emissions by or around 2050).

Practical Application for the Chief Engineer

While a Chief Engineer does not file reports directly to the UNFCCC, its macro-level targets directly shape shipboard operations. The resulting IMO updates require the CE to continuously optimize energy efficiency profiles, manage complex alternative fuels, and face severe commercial and legal penalties if the vessel's operational index fails to meet the descending carbon caps.

Examiner Angle — Simon

Simon evaluates whether you understand the geopolitical source of maritime environmental laws. He will ask you why the IMO is the body making the rules rather than the UN climate summit itself, testing your understanding of institutional delegation.

Chief Engineer Relevance

The political decisions made at the UNFCCC level drive the rapid introduction of regional market-based measures (like the EU Emissions Trading System - EU ETS) that require meticulous fuel and emission data logging by the ship’s command team.

Key Numbers / Regs to Remember

  • 1.5°C: The global temperature rise limit target established under the Paris Agreement that dictates current IMO decarbonisation curves.
  • Net-Zero by or around 2050: The aligned target of the 2023 IMO GHG Strategy driven by UNFCCC pressure.

Applies To

All commercial vessels engaged in international trade globally.

  • A: No, sir. The Paris Agreement applies to nations. A ship demonstrates compliance with international climate goals by satisfying MARPOL Annex VI Regulations, which represent the IMO's enforcement of those high-level climate targets.
