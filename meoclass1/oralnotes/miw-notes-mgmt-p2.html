Topic 7

The IMO Legislative Framework & Amendment Mechanisms

IMO Governance Uday pp. 26–33 Examiner: Nair ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: The quorum (⅓ present) and adoption (⅔ majority) figures below are correctly aligned with SOLAS Article VIII and MARPOL Article 16. The "1/3–2/3–50%" blocking-minority rule is an accurate exam mnemonic summarising those articles, not a word-for-word quotation — for written answers always cite SOLAS Article VIII / MARPOL Article 16 directly rather than quoting the mnemonic as convention text.

📖 Definition

This topic establishes the structural framework of maritime legislation — the legal hierarchy that governs a Chief Engineer's statutory liabilities — and explains how international instruments are proposed, adopted, and systematically amended to keep pace with evolving technology and safety needs.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)

  • A uniform legislative framework prevents conflicting unilateral regional rules — every flag applies the same technical baseline.
  • Understanding how a resolution becomes binding law is critical to avoiding PSC detentions, contractual delays, or criminal liability for non-compliance.
  • Knowing municipal legal hierarchy (Acts → Rules → Regulations) explains why a flag state notice, not the IMO resolution itself, is what actually binds the vessel.

📅 Historical Background

Historically, amendments required "explicit acceptance" — a fixed percentage (typically two-thirds) of contracting states had to formally ratify before entry into force, a process that could take up to a decade. To fix this dangerous lag, IMO introduced Tacit Acceptance through convention revisions in the early 1970s, flipping the legal default from "rejected unless explicitly accepted" to "accepted unless explicitly objected to."

⚖ Regulatory References
IMO Convention, Part XIFunctions and interactions of the principal committees

🏛 Hierarchy of IMO Instruments

CONVENTIONS → Binding primary treaties (SOLAS, MARPOL, STCW) PROTOCOLS → Major structural amendments to an existing convention CODES → Mandatory technical rules once referenced by a convention chapter (ISM, IGF, ISPS) RESOLUTIONS → Formal committee-approved mandates (Assembly / MSC / MEPC) CIRCULARS → Interpretative guidance and clarifications (non-mandatory)

🔢 Statutory Nomenclature

  • Resolution format — Body.Number(Session): e.g. MSC.374(93) = 374th resolution of the Maritime Safety Committee, adopted at its 93rd session.
  • Circular format — Body.Category/Circ.Number: e.g. MSC.1/Circ.1501 = sub-category 1 under MSC, informational circular #1501.

🎯 Oral Q&A

Q1: Explain the fundamental difference between an IMO Resolution and an IMO Circular, and how each affects your day-to-day management.
A Resolution is a formal legislative instrument adopted by the Assembly, MSC, or MEPC to adopt conventions, protocols, or amendments; once it passes tacit acceptance and enters into force it becomes a mandatory international requirement enforced through domestic flag-state law. A Circular is an informational document issued by a committee or sub-committee providing technical clarification or uniform interpretation — generally non-mandatory, but widely used by PSC officers and ROs as the definitive technical benchmark during surveys. As Chief Engineer, I treat resolutions as mandatory structural/operational targets and circulars as the practical guide for meeting them.
Q2: What are the precise legal conditions for a MARPOL amendment to be rejected via tacit acceptance?
A valid blocking minority of contracting governments must file formal written objections with the IMO Secretary-General within the tacit-acceptance window (minimum 10 months for MARPOL). Rejection requires either (1) at least one-third of all contracting governments to object, or (2) objecting states whose combined merchant fleets represent not less than 50% of world gross merchant shipping tonnage. If neither threshold is met, the amendment is deemed accepted and enters into force exactly 6 months later.
Q3: Decode MSC.1/Circ.1501.
"MSC.1/" identifies the originating body (Maritime Safety Committee) and its technical sub-category; "Circ." identifies the instrument as an informational/interpretative circular rather than a mandatory resolution; "1501" is the unique chronological index number. Operationally it provides unified global interpretation of a safety matter which shipboard management should align with for flag-state and PSC expectations.

🧠 Memory Box

  • 6-month buffer — fixed for both SOLAS and MARPOL between acceptance and entry into force.
  • Acts → Rules → Regulations — Parliament → ministerial decree → statutory technical directive.

Failure to Implement — PSC Consequence

Any failure to implement an adopted amendment after its formal entry-into-force date is a high-risk PSC detention ground. Claiming ignorance of a tacitly accepted amendment, or a delay in receiving a company circular, is not accepted as a valid defense by boarding officers.

Municipal Transposition

International conventions are not self-executing — they must be transposed into domestic legislation. In India, the Merchant Shipping Act provides the constitutional/legal authority; rules and regulations issued under DGS authority give the technical detail that flag-state surveyors and PSC actually enforce.

Topic 8

Global Maritime Sustainability — GESAMP & the UN SDGs

Environmental Governance Uday pp. 34–39 Examiner: Senthil Medium Frequency
🔎 Verification note: GESAMP's 1969 founding and multi-agency sponsorship are accurately described. The SDG mapping is conceptually sound for oral answers, but the SDGs are UN policy goals, not MARPOL/SOLAS regulations — wording below uses "supports/aligns with" rather than "mandates/codifies" for SDG items. The 1.2% women-seafarer figure and 45.8% increase are industry statistics from workforce studies, not regulatory values.

📖 Definition

This topic covers the Group of Experts on the Scientific Aspects of Marine Environmental Protection (GESAMP) and the operational integration of the UN 2030 Agenda's 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) into the maritime industry — how scientific risk assessment and UN policy goals translate into shipboard engineering management and culture.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)

  • GESAMP's scientific evaluations underpin the discharge limits and chemical carriage classifications the IMO writes into MARPOL Annex II.
  • The UN SDGs give charterers, financiers, and PSC an ESG lens on top of pure technical compliance — increasingly relevant to vetting and inspection.
  • SDG-aligned practices (waste reduction, workforce inclusion, energy efficiency) are audited through existing certification pathways (MLC, MARPOL Annex V, HSSC), not a separate SDG inspection regime.

📅 Historical Background

1969 → GESAMP established as a multidisciplinary, independent scientific advisory body to the UN agency cluster on marine pollution 2015 → UN 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development adopted by all 193 member states; 17 SDGs established 2024–2029 → IMO Strategic Plan formally aligns IMO's roadmap with the UN SDGs
⚖ Regulatory References

MARPOL Annex II, Appendix IUses the GESAMP Hazard Profile to classify harmful liquid substances carried in bulk

🏛 GESAMP Institutional Architecture

10 UN SPONSOR AGENCIES (IMO, UN, FAO, and others) ↓ GESAMP MAIN BODY — independent experts ↓ TECHNICAL WORKING GROUPS — e.g. WG 1: Evaluation of Hazards of Harmful Substances Carried by Ships ↓ GESAMP HAZARD PROFILE (GHP) — bioaccumulation, aquatic toxicity, human-health hazard, physical properties

🌍 SDGs Most Relevant to Engine Room Operations

  • SDG 5 (Gender Equality): supports recruitment, visibility and professional advancement of women at sea.
  • SDG 6 (Clean Water & Sanitation): aligns with MARPOL Annex IV controls on sewage and graywater discharge.
  • SDG 7 (Clean Energy): supports alternative-fuel research, waste-heat recovery, and machinery efficiency.
  • SDG 8 (Decent Work): aligns with MLC 2006 seafarer welfare and safe working conditions.
  • SDG 12 (Responsible Consumption): supports MARPOL Annex V waste segregation and Hong Kong Convention recycling practices.
  • SDG 13 (Climate Action): aligns with the IMO GHG Strategy via EEDI, EEXI, and CII.
  • SDG 14 (Life Below Water): supports Ballast Water Management, oil-spill prevention, underwater-noise mitigation.

Global data indicates women make up approximately 1.2% of the global seafarer workforce — an industry statistic, not a regulatory figure — reflecting an estimated 45.8% increase since 2015, driven by targeted training, visibility, and recognition programs.

CE Oral Tip — Senthil Sir: Senthil probes whether candidates conflate SDGs with binding regulation. Always frame SDG answers as "supports/aligns with" a named convention (MLC, MARPOL Annex V, the GHG Strategy) rather than claiming the SDG itself is enforceable law.

🎯 Oral Q&A

Q1: What is the role of GESAMP, and how do its findings impact shipboard operations?
GESAMP is an independent scientific advisory body sponsored by 10 UN agencies, including the IMO, conducting scientific risk assessments for marine environmental protection. Its GESAMP Hazard Profile classifies bulk liquid chemicals by environmental and human-health risk, which in turn dictates the ship type required under the IBC Code, tank-stripping limits, and discharge criteria the engineering crew must follow.
Q2: How can a Chief Engineer directly support SDG 5 (Gender Equality) on board?
By enforcing zero tolerance for harassment or bias, assigning duties, training and evaluations strictly by capability and rank, and verifying accommodation and PPE are properly configured for a mixed-gender crew — expanding the talent pool and strengthening overall shipboard safety culture.
Q3: Name three SDGs that intersect with engine room operations and their technical application.
SDG 7 (Clean Energy) — waste-heat recovery and machinery optimization to cut fuel consumption. SDG 12 (Responsible Consumption) — waste segregation and Garbage Record Book discipline. SDG 13 (Climate Action) — CII monitoring and correct operation of fuel and exhaust systems.

Decarbonisation Pathways & the IMO Net-Zero Framework (MEPC-83)

GHG / Decarbonisation Uday pp. 40–45 Examiner: Simon ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note — read before studying: MEPC-83 (April 2025) approved in principle a global GHG pricing framework and Net-Zero Fund, to be integrated as new MARPOL Annex VI Chapter 5 — this is correct. However, the fixed dollar tier prices (Tier 1 $300/t, Tier 2 $100/t) and the year-by-year Z-factor percentages below are draft/indicative values from the approved-in-principle framework, not final adopted statutory figures. MEPC ES.2 (October 2025) adjourned without final agreement and formal adoption has been rescheduled to November 2026. Treat every number in this topic as illustrative of the tier structure and reduction trend, not as law to quote in a written answer without a "subject to formal adoption" qualifier.

📖 Definition

This topic covers the regulatory mechanisms, market-based instruments, and mid-term technical frameworks approved in principle by MEPC-83 to decarbonise shipping — the GHG pricing concept, the IMO Net-Zero Fund, and extended Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) reduction trajectories.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)

  • Once adopted, non-compliance with GHG intensity targets converts CII from a reporting exercise into a direct financial penalty per tonne of CO₂e.
  • Fuel procurement, engine-load planning, and SEEMP Part III strategy must anticipate a steepening reduction curve through 2030.
  • DCS data accuracy becomes financially material — reporting errors can translate directly into penalty exposure once the framework is adopted.

📅 Historical Background

2018 → Initial IMO GHG Strategy: 50% total reduction by 2050 vs 2008 baseline 2023 → Revised Strategy (Resolution MEPC.385(80)): net-zero by/around 2050, indicative 2030/2040 checkpoints Apr 2025 → MEPC-83 approves in principle the GHG pricing mechanism, Net-Zero Fund, and extended CII trajectory Oct 2025 → MEPC ES.2 adjourns without final agreement Nov 2026 → Formal adoption rescheduled
⚖ Regulatory References
MARPOL Annex VI, Ch.4Carbon intensity of international shipping — currently in force (CII/EEXI)
Resolution MEPC.385(80)2023 IMO Strategy on Reduction of GHG Emissions from Ships
MARPOL Annex VI, Ch.5 (draft)IMO Net-Zero Framework — approved in principle at MEPC-83, formal adoption pending (rescheduled Nov 2026)

💰 GHG Pricing Concept (Illustrative — Not Yet Final Law)

TOTAL VESSEL EMISSIONS ↓ Exceeds Direct Target ──► Higher-priced Tier (draft ~$300/t CO2e, illustrative) Exceeds Base Target only ─► Lower-priced Tier (draft ~$100/t CO2e, illustrative) ↓ IMO NET-ZERO FUND ──► fuel subsidisation for ZNZ fuels + just-transition support for developing states/SIDS

Under the concept as approved in principle, a higher-priced tier applies to larger compliance deficits and a lower-priced tier to smaller deficits, with over-compliant vessels earning tradeable surplus units. Exact US-dollar values remain subject to final adoption — for oral exams, describe the tier structure and the Fund's dual purpose (fuel subsidy + just transition) rather than quoting fixed figures as settled statutory rates.

🎯 Oral Q&A

Q1: Explain the GHG pricing concept discussed at MEPC-83.
MEPC-83 approved in principle a market-based GHG pricing framework applying financial penalties tied to a vessel's emissions relative to its targets, structured as a higher-priced tier for larger deficits and a lower-priced tier for smaller deficits, with over-compliant vessels earning tradeable surplus units. All collected revenue would flow into the IMO Net-Zero Fund to subsidise zero/near-zero fuels and support developing states. Exact dollar values remain subject to formal adoption, rescheduled for November 2026.
Q2: What CII reduction trend was discussed at MEPC-83 for 2027–2030?
MEPC-83 discussed extending the mandatory Z-factor reduction trajectory beyond the confirmed 2026 baseline (11.0%) through progressively steeper annual steps toward 2030, broadly in the range of low-teens rising to low-twenties percent. These are draft/indicative figures — the confirmed statutory factors are set only through the formal Annex VI amendment process.
Q3: How would an Onboard Carbon Capture and Storage (OCCS) system affect a vessel's carbon profile under the proposed framework?
Under the draft accounting approach discussed at MEPC-83, verified captured and stored CO₂ from a type-approved OCCS unit could be deducted from the vessel's gross reported emissions in the IMO Data Collection System, reducing the reported operational CII and helping the vessel meet steeper future reduction targets — subject to the framework's formal adoption.

Topic 10

MARPOL Annex I — Statutory Certification, Record-Keeping & Oily Waste Management

MARPOL Annex I Uday pp. 46–50 Examiner: Nair ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: Two corrections applied to the original draft: (1) the Special Areas list was missing Southern South African waters, and "Oman area" is formally "Oman area of the Arabian Sea" — both added below. (2) The original draft stated cargo-tank-washing discharge must stay "below 34 ppm" — MARPOL Annex I Regulation 34 does not use a fixed ppm threshold for tanker ODMCS operations; it uses instantaneous-rate (≤30 L/NM) and total-quantity (≤1/30,000 of cargo) limits. 15 ppm is the separate machinery-bilge discharge limit. The 34 ppm figure has been removed and replaced with the correct rate/quantity criteria.

📖 Definition

This topic covers the statutory framework for preventing oil pollution under MARPOL Annex I — international certification (IOPP), the Oil Record Book (Parts I & II), the physics of the Load on Top (LOT) procedure, and the structural/volumetric design criteria for slop tanks.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)

  • Oil pollution carries some of the most severe consequences in shipping — criminal liability for engineering officers, heavy fines, and immediate detention.
  • Falsified ORB entries or "magic pipe" bypasses are treated as serious criminal offences in most jurisdictions.
  • Every entry the CE signs in the ORB must reconcile with physical tank soundings — discrepancies are read by PSC as evidence of concealment.

📅 Historical Background

1973 → MARPOL adopted but insufficient ratification to enter into force 1976–77 → Series of severe tanker accidents drives revision 1978 → Protocol of 1978 modifies the convention 2 Oct 1983 → MARPOL 73/78 (with Annex I) enters into force

📜 Statutory Certification & Record-Keeping

  • IOPP Certificate: mandatory for oil tankers ≥150 gross tonnage and all other ships ≥400 gross tonnage. Supplement Form A (non-tankers) / Form B (tankers).
  • Oil Record Book: retained onboard ≥3 years from the date of the last entry.
  • ORB Part I (Machinery Space, all ships ≥400 GT, tankers ≥150 GT): Codes A–I covering bunkering, sludge disposal, bilge discharge, and filtering-equipment condition.
  • ORB Part II (Cargo/Ballast, tankers ≥150 GT): Codes A–R covering loading, COW, ballasting, slop discharge, and ODMCS condition.

⚗ The Physics of Load on Top (LOT)

1. TANK WASHINGS → wash water routed to slop tank 2. GRAVITY SEPARATION → oil floats, water settles 3. DECANTING → clean bottom water discharged via ODMCS; oil residue retained 4. CO-MINGLED LOADING → next cargo loaded on top of retained residue

📏 Tanker Discharge Criteria — Regulation 34 (Corrected)

  • Outside a designated Special Area
  • More than 50 nautical miles from the nearest land
  • Vessel underway, en route, at ≥7 knots
  • Instantaneous rate of discharge ≤ 30 litres per nautical mile
  • Total oil discharged ≤ 1/30,000 of the total quantity of the particular cargo from which the residue formed
  • ODMCS and slop tank arrangement operational — oil content and discharge rate are governed by these rate/quantity limits monitored by the ODMCS, not by a fixed ppm figure (that 15 ppm limit applies to machinery-space bilge discharge under Reg. 15, a separate regime)

CE Oral Tip — Nair Sir: Never quote a fixed ppm figure for tanker cargo-washing discharge — Nair specifically tests for this confusion. State the rate (30 L/NM) and quantity (1/30,000) limits, and reserve 15 ppm strictly for machinery-space bilge water under Regulation 15.

🎯 Oral Q&A

Q1: Explain the mandatory discharge parameters for an oil tanker discharging cargo tank washings under Regulation 34.
The vessel must be outside a designated Special Area, more than 50 NM from the nearest land, underway en route, with the instantaneous discharge rate not exceeding 30 L/NM, and the total oil discharged not exceeding 1/30,000 of the cargo quantity from which the residue formed, with an operational ODMCS and slop tank arrangement.
Q2: Under what code must a routine bilge water transfer and discharge be recorded in the Oil Record Book Part I?
Internal transfer of bilge water from the bilge wells to the holding tank is logged under Code (D); the subsequent processing and discharge via the 15 ppm oily water separator is logged separately, recording start/stop times, the processing tank, and confirmation that the filtering equipment was operational below the 15 ppm alarm threshold.
Q3: What are the slop tank capacity rules for a tanker fitted with Segregated Ballast Tanks (SBT)?
The baseline 3% requirement drops to 2% for SBT-fitted tankers, and further to 1.5% if the tank-washing system also relies on eductor water supply rather than cargo pumps.

Why 34 ppm was wrong

MARPOL Annex I distinguishes two separate discharge regimes: machinery-space bilge water (Regulation 15, 15 ppm limit, applies to all ships) and tanker cargo-tank washings/oily mixtures from slop tanks (Regulation 34, rate- and quantity-based limits, applies to oil tankers). Conflating the two — quoting "34 ppm" — mixes a machinery-space concentration limit with a cargo-operations rate limit that does not use ppm at all in the regulation text.
