P.81
Great Circle Navigation
Shortest spherical path · curved on Mercator · vs Rhumb Line · composite sailing
  • Definition: Shortest possible distance between two points on a sphere.
  • On Mercator chart: Appears as a curved arc — not a straight line.
  • Rhumb Line: Constant heading, straight on Mercator, longer distance.
  • Great circle: Shorter but requires continuous course adjustment.
  • Composite sailing: Limits poleward extent — used to avoid ice/harsh weather at high latitudes.
⚡ Simon Sir typically asks

"What is great circle navigation?" — Shortest route on a sphere, curved on Mercator, continuous course changes. When NOT to use: polar/ice regions, short voyages (negligible saving), shallow water. Composite great circle limits the latitude reached.

⚖ Regulatory References
SOLAS V / Reg 34"Regulation 34 — Safe navigation and avoidance of dangerous situations" — voyage planning must consider route type, including great circle options
SOLAS V / Reg 27"Regulation 27 — Nautical charts and publications" — adequate and up-to-date charts required to cover the intended voyage

P.86
PSC Inspection Types & Clear Grounds
Initial → More Detailed → Expanded → CIC · 6 clear grounds triggers

4 PSC Inspection Types

  1. Initial: Baseline walk-through — certificates, general condition, cleanliness.
  2. More Detailed: Triggered by clear grounds — deep equipment verification.
  3. Expanded: High-risk vessel categories by age, type, or flag performance.
  4. CIC (Concentrated Inspection Campaign): Regional campaign on specific system — 3-month window, announced annually by MOUs.

6 Clear Grounds Triggers

  • Structural issues, severe hull corrosion, compromised watertight integrity.
  • Critical safety or pollution equipment missing or non-operational.
  • Invalid, missing, or expired seafarer certificates.
  • Bridge or E/R crew unable to communicate effectively.
  • Master or crew unfamiliar with essential shipboard procedures.
  • Third-party report or crew complaint alleging substandard conditions.
⚡ Simon Sir typically asks

"What triggers a more detailed PSC inspection?" — Any of the 6 clear grounds. CIC — MOU announces annual focus area. Maersk vessels receive CIC briefings before port calls. Prepare by ensuring equipment in the focus area is operational and records are complete.

⚖ Regulatory References
IMO Res. A.1206(34)"Procedures for Port State Control, 2025" (supersedes A.1185(33), effective 1 Jan 2026) — Chapter 2 defines inspection types (Initial/More Detailed/Expanded/CIC) and clear grounds criteria
IOMOU / Paris MOU"Indian Ocean MOU / Paris MOU — Concentrated Inspection Campaign announcements" — annual campaign topics published on each MOU's website

PSC Suspension & Post-Detention — 4 Steps
Flag/RO notified · Superintendent SMC audit · separate RO surveyor · clear before sailing

Suspension Protocol

PSCO logs all detainable deficiencies found up to point of suspension
▼
Formally notify Flag Administration and acting RO
▼
Vessel frozen in port — Flag/RO commits to full independent survey before reinspection

4 Mandatory Steps to Clear Detention

  1. Company deploys Superintendent — full structural inspection + internal SMC audit.
  2. Full survey by Classification surveyor from RO different from the last annual survey.

SOLAS Liferaft Packs — A vs B
Pack A = extended ocean · Pack B = short/coastal · Pack A has extra provisions
  • Pack A: Extended survival on international ocean voyages. Contains everything in Pack B plus: extra drinking water, survival food, additional hull repair kits, extra flares, expanded first aid kits.
  • Pack B: Scaled-down inventory for short international voyages or domestic coastal routes.

Your Maersk container vessel on international voyages carries Pack A liferafts.

⚡ Simon Sir typically asks

"What is the difference between SOLAS Pack A and Pack B?" — Pack A = ocean (more equipment). Pack B = short/coastal (reduced). He may ask specific Pack A extras: extra drinking water, survival food, additional flares, expanded first aid.

Hull Steel Grades — Mild & High-Tensile
A/B/D/E mild · AH/DH/EH 32/36/40 · keel/sheer strake HTS zones
  • Mild Steel: Impact toughness classes A, B, D, E — A = no impact test; B/D/E = increasing toughness at lower temperatures.
  • High-Tensile Steel (HTS): Strength parameters 32, 36, 40 kgf/mm² yield strength. Notations: AH32/36/40, DH32/36/40, EH32/36/40.
  • High-stress zones (AH36/DH36/EH36): keel strake, garboard strake, bilge strake, sheer strake, deck stringers.
  • Anchors and chain links: forged/cast steel.
⚡ Simon Sir typically asks

"What is the difference between grade A and grade E steel?" — Both mild steel, but Grade E has the highest notch toughness — used in polar/arctic applications. Grade A has no impact test. AH36 is the most common HTS grade on container vessel hulls. HTS allows thinner plating at same strength — reduces hull weight.

⚖ Regulatory References
IACS UR W11"IACS UR W11 — Normal and Higher Strength Hull Structural Steels" — grade definitions (A/B/D/E mild, AH/DH/EH high-tensile) and impact toughness testing requirements
SOLAS II-1 / Reg 3-2"Regulation 3-2 — Steel and other materials" — Administration approval requirements for hull structural steel used in construction

P.80
Life Cycle Assessment — Well-to-Wake Framework
WTW = WTT + TTW · LNG methane slip · FuelEU Maritime
Well-to-Wake (WTW) = WTT + TTW
WTT (Well-to-Tank): GHG from feedstock → refining → transport → bunker tank TTW (Tank-to-Wake): GHG from onboard combustion → exhaust

LCA Implications for Alternative Fuels

  • LNG: Low TTW CO₂ but methane slip (CH₄, GWP 27–30) can negate the advantage.
  • Ammonia: Zero TTW CO₂ but N₂O combustion byproduct (GWP 273); grey ammonia production is energy-intensive.
  • Green H₂: Zero WTW if from renewable electrolysis — but expensive.
  • FAME: Low WTW from waste oils; higher if from food crops (land use change).
⚡ Simon Sir typically asks

"What is Well-to-Wake?" — WTW = WTT + TTW. "Why isn't LNG zero-carbon?" — Methane slip — unburned CH₄ escapes, GWP 27–30. FuelEU Maritime (from 2025) uses WTW GHG intensity for compliance — relevant on Maersk vessels calling EU ports.

⚖ Regulatory References
FuelEU Maritime / Reg 2023/1805"Regulation (EU) 2023/1805 on the use of renewable and low-carbon fuels in maritime transport (FuelEU Maritime)" — well-to-wake GHG intensity limits phased in from 1 January 2025
IMO 2023 GHG Strategy"2023 IMO Strategy on Reduction of GHG Emissions from Ships" — life-cycle (well-to-wake) GHG intensity approach endorsed for future guidelines/regulation
P.96
Propeller Law & Slow Steaming
Power ∝ RPM³ · 80% RPM = 51.2% power · 10% RPM cut = ~27% power reduction
Propeller Law — Power vs RPM
Power ∝ RPM³ At 80% RPM: Power = (0.8)³ = 51.2% of MCR At 90% RPM: Power = (0.9)³ = 72.9% of MCR 10% RPM reduction → power drops by approx. 27%

Slow steaming is the most effective CII improvement measure operationally available to the CE. 10% RPM reduction = ~27% power drop = proportional fuel and CO₂ savings.

⚡ Simon Sir typically asks

"What is the Propeller Law?" — Power ∝ RPM³. He will ask: "What operational measures improve CII?" — Slow steaming, trim optimisation, weather routing, hull cleaning, cargo planning. As CE, slow steaming through RPM reduction is your primary lever.

⚖ Regulatory References
MARPOL VI / Reg 26"Regulation 26 — Ship Energy Efficiency Management Plan (SEEMP)" — Part I speed/route optimisation measures; Part III links SEEMP to CII compliance planning
MARPOL VI / Reg 28"Regulation 28 — Operational Carbon Intensity" — Annual Efficiency Ratio (AER) = CO₂ emitted ÷ (DWT × distance travelled); slow steaming reduces the CO₂ numerator

  • Seafarer (MLC 2006 Art. II): Any person employed, engaged, or working in any operational capacity aboard a ship going to sea — includes the Master.

MACN & IACS Regulatory Instruments — UR / UI / PR

MACN — Maritime Anti-Corruption Network

  • Global network established 2011, HQ in Copenhagen — maritime companies combating corruption in shipping.
  • Operates a secure anonymous reporting system — seafarers and operators report corruption/extortion globally.

IACS Regulatory Instruments

  • UR (Unified Requirements): Minimum technical standards for design, construction, and survey — all IACS members must adopt.

IWIS — In-Water Survey Preparation in Drydock
10-step checklist · cathodic protection · 5-yr paint · bead welds · propeller numbering

10 IWIS Preparation Steps (in drydock)

  1. Fit approved cathodic protection system.
  2. Paint hull with coating designed to last 5 years.
  3. Paint colour provides optimal contrast with underwater environment (assists diver visibility).
  4. Shell openings with gratings: secure hinged openings.
  5. All suction, discharge, and shell openings marked by bead welding for easy diver identification.
  6. All transverse and longitudinal butt welds clearly marked.
  7. Propeller blades numbered (normally pre-marked).
  8. Hull areas inaccessible at LWL during IWIS given particular attention.
  9. Documentation to diver: shell expansion plan, drain plug plan, rudder/stern frame drawings.
  10. Agreed method for blanking shell openings established for diver safety.

Note: propeller blade count and engine unit count should NOT be integral multiples — avoids resonance.

⚡ Simon Sir typically asks

"What is IWIS and how do you prepare for it?" — In-Water International Survey — underwater hull inspection in lieu of drydocking (requires BIS class notation). He expects the 10-step drydock preparation checklist. "What is the advantage of IWIS?" — Avoids drydocking costs and off-hire time.

⚖ Regulatory References
IACS UR Z17"IACS UR Z17 — Procedural Requirement for In-Water Survey" — conditions under which an in-water survey may substitute for drydocking; requires BIS (Bottom Inspection in lieu of Survey) class notation — verify exact UR designation/number

Multiple Load Lines
One valid at a time · non-active concealed · logbook entry · assignment booklet

Used when a vessel needs to operate with greater freeboard allowance (e.g., draft restrictions at specific ports, low-density cargo).

  • At any time only one load line is valid.
  • Non-active load lines painted the same colour as the ship's hull to conceal them.
  • Master must make an entry in the official logbook whenever a load line is changed.
  • Procedure for changing load lines must be in the vessel's Environmental Management System (EMS).

Procedure to Obtain Multiple Load Lines

  1. Owner sends request to Administration.
  2. RO carries out calculations, performs surveys, and issues full-term certificate.
  3. Freeboard assignment and approved copies sent to Administration.
  4. Administration issues an assignment booklet.
  5. Each load line change entered in assignment booklet — endorsed at annual and renewal surveys.
⚡ Simon Sir typically asks

"What are multiple load lines?" — Only ONE active at a time; non-active lines concealed (painted hull colour); Master logs every change. He may ask about the alternate tonnage certificate — issued by Administration showing both original and reduced tonnage.

⚖ Regulatory References
ILLC 1966 / Annex I"Annex I — Regulations for Determining Load Lines" (International Convention on Load Lines, 1966) — permits assignment of multiple load lines with specified marking/concealment conditions
LL Protocol 1988"Protocol of 1988 relating to the International Convention on Load Lines, 1966" — aligns load line survey/certification cycle with the Harmonised System of Survey and Certification (HSSC)

Sustainable Development

Meeting present needs without compromising future generations. Guided by UN SDG 2030 Agenda (17 goals). IMO aligns GHG strategy with UN SDGs — net-zero shipping by or around 2050.

Incinerator Regulations (MARPOL VI / Reg 16)

Incinerator Temperature Requirements
Continuous feed: flue gas temperature ≥ 850°C Batch-loaded: must reach 600°C within 5 minutes Onboard incineration outside approved incinerator: prohibited Exception: sewage sludge and oil sludge may be burned in auxiliary boilers/power plants when NOT in port
