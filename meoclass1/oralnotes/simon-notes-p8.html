SUA Convention — History, Scope & Jurisdiction (Pages 175–176)
P.176ISPSSOLAS

Full Name & Background

Convention for the Suppression of Unlawful Acts Against the Safety of Maritime Navigation (SUA). Triggered by the hijacking of the Italian cruise ship Achille Lauro on 7 October 1985 near Alexandria, Egypt — resulting in the murder of a passenger.

  • IMO was mandated by member states to develop a framework protecting ships from terrorist acts.
  • Resolutions and MSC circulars were deemed insufficient → full convention adopted in Rome, March 1988.

Operational Timeline

InstrumentAdoptedEIF
Original SUA ConventionMarch 1988March 1992
Fixed Platforms ProtocolMarch 1988March 1992
2005 SUA Protocol2005July 2, 2010

Scope — Unlawful Acts Under SUA

  • Forcible seizure or hijacking of a vessel.
  • Acts of violence against any person onboard.
  • Planting devices likely to damage or destroy the vessel, cargo, or equipment.
  • Destroying or tampering with maritime navigational facilities.
  • Communicating intentionally false information endangering the vessel.
  • Extradite or Prosecute: Contracting governments must either extradite offenders or prosecute them domestically.

Jurisdictional Limits

  • Territorial waters (≤ 12 NM): National domestic laws apply — coastal state has full sovereign authority.
  • International waters (> 12 NM): SUA jurisdiction governs. No state may board a foreign vessel without flag state permission.
  • Vessel exemptions: Warships, state-owned vessels (naval/customs/police), vessels fully withdrawn from commercial navigation.
⚖ Regulatory References
SUA Convention 1988Convention for Suppression of Unlawful Acts — base maritime security framework
SUA Protocol 2005Expanded SUA — WMD transport, use of ships as weapons, knowingly transporting terrorists
SOLAS XI-2Special measures to enhance maritime security — ISPS Code, continuous synopsis record
Simon Sir typically asks: "What is the SUA Convention and what triggered it?" — Achille Lauro 1985 → Rome 1988 adoption. Key point: applies in international waters only; flag state permission required for boarding. The 2005 Protocol added WMD transport and using ships as weapons — new criminal offences under Article 3 bis.

SUA — Boarding Protocol, Offender Handling & 2005 Protocol (Pages 177–180)
P.179ISPSSOLAS

Protocols for Onboard Offenders

  • Master may deliver the suspect to competent authorities of any contracting state at the vessel's next port (the Receiving State).
  • Master must notify the receiving state before entering territorial sea.
  • Master must preserve all physical evidence and hand it to the receiving state.
  • In India: Indian Coast Guard is authorised to take custody on behalf of the government.

High-Seas Boarding Protocol

Requesting State has reasonable suspicion │ ▼ Request sent to Flag State to confirm nationality │ ▼ Nationality confirmed → Request authorisation to board │ ┌──┴──┐ ▼ ▼ Authorised Denied │ │ ▼ ▼ Board, search, Abort / Diplomatic question, inspect dispute channels

Flag State's Four Response Options

  • Option I: Authorise boarding and search.
  • Option II: Formally decline.
  • Option III: Permit boarding conditional on own law enforcement officers being present.
  • Option IV: Conduct a joint boarding operation.

2005 SUA Protocol — New Offences (Article 3 bis)

  • Using ships as weapons/platforms to cause death, injury, or intimidate governments.
  • Transporting Weapons of Mass Destruction (WMD) — nuclear, biological, chemical, explosive.
  • Knowingly transporting a person who has committed a SUA offence.

4-Hour Urgent Boarding Rule (2005 Protocol)

A state party may notify the IMO Secretary-General that if an urgent boarding request receives no response within 4 hours, the requesting state may proceed with stopping, boarding, and searching the vessel.

Extradition Principles

  • Dual criminality: Act must be criminal under both states' law for extradition to apply.
  • Extradition may be denied if politically motivated or based on persecution of race/religion/nationality.
⚖ Regulatory References
SUA Convention 1988 / Art. 7–10Master's duties, custody transfer, extradition, and prosecution obligations
SUA Protocol 2005 / Art. 3bisNew offences — WMD transport, ships as weapons, transporting terrorists
UNCLOS Art. 110Right of visit — warships may board foreign ships on the high seas under specified circumstances
Simon Sir typically asks: "What are the Flag State's options when a boarding request is received under SUA?" — Give all four options clearly. The 4-hour rule under the 2005 Protocol is a hot exam question. Know Article 3 bis for WMD transport — this expanded SUA beyond basic hijacking to counter modern terrorism.

London Dumping Convention (LC-72) & 1996 Protocol — Comparison (Pages 181–186)
P.183MARPOLEnvironmental

Overview

  • Convention Name: Convention on Prevention of Marine Pollution by Dumping of Wastes and Other Matter.
  • LC-72: Adopted 1972; EIF August 1975.
  • 1996 Protocol: Adopted 1996; EIF March 2006 — replaces the original convention.

Definition of Dumping

  • IS Dumping: Deliberate disposal at sea of waste from vessels, aircraft, platforms, or deliberate disposal of vessels/platforms themselves.
  • NOT Dumping: Operational discharges from vessels; pipeline discharges; waste to rivers; placement of matter for a purpose other than disposal. Activities incidental to normal operation of vessels are not considered dumping.
  • Exemptions: Safety of life or property; force majeure; emergency situations.

Convention vs 1996 Protocol — Key Comparison

ElementLC-72 (Original)1996 Protocol
ApproachBlack-list & Grey-list — permitted everything except listed itemsReverse List — prohibits ALL dumping except explicitly listed waste
Annex 1Black-list: complete prohibition of hazardous materialsReverse List: permitted materials subject to stringent permits
Annex 2Grey-list: special permit requiredWaste Assessment Framework
Annex 3Technical criteria for permitsEvaluation criteria
Annex 4Not presentMarine geo-engineering regulation

1996 Protocol — Permitted Reverse List (Annex 1)

  • Dredged material; sewage sludge; fish waste; inert inorganic geological material; mining waste; organic material; CO₂ streams from carbon capture processes.

1996 Protocol Amendments

  • 2006 Carbon Sequestration: Allows long-term storage of CO₂ beneath the seabed; 2019 update allows transboundary export of CO₂ for sequestration.
  • 2013 Marine Geo-engineering (Annex 4): Regulates and prohibits most ocean fertilisation activities unless for legitimate scientific research.
⚖ Regulatory References
London Convention 1972Convention on Prevention of Marine Pollution by Dumping — original black-list/grey-list framework
London Protocol 1996Replaces LC-72 — reverse list approach; precautionary principle; waste assessment mandatory
LP.4(8) 2006Amendment to London Protocol — CO₂ sequestration under seabed permissible
Simon Sir / Nair typically asks: "What is the difference between the London Dumping Convention and the 1996 Protocol?" — Original: everything permitted except black-listed items. Protocol: everything prohibited except Reverse List items. Key principle of 1996 Protocol: Precautionary Approach — take action even without conclusive scientific proof of harm.

Basel Convention — Hazardous Waste Trade Control & PIC Procedure (Pages 187–189)
P.186MARPOLEnvironmental

Overview

  • Full name: Basel Convention on the Control of Transboundary Movements of Hazardous Wastes and Their Disposal.
  • Adopted: 1989; EIF: 1992.
  • Context: Created to combat "toxic trade" — developed countries disposing hazardous waste in developing nations (e.g., Africa).
  • Objective: Protect human health and the environment from adverse effects of hazardous and other wastes.

Core Aims

  • Reduce hazardous waste generation; promote Environmentally Sound Management (ESM).
  • Restrict transboundary movements except under ESM frameworks.
  • Treat and dispose of waste as close as possible to the place of generation.

Prior Informed Consent (PIC) Procedure

Step 1: Exporter notifies home Competent Authority │ ▼ Step 2: Authority forwards dossier to importing & transit states │ ▼ Step 3 & 4: Importing state reviews (≈ 60-day window) Options: Consent / Refuse / Request more details │ ▼ Step 5: If consented → export authorised Shipment must carry Movement Document │ ▼ Step 6: Disposal facility issues Certificate of Disposal/Recovery

Note for India: Designated national authority for PIC = MOEFCC (Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change).

Key Basel Amendments

  • Ban Amendment (adopted 1994, EIF Dec 2019): Prohibits export of hazardous wastes from OECD (developed) to developing countries.
  • Plastic Waste Amendment (EIF Jan 2021): Most plastic waste exports require PIC procedure.
  • E-Waste Amendment (effective Jan 2025): All electronic/electrical waste trade subject to PIC procedures.
⚖ Regulatory References
Basel Convention 1989Transboundary hazardous waste control — PIC procedure, ESM principles, national competent authorities
Basel Ban Amendment (COP.2)Prohibition on export of hazardous wastes from Annex VII (OECD) to non-Annex VII countries
Hong Kong Convention 2009Ship Recycling Convention — end-of-life vessels subject to hazardous material control similar to Basel principles
Simon Sir typically asks: "What is the Basel Convention?" — 1989, hazardous waste transboundary control, PIC procedure. India's competent authority: MOEFCC. Current affairs: E-Waste Amendment effective January 2025 — examiners are asking this in 2025–2026 orals. Governing body: Conference of the Parties (COP).

HNS Convention — Liability, Fund & Compensation Tiers (Pages 190–195)
P.190MARPOLCommercial

Overview

  • Full name: International Convention on Liability and Compensation for Damage in Connection with the Carriage of Hazardous and Noxious Substances by Sea.
  • 1996 version: Never entered into force. 2010 Protocol: Awaiting ratification — not yet in force.
  • Purpose: Final major IMO liability convention filling gaps left by CLC and Bunker Convention — specifically covers personal injury, death, property damage from chemical fires/explosions.

HNS Categories

  • Oils carried in bulk (MARPOL Annex I).
  • Noxious liquid substances in bulk (MARPOL Annex II).
  • Dangerous liquids in bulk (IBC Code).
  • Liquefied gases; liquid substances with flash point ≤ 60°C; hazardous solid bulk materials.
  • Packaged dangerous goods (IMDG Code).

Two-Tier Compensation System

  • Tier I: Paid by the shipowner's insurance.
    • Bulk HNS: Max 100 million SDR; Packaged HNS: Max 115 million SDR.
  • Tier II: Paid by the HNS Fund (funded by receivers of contributing cargo — bulk HNS only). Total compensation cap: 250 million SDR (inclusive of Tier I).

HNS Fund Accounts (4 accounts when operational)

  • Oil Account; LNG Account; LPG Account; General Account (Bulk Solids + Other HNS).
  • No cross-subsidisation — compensation from same account as the substance causing damage.

Shipowner Liability Limits (Bulk HNS)

  • Ships ≤ 2,000 GT: 10 million SDR.
  • 2,001–50,000 GT: 10 million SDR + 1,500 SDR/ton.
  • > 50,000 GT: 10M + 1,500/ton (to 50k) + 360 SDR/ton thereafter.

EIF Requirements for 2010 HNS Protocol

  • At least 12 states ratified; total registered tonnage ≥ 2 million GT; contributing bulk HNS cargo ≥ 40 million tonnes per year.
⚖ Regulatory References
HNS Convention 1996Original convention — never entered into force; replaced by 2010 Protocol structure
HNS Protocol 2010Revised convention — two-tier system, fund accounts, strict liability, awaiting ratification
CLC 1992 / IOPC Fund 1992Related oil pollution conventions — HNS explicitly excludes CLC-covered pollution to avoid overlap
Simon Sir / Nair typically asks: "What is the HNS Convention?" — Full name, 2-tier system, why it fills gaps (covers fire/explosion/injury that CLC doesn't). Total cap: 250 million SDR. Fund contributor: the RECEIVER of contributing cargo (bulk HNS). Not yet in force — 2010 Protocol still awaiting ratification as of 2026.

BWM Convention — Overview, D-1/D-2 Standards & BWMS Code (Pages 196–197)
P.193MARPOLSurvey

Overview

  • Full name: International Convention for the Control and Management of Ships' Ballast Water and Sediments, 2004.
  • EIF: September 2017.
  • Purpose: Prevent transfer of harmful aquatic organisms and pathogens via ballast water discharge — protect biodiversity and prevent invasive species.

Compliance Standards

  • D-1 (Ballast Water Exchange): Exchange ballast water at ≥ 200 NM from nearest land and in ≥ 200 m water depth. Efficiency ≥ 95% volumetric exchange (flow-through: pump ≥ 3 × volume).
  • D-2 (Ballast Water Performance Standard): Treated ballast water must contain:
    • < 10 viable organisms/m³ (≥ 50 μm).
    • < 10 viable organisms/mL (10–50 μm).
    • E. coli < 250 CFU/100 mL; Intestinal enterococci < 100 CFU/100 mL.

Applicability

  • Applies to all ships designed to carry ballast water.
  • Ships ≥ 400 GT: Must hold approved BWMP, maintain BWRB, meet D-1/D-2 standards with approved BWTS.
  • Exceptions: Vessels operating exclusively within a state's jurisdiction; warships; permanent sealed ballast tanks.

BWMS Code — Transition from G8 Guideline

  • G8 Guideline (2016): Recommendatory — allowed non-uniform type approval across states.
  • BWMS Code (MEPC 72, mandatory): Systems installed on or after 28 October 2020 must comply with this Code for type approval — ensures uniformity across flag states and ROs.
⚖ Regulatory References
BWM Convention 2004Base convention — D-1 exchange standard, D-2 performance standard, Regulation D-3 type approval
MEPC.300(72)BWMS Code — mandatory code for type approval of ballast water management systems from Oct 2020
BWM Convention Reg D-3Type approval — systems using active substances require GESAMP BWWG evaluation and MEPC approval
Simon Sir typically asks: "What is the D-2 standard?" — < 10 organisms/m³ (≥ 50 μm). He may ask: "Why was the BWMS Code introduced instead of keeping G8 Guideline?" — Guidelines are recommendatory; Code makes type approval mandatory and uniform across all flag states. Know mandatory date: October 28, 2020.

BWTS Type Approval — G8/G9, Land & Shipboard Testing (Pages 198–199)
P.197MARPOLSurvey

Type Approval Phases for Non-Active Substance Systems (G8/BWMS Code)

Phase 1: Pre-Test Evaluation Technical specs, piping diagrams, manuals, SDL submitted │ ▼ Phase 2: Type Approval Testing ├── Land-Based Performance Testing (independent labs) ├── Environmental Testing (vibration, temp, moisture) └── Shipboard Operational Testing (≥ 6 months aboard) │ ▼ Phase 3: Issuance of Type Approval Certificate │ ▼ Phase 4: Final On-Board Installation & Inspection

Salinity Testing (Land-Based) — 3 Salinities Required

  • Marine Water: 28 to 35 PSU.
  • Brackish Water: 10 to 20 PSU.
  • Freshwater: < 1 PSU.

Previously, single-salinity testing gave restricted approval. Now all 3 required for unrestricted Type Approval Certificate. SDL (System Design Limitations) stamped on certificate.

Shipboard Testing

  • Full-scale test onboard an active vessel in normal sea conditions.
  • System must operate in continuous compliant condition for at least 6 months.
  • Failed Test Cycle: Non-compliant D-2 discharge results, or engineering failure interrupting the run.
  • Invalid Test Cycle: Environmental conditions (salinity/temperature) outside SDL limits — does not count as failure; can be re-run.

G9 — Active Substance Systems

  • Applies to: electrolytic disinfection, chemical dosing, electrochemical reactions, ozone treatment.
  • GESAMP BWWG: Must evaluate toxicological impact, persistence, bioaccumulation, corrosion effects. System requires formal MEPC/IMO approval.
  • Basic Approval → Land/Ship Testing → Final Approval (resubmitted to IMO).
  • UV + TiO₂ (Advanced Oxidation) systems require G9 approval because hydroxyl radicals function as an active chemical biocide.
⚖ Regulatory References
BWM Convention Reg D-3Active substance approval — GESAMP BWWG evaluation required; MEPC final approval
MEPC.300(72) — BWMS CodeLand-based and shipboard testing protocols; 3-salinity requirement; SDL stamping on certificate
MEPC.126(53)Revised G8 guidelines (superseded by BWMS Code for systems installed from Oct 2020)
Simon Sir typically asks: "How is a BWTS type-approved?" — Give the 4 phases. Key: land-based testing in 3 salinities; shipboard testing for minimum 6 months. He may ask: "What is an Invalid Test Cycle?" — Environmental conditions outside SDL limits; can be re-run without penalty. G9 = active substance systems → GESAMP evaluation required.

IMO vs USCG BWTS Requirements — Key Differences (Page 200)
P.200MARPOLSurvey

IMO vs USCG Type Approval — Comparison

ParameterIMO (BWMS Code / G8)USCG (46 CFR Part 162)
Organism metricViable organisms (capable of reproduction)Living organisms (direct mortality counts)
Testing methodMost Probable Number (MPN)Vital Stain (FDA/CMFDA)
Discharge limit< 10 viable/m³ (≥ 50 μm)< 10 living/m³ (≥ 50 μm)
Test cycle quantity3 onboard + 5 land-based cycles5 ashore land-based cycles
Minimum holding time5 days in tanks24 hours only
Oxidant monitoringIMO TRO standardsUS-TRO (Total Residual Oxidant)
Validation frameworkG8/G9 guidelines + BWMS CodeUS-ETV (Environmental Technology Verification)

Practical Implication for Maersk Container Vessels

Vessels calling US ports must have a USCG type-approved BWTS (or use an AMS — Alternate Management System for up to 5 years during evaluation). IMO-approved systems alone are insufficient for US port entry compliance. Dual-approved systems or AMS status must be verified before calling US ports.

⚖ Regulatory References
USCG 33 CFR Part 151USCG ballast water management regulations — D-2 equivalent standards for US ports
USCG 46 CFR Part 162USCG BWTS type approval — living organism metric, vital stain method, 24-hour holding
BWM Convention Reg D-2IMO performance standard — viable organism metric, MPN method, 5-day holding
USCG AMS PolicyAlternate Management System — 5-year temporary authorization for IMO-approved systems in US waters
Simon Sir typically asks: "What is the difference between IMO and USCG BWTS requirements?" — Key differences: IMO measures VIABLE organisms (MPN method); USCG measures LIVING organisms (vital stain). IMO: 5-day minimum holding; USCG: 24 hours. As a Maersk 2/E on container vessels calling US ports, you must understand AMS status and dual approval requirements.
