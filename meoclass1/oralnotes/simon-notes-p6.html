Formal Safety Assessment (FSA) — 5 Steps & Risk Matrix (Page 117)
P.126ISMIMO Methodology

Definition

FSA is a structured risk assessment and cost-benefit methodology used by IMO member states, committees, and organisations with consultative status when proposing new or revised maritime regulations. It is a tool — not a regulation itself.

Five Steps of FSA

1. Hazard Identification (HAZID) │ Identify and prioritise hazards ▼ 2. Risk Analysis │ Likelihood × Consequence assessment ▼ 3. Risk Control Options │ Formulate risk control measures ▼ 4. Cost–Benefit Assessment │ Does risk reduction outweigh cost? ▼ 5. Recommendations for Decision Making │ Final implementation proposal to IMO

Risk Score Formula

Risk Score = Likelihood × Consequence

Likelihood scale: 1 (Low) → 2 (Medium) → 3 (High). Consequence scale: 1 (Minor) → 2 (Major) → 3 (Catastrophic). Higher scores receive priority for risk control measures.

⚖ Regulatory References
IMO MSC-MEPC.2/Circ.12Revised guidelines for FSA for use in the IMO rule-making process
ISM Code Sec. 1.2.3Safety management objectives — risk assessment as a foundation of ISM culture
SOLAS XI-1 / Reg 3Requirements for enhanced surveys and structural assessment linked to FSA findings
Simon Sir typically asks: "What are the 5 steps of FSA?" — State them in order with the acronym: Hazard Identification → Risk Analysis → Risk Control Options → Cost-Benefit → Recommendations. He may ask: "What tool is used in Step 1?" — HAZID (Hazard Identification study).

Certificate of Class vs Statutory Certificates & AR/VR in Shipping (Pages 118–119)
P.131ISMSurveyIACS

Certificate of Class

  • Mandatory but NOT statutory — required commercially, not by a convention directly.
  • Issued by a Classification Society confirming compliance with Class Rules for design, construction, and maintenance.
  • Required for: P&I Club coverage, Hull & Machinery insurance, charter party commitments.

Statutory Certificates

  • Issued by the Flag Administration or Recognised Organisation (RO) acting on its behalf.
  • Confirms compliance with international conventions (SOLAS, MARPOL, STCW, MLC).
  • Legal requirement for global trade — cannot operate commercially without them.

AR vs VR in Shipping

  • AR (Augmented Reality): Overlays digital information onto the real world — uses smartphones, tablets, or smart glasses. Example: Wärtsilä AR system where seafarers receive live shore-based repair instructions on a see-through display during stern tube maintenance.
  • VR (Virtual Reality): Creates a completely artificial simulated environment — requires VR headsets. Used for engine room emergency drills, stability training, enclosed space rescue simulation.
⚖ Regulatory References
SOLAS I / Reg 6Survey and certification — flag state or RO statutory certification obligations
IMO RO Code (Res. A.739(18))Recognition of organisations acting on behalf of the Administration — RO authorisation
IACS PR 1IACS procedure for condition of class — classification survey standards
Simon Sir typically asks: "What is the difference between a Class certificate and a statutory certificate?" — Class = society-issued, mandatory for insurance/charter; Statutory = administration/RO-issued, legally required by convention. AR question likely from Srivastava; Simon may ask as a technology update question.

COP-29 Key Outcomes & Latest MARPOL Amendments (Pages 119–120)
P.133GHGMARPOL

COP-29 — Key Takeaways (Baku, Azerbaijan, Nov 2024)

  • NCQG (New Collective Quantified Goal): Replaced the historic $100 billion annual climate finance target. Designed to reflect actual financial needs of developing nations.
  • Global Carbon Market: Agreement reached on UN-backed global carbon trading rules under Article 6 of the Paris Agreement.
  • Finance Target: Discussions around tripling climate finance to $300 billion — notable disagreements between developing nations on implementation remained.

MARPOL Annex VI — Latest Amendments

  • Mediterranean Sea ECA (SOx): Designated as an Emission Control Area for SOx and PM — effective 1 May 2025. Max sulphur content 0.10% in ECA.
  • BDN Flash Point: Bunker Delivery Note must now include the measured flash point or a declaration confirming flash point ≥ 70°C (SOLAS requirement).
  • EEXI and CII: Energy Efficiency Existing Ship Index and Carbon Intensity Indicator regulations now in force under Annex VI.

MARPOL Annex I — Latest Amendments

  • HFO Arctic Ban: Carriage and use of Heavy Fuel Oil in Arctic waters prohibited from 1 July 2024 (with certain flag state exemptions for coastal state vessels).
  • IOPP Certificate simplification: Administrative changes for ships flying non-party flags.
  • Form B Simplification: Simplified completion for Supplement Form B — entries relating to SBT, protective location, double hull, and dedicated clean ballast tanks.
⚖ Regulatory References
MARPOL Annex VI / Reg 14Sulphur oxide (SOx) control — 0.5% global cap; 0.1% in ECAs; Mediterranean ECA from May 2025
MARPOL Annex VI / Reg 28Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) — operational rating system A–E, annual reporting
MARPOL Annex I / Reg 43AProhibition on carriage and use of HFO as fuel in Arctic waters from 1 July 2024
MEPC.338(76)Guidelines on operational carbon intensity rating of ships — CII rating guidelines
Simon Sir typically asks: "What is the latest ECA added under MARPOL Annex VI?" — Mediterranean Sea, effective 1 May 2025. He may ask about the HFO ban in Arctic waters — effective 1 July 2024. BDN flash point amendment is an examiner favourite for Chief Engineer orals on Maersk container vessels bunkering VLSFO.

Casualty Investigator, GESAMP & AMS — Ballast Water (Page 121)
P.136ISMMARPOLSurvey

Marine Casualty Investigator Qualifications (MS Act S.359)

  • Academic: M.Sc. in Maritime Affairs from World Maritime University (WMU), Malmö, Sweden.
  • Field training: Casualty investigation training at WMU.
  • Practical experience: Under Part XI (Investigation of Marine Incidents), Merchant Shipping Act, 2025 — in force 15 March 2026, formerly Section 359 of the repealed 1958 Act — complete at least one Preliminary Inquiry under an approved experienced investigator.
  • CPD: Periodic refresher training in advanced casualty investigation techniques.

GESAMP — Joint Group of Experts

  • Stands for: Joint Group of Experts on the Scientific Aspects of Marine Environmental Protection.
  • Established: 1969. Jointly sponsored by 10 UN organisations including IMO.
  • Function: Conducts studies and reviews on marine environmental protection — advises the UN system.
  • EHS Working Group: Evaluates environmental hazards of harmful substances carried by ships — provides technical advice directly to IMO.

Alternate Management Systems (AMS) — USCG

  • Definition: A Ballast Water Treatment System (BWTS) type-approved by a foreign Administration but not yet USCG-approved.
  • USCG allows temporary use in US waters while the system undergoes formal USCG type-approval testing.
  • Validity window: Maximum 5 years.
⚖ Regulatory References
Merchant Shipping Act, 2025, Part XIPreliminary inquiry — qualification and appointment of marine casualty investigators in India; in force 15 March 2026 (formerly Sec 359 of the repealed 1958 Act)
Casualty Investigation Code (Res. A.849(20))Code for the investigation of marine casualties and incidents — international framework
BWM Convention Reg D-3Type approval of BWTS using active substances — GESAMP BWWG evaluation required
USCG 33 CFR Part 151AMS provisions — allows IMO-approved systems in US waters during USCG evaluation period
Simon Sir typically asks: "What is GESAMP?" — Give the full form, 1969 establishment, 10 UN bodies, and EHS Working Group. AMS and USCG 5-year window is more likely from Nair or Srivastava given its regulatory complexity. Casualty investigator qualifications are a Kochi MMD examiner favourite — S.359 is the key section number.

Maritime Zones Act 1976, BDN & Bunker Calculations (Pages 126–127)
P.138MARPOLCommercial

Maritime Zones Act 1976 (India)

Official title: The Territorial Waters, Continental Shelf, Exclusive Economic Zone, and Other Maritime Zones Act 1976.

  • Defines India's maritime boundaries and sovereign rights — aligned with UNCLOS.
  • Protects marine resources and regulates fishing, oil exploration, and shipping in Indian maritime zones.

Bunker Delivery Note (BDN) — MARPOL Annex VI Reg 18

Official legal record of a fuel transfer between bunker supplier and receiving vessel.

  • Mandatory contents: Vessel name, IMO number, port, delivery date, supplier details, quantity/volume, fuel type (VLSFO/ULSFO/MGO), density, kinematic viscosity, sulphur content %, and seal numbers of collected fuel samples.
  • Signatures: Must be signed and stamped by both Chief Engineer and bunker barge representative.
  • Retention: BDN retained onboard for minimum 3 years; fuel sample retained for minimum 12 months.
  • NEW: BDN must now include measured flash point or declaration that flash point ≥ 70°C.

CE Decision-Making Hierarchy

Safety of Life & Ship (First priority) │ ▼ Regulatory Compliance (SOLAS · MARPOL · Flag State) │ ▼ Company SMS │ ▼ Risk Assessment → Best Course of Action

Volumetric-to-Mass Bunker Calculations

Fuel ordered in MT but delivered in m³ — must correct for temperature and density.

V₁₅ = Vt × [1 − α(T − 15)] Where: Vt = observed volume; α = expansion coefficient; T = fuel temp (°C) HFO: α = 0.00065/°C | MGO: α = 0.00085/°C

Mass (MT) = V₁₅ × ρ₁₅ (density at 15°C in t/m³)

Always apply: temperature correction, trim correction, and list correction before accepting BDN quantity.

⚖ Regulatory References
MARPOL Annex VI / Reg 18Fuel oil availability and quality — BDN mandatory; sample retention 12 months; BDN retention 3 years
MARPOL Annex VI / Reg 14SOx control — BDN sulphur content must demonstrate compliance with applicable limits
ISO 8217:2024Marine fuel oil specification — density, viscosity, flash point, and sulphur content standards
Simon Sir typically asks: "What is a BDN and what does it contain?" — Give the 3-year retention for BDN and 12-month for sample — these are the most tested figures. The volumetric correction formula is a CE-level exam staple. Know HFO coefficient (0.00065) vs MGO (0.00085).

Why Classification is Important

A vessel requires Class throughout its service life to demonstrate structural and machinery compliance with Class Rules. Required for:

  • Design approval and construction supervision.
  • P&I Club and H&M insurance coverage.
  • Charter party terms.
  • Technical support and statutory compliance.
  • Periodic surveys to confirm continued seaworthiness.

Flag State Delegation to RO — Process

  • Step 1 — Application: Organisation applies to become an RO under the IMO RO Code.
  • Step 2 — Credential Verification: Flag State audits technical credentials and capabilities.
  • Step 3 — MoU: Memorandum of Understanding signed defining scope, limitations, and oversight programme.
  • Step 4 — Validity: Instructions on duration and periodic renewal of delegated authority.
⚖ Regulatory References
IMO Res. A.739(18)Guidelines for authorisation of organisations acting on behalf of the Administration (RO Code)
IMO RO Code (Res. MSC.349(92))Mandatory RO Code — requirements for recognised organisations' technical competence
SOLAS I / Reg 6Surveys — Administration may entrust surveys to inspectors or ROs
Simon Sir typically asks: "What is a Builder's Certificate?" — New-build only, from the shipyard, used for first registration. The RO delegation process (MoU, IMO RO Code) is a Nair/Srivastava question. Know the 4-step process clearly — it appears in commercial law orals.

Load Line Survey, SAFCON Survey & MS Act 2025 Structure (Pages 128–130)
P.143SurveyLoad LineSOLAS

Load Line Survey Protocol

  • Certificate validity: International Load Line Certificate valid for maximum 5 years.
  • Mandatory documents: Trim and Stability Booklet, Loading Manual, Record of Conditions of Assignment, Cargo Loading Indicator Type-Approval Certificate, Bow Height Certificate.

Inspection checklist: Freeboard marks, draft marks, vent heads/flame screens, scuppers and freeing ports, weathertight doors, guard rails, gangway condition, machinery space openings, overboard valves, bilge pumps, emergency bilge suction, sounding pipes.

SAFCON Survey — Practical Examination Steps

Anchoring equipment → Collision bulkhead → Watertight doors (local & remote) → Bilge system → Main engine remote stops → Steam piping insulation → Standby machinery auto-start → Engineers' alarm → Electrical insulation (megger) → Emergency power (generator/batteries) → Review previous survey memoranda

Merchant Shipping (MS) Act 1958 — Structure

18 Parts, 460 Sections. Key sections for CEOs:

  • Section 334: Unseaworthy ships — detention provisions.
  • Section 336: Owner's obligation — duty to ensure vessel safety.
  • Section 359: Preliminary inquiry — appointment of marine casualty investigators.
  • Section 360: Formal investigation — judicial courts.
  • Part VII (Sec. 95): Relief and Repatriation of Seamen (RPS).
  • Part IX: Safety — covers LSA Code and FSS Code.
⚖ Regulatory References
LL Convention 1966 / Art. 14Surveys — initial, annual, and renewal survey requirements for Load Line Certificate
SOLAS I / Reg 10Maintenance of conditions after survey — SAFCON continuous obligations
Merchant Shipping Act, 2025, Part XIPreliminary inquiry and formal investigation of marine casualties under Indian law; in force 15 March 2026 (formerly Sec 359–360 of the repealed 1958 Act)
Simon Sir typically asks: "What do you check during a SAFCON survey?" — Give the 10-step sequence. Key MS Act sections: 334 (unseaworthy), 336 (owner's duty), 359 (preliminary inquiry), 360 (formal investigation). Load Line Certificate: 5-year validity with annual endorsements.

Parametric Rolling & Synchronous Rolling — Container Ship Risk (Pages 136–137)
P.145StabilityContainer

Parametric Rolling

Occurs on modern vessels with fine bow lines and wide transom sterns when wave length matches ship length and waves encounter the vessel from ahead or astern.

  • Core condition: Pitching period = rolling period, OR pitching period = ½ rolling period.
  • Mechanism: As vessel pitches, submerged hull area forward and aft changes dynamically → shifting centre of buoyancy → oscillating rolling moment.
  • Vessel behaviour: Alternates between:
    — Wave crest under midship → waterplane narrows → lower GM → tender condition.
    — Wave trough under midship → wider waterplane → higher GM → stiff condition.
  • Hazards: Severe structural stress, stack failures, loss of containers overboard, risk of capsizing.
  • Corrective action: Immediate alteration of course and speed.

Synchronous Rolling

  • Definition: Resonance phenomenon when the vessel's natural rolling period matches the wave encounter period.
  • Hazards: Heavy water on deck, cargo shifting, loss of stability, capsizing.
  • Corrective action: Change course, change speed, or ballast to alter the rolling period.
⚖ Regulatory References
IS Code 2008 / Part BGuidance for dynamic stability — parametric rolling and pure loss of stability scenarios
IMO MSC.1/Circ.1228Revised guidance on avoiding dangerous situations in adverse weather and sea conditions
SOLAS VI / Reg 5-2Container securing — lashing requirements to resist parametric roll-induced forces
Simon Sir typically asks: "What is parametric rolling and how do you correct it?" — Explain the pitching = ½ rolling period condition; the tender-stiff alternation; and that course and speed change is the immediate corrective action. As a Maersk container ship 2/E, this is directly relevant — know it thoroughly.

Inclining Experiment — Conditions, Method & Formulas (Pages 137–138)
P.148StabilitySurvey

Objective

Determines the precise vertical centre of gravity (KG) and baseline metacentric height (GM) of a newly built vessel or one that has undergone major structural modification.

Mandatory Conditions

  • Vessel must be structurally complete, upright, and floating in sheltered water — free from wind, waves, tides, or current.
  • All tanks completely empty or completely full — minimise free surface effect.
  • Mooring ropes kept slack; gangway lifted; ship floating freely.
  • Record drafts at 6 points (FP, AP, midships both sides) and measure dock water density.
  • All loose gear secured, bilges pumped dry, cranes/derricks in sea-going position.
  • Non-essential personnel must leave the vessel.

Procedure

  • Equal test weights (w) placed away from centreline on both sides of midships deck.
  • Weights must induce a list of 1° to 4° — enough to be measurable, not too large to invalidate.
  • Minimum two pendulums (stabilograph) suspended at maximum vertical distance — bobs submerged in oil or water to dampen oscillation.
  • Weights shifted transversely over distance (d) across 8 separate movements — recording deflections each time.

Key Formulas

tan θ = Deflection / Pendulum Length

GM = (w × d) / (Δ × tan θ) Where: w = weight shifted; d = distance moved; Δ = displacement

KG = KM − GM KM taken from hydrostatic tables at measured draft

⚖ Regulatory References
SOLAS II-1 / Reg 5Stability information — inclining experiment required for new ships; results form basis of stability booklet
IS Code 2008 / Sec. 7Procedures for conducting inclining experiments and lightweight checks
IMO MSC.1/Circ.1591Revised guidelines on inclining experiment and lightweight check for ships
Simon Sir typically asks: "What are the conditions required for an inclining experiment?" — Give 6 conditions. Then give the formula: GM = (w × d)/(Δ × tanθ). He may ask: "Why must the list be between 1° and 4°?" — Less than 1° is too small to measure accurately; more than 4° invalidates the wall-sided formula assumption.

Free Surface Effect, Angle of Loll & Tender vs Stiff Ships (Pages 139–141)
P.150Stability

Free Surface Effect (FSE)

  • Mechanism: Liquid in a partially filled tank shifts to the low side when the vessel heels → centre of gravity shifts (G → G₁) → reduces righting lever (GZ) → increases effective KG.
  • FSE represents a virtual rise in KG (reduction in effective GM).
  • Mitigation: Subdivide tanks with liquid-tight longitudinal bulkheads, swash plates, or baffles.
  • Formula: Free Surface Moment = ρ × i / Δ, where i = second moment of area of the free surface about its own longitudinal axis.

Angle of Loll (AOL)

The angle to which an unstable vessel (negative initial GM) will heel under environmental forces. At this angle, GZ = 0 and B₁ is directly below G — temporary neutral equilibrium.

  • Behaviour: Vessel lists to AOL — if heeled further in same direction, a transient positive GM pushes back to AOL. If heeled to the other side, vessel lolls aggressively.
  • Causes: Improper ballasting, top-heavy cargo, emptying low DB tanks, structural errors, excessive FSE.
  • CORRECT remedy: Ballast low DB tanks first (to lower G) — do NOT use transverse weight shifting (extremely dangerous).

Tender vs Stiff Ships

FeatureTender Ship (small GM)Stiff Ship (large GM)
CauseHeavy topside weights → G raisedHeavy low weights → G lowered
Rolling period≈ 30–35 seconds (slow, comfortable)≈ 15–18 seconds (fast, violent)
RiskEasy to incline, slow to recoverViolent snapping motion — structural stress
RemedyLower heavy weights to DB tanksRaise weights to increase rolling period
⚖ Regulatory References
IS Code 2008 / Part A Reg 2.3Stability criteria — FSE correction mandatory in all GM calculations
IS Code 2008 / Sec. 2.2.3Angle of loll — vessel with negative GM, stability at angles of inclination
SOLAS II-1 / Reg 22Damage stability — residual stability requirements with FSE included
Simon Sir typically asks: "What is free surface effect?" — Virtual rise in G due to liquid shift in partially filled tanks. "What is angle of loll and how do you correct it?" — Loll = neutral equilibrium when GM is zero due to initial negative GM; correct by ballasting DB tanks (never use cranes). Tender (30–35 s) vs stiff (15–18 s) rolling periods are oral exam standards.
