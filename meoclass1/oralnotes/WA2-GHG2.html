MARPOL Annex VI Chapter 5 — The IMO Net-Zero Framework

Expanded model-answer notes for MEO Class 1 written & oral exam preparation — GFI, Well-to-Wake accounting, Base/Direct Compliance targets

Model Answer · Written Question (15 marks)
Discuss the key elements of the revised IMO strategy on the reduction of greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions from ships, including the new Chapter 5 of MARPOL Annex VI, and explain the mid-term and long-term measures proposed to achieve the targets and their potential impact on the shipping industry.

1 · Definition & Purpose

The IMO Net-Zero Framework is the set of mid-term technical and economic measures that will implement the 2023 IMO GHG Strategy's decarbonisation targets. It is drafted as a new Chapter 5 of MARPOL Annex VI ("Regulations on the IMO Net-Zero Framework") and combines two elements:

  1. A technical element — a goal-based marine fuel standard mandating a phased reduction in the Well-to-Wake (WtW) GHG Fuel Intensity (GFI) of the energy used on board.
  2. An economic element — a global maritime GHG emissions pricing mechanism, linking a ship's compliance balance to a levy/reward system.

Unlike the short-term measures covered in Chapter 1 (EEXI, CII), which regulate a ship's own operational efficiency, Chapter 5 regulates the carbon content of the fuel itself, evaluated across its full lifecycle rather than only at the exhaust stack.

2 · Importance to International Shipping

International shipping accounts for roughly 3% of global anthropogenic GHG emissions, but because it operates across international jurisdictions, territorial frameworks such as the Paris Agreement cannot directly regulate it. MARPOL Annex VI Chapter 5 fills that gap with a single global standard, preventing operators from routing tonnage through less-regulated flags to avoid climate obligations. For marine engineers, it shifts the centre of gravity of fuel-related compliance from tank-to-wake operational efficiency (CII) to full life-cycle well-to-wake fuel accounting — making zero/near-zero (ZNZ) fuel adoption a matter of commercial necessity rather than voluntary ESG positioning.

In an exam answer, always frame this chapter as "approved, pending adoption."

5 · Engineering Explanation & Core Technical Mechanisms

A. Well-to-Wake (WtW) Accounting Principles

Traditional MARPOL Annex VI regulation (EEDI/EEXI/CII) measures emissions only from the ship's exhaust stack — a Tank-to-Wake (TtW) boundary. Chapter 5 instead measures the complete fuel lifecycle, from production through combustion — Well-to-Wake (WtW):

$$\text{Total WtW Intensity} = \text{Well-to-Tank (WtT)} + \text{Tank-to-Wake (TtW)}$$
|<---------------------------- WELL-TO-WAKE (WtW) ---------------------------->| | | |<- WELL-TO-TANK (WtT) ->| |<- TANK-TO-WAKE (TtW) --------->| +------------------------+ Bunkered onto +---------------------+ Combusted +--------------+ | Extraction, Refining, |------------------->| Shipboard Storage |----------->| Propulsion & | | Transport, Logistics | the Vessel | Tanks & Fuel Lines | in Engine | Stack Gas | +------------------------+ +---------------------+ +--------------+

This boundary shift ensures alternative fuels are only credited as low-emission if produced sustainably — e.g. "grey" hydrogen or ammonia made from unabated fossil energy shows a poor WtT score even though it burns cleanly on board. Three greenhouse gases are tracked, normalised by 100-year Global Warming Potential ($GWP_{100}$): CO2 = 1, CH4 = 28, N2O = 265.

B. The Attained Annual GFI Equation

The Attained Annual GHG Fuel Intensity ($GFI_{attained}$), in grams of CO2-equivalent per megajoule of energy delivered on board:

$$GFI_{attained} = \frac{\sum_{i} \left(M_{i} \times LCF_{WtW,\,i}\right) + E_{remedial}}{\sum_{i} \left(M_{i} \times LHV_{i}\right) \times f_{s}}\ \ \left[gCO_2eq/MJ\right]$$

Where: $M_i$ = mass of fuel type $i$ consumed in the 12-month reporting period (tonnes); $LCF_{WtW,i}$ = Well-to-Wake life-cycle GHG emission factor for fuel $i$ (combining CO2, CH4, N2O via $GWP_{100}$), sourced from the IMO LCA Guidelines (Res. MEPC.376(80)); $LHV_i$ = lower heating value of fuel $i$ (MJ/g); $E_{remedial}$ = emission adjustment from purchased Remedial Units; $f_s$ = structural correction factor for specialised energy systems (wind-assist, onboard carbon capture).

C. The Two-Tier Target Mechanism

Chapter 5 sets two parallel reduction trajectories against a 2008 WtW reference value (≈93.3 gCO2eq/MJ):

ElementBase TargetDirect Compliance Target
ObjectiveMinimum baseline decarbonisation curve for fleet-wide survival.Accelerated curve driving faster ZNZ fuel adoption — the target every ship must actually meet.
2028 reduction4% vs reference.17% vs reference.
2035 reduction30% vs reference.43% vs reference.
2036–2040 targetsTo be decided by 2032 (5-yearly review mechanism).

Emissions between the Direct Compliance line and the (less strict) Base Target line fall in Tier 1, priced at US$100 per tonne CO2eq (well-to-wake basis). Emissions above the Base Target — i.e. failing even the minimum curve — attract a materially higher penalty rate under Tier 2. A ship is required to meet the Direct Compliance Target; the Base Target functions as the higher-penalty backstop.

D. The GFI Compliance Balance

At the close of each reporting period, the compliance balance (CB), in tonnes of CO2-equivalent, is:

$$CB = \left(GFI_{Target} - GFI_{attained}\right) \times Energy_{total} \times 10^{-6}$$

where $Energy_{total} = \sum_i (M_i \times LHV_i)$ in MJ. If $CB \geq 0$, the ship is in direct compliance and generates Surplus Units — bankable, poolable within a corporate fleet, or tradeable via the IMO GFS/Net-Zero Fund register. If $CB < 0$, the ship must offset the deficit by purchasing Remedial Units at the applicable tier price.

6 · Chief Engineer & Flag State Responsibilities

  • Fuel Lifecycle Label (FLL) verification: confirm every bunker delivery carries a certified FLL with WtT and TtW emission factors; a BDN without a certified FLL defaults the fuel to a worst-case fossil baseline in the GFI calculation.
  • GFI Register data integrity: maintain accurate consumption records by fuel type to support the annual attained GFI submission through IMO DCS.
  • Flag State role: verify annual GFI submissions, issue the Statement of Compliance, and administer Surplus/Remedial Unit transactions through the (proposed) IMO GFS Register once the framework is adopted and in force.

7 · Practical Shipboard Application

Engineering Viability of Candidate Alternative Fuels

  • LNG: clean stack combustion (TtW CO2 roughly 20–25% below conventional fuel oil), but WtW performance is undermined by upstream extraction venting and unburnt methane slip in the engine — since CH4 carries a $GWP_{100}$ of 28, even modest slip can push $GFI_{attained}$ into deficit.
  • Bio-methanol: clean, sulphur-free combustion; sustainably sourced biomass feedstock can give a very low or even negative WtT value, making it one of the stronger near-term paths to the Direct Compliance Target.
  • Green ammonia (NH3): carbon-free combustion (no CO2 from the fuel molecule itself), but only credited as low-emission if produced via renewable electrolysis — "grey" (fossil-derived) ammonia carries a poor WtT score. Unburnt ammonia slip and N2O formation ($GWP_{100}$ = 265) must be minimised.

On My Vessel (Maersk Container, 2-Stroke Main Engine)

For a large ABS-classed container vessel on a conventional 2-stroke main engine burning VLSFO, Chapter 5 compliance today means preparing the ground rather than reacting to a live obligation: verifying that current bunker deliveries already carry FLL-equivalent emission-factor data where the supplier provides it, and flagging any future dual-fuel retrofit decision (methanol-ready newbuild orders, in particular) as directly tied to the Direct Compliance Target trajectory rather than the more lenient Base Target.

8 · Survey & PSC Focus

  • Statement of Compliance (SoC): once in force, ships must hold an SoC certifying the prior year's attained GFI and compliance balance, verified by the Flag Administration or an authorised RO.
  • Enhanced SEEMP Part II data collection: fuel consumption reporting by consumer type and by "underway"/"not underway" status underpins the GFI calculation, mirroring the existing IMO DCS enhancements already in force for CII.
  • FLL/BDN cross-check: PSC and RO auditors will expect Fuel Lifecycle Labels to accompany the Bunker Delivery Note for any fuel claiming a non-default emission factor.

10 · Examination Question Bank

Part 1 — Strategic Architecture and the Role of Chapter 5

The 2023 IMO GHG Strategy commits the fleet to net-zero by or around 2050. Chapter 5 of MARPOL Annex VI, approved at MEPC 83 and pending formal adoption, is the primary regulatory vehicle for the mid-term measures: a technical goal-based marine fuel standard (GFS) and an economic GHG pricing mechanism, working together to force annual reductions in fuel lifecycle intensity.

Part 2 — Technical Mechanics of the GFS

The GFS caps the annual Well-to-Wake GHG intensity (GFI) of shipboard energy, in gCO2eq/MJ. By including the Well-to-Tank component, it prevents shifting emissions upstream during fuel production, and by weighting CH4 and N2O by their global warming potentials it captures the true lifecycle footprint, not just tailpipe CO2.

Part 3 — Compliance Mechanics

Ships beating their Direct Compliance Target earn Surplus Units, bankable or tradeable. Ships in deficit must buy Remedial Units, priced by tier (Tier 1 between the Direct Compliance and Base lines at $100/tCO2eq; a materially higher rate above the Base line). Long-term compliance eventually phases out conventional fuel oil entirely as targets tighten toward 2050.

Part 4 — Evaluation of Shipping Industry Impact

A strong "evaluate" answer weighs both the enabling and the burdensome effects, then reaches a judgement — not just a list.

Enabling / Positive Impacts

  • First-mover commercial advantage: ships beating the Direct Compliance Target generate Surplus Units, bankable and tradeable across a corporate fleet — compliance becomes a potential revenue stream rather than a pure cost centre for early adopters.
  • Asset value protection: fuel-flexible tonnage (dual-fuel, methanol-ready) retains charter appeal and resale value as the compliance curve tightens toward 2035 and beyond; single-fuel VLSFO-only ships face accelerating commercial obsolescence.
  • Single global benchmark: once adopted, one worldwide GFI curve replaces a patchwork of regional schemes, giving owners a stable target to design newbuilds against rather than reconciling multiple overlapping frameworks.

Burdensome / Negative Impacts

  • Fuel infrastructure hurdles: cryogenic/low-flashpoint handling systems, dedicated fuel-preparation spaces, double-walled high-pressure piping for methanol/ammonia.
  • Energy density penalty: alternative fuels need materially larger storage volume for equivalent range, reducing cargo capacity — approximate volumetric comparison for equivalent energy: VLSFO/MGO ≈1×, LNG ≈1.8×, green methanol ≈2.4×, liquid ammonia ≈3×.
  • Capex barrier for older/smaller tonnage: retrofit costs are often uneconomic on ships with limited remaining trading life, creating a two-tier fleet split between compliant tonnage and ships that simply absorb Remedial Unit costs until scrapping.
  • Fuel availability and bunkering-route risk: ZNZ fuel supply infrastructure is not yet globally distributed; a technically compliant ship may still face routing constraints based on where green methanol or ammonia is actually bunkerable.
  • Overlap with regional schemes: until international and regional carbon frameworks (e.g. EU ETS, FuelEU Maritime) are reconciled with Chapter 5, some owners face compounding cost exposure on the same voyage.

Evaluative Judgement

The framework's structure — a lenient Base Target as a floor, a materially stricter Direct Compliance Target as the real bar, and Tier 1 pricing at $100/tCO2eq — is designed to reward early movers financially while pricing laggards out gradually, rather than banning non-compliant ships outright. Net effect: the framework accelerates fleet renewal and fuel diversification, but the transition cost falls disproportionately on operators of older, less flexible tonnage, and the pace of real-world benefit depends heavily on how quickly ZNZ fuel supply chains scale — a factor outside any individual shipowner's control.
