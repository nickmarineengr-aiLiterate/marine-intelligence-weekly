Topic 27

The Maritime Labour Convention (MLC) 2006 — Flag State, Port State & Grievance Infrastructure

MLC 2006 Uday pp. 126–133 Examiner: Senthil ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: Two corrections applied to the source draft after cross-checking against the ILO's official MLC 2006 consolidated text and DGS/flag-state circulars. (1) The hours-of-rest figures were correct but have been re-worded to match the Convention's own phrasing (Standard A2.3) more closely for oral recall, and the equivalent maximum-hours-of-work limits (14 hrs/24 hrs, 72 hrs/7 days) have been added since examiners frequently ask for both sides of the comparison. (2) The "3-7-30 Rule" on-board complaint escalation timeline is not prescribed word-for-word in MLC 2006 itself — Standard A5.1.5 only requires that on-board procedures be "fair, effective and expeditious" and that seafarers retain the right to bypass the internal chain and complain directly to the Master, the Flag State, or an external authority (including PSC) without victimisation. The 3-7-30 day figures are a typical flag-state/company implementation model (matching DGS MS Notice-style practice), not the Convention text — this has been explicitly labelled below rather than presented as statutory wording.

📖 Definition

The Maritime Labour Convention, 2006 (MLC 2006), frequently called the "Fourth Pillar" of international maritime regulation alongside SOLAS, MARPOL and STCW, is a single consolidated instrument of the International Labour Organization (ILO). It establishes a globally uniform, enforceable set of minimum standards for seafarers' working and living conditions, and creates a level playing field so that quality shipowners flying convention-compliant flags are not undercut by sub-standard operators. It consolidates and updates over 60 earlier ILO maritime labour instruments dating back to 1920.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)

  • MLC governs the rest-hour discipline that directly determines how the Chief Engineer plans maintenance, bunkering, and UMS watch schedules without breaching statutory limits.
  • The Chief Engineer is directly accountable for Title 4 safety/accident-prevention controls in hazardous spaces (purifier rooms, chemical lockers, high-pressure fuel lines) and for PPE and gas-detection calibration records.
  • PSCOs routinely cross-check engine room alarm/UMS logs against signed rest-hour sheets — a discrepancy is treated as falsified documentation and is a high-risk detainable offence.
  • In an abandonment scenario, the CE is often the first officer to recognise the operational symptoms (fuel/provisions/spares stoppage) and must know how the Financial Security Certificate and P&I activation process works.
  • The DMLC Part II — which the CE's own department procedures feed into — is the shipowner's evidence to the PSCO that "practical steps match the Flag State's rules."

📅 Historical Background

⏱ Hours of Rest — Standard A2.3

Minimum hours of rest shall be not less than 10 hours in any 24-hour period and 77 hours in any 7-day period. Hours of rest may be divided into no more than two periods, one of which must be at least 6 hours in length, and the interval between consecutive rest periods must not exceed 14 hours. As an alternative compliance route, maximum hours of work shall not exceed 14 hours in any 24-hour period and 72 hours in any 7-day period — this is the figure most often compared against STCW Section A-VIII/1 in orals.

📋 On-Board Complaint Escalation — Reg. 5.1.5

Standard A5.1.5 requires every ship to carry documented on-board procedures for the fair, effective and expeditious handling of seafarer complaints alleging a breach of MLC 2006. Seafarers retain a non-infringeable right to complain directly to the Master or, where necessary, to the Flag State or an appropriate external authority (including PSC) — and must never be victimised for doing so.

SEAFARER → Files complaint (directly or via chosen representative) HOD → Typical company target: 3 days* MASTER → Typical company target: 7 days* DPA/OWNER → Typical company target: ~30 days* FLAG STATE→ Grievance Redressal Officer / DGS (bypass right at any stage) * Day-counts are a common flag-state/company implementation model, NOT MLC 2006 statutory text. MLC only requires "fair, effective, expeditious" handling + no victimisation + bypass right.
CE Oral Tip — Senthil Sir: Senthil frequently probes whether candidates present the "3-7-30" timeline as if it were quoted Convention text — this is a common trap. State the Convention principle first (fair/effective/expeditious, right to bypass, no victimisation, Std A5.1.5), then say "on our ships this is commonly implemented as a 3-7-30 day internal target," clearly framing it as implementation practice, not statute.

🎯 Oral Q&A — 3 Questions

Q1: An engine room rating wants to file an MLC complaint over unpaid overtime, and a PSCO is boarding simultaneously. How do you manage this?
I run a two-pronged approach that separates the internal grievance from the external PSC inspection. First, I acknowledge the complaint immediately and assure the rating there will be no victimisation under Std A5.1.5. As HOD I have a typical 3-day window to investigate the overtime sheets against the engine log and rest-hour records, escalating to the Master (typically 7 days) if unresolved, while making clear the rating may bypass this chain and speak to the PSCO directly at any time. Second, for the PSCO I present the valid MLC Certificate and DMLC Parts I & II immediately, keep my rest-hour logs, wage receipts and safety records transparent, and cooperate fully if the rating chooses to speak to the officer directly.
Q2: What is the difference between DMLC Part I and Part II, and why does a PSCO need both when the ship already has a valid MLC Certificate?
The MLC Certificate only confirms the ship was inspected and found compliant — it does not show how compliance is maintained day to day, so it is prima facie evidence only. DMLC Part I, drawn up by the Flag State, lists the national legislative provisions covering the 14 mandatory areas — it tells the inspector "these are the rules this ship must follow." DMLC Part II, drawn up by the shipowner, lists the practical operational measures and monitoring systems the crew actually uses to meet those rules — it tells the inspector "these are the steps we take to comply." Reading both together lets the PSCO verify that stated practice matches the Flag State's legal requirements and is being actively executed on board.
Q3: The shipowner has stopped bunker payments and food provisioning for over two months. What legal protections exist under MLC 2006?
This is a classic Seafarer Abandonment scenario governed by the 2014 Amendments (Std A2.5.2 financial security for repatriation, Std A4.2 shipowners' liability), in force since 2017. The vessel must carry a valid Financial Security Certificate, displayed on board, from an authorised provider (typically a P&I Club). Because the owner has defaulted, the Master and crew may bypass the owner entirely and contact the insurer directly to activate cover for up to four months of outstanding wages, full repatriation costs, and essential needs (food, medical care, fuel for survival systems) while awaiting repatriation. As Chief Engineer, I would still maintain safe manning and machinery operation — abandonment financially does not suspend the vessel's safety obligations — while the financial security mechanism resolves the crew's position.

The 14 Mandatory Reg. 5.1 Inspection Areas

Minimum age; medical certification; qualifications of seafarers; use of licensed/regulated recruitment and placement services; hours of work or rest; manning levels; accommodation; on-board recreational facilities; food and catering; health and safety/accident prevention; on-board medical care; on-board complaint procedures; payment of wages; financial security for repatriation and shipowners' liability.

Provisioning Endurance — An Engineering Tool, Not a Statutory Formula

Chief Engineers commonly track victualing endurance for Title 3 compliance as VE = Qtotal ÷ (Σ Cper_capita × Kf) ≥ Dvoyage + Dreserve, where Qtotal is stored provisions/water, Cper_capita is the daily statutory consumption baseline, Kf is a degradation safety factor (≥1.15), and Dreserve is a contingency margin (≥5 days). This equation is an engineering planning tool used to demonstrate compliance with Title 3 — MLC 2006 itself does not prescribe this exact formula.

HSSC Survey Cycle for the MLC Certificate

Initial survey (full 14-area audit before first certificate); Intermediate survey (mandatory, executed between the 2nd and 3rd anniversary of the certificate); Renewal survey (within 3 months before expiry). Certificate validity: maximum 5 years.

Clear Grounds for a More Detailed MLC Inspection

Missing/falsified MLC documentation; visibly unhygienic accommodation or galley spaces; visible signs of crew fatigue; a direct complaint from a seafarer or welfare organisation. High-risk detainable findings include falsified rest-hour records (double logbooks contradicting UMS/automation printouts), unpaid wages or invalid financial security certificates, critical galley/accommodation health failures, and absence of a functioning on-board complaint system.

Topic 28

IMO Goal-Based Ship Construction Standards (GBS) & The Ship Construction File (SCF)

SOLAS II-1/3-10 Uday pp. 134–141 Examiner: Nair Medium Frequency
🔎 Verification note: The source draft's applicability wording was incomplete/incorrect against SOLAS II-1/3-10 and has been corrected: (1) it omitted the mandatory bulk-carrier configuration qualifier — GBS applies to bulk carriers of 150 m+ constructed with single deck, topside tanks and hopper side tanks in cargo spaces, excluding ore carriers and combination carriers; (2) the "in absence of a building contract" date was wrongly stated as 1 January 2017 — the correct date is 1 July 2017 (keel-laid trigger), aligned with the 1 July 2016 building-contract trigger and 1 July 2020 delivery trigger. Both corrected below.

📖 Definition

Goal-Based Ship Construction Standards (GBS) mark a shift from prescriptive rule-making to a results-oriented framework: IMO defines the safety and environmental Goals and Functional Requirements a ship design must achieve, rather than dictating exact structural dimensions. The purpose is to guarantee that new commercial vessels are designed and built to be structurally safe and environmentally sound for their full intended service life, and to give IMO a transparent mechanism for verifying that Classification Society rules actually satisfy those safety objectives.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)

  • GBS design-life and fatigue-strength requirements directly influence how conservatively the hull, tank boundaries, and shafting alignment are built — which the CE inherits as the vessel's structural baseline for the next 25+ years.
  • The Ship Construction File (SCF), which the CE is the custodian of on board, is the primary reference for structural repairs, plate renewals, and "Critical Structural Area" identification.
  • North Atlantic wave-environment design criteria translate into enhanced structural stiffness near midship/engine-room double-bottom boundaries — relevant to shafting alignment and sea-chest integrity in heavy weather.
  • Any class-approved structural modification during the CE's tenure must be reflected in the on-board SCF, or the vessel risks a PSC/Class non-conformity at the next survey.

📅 Historical Background

Early 2000s → GBS concept introduced at IMO, driven by bulk carrier structural-failure and tanker pollution concerns MSC 87, May 2010 → SOLAS II-1/3-10 (GBS) adopted, mandatory for specified bulk carriers and oil tankers 1 Jan 2012 → SOLAS II-1/3-10 entered into force 1 Jul 2016 onward → Practical applicability begins per building-contract/keel-laid/delivery triggers (see below)

📐 Applicability (Corrected)

SOLAS II-1/3-10 applies to oil tankers of 150 m in length and above, and to bulk carriers of 150 m in length and above constructed with single deck, topside tanks and hopper side tanks in cargo spaces — excluding ore carriers and combination carriers — where any one of the following date triggers is met:

  • the building contract is placed on or after 1 July 2016; or
  • in the absence of a building contract, the keel is laid (or equivalent stage of construction) on or after 1 July 2017; or
  • the ship is delivered on or after 1 July 2020.

🔧 The 5-Tier GBS Architecture

GBS 5-TIER ARCHITECTURE │ ├── TIER I — GOALS (high-level safety/environmental objectives) — mandatory, SOLAS II-1/3-10 ├── TIER II — FUNCTIONAL REQUIREMENTS (criteria the design must satisfy) — mandatory │ • Design life ≥ 25 years │ • North Atlantic wave environment as the design sea state │ • Ultimate/fatigue/residual strength under damage │ • Corrosion protection (coating life, corrosion additions, CP systems) │ • Design transparency (structural data must be accessible) ├── TIER III — VERIFICATION OF CONFORMITY — IMO/MSC audits Class rules against Tiers I & II ├── TIER IV — RULES & REGULATIONS FOR SHIP DESIGN — detailed Class Society rules (non-mandatory for IMO verification) └── TIER V — INDUSTRY PRACTICES & STANDARDS — shipyard QA systems, maintenance codes
CE Oral Tip — Nair Sir: Nair will almost always test the exact bulk-carrier qualifier ("single deck, topside tanks, hopper side tanks... excluding ore carriers and combination carriers") and the three separate date triggers. Candidates who quote only "150 m and above" without the configuration and date detail are marked down — state all three date triggers even if only one applies to the ship being discussed.

📁 The Ship Construction File (SCF)

SOLAS II-1/3-10(4) requires a Ship Construction File documenting how the GBS functional requirements were applied in design and construction, provided on delivery and kept updated on board and/or ashore throughout the ship's service life. Industry/Class practice (not the bare SOLAS text) splits the SCF into an on-board operational dossier (structural drawings, material certificates, loading limits, Critical Structural Areas) and a shore-side secure archive (detailed FE models, proprietary design data) to balance design transparency against shipyard intellectual property protection.

🎯 Oral Q&A — 3 Questions

Q1: As new CE on a 2019-built Capesize bulk carrier, where is the SCF kept, what does it contain, and what are your duties regarding it?
The SCF is under my direct stewardship, kept securely in the ship's technical library/central office with electronic access linked to the vessel's IMO number. It contains structural blueprints, as-built drawings, material/steel-grade certificates, welding specifications, lines plans, permissible loading distribution charts, corrosion-protection documentation, and the identified Critical Structural Areas. My duties are to keep it secure and accessible to Flag State/PSC inspectors, and to ensure it stays a living document — any structural repair, plate renewal, or class-approved modification during my tenure must have updated drawings, thickness-measurement reports, and class certificates incorporated into it.
Q2: How does the GBS framework achieve design transparency while protecting shipyard intellectual property?
Through a two-component SCF architecture under Tier II functional requirements. Information needed for daily operations, structural inspection, and emergency damage assessment is kept on board for transparency. Highly sensitive proprietary data — full finite-element models, detailed welding blueprints, hull-form matrices — is stored in a secure shore-side archive held by the Class society or an escrow provider, with access restricted to authorised personnel such as accident investigators or Class surveyors under defined triggers.
Q3: Why does SOLAS II-1/3-10 baseline environmental calculations on the North Atlantic wave environment, and how does this affect the machinery spaces?
The North Atlantic is one of the most hydrodynamically severe sea environments globally, so designing hull-girder ultimate strength and fatigue limits against it builds in a conservative global safety margin regardless of the vessel's actual trading route. For the machinery spaces, this means enhanced structural stiffness at the forward/midship regions, which reduces dynamic hull deflection and helps maintain alignment of the main propulsion shafting and auxiliary machinery baselines during heavy weather, while sea chests, cofferdams and double-bottom tank boundaries near the engine room get added thickness margins that reduce fatigue-cracking and localized-flooding risk.

The Hull Girder Reliability Index — An Engineering Tool, Not a Statutory Formula

Class societies express hull-girder ultimate-strength adequacy using a reliability index β = (μR − μS) ÷ √(σR² + σS²) ≥ βtarget, where μR/σR describe structural resistance uncertainty and μS/σS describe extreme vertical bending-moment load uncertainty, with a typical target β ≥ 3.5 over a 25-year design life. This is a structural-reliability engineering tool used by Class societies to demonstrate Tier I/II compliance — GBS itself does not prescribe this exact formula in SOLAS text.

Tier III Verification Process

Classification Society structural rules are submitted to an IMO GBS Verification Audit, which checks the rules against Tier I Goals and Tier II Functional Requirements through document review and technical interviews. If conformity is confirmed, the rules are recommended to MSC for formal approval and publication as GBS-compliant; if not, they are returned to the Class society for correction and resubmission.

Topic 29

Port State Control (PSC) Regimes, The New Inspection Regime (NIR) & Enforcement Dynamics

PSC / NIR Uday pp. 142–150 Examiner: Nair ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: The source draft's Ship Risk Profile inspection windows (HRS 5–6 months, SRS 10–12 months, LRS 24–36 months) and the 9-MoU network are accurate but are Paris MoU New Inspection Regime specifics, not a universal IMO rule that applies identically across every regional MoU (e.g. Tokyo MoU aligns closely but is not identical; USCG uses a separate Qualship 21/Matrix system entirely). All NIR-specific figures below are explicitly framed as "under the Paris MoU NIR" rather than "under PSC generally."

📖 Definition

Port State Control (PSC) is the internationally mandated inspection regime applied to foreign-flagged ships calling at national ports, verifying that the vessel's physical condition, machinery, manning and operational workflows comply with the major IMO/ILO conventions. PSC is an enforcement backstop, not a certifying body — it exists to catch cases where Flag State or Recognized Organization oversight has failed, and to keep sub-standard ships out of the trading fleet.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)

  • PSC deficiency codes (16/17/30) directly determine whether the CE can sail on schedule or faces detention — understanding "Clear Grounds" lets the CE anticipate and pre-empt an expanded inspection.
  • Emergency generator/fire pump readiness, OWS 15 ppm integrity, and quick-closing valve function are the highest-frequency detainable machinery-space findings — all fall under the CE's direct operational control.
  • A well-documented breakdown (logbook entry, spares ordered, repair timeline) demonstrates active management to a PSCO and can prevent a defect being escalated to Clear Grounds.
  • Concentrated Inspection Campaigns (CICs) give advance warning of the technical area PSC will focus on each September–November — the CE can pre-audit that system before the campaign window opens.

📅 Historical Background

1978 → Amoco Cadiz oil spill exposes weaknesses in Flag State-only enforcement 1982 → Paris Memorandum of Understanding (Paris MoU) established — first major regional PSC regime Since 1982 → IMO encourages global proliferation of regional MoUs; network now covers virtually all major trading blocks 2011 onward → Paris MoU transitions to the algorithmic New Inspection Regime (NIR), replacing older Target Factor selection

🎯 The New Inspection Regime (NIR) — Paris MoU

Under the Paris MoU NIR, every vessel is assigned a dynamic Ship Risk Profile (SRP) — High, Standard, or Low — calculated over a rolling 36-month window using vessel type, age, Flag State performance (black/grey/white list), RO/Class performance, Company Performance Index, and the ship's own deficiency/detention history. The SRP sets the inspection window:

Ship Risk Profile (SRP) — Paris MoU NIR ├── High Risk Ship (HRS) — inspection window: 5–6 months ├── Standard Risk Ship (SRS) — inspection window: 10–12 months └── Low Risk Ship (LRS) — inspection window: 24–36 months

An Overriding Factor — e.g. involvement in a collision/grounding/stranding en route, suspended/withdrawn Class certificates, a formal sub-standard report from a pilot/port authority/agent, or failure to rectify ordered deficiencies within a 14-day window — immediately upgrades a ship to Priority I status, making inspection mandatory before the vessel is permitted to leave that port, regardless of the calculated SRP window.

CE Oral Tip — Nair Sir: Nair will test whether a candidate can distinguish "Overriding Factor → Priority I" (immediate, mandatory) from the routine SRP-based inspection window. He also frequently asks candidates to name the 3-month CIC window (September–November) and at least four Clear Grounds examples specific to the machinery space — have OWS/ORB falsification, emergency generator failure, quick-closing valve defects, and blocked/unlit escape routes ready.

🎯 Oral Q&A — 3 Questions

Q1: A PSCO finds oil near the steering gear hydraulic pump and declares Clear Grounds. Explain what this means and your response.
Clear Grounds means the PSCO has physical evidence the vessel's condition does not comply with international regulations — here, SOLAS II-1 steering-gear integrity requirements — which legally empowers escalation from a general walkthrough to a More Detailed Inspection. I remain cooperative and non-defensive, explain the leak was already logged and monitored, and present the maintenance log. Technically, I isolate the affected pump block, activate the redundant secondary steering system to preserve steering capability, clean up the spill, replace the worn seal/fitting, top off the reservoir, and then invite the PSCO to witness a full operational test including an emergency change-over drill before departure.
Q2: Explain how a Concentrated Inspection Campaign (CIC) works, and what you would check in the engine room if the CIC targets Fire Safety.
A CIC is a targeted inspection drive run by regional MoUs over a fixed 3-month window (September–November) each year, applying a standardised questionnaire on a specific high-risk topic to every ship selected for a normal PSC inspection during that window. For a Fire Safety CIC I would verify: the emergency fire pump starts readily and holds pressure at the furthest/highest hydrants with two hoses running; all ventilation dampers, funnel flaps and skylights close tightly with corrosion-free linkages; all quick-closing valves on fuel/lube tanks trip instantly from the remote station; the CO₂ room/water-mist panel shows zero faults with correctly aligned distribution valves; and that the crew can competently use EEBDs and execute fire drills without hesitation.
Q3: What criteria define an "Overriding Factor" under the NIR, and why does it trigger immediate Priority I status?
An Overriding Factor is a critical safety/environmental trigger that bypasses the normal time-based inspection window and immediately upgrades the vessel to Priority I. The criteria are: involvement in a collision, grounding, or stranding en route to the port; suspension or withdrawal of Class certificates for safety reasons since the last inspection; a formal sub-standard report from a pilot, port authority, or agent; or failure to rectify deficiencies within an ordered 14-day window. These trigger immediate inspection because they represent direct evidence of an active safety failure or non-compliance — the routine risk-based calculation is no longer sufficient, and the Port State must verify the vessel's condition before allowing it to continue trading.

Comparative MoU Snapshot

Paris MoU uses the NIR with Low/Standard/High risk profiles and standard Paris/IMPA deficiency codes (16, 17, 18, 30) recorded in the THETIS database. Tokyo MoU uses a closely aligned risk-based targeting system recorded in APCIS. The USCG operates an independent Qualship 21/Matrix Targeting system with its own domestic Form CG-5437 documentation and MISLE database — it is not part of the NIR framework and should never be described as such in an oral answer.

Worked Case — OWS Bypass Valve Failure

A PSCO ordering a full OWS operational demonstration found the three-way bypass valve failed to actuate on alarm, due to a faulty solenoid coil blocking pneumatic supply to the actuator diaphragm. Because the CE halted the test immediately, produced ORB Part I fully consistent with tank soundings/engine logs/OWS data-logger records (proving no illegal discharge had occurred), sourced and fitted a spare solenoid, and successfully re-tested under PSCO supervision, the deficiency was logged as Code 17 (rectify before departure) rather than escalated to a Code 30 detention — transparency and a fully resolved defect avoided the more severe outcome.

High-Risk Detainable Findings — Engine Room Specific

Emergency generator failing to auto-start/connect to the emergency switchboard on black-out test; emergency fire pump failing to draw suction or hold pressure at forward hydrants; ORB falsification or "magic pipe" bypasses; unserviceable quick-closing valves or disabled ventilation dampers; blocked or unlit emergency escape routes; watch officers unable to operate safety systems or execute change-over drills during a PSC test.
