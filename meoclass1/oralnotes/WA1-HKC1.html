Model Answer · Written Question
What is the significance of the Hong Kong Convention for India and how does India's ship-breaking industry stand to benefit or be impacted by its implementation?

1 · Definition & Purpose

The Hong Kong International Convention for the Safe and Environmentally Sound Recycling of Ships (HKC) is a legally binding IMO treaty ensuring that ships, at the end of their operational lives, do not pose unnecessary risks to human health, safety, or the marine environment during recycling.

Its purpose is to regulate the entire lifecycle of a vessel from an environmental-engineering perspective: controlling hazardous materials embedded in a ship's structure and machinery, setting technical criteria for Ship Recycling Facilities (SRFs), and enforcing compliance through continuous documentation, certification, and verification.

2 · Importance to International Shipping

Before global enforcement, the industry relied largely on unregulated beaching operations, associated with toxic spills, occupational illness, and fatal accidents. The HKC introduces global harmonisation, removing incentives for "flag-hopping" or "yard-shopping" to weaker jurisdictions.

The Convention converts ship recycling from an unmonitored scrap transaction into a structured, audited, and traceable industrial process — enforcing a "cradle-to-grave" asset-management concept across shipowners, shipbuilders, equipment manufacturers, classification societies, and recycling yards.

3 · Historical Background & Timeline

DateMilestone
May 2009HKC formally adopted at a Diplomatic Conference in Hong Kong, China, developed jointly with IMO, ILO, and Parties to the Basel Convention.
2019India enacts the Recycling of Ships Act, 2019, transposing HKC technical provisions into domestic law, establishing the Directorate General of Shipping (DGS) as National Authority.
June 2023Bangladesh and Liberia ratify, satisfying Article 17 entry-into-force criteria: ratification by ≥15 States representing ≥40% of world merchant shipping GT, with combined annual recycling volume ≥3% of that GT.
26 June 2025HKC enters into force globally — 24 months after the 2023 threshold was met — shifting the industry from voluntary to binding compliance.
CE Oral Tip: Examiners at Kochi MMD frequently probe the Article 17 mechanics — be ready to state the dual criteria (15 States / 40% GT / 3% recycling volume) and that entry into force followed 24 months after the June 2023 threshold, landing on 26 June 2025.

5 · Engineering Explanation & Core Technical Mechanisms

The IHM Framework — Three-Part Engineering Logbook

+-------------------------------------------------------------------------------+ | INVENTORY OF HAZARDOUS MATERIALS (IHM) | +-------------------------------------------------------------------------------+ | PART I: Materials in Ship's Structure and Equipment | | - Maintained continuously throughout the ship's operational life. | | - Covers paint systems, insulation, machinery seals, and piping. | +-------------------------------------------------------------------------------+ | PART II: Operationally Generated Wastes | | - Compiled prior to recycling by the Shipboard Management team. | | - Quantifies sludges, bilge waters, and tank residues. | +-------------------------------------------------------------------------------+ | PART III: Stores (Consumables, Spare Parts, Provisioning Liquids) | | - Logged immediately before decommissioning/sale to the SRF. | | - Identifies refrigerant gases, chemical treats, and batteries. | +-------------------------------------------------------------------------------+

Appendix 1 vs. Appendix 2 Materials

  • Appendix 1 — Strictly Prohibited / Restricted Installation: Asbestos, Ozone Depleting Substances (ODS), Polychlorinated Biphenyls (PCBs), Organotin Compounds (anti-fouling TBT).
  • Appendix 2 — Compulsory Monitoring & Quantification: Cadmium, Hexavalent Chromium, Lead, Mercury, Polybrominated Biphenyls (PBBs), Polybrominated Diphenyl Ethers (PBDEs), Radioactive Substances, Short-Chain Chlorinated Paraffins (SCCPs).

Technical Calculation — Safe Yard Capacity

An authorised Ship Recycling Facility (SRF) calculates its Maximum Annual Ship Recycling Capacity (CSRF) in Gross Tonnage using an empirically-framed throughput relation:

CSRF  =  Σ  [  (GTi × Doperational)  ⁄  Tdismantle, i  ]  ×  ηyard
GTi — Gross Tonnage of representative vessel type i.
Doperational — Active operational days of the facility per year (typically normalised to 300 days).
Tdismantle, i — Structural cutting/processing time (days) to fully reduce vessel i to zero blocks.
ηyard — Yard Efficiency & Safety Factor (≤ 1.0), set by crane capacity, cutting-zone area, and waste-storage constraints.
CE Oral Tip: This capacity relation is a teaching construct to illustrate the engineering variables a yard must control (throughput days, dismantling time per vessel type, efficiency factor) — it is not a formula printed in the HKC Annex itself. Present it as "an illustrative capacity-planning relation," not as a quoted regulatory formula, if an examiner asks for its source.

6 · Chief Engineer & Flag State Responsibilities

Chief Engineer Responsibilities

  • IHM Maintenance — MD/SDoC Protocol: Statutory custodian of IHM Part I. Every repair, pump overhaul, or repaint requires extracting a Material Declaration (MD) and Supplier's Declaration of Conformity (SDoC), logged in the SMS to update the IHM.
  • Pre-Recycling Preparation: On decommissioning instructions, draft Part II (wastes) and Part III (stores) of the IHM.
  • System Optimisation: Transfer remaining HFO to settling tanks; keep gas-freeing certificates current for cargo blocks, bunker tanks, and pump rooms.

Flag State Responsibilities

  • Survey Verification: Flag State / authorised RO verifies onboard systems match the IHM log via periodic surveys.
  • Certification: Issues the ICIHM on satisfactory survey; issues the IRRC prior to scrapping, following the verified Final Survey — the IRRC is normally valid for a period not exceeding three months, timed to cover the ship's final voyage.

7 · Practical Shipboard Application

Case Study — End-of-Life Capesize Bulk Carrier, Alang

A 180,000 DWT Capesize bulk carrier reaches end of commercial life, scheduled for dismantling at an authorised yard in Alang, India.

STEP 1: IHM PART I, II & III CONSOLIDATION - Chief Engineer collects MD/SDoCs from past drydocks. - Quantifies remaining fuel oil, lubes, sludge, ODS inventory. | v STEP 2: SHORE-SIDE FACILITY AUDIT & SRP MATCHING - Yard provides a vessel-specific Ship Recycling Plan (SRP). - Plan maps cutting sequence to protect structural integrity. | v STEP 3: STATUTORY FINAL SURVEY BY FLAG STATE RO - Surveyor verifies onboard items against submitted IHM. - Confirms tanks are ready for safe access and hot work. | v STEP 4: IRRC ISSUANCE & SAFE TRANSIT TO ALANG - International Ready for Recycling Certificate issued. - Vessel departs for beaching at the authorized facility.

Incident: Unmapped Asbestos Found During Pre-Recycling Inspection

  1. Immediate Isolation & Containment: Isolate the auxiliary boiler room space; secure ventilation fans, seal dampers to prevent fibre migration into engine room casing or accommodation.
  2. Statutory Logging & IHM Amendment: Log location, estimated volume, physical state in IHM Part I; notify the DGS/RO surveyor.
  3. SRP Revision Request: Transmit the amended IHM to the SRF; the SRF revises its SRP to include wet-containment and hazardous disposal protocols before torch-cutting proceeds in that block.

10 · Expanded Model Answer

1. Introduction & Structural Alignment

The ratification and enforcement of the HKC is a major turning point for India's maritime economy. India accounts for a substantial share of global ship recycling volumes, concentrated primarily at the Alang-Sosiya hub in Gujarat. By enacting the Recycling of Ships Act, 2019, India aligned its national legal framework with the HKC well ahead of global entry into force, enabling recycling yards to upgrade to international green standards before 26 June 2025.

2. Structural & Strategic Benefits

  • Market consolidation & higher tonnage inflow: Shipowners who previously avoided South Asian beaching yards on ESG grounds can now route vessels to compliant Indian facilities.
  • Circular economic linkage (recycled steel): Indian yards recover large volumes of high-grade marine steel annually; processing recycled steel requires markedly less energy than virgin ore smelting, feeding domestic construction and manufacturing.
  • Shipbreaking Credit Note Scheme: Under Maritime India Vision 2030, the government offers a scrap-value credit note to shipowners recycling at compliant domestic yards, fundable toward new construction at Indian shipyards.

3. Technical Impacts & Operational Challenges

  • CapEx for secondary infrastructure: Shift from mud-beach dismantling to hybrid systems using impermeable concrete heavy-lift intertidal landing pads.
  • Downstream waste-management infrastructure: The core challenge is building authorised, high-capacity Treatment, Storage and Disposal Facilities (TSDFs) for PCB, asbestos, and bilge-sludge volumes.
  • "Double compliance" pressure: European shipowners remain bound by the stricter EU-SRR, which does not permit intertidal beaching — pushing Indian yards to keep upgrading to compete with dry-docked facilities elsewhere.
8 marks
Detail the specific survey and certification requirements that an active vessel must undergo under the HKC framework prior to entering an authorized ship recycling facility.
  • Regulatory reference: HKC Annex Regulations 8, 9 and 10; harmonisation guidance in MEPC.222(64).
  • 4-stage survey cycle: Initial → Renewal (5-year loop) → Additional → Final.
  • Documentation output: ICIHM (operational-life certificate) → IRRC (pre-recycling certificate, valid ≤ 3 months).
  • Cross-check requirement: Surveyor confirms the vessel-specific SRP is formally authorised by the recycling state's competent authority before the final IRRC is issued.
10 marks
As Chief Engineer, explain how you would establish a robust on-board maintenance programme to ensure continuous validity of the IHM Part I throughout a ship's operational lifespan.
  • System integration: Embed IHM-updating into the PMS and ISM Safety Management Manual.
  • MD/SDoC procurement control: No spares, paint drums, or seals aboard without MD and SDoC.
  • Crew training & sampling restrictions: Prohibit unrecorded installation of gaskets, linings, or insulation potentially containing Appendix 1 materials.
  • Auditing: Quarterly audit by the Designated Person (IHM DP), cross-checking repair logs against the electronic IHM Part I register.
15 marks
Analyze the technical conflicts and regulatory intersections between the HKC, the EU-SRR, and the Basel Convention, and their impact on commercial engineering choices of global ship operators.
  • Jurisdictional core: HKC — flag-and-recycling-state treaty; EU-SRR — regional mechanism with global port-state application; Basel — transboundary hazardous-waste dumping.
  • Beaching debate: HKC permits controlled intertidal beaching; EU-SRR requires impermeable concrete structures, narrowing EU-flagged vessel options.
  • The "waste challenge": An end-of-life ship can be simultaneously a transport asset under IMO rules and "hazardous waste" under Basel — driving enforcement actions for unapproved exports.
  • Commercial mitigation: Operators use independent Recognized Organizations to maintain one hyper-compliant IHM profile satisfying all three regimes.
