Topic 37

IOPC Funds & Voluntary Agreements — STOPIA/TOPIA

Liability & Compensation Uday pp. 201–205 Examiner: Nair ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: IOPC Fund/STOPIA/TOPIA figures checked against IOPC Funds' published compensation limits and Nixon's standing-verified facts: 203M SDR standard Fund cap, 300.74M SDR special cap (≥600M t contributing oil, top-3 states), 750M SDR Supplementary Fund aggregate cap, STOPIA 29,548 GT / 20M SDR threshold. No discrepancies found. India's non-ratification of the 2003 Supplementary Fund Protocol should be reconfirmed against current DG Shipping status before an oral exam, as ratification positions can change.

📖 Definition

The International Oil Pollution Compensation (IOPC) Funds are two intergovernmental organisations — the 1992 Fund and the 2003 Supplementary Fund — established to compensate victims of oil pollution damage from laden tankers in Contracting States who cannot obtain full compensation under the first tier of the compensation regime, the 1992 Civil Liability Convention (CLC). Together the CLC and the IOPC Funds share the financial burden of tanker oil spills equitably between the shipowner (via CLC and P&I Club cover) and the global oil-importing industry (via the Funds).

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)

  • The Chief Engineer maintains the Oil Record Book Part II and ensures cargo quantities match the bills of lading — the primary evidentiary trail if a Fund claim is ever triggered.
  • Valid CLC certificates and P&I blue cards must be on board and accessible — a PSC staple check on any tanker.
  • Understanding the three-tier structure (CLC → 1992 Fund → Supplementary Fund) lets a CE correctly explain liability exposure during an incident debrief or investigation.
  • STOPIA/TOPIA are P&I Club industry agreements, not IMO instruments — CEs are frequently tripped up trying to cite an IMO resolution number for them.

📅 Historical Background

1967 → Torrey Canyon grounding off Cornwall exposes the absence of an international pollution-liability regime 1969/1971 → Original CLC (1969) and Fund Convention (1971) adopted 1992 → 1992 CLC and 1992 Fund Convention adopted (widely ratified successors) 2003 → Supplementary Fund Protocol adopted — third compensation tier for catastrophic spills 2006 → STOPIA and TOPIA voluntary agreements introduced by the International Group of P&I Clubs 20 Feb 2017 → STOPIA 2006 (as amended 2017) and TOPIA 2006 (as amended 2017) take effect

🔧 Compensation Tier Structure & Activation

The 1992 Fund is triggered when: (1) the shipowner proves an exemption from CLC liability (e.g. act of war, exceptional natural disaster); (2) the shipowner or their P&I Club is financially incapable of meeting CLC obligations; or (3) proven damages exceed the shipowner's CLC liability limit.

🔧 STOPIA & TOPIA Mechanics

  • STOPIA (Small Tanker Oil Pollution Indemnification Agreement): applies to tankers ≤29,548 GT entered in an International Group P&I Club and reinsured via the Group pool. It raises the effective shipowner liability limit to 20 million SDR — the 1992 Fund pays claimants first above the low CLC limit for a small tanker, then the shipowner's Club indemnifies the Fund up to the 20 Mn SDR ceiling.
  • TOPIA (Tanker Oil Pollution Indemnification Agreement): applies to all International-Group-entered tankers regardless of size. The shipowner indemnifies the Supplementary Fund for 50% of any compensation it pays for a covered incident.
  • Both are voluntary industry (P&I Club) agreements, not IMO conventions — the Funds are not parties to them but hold enforceable indemnity rights under them.
CE Oral Tip — Nair Sir: Nair frequently tests whether candidates conflate STOPIA/TOPIA with IMO instruments. State clearly: "STOPIA and TOPIA are contractual agreements among International Group P&I Clubs — they are not IMO conventions and carry no IMO resolution number." Also be ready to state the exact GT threshold (29,548 GT) and SDR figure (20 million) without hesitation — Nair marks down vague answers like "around 30,000 tons."

🎯 Oral Q&A

Q1: What happens if pollution damage exceeds the shipowner's CLC liability limit?
The 1992 IOPC Fund is activated as the second tier, paying the balance to claimants up to 203 million SDR (or 300.74 million SDR if the special high-volume-state condition is met). This aggregate cap includes the amount already paid by the shipowner's P&I Club under CLC.
Q2: Explain the purpose and operation of STOPIA.
STOPIA is a voluntary agreement among small-tanker owners (≤29,548 GT) in International Group P&I Clubs that raises their effective liability limit to 20 million SDR. It shifts a fairer share of small-spill costs back to shipowners: the 1992 Fund pays claims above the low statutory CLC limit for the small vessel, and the owner's Club then indemnifies the Fund up to the 20 million SDR ceiling.
Q3: Does the IMO govern the IOPC Funds?
No. The IOPC Funds are independent intergovernmental organisations with their own Assembly, Auditors and Secretariat in London. The IMO has no administrative role in managing them; the Funds' governing bodies meet twice a year.

Worked Example — Layered Payout

For a serious incident where STOPIA and TOPIA both apply: a small tanker owner (≤29,548 GT) is liable up to 20 million SDR under STOPIA; the 1992 Fund covers the balance up to 203 million SDR; any amount above that draws on the Supplementary Fund up to 750 million SDR total, with the shipowner indemnifying the Supplementary Fund for 50% of whatever it pays out under TOPIA.

India's Position

India is a Contracting State to the 1992 CLC and 1992 Fund Convention but has not ratified the 2003 Supplementary Fund Protocol. Always verify current ratification status close to exam date, as India's position on international liability instruments is periodically reviewed.

Maersk A/S Container Vessel Relevance

As a 2/E on Maersk container vessels, direct CLC/Fund exposure is limited since these instruments govern persistent oil-cargo tankers. However, bunker spills from container vessels fall under the Bunker Convention 2001 (Topic 2 of this Part) rather than CLC/Fund — a distinction oral examiners specifically probe.

Topic 38

Bunker Convention 2001 vs. CLC 92

Liability & Compensation Uday pp. 206–210 Examiner: Nair ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: Bunker Convention 2001 figures checked: 1,000 GT compulsory-insurance threshold and its reliance on LLMC (not its own limitation figures) confirmed against the Convention's Article 6/7 text. No discrepancies found.

📖 Definition

The International Convention on Civil Liability for Bunker Oil Pollution Damage, 2001 (Bunker Convention) ensures adequate, prompt compensation for damage caused by spills of oil carried as a ship's fuel. It complements CLC 92, which governs spills of persistent hydrocarbon mineral oil carried as tanker cargo. Together they cover the two main categories of shipboard oil pollution liability — cargo oil and bunker/fuel oil.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)

  • This is the convention directly relevant to every vessel type on Maersk container ships — a bunker spill on a container vessel is governed by this Convention, not CLC.
  • The CE must maintain Bunker Delivery Notes (BDNs) and representative fuel samples for a minimum of 3 years.
  • The Bunker Convention Certificate must be verified during PSC — a common detention item if the financial guarantor doesn't match the active P&I listing.

📅 Historical Background

1992 → CLC 92 addresses tanker cargo oil pollution but leaves non-tanker bunker spills unregulated internationally 23 Mar 2001 → Bunker Convention adopted at IMO Diplomatic Conference, London 21 Nov 2008 → Bunker Convention enters into international force

CE Oral Tip — Nair Sir: Nair's favourite trap question: "A 5,000 GT container vessel spills bunker fuel — which convention applies?" Answer immediately with "Bunker Convention 2001" and follow with the 1,000 GT threshold and the fact that it borrows LLMC limits rather than setting its own — candidates who search for a CLC answer lose marks instantly.

🎯 Oral Q&A

Q1: How does the definition of "shipowner" differ between CLC 92 and the Bunker Convention 2001?
CLC 92 restricts the shipowner definition to the Registered Owner alone. The Bunker Convention expands this to Registered Owner, Bareboat Charterer, Manager and Operator, giving claimants direct recourse against a broader range of entities.
Q2: A 5,000 GT container vessel spills bunker fuel. Which convention governs, and how are financial caps calculated?
The Bunker Convention 2001 governs since the vessel is a non-tanker releasing propulsion fuel. The compulsory insurance threshold is 1,000 GT, so a Bunker Convention Certificate is mandatory. The Convention has no built-in limitation figures — caps are calculated by reference to the LLMC Convention based on the vessel's Gross Tonnage.
Q3: What is the time bar for a Bunker Convention claim?
Rights are extinguished unless a claim is brought within 3 years from the date the pollution damage occurred, with an absolute cap of 6 years from the date of the incident — identical to the CLC 92 time-bar structure.

Why the Bunker Convention Has No Own Limits

During drafting, delegations debated whether the Bunker Convention should carry independent limitation figures. The final text (Article 6) opted instead to defer entirely to whatever limitation regime applies nationally or internationally — in practice, the LLMC Convention as amended. This keeps the Bunker Convention's liability caps automatically current whenever LLMC limits are revised (e.g. the 2012 LLMC amendments flowed straight through into Bunker Convention exposure without a separate amendment).

Practical CE Evidence Trail

Following a bunker spill: secure bunker samples, tank soundings, engine room alarm printer logs, and ensure the Oil Record Book Part I is completed without erasure. This evidentiary chain determines whether liability is contested and supports the P&I Club's defence.

Topic 39

Convention on Limitation of Liability for Maritime Claims (LLMC)

Liability & Compensation Uday pp. 211–215 Examiner: Nair ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: LLMC figures and tonnage-banded structure checked against the LLMC 1976/1996 Protocol/2012 amendment framework. No discrepancies found; note that GT-banded compensation figures should be reconfirmed against the current 2012 amendment tables before quoting exact numbers in an oral exam.

📖 Definition

The LLMC establishes a global, uniform system allowing shipowners and salvors to cap their total financial liability for general maritime claims (personal injury and property damage) arising from a single incident, calculated deterministically from the vessel's Gross Tonnage. It is the bedrock of maritime insurance, giving P&I Clubs a predictable ceiling on which to price cover.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)

  • The vessel's International Tonnage Certificate GT figure directly determines the shipowner's maximum exposure — the CE must ensure it is accurate and matches insurance binders.
  • A CE's own conduct can break the shipowner's right to limit liability — operating known-defective critical equipment (e.g. steering gear) with knowledge that loss would probably result can be construed as reckless conduct under Article 4.
  • LLMC limits flow through automatically into the Bunker Convention and Nairobi Wreck Removal Convention — a CE who knows current LLMC figures can answer questions on either.

📅 Historical Background

1957 → Brussels Convention — early limitation regime, values tied to outdated gold-standard/asset valuations 1976 → IMO adopts LLMC 1976, introducing SDR-based limits tied to Gross Tonnage 1986 → LLMC 1976 enters into force 1996 → 1996 Protocol adopted — raises ceilings, introduces Tacit Acceptance mechanism 2004 → 1996 Protocol enters into force Apr 2012 → 2012 Amendments adopted (Resolution LEG.5(99)) — 51% increase in limits, prompted partly by the 2009 Pacific Adventurer bunker spill in Australia 8 Jun 2015 → 2012 Amendments enter into international force via tacit acceptance

🔧 Strict Statutory Exclusions (Article 3)

  • Claims where loss resulted from the shipowner's personal act/omission, done with intent or recklessly and with knowledge that such loss would probably occur.
  • Crew/employment claims where the governing law of the service contract prohibits limitation or sets higher limits.
  • Oil pollution damage falling within the CLC's scope.
  • Salvage claims (incl. Article 14 special compensation, Salvage Convention 1989) and General Average contributions.
  • Nuclear damage claims governed by international/national nuclear liability law.
CE Oral Tip — Nair Sir: Nair expects the exact 2012 base figures without rounding — "about 3 million SDR" is marked down versus "3.02 million SDR." Also be ready to explain why intentional/reckless conduct breaks limitation: it is a deliberately near-unbreakable regime, so the bar (Article 4) is proof of intent or reckless knowledge, not mere negligence.

🎯 Oral Q&A

Q1: What is the Tacit Acceptance mechanism under the LLMC 1996 Protocol?
A fast-track amendment procedure letting the IMO Legal Committee raise financial limits for inflation without a full diplomatic conference. An adopted amendment automatically enters into force 18 months after notification, unless at least one-quarter of Contracting States formally object within that window.
Q2: Under what conditions can a claimant "break" a shipowner's right to limit liability?
Under Article 4, a claimant must prove the loss resulted from the shipowner's personal act or omission committed with direct intent to cause the loss, or recklessly and with actual knowledge that such loss would probably result. This is an exceptionally high evidentiary bar.
Q3: Why are salvage claims and CLC-covered tanker spills excluded from LLMC?
Salvage claims are excluded because salvors are separately incentivised via special compensation regimes (e.g. Salvage Convention 1989 Article 14) to protect the marine environment. Tanker cargo spills are excluded because they already sit under their own dedicated, higher-cap regime — CLC and the IOPC Funds — so including them in the general LLMC pool would dilute funds for ordinary claimants.

Worked Calculation Example

For a 42,000 GT vessel, property-damage cap under 2012 Amendments: base 1,510,000 SDR (first 2,000 GT) + 28,000 GT × 604 SDR (2,001–30,000 GT band) = 16,912,000 SDR + 12,000 GT × 453 SDR (30,001–42,000 GT band) = 5,436,000 SDR. Total = 1,510,000 + 16,912,000 + 5,436,000 = 23,858,000 SDR.

The Fund Constitution Process

Following a limitable incident, the P&I Club deposits the calculated ceiling into the competent national court as a "Limitation Fund." All local property claims are then legally forced to settle from within that single pool, shielding the shipowner from the balance of any higher aggregate claim.

Topic 40

Intervention Convention 1969 & OPRC 1990

Emergency Response & Preparedness Uday pp. 216–220 Examiner: Senthil ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: Intervention Convention 1969 (adopted 1969/force 1975), 1973 Protocol (force 1983, India not ratified), OPRC 1990 (adopted 1990/force 1995), OPRC-HNS Protocol 2000 (force 2007), and the SOPEP 150 GT (tankers) / 400 GT (other ships) threshold split all checked against primary IMO instrument summaries. No discrepancies found.

📖 Definition

The Intervention Convention 1969 affirms a coastal State's right to take preventative or mitigating measures on the high seas — outside its normal jurisdiction — against a foreign-flagged vessel following a maritime casualty that poses grave and imminent pollution danger to its coastline. OPRC 1990 complements this by mandating proactive shipboard and national oil-spill preparedness, response and international cooperation systems.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)

  • SOPEP locker readiness is a direct CE responsibility — inventory, condition, and deployability are checked monthly and during every PSC boarding.
  • Overboard valve locking protocols (oily bilge water, sludge, fuel transfer manifolds) prevent the accidental discharges that could trigger both OPRC reporting obligations and Intervention Convention exposure.
  • A CE must know the exact SOPEP tonnage thresholds — a frequently tested oral fact.

📅 Historical Background

1967 → Torrey Canyon disaster exposes the UK's lack of legal authority to act against a foreign tanker grounded outside its territorial waters 1969 → Intervention Convention adopted 1975 → Intervention Convention enters into force 1973 → Protocol extends intervention rights to substances other than oil; enters into force 1983 (India has ratified the 1969 Convention but not the 1973 Protocol) 1990 → OPRC Convention adopted 1995 → OPRC Convention enters into force 2000 → OPRC-HNS Protocol adopted, extending preparedness mandates to Hazardous and Noxious Substances; enters into force 2007

🔧 Intervention Rights — Structural Conditions

  • Grave and Imminent Danger: a validated, immediate threat of major pollution damage to coastline, fisheries, or marine ecosystems.
  • Maritime Casualty Origin: the threat must stem from a collision, stranding, structural failure, or similar navigational incident.
  • Proportionality: measures taken must be strictly proportionate to the actual/threatened damage and must not unnecessarily interfere with flag-State or crew rights.
  • Mandatory Consultation: the intervening State must notify and consult the flag State, registered owners, and recognised experts before acting — except in extreme emergencies requiring instant action.

CE Oral Tip — Senthil Sir: Senthil probes the exact SOPEP GT split — "150 GT for tankers, 400 GT for everyone else" must come out instantly, without hesitation. He also likes to test whether candidates understand that Intervention Convention rights only activate after a casualty and require the danger to be both grave and imminent — not one without the other.

🎯 Oral Q&A

Q1: What gives a coastal State authority to intervene on a foreign vessel on the high seas?
Under the Intervention Convention 1969, a coastal State may take necessary measures on the high seas following a maritime casualty to prevent, mitigate or eliminate grave and imminent pollution danger to its coastline or related interests — a legal exception to standard flag-State sovereignty on the high seas.
Q2: What is the GT threshold for mandatory SOPEP carriage?
An approved SOPEP is mandatory for every oil tanker of 150 GT and above, and for every other commercial vessel (bulk carriers, container ships, etc.) of 400 GT and above.
Q3: What does the 1973 Protocol add, and what is India's position?
The 1973 Protocol extends the coastal State's high-seas intervention right to cover pollution by substances other than oil — dangerous chemicals, liquefied gases, hazardous substances. India has ratified the original 1969 Intervention Convention (oil) but has not ratified the 1973 Protocol.

Why Intervention Rights Are Narrowly Scoped

The Convention deliberately confines intervention to post-casualty scenarios with proportional, consulted action, because it is a carve-out from the otherwise near-absolute principle of flag-State jurisdiction on the high seas. Broader powers were rejected during drafting to avoid opening the door to pretextual boarding of foreign vessels.

SOPEP Approval Chain

SOPEP plans are drafted to IMO Resolution MEPC.54(32) guidelines (as amended), approved by the flag State Administration — in practice usually delegated to a Recognised Organisation (class society). Any content deviation from the approved plan without RO sign-off is itself a deficiency.

Topic 41

UNCLOS Framework & Rights of Passage

Jurisdiction & Navigation Law Uday pp. 221–225 Examiner: Nair ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: UNCLOS dates (signed 1982, force 16 Nov 1994) and zone limits (Territorial Sea 12 NM, Contiguous Zone 24 NM, EEZ 200 NM, Continental Shelf max 350 NM) checked against the Convention text. No discrepancies found.

📖 Definition

The United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS), often called the "Constitution of the Oceans," is the comprehensive treaty defining maritime boundaries, resource rights, and navigation freedoms. It establishes exactly where a coastal State's sovereignty and jurisdiction begin and end, balancing national security and economic interests against the traditional freedom of navigation for the global merchant fleet.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)

  • Within the 12 NM Territorial Sea, environmental discharge rules intensify — OWS/15-ppm bilge alarm integrity becomes critical, as any wilful pollution is itself grounds for "non-innocent passage."
  • Engine breakdowns causing slow/erratic transit through a foreign EEZ or Contiguous Zone must be clearly logged, or low speed risks being misread as illegal fishing or smuggling.
  • Unauthorised hydrographic/sonar surveys inside foreign waters (even incidental echo-sounder use beyond normal navigation) can constitute a UNCLOS violation.

📅 Historical Background

Pre-1958 → 17th-century "freedom of the seas" doctrine limits national jurisdiction to a 3 NM cannon-range band 1956 → UNCLOS I (Geneva) — four preliminary conventions on territorial sea and continental shelf 1960 → UNCLOS II (Geneva) — fails to reach consensus on territorial sea breadth 1973–1982 → UNCLOS III (New York) — landmark restructuring of maritime law; convention signed 1982 16 Nov 1994 → UNCLOS enters into international force; over 180 nations have since ratified

🔧 Article 19 — Prejudicial (Non-Innocent) Passage Checklist

Passage is innocent unless prejudicial to the peace, good order or security of the coastal State. Under Article 19, a foreign ship's passage through the territorial sea is non-innocent if it engages in any of these activities:

  1. Threat or use of force against the coastal State's sovereignty, territorial integrity or political independence
  2. Any weapons exercise or practice
  3. Collecting information prejudicial to the coastal State's defence/security
  4. Propaganda aimed at affecting defence/security
  5. Launching, landing or taking on board any aircraft
  6. Launching, landing or taking on board any military device
  7. Loading/unloading commodities, currency or persons contrary to customs, fiscal, immigration or sanitary laws
  8. Wilful and serious pollution contrary to the Convention
  9. Fishing activities
  10. Research or survey activities
  11. Interfering with communication systems or other coastal-state facilities

Plus a general 12th category: any other activity not having a direct bearing on passage.

🎯 Oral Q&A

Q1: What is the difference between the right of transit through Internal Waters versus the Territorial Sea?
In Internal Waters, the coastal State holds absolute sovereignty and there is no automatic right of passage for foreign ships without explicit authorisation. In the Territorial Sea (up to 12 NM), the coastal State retains sovereignty but must respect the international right of Innocent Passage, allowing foreign commercial ships to transit as long as their activities are not prejudicial to security.
Q2: A coastal state arrests a vessel for discharging oily water 18 NM from the baseline. What zone, and does the state have authority?
This occurs in the Contiguous Zone (12–24 NM). Under UNCLOS, the coastal State has explicit jurisdiction there to punish violations of its sanitary/environmental laws if the actual infringement originally occurred within its territory or territorial sea.
Q3: What is the International Seabed Authority and what does it control?
The ISA is an independent intergovernmental organisation established under UNCLOS. It regulates and controls all mineral-related prospecting, exploration and exploitation activities in the international seabed area and subsoil ("The Area") beyond national jurisdiction.

Archipelagic Waters

States composed entirely of islands (e.g. Fiji) may draw baselines connecting the outermost points of their outermost islands. The enclosed water is termed Archipelagic Waters, where sovereignty matches the territorial sea, subject to preserving innocent passage through designated archipelagic sea lanes.

Illustrative Case — Reduced Speed Misread as Fishing

A container vessel transiting a foreign EEZ at reduced speed due to a turbocharger fault was intercepted on suspicion of illegal fishing. Under UNCLOS Articles 56/58, merchant ships retain complete freedom of navigation in the EEZ; preserved engine-room logs, bell-logger printouts and alarm data proved the mechanical cause, confirming the passage remained innocent and normal.

Why the Baseline Matters to Engineering Compliance

Every subsequent zone (Territorial Sea, Contiguous Zone, EEZ) is measured from the baseline — so its accurate charting underpins where MARPOL discharge rules, PSC jurisdiction, and Intervention Convention rights each apply.
