The ISPS Code & Ship Security Systems

ISPS · SOLAS XI-2 · MARSEC Uday pp. 326–330 (approx.) Examiner: Senthil / Paul ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: The source draft's core regulatory dates and thresholds were checked against SOLAS Chapter XI-2 and the ISPS Code text and are correct as given: adoption at the December 2002 Diplomatic Conference on Maritime Security, entry into force via SOLAS tacit acceptance on 1 July 2004, and the 500 GT applicability threshold for cargo ships. The ISSC validity (max 5 years, intermediate audit between the 2nd and 3rd anniversary), the Interim ISSC 6-month non-extendable validity, and the 3-monthly/1-week (if >25% crew change) drill frequency were all cross-checked and are correct. No numeric errors were found in this topic's source draft.
📖 Definition

The International Ship and Port Facility Security (ISPS) Code is a mandatory, risk-based security framework adopted as an amendment to SOLAS to standardise the way Contracting Governments, shipping companies, and port facilities detect, assess, and respond to security threats — terrorism, sabotage, piracy, and unauthorised access — against ships and port facilities engaged in international trade. It works by scaling a fixed set of baseline defences up or down according to a declared threat level, rather than leaving security arrangements to ad-hoc local judgement.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)
  • As Chief Engineer you are the technical custodian of the Ship Security Plan (SSP) even though the Master holds overriding authority — machinery spaces, steering gear flat, workshops, and chemical lockers are your access-control responsibility, and the citadel's autonomous power, ventilation cut-off, and communications depend on your engineering readiness.
  • Paul frequently probes whether candidates can distinguish the ISSC's full 5-year validity structure from the non-extendable 6-month Interim ISSC — a classic statutory-survey trap.
  • At MARSEC Level 3 the main engine must be on immediate standby with auxiliary machinery aligned for emergency breakout — this is a direct engine-room readiness obligation, not just a bridge/deck matter.

🎓 CE Oral Tip (Paul): Paul's standard question sequence: "What is the validity of the ISSC, and what happens at the intermediate stage?" Answer precisely — full-term ISSC is valid a maximum of 5 years, subject to a mandatory intermediate audit/verification between the 2nd and 3rd anniversary dates. An Interim ISSC, issued for new ships, change of flag, or change of company, is valid a strict maximum of 6 months and cannot be extended.
🎯 Oral Q&A
Q1. Who has the authority to set the MARSEC level for your vessel, and can the Master unilaterally raise it?
The applicable MARSEC level is set solely by the Contracting Government in whose territorial waters the ship is operating, or by the flag State when the ship is on the high seas. The Master cannot lower a level set by a coastal or port state, but the Master always retains the independent right and overriding authority under SOLAS XI-2/8 to take whatever additional precautionary security measures are judged necessary for the safety of the ship, crew, and cargo, even above the declared MARSEC level.
Q2. Your vessel is at MARSEC Level 2 alongside. What specific engine-room actions do you take as Chief Engineer?
I prepare main propulsion for manoeuvring at short notice per the Ship Security Plan, increase engine-room and steering-gear compartment access control, verify the citadel's independent ventilation cut-off and emergency lighting/communications are functional, and coordinate with the SSO to ensure any additional security-related electrical loads (deck floodlighting, monitoring equipment) can be supported without compromising blackout-prevention margins.
Q3. What is a Declaration of Security (DoS) and when is it mandatory?
A DoS is a formal agreement between a ship and a port facility (or another ship) setting out the security measures each party will implement during an interface. At MARSEC Level 1 it is executed only if requested by either party or where the port facility is non-ISPS-compliant. At MARSEC Level 2 it becomes mandatory between ship and PFSO. At MARSEC Level 3 it is mandatory and executed by the Port State Control authority together with the Master.

Citadel Architecture

A designated citadel must provide autonomous ventilation isolation (to defeat gas/smoke attack), independent battery-backed lighting and communications separate from the main switchboard, and pre-positioned emergency rations — all verified as part of the SSP audit trail, not improvised during an actual boarding.

Blackout Prevention at Elevated MARSEC

Running auxiliary generators in parallel through high-risk transit corridors removes the single-point-of-failure risk that a targeted attack on one generator could black out the ship at the exact moment manoeuvring power is most needed.

On My Vessel (Maersk A/S container tonnage)

Transiting recognised high-risk areas, the engine room runs an elevated standby configuration well ahead of the corridor per the company SMS, with the Chief Engineer confirming citadel systems and emergency fire pump readiness as a standing pre-arrival checklist item — independent of whatever MARSEC level the flag/coastal state has formally declared.

Ship Security Alert System (SSAS) — Engineering & Operational Testing

SSAS · SOLAS XI-2/6 Uday pp. 330–334 (approx.) Examiner: Senthil / Srivastava ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: The source draft's regulatory chain was checked against SOLAS Regulation XI-2/6 and confirmed correct: performance standards sit under IMO Resolution MSC.136(76), later amended by MSC.147(77), applicable to ships of 500 GT and above. The two-activation-point requirement (bridge + one concealed location), the fully silent/no-local-indication operating principle, and the 1 July 2004 SOLAS implementation trigger are all correct as given. The source draft's own EXAM FOCUS callout — that SOLAS XI-2/6 and MSC.136(76)/147(77) govern performance standards only, and do not prescribe fixed test-timing parameters such as a "24-hour advance notice" window — is itself an accurate and useful distinction; those timings are standard company SMS/SOP practice, not codified international law, and this has been retained as written.
📖 Definition

The Ship Security Alert System (SSAS) is a dedicated, silent-activation distress system that transmits a covert security alert to the flag State and Company Security Officer when a vessel is under threat — piracy, terrorism, or sabotage — without triggering any local audible or visual indication onboard. The silence is the entire engineering point: it lets external assistance be summoned without an attacker realising help has been called, protecting the crew from retaliation during a hostile boarding.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)
  • The Chief Engineer owns the SSAS's physical infrastructure integrity — power feed integration, wiring protection, and periodic insulation/voltage checks — even though operational command sits with the Master/SSO.
  • Srivastava tests whether candidates understand that SSAS uses dedicated satellite telemetry, independent of the ship's GMDSS distress alerting chain — confusing the two systems is a common examiner trap.
  • Once a functional test window closes, every subsequent activation is treated as a genuine live emergency by naval/MRCC assets — a CE who fails to confirm a clean reset after maintenance risks triggering a real-world response.

🎓 CE Oral Tip (Senthil): Senthil's standard trap: "Does SOLAS specify a mandatory 24-hour advance-notice period before an SSAS test?" The correct answer is no — SOLAS XI-2/6 and MSC.136(76)/147(77) govern only performance standards (silence, dual activation, no local indication). The 24-hour advance email to the Designated Competent Authority (D.C.) and CSO is a standard SMS/SOP industry practice, not codified international law. Stating this distinction precisely is exactly what separates a top-tier answer.
🎯 Oral Q&A
Q1. Walk me through the step-by-step procedure for conducting an SSAS functional test.
Following standard company SMS practice, we send an advance test notification (typically 24 hours ahead) to the D.C. and CSO stating vessel name, IMO number, position, and the planned test window. Once written/electronic authorisation is secured, the Master or SSO activates the bridge push-button; the D.C. confirms receipt and the switch is reset. With continued D.C. permission, the hidden second activation point is tested the same way. Finally, we contact the D.C. to confirm all test packets were logged as test protocols, reset the covert switch to standard readiness, and send a completion report with exact execution times.
Q2. Why must the SSAS produce absolutely no local indication when activated?
The entire protective value of SSAS depends on covertness. If activation produced any audible or visual signal onboard, an attacker present during a hijacking or piracy incident would immediately realise external assistance had been summoned, which could trigger retaliatory violence against the crew before help arrives. The system is deliberately engineered to be silent to the vessel and only detectable by the designated shore recipients.
Q3. As Chief Engineer, what is your technical responsibility toward the SSAS installation?
I verify that the SSAS interface module and satellite terminal power feeds are correctly integrated into the main switchboard, emergency switchboard, and dedicated GMDSS reserve battery bank; that wiring from the concealed activation point to the processing unit is routed through protected conduit to prevent accidental or deliberate damage; and that insulation resistance and supply voltage checks are logged during routine planned maintenance, ready for RSO verification at survey.

Post-Test Reset Discipline

Once the D.C. confirms a test window is closed, any further activation from that terminal — even accidental — will be treated as a genuine emergency by national naval and MRCC networks. A disciplined close-out log (time, date, both switches confirmed reset) is the CE's evidence trail if a false alert is ever disputed.

Reconfiguration on Change of Flag

Changing flag state requires the SSAS terminal's stored alert address to be updated to the new flag administration and CSO — typically done remotely by the equipment manufacturer's technician, requiring no physical crew intervention, but the CE must confirm the reconfiguration is verified and tested before the change-of-flag survey is signed off.

On My Vessel (Maersk A/S container tonnage)

SSAS functional tests are scheduled as part of the annual security drill programme; engine room involvement is limited to confirming the dedicated GMDSS battery bank voltage is within spec before the test window, since a low-voltage reserve supply is a common root cause of an SSAS failing to transmit on the emergency backup path.

The CSC Convention & the BLU Code

CSC 1972 · BLU Code · SOLAS VI/7 Uday pp. 334–339 (approx.) Examiner: Nair Medium Frequency
🔎 Verification note: CSC 1972's adoption (2 December 1972) and entry-into-force date were cross-checked against the IMO's own convention summary and confirmed correct: 6 September 1977 (12 months after the tenth instrument of ratification was deposited). The 30-month re-examination interval under the Periodic Examination Scheme (introduced by the 1983 amendments) and the initial 5-year first-inspection window are correct as given. The 1.8g allowable-stacking-load test factor stated on the CSC Safety Approval Plate is also correct. The BLU Code's adoption by IMO Resolution A.862(20) in November 1997 and its statutory linkage via SOLAS Chapter VI, Regulation 7 match the IMO's published record. One presentation note: the source draft's sample CSC plate diagram showed a leftover raw citation marker ("[cite: 5]") inside the plate mock-up — this has been removed as it is a drafting artefact, not part of the actual plate format.
📖 Definition

The International Convention for Safe Containers (CSC), 1972 is a statutory framework setting uniform international testing, inspection, and maintenance standards for intermodal freight containers, preventing structural failures such as corner-post collapse or floor buckling. The Code of Practice for the Safe Loading and Unloading of Bulk Carriers (BLU Code) is a complementary operational code that coordinates cargo distribution, loading rates, and ballasting sequences between ship and terminal to keep hull-girder stresses within the vessel's approved limits during bulk cargo operations.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)
  • On a container vessel, the CSC plate's Maximum Gross Mass and Allowable Stacking Load values are what the ship's loading computer and cargo planning team rely on — a container with an expired or invalid plate cannot legally be stacked or carried.
  • During BLU Code cargo operations the Chief Engineer's ballast pump readiness and generator loading directly determine whether deballasting can keep pace with the terminal's contracted loading rate — a mismatch is what actually triggers hull-stress alarms.
  • Nair frequently tests the PES-vs-ACEP distinction precisely, since candidates often confuse ACEP's "inspected at every repair" logic with a fixed calendar interval.

🎓 CE Oral Tip (Nair): Nair's standard question: "A container's CSC plate is valid but the corner casting is visibly cracked — is it fit to load?" The correct answer is no — plate validity confirms only that scheduled examination is current, not current structural condition. A structurally defective container must be rejected or discharged for repair regardless of plate status, per CSC Annex I's ongoing maintenance obligation.
🎯 Oral Q&A
Q1. PSC finds a container with a valid CSC plate but a deeply gouged, cracked corner casting. What do you do?
A valid plate confirms the scheduled examination interval has been met — it does not certify current structural condition. A cracked corner casting is a severe structural defect that can compromise the entire stack. I inform the Chief Officer and Master, label the container defective, refuse to load it if still ashore, or arrange safe discharge for repair if already aboard, and ensure no further carriage until an authorised surveyor verifies the repair against CSC Annex I standards.
Q2. The terminal's loading rate is outpacing your deballasting capacity during a BLU Code operation. What is the required response?
The duty officer immediately notifies the Terminal Representative via the agreed communication channel to request a reduced loading rate or a temporary halt, per the signed BLU Code Ship/Shore Checklist. If the terminal does not respond promptly and the hull stress monitoring system alarms, the Master or duty officer triggers the agreed Emergency Stop signal, halting all conveyor feeds until ballast pumping brings shear forces and bending moments back within the loading plan's safe limits.
Q3. What is the practical difference between PES and ACEP for a large container line's fleet?
Under PES, each container carries a fixed Next Examination Date and must be formally re-inspected within 30 months of the previous check — administratively simple but requires taking containers out of normal circulation for scheduled inspection. Under ACEP, examination happens naturally every time a container passes through a repair or depot facility, with the ACEP scheme number (not a date) shown on the plate — this suits large fleets where containers are constantly cycling through maintenance touchpoints, avoiding dedicated inspection downtime.

  • "5-then-30" — CSC PES: first inspection within 5 years, then every 30 months.

Hull Stress Monitoring Systems

Deck-mounted strain gauges feed the loading computer's real-time bending moment and shear force display; the Chief Officer cross-checks live readings against the loading plan's step-by-step limits, while engineering confirms the ballast pump discharge rate is tracking the planned deballasting curve so buoyancy changes stay synchronised with added cargo weight.

Enhanced Survey Programme (ESP) Linkage

Bulk carriers loaded repeatedly at high rates under BLU Code operations are exactly the tonnage the Enhanced Survey Programme targets for closer hull-corrosion and weld-fracture scrutiny — the operational stresses managed under BLU Code and the structural condition verified under ESP are two halves of the same risk picture.

On My Vessel (Maersk A/S container tonnage)

Container operations do not use the BLU Code (it applies to solid bulk cargo), but the CSC discipline is a daily reality — every gangway inspection during loading includes a visual check for corner-casting damage or an overdue CSC plate before a box is accepted for stowage.

Solid Bulk Cargo Engineering: Mechanisms of Liquefaction & Ship Stability

IMSBC Code · TML/FMP · Group A Uday pp. 339–344 (approx.) Examiner: Rajappan ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: The core moisture-safety rule was checked against the IMSBC Code and is correct: TML = 0.90 × FMP, and a cargo must have actual moisture content strictly below its calculated TML to be accepted. The Group A/B/C classification logic, the "Can Test" procedure (half-fill a cylindrical container, strike sharply against a hard surface approximately 25 times from a drop height of roughly 200 mm/20 cm at 1–2 second intervals), and Coulomb's shear-strength relationship τ = (σ − u)tan(φ) as the underlying soil-mechanics basis for liquefaction are all consistent with the IMSBC Code and standard naval architecture treatment of the topic. One terminology point corrected: the source draft's example question referenced Section 8 for the Can Test; the Can Test is described in Appendix 2 of the IMSBC Code (Section 8 covers general stowage/segregation provisions) — the procedural description itself was accurate, only the section cross-reference has been corrected.
📖 Definition

Cargo liquefaction is the abrupt transition of a fine-grained, soil-like solid bulk cargo from a solid to a fluid-like state, driven by cyclic vessel motion and vibration compressing the cargo matrix and raising pore-water pressure until it overcomes the inter-particle friction holding the material together. The regulatory purpose of the IMSBC Code's moisture controls is to prevent this transition ever occurring at sea, where its onset can be sudden and catastrophic.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)
  • Liquefaction is a stability, not a strength, failure — it destroys GM through an effective free surface effect, and Rajappan expects candidates to connect the soil-mechanics cause (pore pressure rise) directly to the naval-architecture consequence (GZ curve collapse), not treat them as separate topics.
  • As Chief Engineer, hold bilge system readiness and vibration management during high-risk transits are direct engineering responsibilities that reduce (though never eliminate) the compaction forces driving pore pressure rise.
  • The Master's authority to reject a cargo on visual/Can Test evidence — even against a signed shipper's certificate — is a legal power every officer must be able to explain precisely in an oral exam.

🧮 Critical Safety Parameters
TermMeaning
Flow Moisture Point (FMP)Moisture % at which the cargo begins to exhibit fluid flow under standard lab vibration testing

τ = (σ − u) tan(φ) — as pore water pressure (u) rises toward the normal stress (σ), effective shear strength (τ) collapses toward zero and the material liquefies. Once liquefied, the cargo behaves like a free-surface liquid: GG₁ = (i × ρ_cargo) / (V × ρ_sea), producing a virtual rise in G and a direct reduction in GM, which degrades the GZ righting-lever curve across all heel angles.

🎓 CE Oral Tip (Rajappan): Rajappan's standard escalation: "The shipper's certificate shows FMP 12.0% and actual moisture 11.2% — is this cargo acceptable?" Show the working: TML = 0.90 × 12.0% = 10.8%. Since actual moisture (11.2%) exceeds TML (10.8%), the cargo must be rejected. He then typically asks you to sketch how the resulting free surface effect degrades the GZ curve — be ready to draw a reduced-GZ curve overlay, not just state the rule verbally.
🎯 Oral Q&A
Q1. The shipper's declaration shows actual moisture 11.2% and FMP 12.0%. As Master, what is your decision?
TML = 0.90 × 12.0% = 10.8%. The declared actual moisture content of 11.2% exceeds this calculated TML, so the cargo violates IMSBC Code safety limits and must be rejected. I would close hatch covers, log the dispute formally, and request an independent laboratory verification via the CSO before any further cargo operations.
Q2. Describe the shipboard Can Test and its evidentiary weight.
A representative cargo sample half-fills a rigid cylindrical can, which is then struck sharply against a hard deck surface approximately 25 times from a drop height of about 20 cm. If the sample stays dry and crumbly it passes the initial screen; if it thins, settles, or produces free water on the surface, it fails, indicating moisture near or above the flow point. The Can Test is an empirical shipboard screening tool only — it cannot override a certified laboratory report, but a failure gives immediate legitimate grounds to suspend loading and demand formal independent analysis.
Q3. How does liquefied cargo alter the vessel's transverse stability mathematically?
Liquefaction creates a massive free surface effect: as the ship rolls, the fluidised cargo shifts to the low side, producing a virtual rise in the centre of gravity from G to G₁ (GG₁ = i·ρ_cargo / V·ρ_sea). This directly reduces effective GM (GM_eff = KM − KG − GG₁), and the righting lever curve is degraded across all heel angles per GZ_effective = GZ_solid − GG₁sin(θ), sharply reducing reserve stability and increasing capsize risk.

🧠 Memory Box
  • TML = 0.9 × FMP — the single formula every candidate must be able to apply instantly under pressure.
  • "25 strikes, ~20 cm" — the Can Test parameters.
  • Liquefaction = stability failure via virtual FSE, not a structural/strength failure.

Rain-Response Sequence

Shiny cargo surface or splattering on drop from the conveyor is the visual trigger to suspend loading immediately and close hatch covers — waiting for a lab result before acting is the wrong sequence; visual suspicion alone is sufficient grounds to halt operations pending the Can Test.

Hold Bilge Discipline

Strum boxes and bilge wells must be sounded and kept clear throughout a Group A loading — a blocked drainage path masks rising moisture inside the stow and removes the one passive early-warning signal available to the engine room team during cargo operations.

On My Vessel (Maersk A/S container tonnage)

Container tonnage does not carry IMSBC bulk cargoes, but the underlying stability principle — free surface effect degrading GM — is directly relevant to slack ballast/fuel tank management during cargo operations, and Rajappan is known to pivot a liquefaction question into a general FSE stability question regardless of vessel type.

Flag State Administration, Vessel Registry Engineering & the Merchant Shipping Act

MS Act 2025 · DGMA · UNCLOS 91/94 Uday pp. 344–350 (approx.) Examiner: Nair ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note — MAJOR correction, read before studying this topic: The source draft frames the Merchant Shipping Act, 1958 as the currently active, binding statute, with the 51% Indian / 49% foreign equity split and the DG Shipping→DGMA rename presented merely as "proposed" under a 2020 Bill. This is now out of date. The Merchant Shipping Act, 2025 (Act No. 24 of 2025) received Presidential assent on 18 August 2025 and its provisions were brought into force from 15 March 2026 — before today's date — so as of this writing the 1958 Act has been repealed and the 2025 Act is the active law. Key operative changes: (1) the office of Director General of Shipping has been renamed Director-General of Maritime Administration (DGMA), with expanded statutory powers; (2) the rigid "100% Indian ownership" rule is gone — Indian-flagged vessels may now be wholly or partly owned by Indian citizens, companies, NRIs, OCIs, or other Central-Government-notified entities, with the exact ownership thresholds fixed by subordinate Rules rather than the Act itself (draft Registration of Vessels Rules, out for public consultation Dec 2025–Jan 2026, specify a minimum 51% Indian ownership share for partly-owned vessels — this is a draft rule figure, not yet a finalised, gazetted requirement, and should be stated as such); (3) the Act formally enables registration of foreign vessels under a Bareboat Charter-cum-Demise (BBCD) structure (Section 16); (4) Part V "Registration of Indian Ships" language from the 1958 Act is superseded by the 2025 Act's own registration Part (Sections 15–19 cover eligible owners, BBCD registration, and provisional registration). The Registrar of Indian vessels remains the Principal Officer of the Mercantile Marine Department (or a person the Central Government notifies) — this did not change. In an oral exam, state clearly that the 2025 Act is now in force and describe the 1958 Act only as the repealed predecessor framework, not as "currently active."
📖 Definition

Flag State Administration is the statutory framework through which a sovereign nation exercises legal authority and technical oversight over vessels flying its flag. Vessel Registry Engineering is the structured legal/technical process of entering a ship into a national register — verifying title, confirming tonnage, and certifying regulatory compliance. In India this framework now sits under the Merchant Shipping Act, 2025, administered by the Directorate General of Maritime Administration (DGMA) through the Mercantile Marine Department (MMD) at regional field offices including Kochi.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)
  • As Chief Engineer you maintain the Continuous Synopsis Record (CSR), Oil Record Book, and engine room log discipline that flag-state and DGMA audits check first — registry compliance is a live, continuous engineering obligation, not a one-time paperwork event.
  • Nair frequently pivots between "1958 Act era" facts and "2025 Act era" facts specifically to test whether candidates are current — stating "2025 Act, now in force" without hesitation is itself part of a strong answer.
  • On a change-of-flag voyage, the engineering department is directly responsible for reprogramming SSAS/AIS/EPIRB/LRIT with new flag identifiers and re-engraving the wheelhouse tonnage plate — this is CE-owned technical work, not administrative-only.

🎯 Oral Q&A
Q1. What is the currently active statutory framework governing Indian vessel registration, and what changed from the earlier law?
The currently active law is the Merchant Shipping Act, 2025, in force since 15 March 2026, which repealed the Merchant Shipping Act, 1958. Under the 1958 Act, Indian registration required 100% Indian ownership by a citizen, eligible company, or co-operative society. The 2025 Act liberalises this: Indian-flagged vessels may now be wholly or partly owned by Indian citizens, companies, NRIs, OCIs, or other Central-Government-notified entities, with exact partial-ownership thresholds to be prescribed by subordinate Rules rather than fixed in the Act itself. The Director General of Shipping has also been renamed the Director-General of Maritime Administration (DGMA) under the new Act.
Q2. What are the statutory duties of a Shipping Master regarding crew agreements and shipboard disputes?
The Shipping Master supervises the formal engagement and discharge of seafarers in line with national law and the Maritime Labour Convention, hears and arbitrates disputes between Master, owners and crew over wages or working conditions, verifies the vessel meets its Safe Manning Document before issuing port clearances for outbound voyages, and maintains records of Certificates of Competency and seafarers' discharge books.
Q3. An Indian company buys a vessel located at a foreign port. How is she registered to make her delivery voyage to India?
The Registrar can authorise a local Indian Consular Officer or an RO surveyor to inspect the vessel at the foreign port, verifying ownership documents and the Carving and Marking Note details against the vessel's tonnage. Once satisfied, a Provisional Certificate of Registration is issued, giving the vessel legal flag status for her transit voyage to India, where permanent registration is then completed. (Note: under the 2025 Act the precise validity period and extension/cancellation procedure for this provisional certificate is left to subordinate Rules rather than a fixed figure in the Act — confirm the current prescribed period against the notified Rules rather than quoting a specific number as statute.)

Administrative Hierarchy

DGMA (Mumbai, national policy and IMO coordination) sits above regional MMD field offices — including Kochi — each headed by a Principal Officer who executes ship surveys, physical registration, and local crew examinations. Technical staff at MMD level include Ship Surveyors, Engineer & Ship Surveyors, Nautical Surveyors, and Radio Surveyors; the Shipping Master handles crew/MLC matters regionally.

Change-of-Flag Technical Sequence

Application and name allotment → Carving and Marking Note issued → GMDSS/AIS/EPIRB/SSAS/LRIT reprogrammed with new flag identifiers → hull markings repainted and wheelhouse brass plate re-engraved → new flag/RO surveys for SMC, ISSC and MLC → interim certificates issued pending permanent documentation.

On My Vessel (Maersk A/S container tonnage)

Continuous Synopsis Record (CSR) entries must be updated within the statutory window whenever registry particulars change — the Chief Engineer's role is ensuring machinery certification records in the technical file match the CSR and current flag documentation before any external flag or PSC audit.
