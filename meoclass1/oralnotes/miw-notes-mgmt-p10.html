Topic 42

Marine Environmental Governance (UNCLOS Pt. XII) & the Tiered Oil Pollution Compensation Regime

UNCLOS · CLC/Fund Uday pp. 226–232 Examiner: Nair ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: The source draft's summary matrix listed the Tier II (1992 Fund) special-case ceiling as 303.56 million SDR in one place and 300.74 million SDR in another. Cross-checked against the IOPC Funds' own published limits: the correct, current figure is 300.74 million SDR (raised from the original 200/300 million SDR figures by the November 2000 amendment, effective Nov 2003). The 303.56 figure has been removed throughout this topic. The base Tier II cap of 203 million SDR and the Tier III (2003 Supplementary Fund) cap of 750 million SDR were checked against IOPC Funds public data and are correct as given. The 18-PSSA count was checked against IMO/industry trackers current to 2025 (post NW-Med PSSA designation at MEPC 80, 2023) and is correct.
📖 Definition

Part XII of UNCLOS (Articles 192–237) is the "constitution" for protection of the marine environment. It places coastal and flag states under a general, standing duty to protect and preserve the marine environment (Art. 192) while recognising their sovereign right to exploit natural resources (Art. 193), subject to that duty. On top of this framework sits the IMO's Particularly Sensitive Sea Area (PSSA) mechanism and a separate three-tier international compensation regime for tanker oil pollution damage (CLC / 1992 Fund / 2003 Supplementary Fund).

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)
  • UNCLOS Art. 194 is the parent obligation behind MARPOL, ballast water, anti-fouling, and hull-coating rules — examiners use it to test whether you understand where "why do we have MARPOL" ultimately comes from.
  • PSSAs and their Associated Protective Measures (APMs) directly affect passage planning — routeing, reporting, and areas-to-be-avoided on your voyage through the Great Barrier Reef, Torres Strait, or the Canary Islands are all APM-driven.
  • As Chief Engineer you will never negotiate a CLC/Fund claim, but you are expected to know the certificate chain (Blue Card → CLC Certificate) carried on tankers >2,000 t oil cargo, and how liability scales with GT — this is a recurring Nair oral trap.

🎓 CE Oral Tip (Nair): Nair's classic follow-up after CLC/Fund questions is "what document does YOUR vessel carry, and who issues it?" — for any vessel carrying oil in bulk as cargo, or a large bunker tanker, know: Blue Card issued by the P&I Club to the flag state → CLC Certificate issued by the flag state, carried onboard. He is testing whether you confuse the insurer's document with the statutory certificate.
🎯 Oral Q&A
Q1. What is a PSSA and how many are there globally?
A Particularly Sensitive Sea Area is a sea area needing special IMO-approved protection due to ecological, socio-economic, or scientific significance vulnerable to shipping activity. Designation always comes bundled with Associated Protective Measures (routeing, reporting, areas-to-be-avoided). As of the NW Mediterranean designation at MEPC 80 (2023), there are 18 PSSAs worldwide, including the Great Barrier Reef, Torres Strait, Malpelo Island, and the Canary Islands.
Q2. Explain the three tiers of oil pollution compensation.
Tier I under CLC 1992 makes the registered shipowner strictly liable up to a GT-scaled cap (max 89.77 million SDR), covered by mandatory P&I insurance. Tier II, the 1992 Fund, is financed by oil-receiving industry and tops up compensation to 203 million SDR (300.74 million SDR under the high-volume trigger), inclusive of Tier I. Tier III, the optional 2003 Supplementary Fund, raises the aggregate ceiling to 750 million SDR for states that ratify it, inclusive of Tiers I and II.
Q3. When does a shipowner lose the right to limit liability under CLC?
Only if it is proven the pollution damage resulted from the owner's own act or omission, committed either with intent to cause the damage, or recklessly and with knowledge that such damage would probably result — a deliberately high bar designed to keep the limitation regime meaningful.

🧠 Memory Box
  • "89 – 203/300 – 750" — the three SDR ceilings in ascending order (Tier I / Tier II base-or-elevated / Tier III), all in millions.
  • 18 PSSAs — think "18 = number after MEPC 80's 2023 NW-Med addition."

Who Pays Into the Fund

The 1992 Fund is financed by any person/entity receiving more than 150,000 tonnes of contributing oil (crude + heavy fuel oil) per calendar year in a contracting state — i.e., the oil industry, not shipowners. It is administered by a permanent Secretariat in London (the IOPC Funds).

Geographic Scope of Claims

The Fund pays for pollution damage within the territorial sea or EEZ of a contracting state, and for preventive measures taken outside the territorial sea specifically to minimise damage inside it — relevant when a casualty occurs just beyond the 12-nm limit but oil drifts inshore.

On My Vessel (Maersk A/S container tonnage)

Container ships are not CLC-regulated cargo tankers, but every vessel carrying bunkers is subject to the Bunkers Convention 2001 (a parallel, separate strict-liability regime modelled on CLC principles) — an easy examiner pivot from a CLC question to "does this apply to your ship?"

Topic 43

The AFS Convention 2001 & Anti-Fouling Coating Technology

AFS Convention · Hull Coatings Uday pp. 233–237 Examiner: Simon Medium Frequency
🔎 Verification note: AFS Convention adoption (2001) and global entry-into-force date (1 September 2008) checked against the IMO AFS Convention summary and confirmed correct. The TBT-ban timeline (1990 IMO recommendation → absolute ban under AFS Annex I) is consistent with IMO's published history. No numeric errors found in this topic's source draft.
📖 Definition

The International Convention on the Control of Harmful Anti-Fouling Systems on Ships (AFS Convention, adopted 2001, in force 1 September 2008) prohibits harmful biocidal substances — principally tributyltin (TBT) — in ship hull coatings and sets a mechanism to control future harmful anti-fouling substances. It applies to all seagoing ships, plus fixed/floating platforms, FSUs and FPSOs.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)
  • Hull fouling directly increases hydrodynamic friction — you will be asked to connect coating condition to main engine fuel consumption and CII performance, tying this topic to GHG/decarbonisation questions.
  • The Coating Technical File (CTF) is a document CEs must know exists and be able to describe, even though it is primarily a ballast-tank/hull-structure record.
  • In-Water Survey (IWS) vs drydock bottom survey scheduling affects your ship's survey planning — CEs are expected to know the 36-month maximum gap rule.
📅 Historical Background

🎓 CE Oral Tip (Simon): Simon likes to ask "why does an SPC coating not lose performance the way a CDP does?" — the answer is the self-polishing chemical erosion mechanism removes the spent layer continuously, so roughness stays near a constant ~20 microns AHR instead of building up a porous leached layer.
🎯 Oral Q&A
Q1. What is the Coating Technical File and what must it contain?
A mandatory onboard document under MSC.215(82) recording the technical specification of ballast-tank/double-skin coatings, surface preparation and application records, Type Approval Certificates, and subsequent inspection/maintenance/repair history — carried for the ship's entire operational life.
Q2. What is the maximum interval between physical bottom surveys, and how can this be extended?
Physical bottom surveys are required twice in any five-year period, with a maximum gap of 36 months between consecutive physical surveys. An In-Water Survey (IWS) can substitute for one of the drydock surveys on eligible vessels, subject to that 36-month ceiling.
Q3. Why was TBT banned, and under what instrument?
TBT caused severe ecotoxicological harm — endocrine disruption (imposex in whelks/snails), shell malformation in oysters, and immune/genetic damage in marine organisms. IMO recommended phase-out in 1990, culminating in an absolute worldwide ban under Annex I of the AFS Convention 2001, in force since 1 September 2008.
✍ Written Exam Focus

5State the objective of the AFS Convention 2001 and its global entry-into-force date.
🧠 Memory Box
  • "2001 adopted, 2008 in force" — same 7-year adoption-to-force gap pattern as several other IMO conventions; don't confuse the two dates.
  • 36 months — both the hybrid-coating performance threshold AND the max bottom-survey gap; one number, two contexts.

Topic 44

Hull Corrosion Dynamics: SACP, ICCP & Marine Growth Prevention

Corrosion Engineering Uday pp. 238–242 Examiner: Simon ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: The anodic/cathodic half-reactions for mild steel in seawater (Fe → Fe²⁺ + 2e⁻; O₂ + 2H₂O + 4e⁻ → 4OH⁻) are standard electrochemistry and correct as given. SACP and ICCP mechanism descriptions match standard marine corrosion-engineering references (consistent with Eyres, Ship Construction). No numeric figures required correction in this topic.
📖 Definition

Hull corrosion is an electrochemical process requiring an anode, a cathode, an electrolyte (seawater), and a metallic return path (the hull itself). Protection is achieved through a combination of barrier coatings and active cathodic protection — either Sacrificial Anode Cathodic Protection (SACP) or Impressed Current Cathodic Protection (ICCP).

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)
  • ICCP is a live engine-room/deck system you monitor and troubleshoot at sea — this is one of the few "environmental law" topics with genuine hands-on engineering content, and examiners exploit that overlap heavily.
  • MGPS failure directly causes sea chest and cooling-water fouling, loss of heat exchanger efficiency, and MIC-driven pipe wastage — a direct link to main engine cooling system reliability.
  • Reference cell readings and rectifier output trends are exactly the kind of "what would you check first" scenario Simon builds into oral questions.
⚛ Core Electrochemistry
Anodic (oxidation, metal loss): Fe → Fe²⁺ + 2e⁻
Cathodic (reduction, consumes electrons): O₂ + 2H₂O + 4e⁻ → 4OH⁻
⚡ ICCP Control Loop
AC Power → Reactor-Rectifier (AC→DC) │ ├──► Inert Anodes (platinized titanium) ──► Seawater electrolyte │ └──► Hull (grounded negative = cathode) │ Reference Cells (Zn / Ag-AgCl) measure hull potential │ Controller compares to setpoint → adjusts rectifier DC output
⚖ Comparison — SACP vs ICCP
FeatureSACPICCP
Driving mechanismNatural galvanic potential (Zn/Mg anodes)External DC power, automatically controlled
AdjustabilityFixed — cannot respond to speed/salinity changesDynamic — controller adjusts output continuously
MaintenancePhysical anode replacement in drydockAnodes inert (non-consumable); electronics need monitoring
Hydrodynamic dragAdds drag (protruding anodes)Minimal — flush-mounted anodes
🎓 CE Oral Tip (Simon): Simon's favourite ICCP trap: "the reference cell shows the hull is under-protected — what does the controller do, and why?" Answer: it increases DC output from the reactor-rectifier because the supplied protective current must continuously exceed the natural corrosion current to keep the hull cathodic; failing to grasp this closed-loop logic is a common fail point.
🎯 Oral Q&A
Q1. Why can SACP not be dynamically adjusted, while ICCP can?
SACP relies purely on the fixed natural potential difference between a sacrificial metal (Zn/Mg) and mild steel — there is no external control variable. ICCP uses an externally powered rectifier feeding inert anodes, with reference cells providing real-time feedback to a controller, allowing the DC output to be continuously varied to match changing conditions such as speed, salinity, or coating degradation.
Q2. Describe how an MGPS unit protects internal seawater systems.
An MGPS is fitted at the sea chest inlet, using paired electrolytic anodes (typically copper with aluminium or iron). An impressed DC current dissolves the anodes: copper ions (~2 ppm) act as a biocide preventing larvae/spores/barnacles from establishing, while aluminium/iron ions hydrolyse into a protective anti-corrosive film lining the internal piping.
Q3. What are the four essential components of a hull corrosion cell?
An anode, a cathode, an electrolyte (seawater), and a metallic return path (the continuous steel hull structure) — remove any one and the corrosion circuit cannot function.
✍ Written Exam Focus
10With a labelled diagram, describe the components and closed-loop control logic of an ICCP system.
5Explain the electrolytic dosing principle used in Marine Growth Preventive Systems.
🧠 Memory Box
  • "A-C-E-M" — Anode, Cathode, Electrolyte, Metallic path = the 4 requirements for any corrosion cell.
  • SACP = Static, ICCP = Intelligent — quick recall for which system dynamically self-adjusts.

Large container vessels typically run ICCP for the underwater hull (given the size and speed profile makes SACP drag/replacement impractical) combined with MGPS on main seawater cooling and sea chests protecting the 2-stroke main engine's central cooling system from biofouling-driven heat exchanger fouling. Loss of MGPS dosing is a common root cause of gradually rising jacket/LT cooling water temperatures traced back to a fouled central cooler — a realistic "on my vessel" answer.

FAL Convention 1965 — the 7 IMO Forms, the Maritime Declaration of Health & Stowaway Management

FAL Convention · Stowaways Uday pp. 243–246 Examiner: Senthil ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: An earlier working draft of this topic added a "FAL Form 8 — Maritime Declaration of Health" to make an 8-form set. That is incorrect and has been removed. The FAL Convention standardises exactly 7 numbered IMO forms (General Declaration through Dangerous Goods Manifest). The Maritime Declaration of Health (MDH) is real and frequently tested — your Master/Chief Officer does complete it pre-arrival — but it is governed by the International Health Regulations (IHR 2005), not the FAL Convention's numbered form series, and IMO does not designate it "FAL Form 8." It is listed in FAL Convention Section 2 as one of a small set of additional declarations public authorities may require alongside the 7 FAL Forms. Getting this distinction right is itself the oral trap — stating "8 FAL Forms" as if MDH were IMO-numbered is the error Kochi examiners are listening for. FAL Convention adoption (1965) and entry-into-force date (5 March 1967) checked and correct. Stowaway definition text matches the FAL Convention Annex definition in substance (paraphrased here, not quoted verbatim, per this platform's copyright policy).
📖 Definition

The International Convention on Facilitation of International Maritime Traffic (FAL Convention), adopted 1965 and in force from 5 March 1967, standardises the paperwork and formalities port authorities may demand of arriving/departing ships, aiming to eliminate unnecessary delay through maximum documentary uniformity and inter-government cooperation.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)
  • The Maritime Declaration of Health is the document your Master/Chief Officer completes pre-arrival under the International Health Regulations — post-pandemic, examiners specifically probe whether candidates know it exists and can correctly place it outside the 7 numbered FAL Forms.
  • Stowaway discovery is a real operational event you may be involved in (search procedures, provisioning, welfare) — Senthil tests both the humane-treatment duty and the P&I financial-liability chain.
  • The "who pays and when can the claim be denied" angle connects directly to your understanding of P&I Club due-diligence requirements, a recurring theme across several Senthil topics.

🎓 CE Oral Tip (Senthil): Senthil's classic trap: "name all the FAL forms." The correct answer is exactly 7 — and if asked "isn't there an 8th, for health?", the strong answer is: the Maritime Declaration of Health exists and is real, but it's an IHR 2005 document, not an IMO-numbered FAL Form. Candidates who confidently say "8 forms" without that distinction lose marks; candidates who name all 7 and then correctly place MDH outside the numbered set score highest.
🧍 Stowaway — Disembarkation & Liability Logic
  • Discovered in departure-state territorial waters: Master requests immediate disembarkation by that state's authorities.
  • Discovered en route / after departure: Master notifies authorities and requests the next port of call to accept disembarkation.
  • Port state refuses: that state must inform the vessel's flag state of the refusal and its reasons.
  • Costs: primary legal responsibility rests on the shipowner/operator (fines, provisioning, guards, medical, embassy/repatriation, agent fees) — P&I insures this, but can deny the claim entirely if the crew failed adequate pre-departure searches or security procedures.
🎯 Oral Q&A
Q1. List the 7 FAL Forms, and explain why the Maritime Declaration of Health is not one of them.
The 7 numbered FAL Forms are: 1) General Declaration, 2) Cargo Declaration, 3) Ship's Stores Declaration, 4) Crew's Effects Declaration, 5) Crew List, 6) Passenger List, 7) Dangerous Goods Manifest. The Maritime Declaration of Health is a real document required pre-arrival, but it is governed by the International Health Regulations (IHR 2005), not the FAL Convention's numbered series — so it is not "Form 8."
Q2. A stowaway is found after the ship has left the departure state's territorial waters — what is the Master's obligation?
The Master must notify the appropriate authorities and may request the next port of call to accept the stowaway for disembarkation. If that port state refuses, it is obliged to inform the vessel's flag state of the refusal and its reasons.
Q3. Under what circumstance can a P&I Club deny a stowaway claim?
If it is established that the ship's crew failed to conduct adequate pre-departure searches, take preventive measures, or follow security procedures to prevent unauthorised access, the Club may deny the claim entirely, leaving the shipowner solely liable.

Topic 46

The SUA Convention (1988/2005) & BMP5 Counter-Piracy Architecture

SUA · Maritime Security · BMP5 Uday pp. 247–250 Examiner: Senthil Medium Frequency
🔎 Verification note: SUA Convention adoption (1988)/entry-into-force (1992), and the 2005 Protocols adoption/entry-into-force (2010) dates checked against IMO's published SUA treaty summary and are correct. The Achille Lauro (Oct 1985) origin story is historically accurate. BMP5's issuing body (ICS-led industry coalition) and its supersession of BMP4 (Somali-piracy focus) match the published BMP5 document ("Piracy & Armed Robbery — Countering Piracy off the Coast of Somalia, Gulf of Guinea and Red Sea/Gulf of Aden region" scope). No numeric corrections required in this topic.
📖 Definition

The Convention for the Suppression of Unlawful Acts against the Safety of Maritime Navigation (SUA Convention, adopted 1988, in force 1992) criminalises unlawful acts of violence against ships and obliges contracting states to extradite or prosecute offenders (aut dedere aut judicare). The 2005 Protocols (in force 2010) extended SUA to cover proliferation of WMD materials by sea. Operational counter-piracy guidance for High Risk Areas is codified in Best Management Practices (BMP5).

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)
  • BMP5 hardening measures (citadels, water cannons, razor wire) are things the CE is directly involved in rigging, maintaining, and operating during HRA transits — this is one of the more hands-on security topics.
  • SUA's jurisdictional logic (flag state / territorial state / national-of-offender state) is a recurring "who has authority" oral trap distinct from UNCLOS flag-state jurisdiction rules.
  • Understanding that SUA excludes internal crew discipline and warships helps you avoid over-applying the convention in oral answers.
📅 Historical Background
1985 Achille Lauro hijacking (Alexandria) — triggers SUA drafting 1988 SUA Convention & Fixed Platforms Protocol adopted 1992 SUA Convention enters into force 2005 SUA 2005 Protocols adopted (WMD proliferation provisions) 2010 2005 Protocols enter into force 2024 BMP5 supersedes BMP4 — extends HRA coverage (Red Sea, Gulf of Aden, Indian Ocean, Arabian Sea)

🎓 CE Oral Tip (Senthil): Senthil often asks a scenario: "your vessel is attacked in international waters but the suspect is later caught in State X's port — who prosecutes?" Answer via SUA Art. 6: State X now has full jurisdiction to arrest and either prosecute or extradite, even though the attack itself did not occur in State X's waters — the offender's presence there is itself a sufficient jurisdictional trigger.
🎯 Oral Q&A
Q1. What event led to the SUA Convention, and what is its core purpose?
The October 1985 hijacking of the Italian cruise liner Achille Lauro off Alexandria by Palestine Liberation Front members. SUA's purpose is to ensure unlawful acts against ships attract appropriate legal/criminal action, obliging contracting states to either extradite or prosecute offenders.
Q2. What did the 2005 SUA Protocols add?
They criminalised using commercial ships to transport, discharge, or facilitate the proliferation of biological, chemical, and nuclear (BCN) weapons — extending SUA beyond classic hijacking/sabotage offences to counter-proliferation and asymmetric security threats.
Q3. Name the four active/passive hardening layers under BMP5.
Enhanced visual/radar surveillance and AIS management (detection); razor wire on rails and low-freeboard areas (physical barrier); water cannons/monitors (active deterrent); and a dedicated citadel with independent communications and propulsion override as a last-resort safe room.

🧠 Memory Box
  • "1985 Achille Lauro → 1988 SUA → 1992 in force" — the SUA origin chain.
  • "Detect – Deter – Delay – Defend" — surveillance, water cannon/razor wire, citadel as the BMP5 layered-defence logic.

What SUA Does NOT Cover

  • Ordinary shipboard disputes or internal crew disciplinary matters — these stay under flag-state maritime/employment law.
  • Warships, naval auxiliaries, and vessels permanently withdrawn from navigation are excluded from scope entirely.

Domestic Incorporation Requirement

SUA is not self-executing — each contracting state must ratify and transpose its offences into domestic criminal law before national courts can prosecute under it, unlike some directly-applicable IMO instruments.
