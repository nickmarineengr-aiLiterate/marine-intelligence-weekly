The Vessel Inclining Experiment — Mechanical & Regulatory Framework

SOLAS II-1/5 · 2008 IS Code Ch.7 · Sister Ship Exemption Uday pp. 401–412 (approx.) Examiner: Rajappan ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: The sister-ship inclining-exemption thresholds in the source draft — lightship mass deviation ≤1.0% for L≥160m, ≤2.0% for L≤50m (linearly interpolated between), and LCG deviation ≤0.5% of L — are confirmed correct against the 2008 IS Code, Regulation 8.1.2 (Chapter 7), and are cross-checked against a published Flag/MOU circular reproducing the exact clause text. Separately, the source's HSSC/periodic lightweight-survey re-inclining trigger (deviation exceeding 2% of lightship displacement, or 1% of LCG) is a different, correctly-stated rule from the sister-ship exemption figures above — the two should not be confused with each other, and the source draft does not confuse them. Both figure sets are retained as originally drafted. The historical references to HMS Captain (1870) and SS Daphne (1883) are well-documented 19th-century stability casualties consistent with standard naval-architecture history and are retained as illustrative context rather than exam-testable dates.
📖 Definition

The inclining experiment is a controlled physical test used to determine a ship's lightship displacement (ΔL), vertical centre of gravity (KGL), and longitudinal centre of gravity (LCGL) — the three foundational numbers that every subsequent stability calculation on that ship depends on. In simple terms: before you can trust any loading-computer stability result, you first need to know exactly how heavy the empty ship is and exactly where its weight is centred. The inclining test answers that question by physically tilting the ship a small amount using known weights, rather than relying purely on design-stage calculations.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)
  • Every stability calculation you run on the loading computer throughout the ship's life — GM, GZ curve, damage stability — traces back to the lightship KG and LCG fixed at this one test. An error here quietly propagates into every future voyage.
  • As Chief Engineer you are not just a spectator: you are responsible for physically preparing the ship (tank condition, personnel clearance, mooring) so the test produces a clean, trustworthy result.
  • Rajappan frequently probes whether candidates understand why free surface effect and mooring restraint corrupt the test — not just that they do. Being able to explain the mechanism (not just recite the fault) is what separates a pass from a borderline answer.
📅 Historical Background
1870 Loss of HMS Captain — capsized due to a serious stability/freeboard design defect 1883 SS Daphne capsizes during launch on the Clyde — inadequate stability at launch Late 19th C. Steel hulls and steam propulsion shift weight distributions vertically, exposing the limits of purely theoretical stability estimates 1993 IMO Res. A.749(18) — detailed inclining-test guidelines consolidated 2008 2008 Intact Stability (IS) Code, Chapter 7 — current comprehensive international standard

📐 Sister Ship Exemption Thresholds
ParameterMaximum Permissible Deviation
Lightship mass (ΔL), L ≥ 160 m1.0% of lead ship's lightship mass
Lightship mass (ΔL), L ≤ 50 m2.0% of lead ship's lightship mass
Lightship mass, 50 m < L < 160 mLinear interpolation between 2.0% and 1.0%
Longitudinal Centre of Gravity (LCG)0.5% of L — applies regardless of ship length

If any of these limits are exceeded, the individual ship cannot rely on its sister ship's data and must undergo its own inclining test.

🧮 The Core Inclining Equation
Centerline | M (Metacenter) /| / | / | / | G----+----G1 (Shift of Centre of Gravity) / | / | V K (Keel)

A known weight (w) is shifted a known transverse distance (d), producing a heeling moment. The ship heels to angle φ, at which point the ship's righting moment balances the heeling moment. The shift of G to G₁ is:

GG₁ = (w × d) / Δ    and, from the triangle geometry,    GG₁ = GM × tan φ

Equating both expressions gives the working inclining formula:

GM = (w × d) / (Δ × tan φ)

Once the fluid GM is found, it is corrected for free surface effect (FSC) to give the solid GM, and KGas-tested = KM − GMsolid (KM taken from the hydrostatic tables at the test displacement). Finally, temporary items are subtracted and missing lightship items added back through a weight-moment audit to reach the true lightship KGL.

🛠 Practical Test Preparation
  • Tanks: every tank must be either completely empty or completely "pressed up" (100% full into the sounding pipe with zero air pockets) — a 98%-full tank is fine for normal operations but unacceptable here, since the 2% void allows liquid to shift and generates free surface error.
  • Personnel & environment: non-essential personnel cleared off; sheltered, quiet moorings; all mooring lines, gangways and shore cables slackened so the ship can heel freely; under-keel clearance verified at multiple stations; initial list kept below 1.5°.
  • Pendulums: minimum two (three recommended), 4–6 m long, terminating in a high-viscosity oil damping trough, positioned away from wind — the length matters because a longer pendulum magnifies small deflections, improving reading accuracy (tan φ = deflection ÷ pendulum length), with a minimum 15 cm deflection required on each side.
  • The 8-movement test: four weights are shifted port-to-starboard and back in a defined sequence (movements 1–8) to build a clean straight-line plot of tan φ against heeling moment; movement 8 (final zero re-check) may be omitted if movements 1–7 already give a clean line.
📊 Diagnosing Test Anomalies
AnomalyPlot SignatureCorrective Action
Ship touching bottom / restrained by mooringLater weight-shift points flatten below the expected straight lineStop test, sound around the hull, slacken all lines, re-do the affected movements
Excessive free liquids (slack tanks / bilge water)Plot curves progressively upward away from the straight lineHalt test, pump out bilges, press up or strip offending tanks dry, re-do all movements
🎓 CE Oral Tip (Rajappan): Rajappan often asks candidates to explain the anomaly plots verbally rather than just naming the fault. Be ready to say, in your own words, why an unquantified external force (bottom contact/mooring) flattens the tangent curve, and why a shifting free liquid mass exaggerates it — the shape of the error tells you its physical cause.
🎯 Oral Q&A
Q1. What are the statutory sister-ship exemption thresholds, and why do they vary with length?
A cargo ship may use a sister ship's inclining data if the lightship mass deviation is within 1.0% (L≥160m) to 2.0% (L≤50m), interpolated linearly for intermediate lengths, and LCG deviation is within 0.5% of L regardless of length. Larger ships get a tighter percentage tolerance because 1% of a much larger displacement is still a substantial absolute error, so the percentage band is deliberately narrower to keep the absolute tolerance sensible.
Q2. Why must tanks be either completely empty or completely pressed up during the test, and how would you handle a fuel tank that cannot be stripped?
Any partially-filled tank allows the liquid surface to shift as the ship heels, creating an unquantified free surface moment that distorts the linear tan φ plot and invalidates the calculated GM. If a fuel tank cannot be emptied, I would press it up completely into the sounding pipe with all air vented out, so there is zero void and therefore zero free surface — never leave it in a semi-filled state.
Q3. During the test you see the tan φ plot flatten out on one side. What does this indicate and what do you do?
A flattening slope means an external restoring force is resisting the ship's free rotation — most likely the ship has touched bottom or a mooring line/gangway is still under tension. I would immediately halt all weight movements, sound the water depth around the entire hull, inspect and slacken all lines and gangways, confirm full freedom of movement, then re-execute the affected weight movements from the zero baseline.

🧠 Memory Box
  • GM = (w·d) / (Δ·tan φ) — the core inclining formula.
  • 1% ≥160m, 2% ≤50m, 0.5% LCG always — sister-ship exemption thresholds.
  • Empty or pressed up — never in-between — the tank-condition rule that eliminates free surface error.

What the Written Notification Must Contain

Full ship/hull identification; exact date, time and location of the test; complete inclining-weight data (type, certified mass, anticipated maximum heel angle); measuring-device details (pendulum locations/lengths or U-tube arrangement, inclinometer approval); calculated approximate trim; condition of all tanks; an itemised estimate of weights to be added, deducted or relocated to reach the true lightship condition; the loading-computer software configuration used; and the name and credentials of the person directing the test.

Who Witnesses It

A surveyor representing the Flag Administration (or a Recognized Organization acting on its behalf) must witness the entire experiment and personally verify the raw data before it can be submitted for statutory approval into the ship's stability booklet.

On My Vessel (Maersk A/S container tonnage)

Large modern container ships are rarely re-inclined after delivery except following major conversions (e.g. scrubber retrofits, lengthening), but the same "empty or pressed up" tank-condition discipline applies whenever the yard or class surveyor conducts a lightweight check — worth remembering if you ever attend a newbuilding or major retrofit as part of a delivery/acceptance team.

Marine Insurance Fundamentals — The Seven Doctrines, H&M vs P&I, and the International Hull Clauses

MIA 1906 · IHC · 3/4ths Collision Clause Uday pp. 413–425 (approx.) Examiner: Nair ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: The 3/4ths Collision Liability Clause split (75% via H&M underwriters, remaining 25% via the P&I Club) is confirmed word-for-word against ITCH (1.10.83) Clause 8.1. The exclusions have been cross-checked directly against the full ITCH Clause 8.4 text, which has five sub-exclusions, not four: 8.4.1 removal/disposal of wrecks, obstructions or cargoes; 8.4.2 any real or personal property or thing whatsoever except other vessels or property on other vessels (i.e. Clause 8 never extends to fixed/floating objects, buoys, or shore structures — only to the colliding vessels themselves and cargo/property on them); 8.4.3 cargo or other property on, or the engagements of, the insured vessel; 8.4.4 loss of life, personal injury or illness; 8.4.5 pollution or contamination. The source draft's four listed exclusions correctly map onto 8.4.1, 8.4.4, 8.4.5, and (after rewording) 8.4.3, but omitted 8.4.2 — added below as a fifth bullet, since Rajappan/Nair-style examiners sometimes probe fixed/floating-object collisions specifically to test whether candidates know this exclusion. The Marine Insurance Act 1906 attribution, the Lloyd's Coffee House origin story, and the Seven Doctrines (Good Faith, Insurable Interest, Indemnity, Subrogation, Contribution, Loss Minimisation, Causa Proxima) are standard, well-established marine insurance law and are retained as drafted.
📖 Definition

Marine insurance is a contract of indemnity: the underwriter agrees to compensate the assured (typically the shipowner) for losses, damage, or liabilities arising from a maritime adventure, in exchange for a premium. In plain terms — it exists so that one bad night at sea doesn't bankrupt the company that owns the ship. The whole framework rests on a small set of legal doctrines that decide, in each claim, who pays, how much, and whether the claim is even valid in the first place.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)
  • The Chief Engineer's maintenance and record-keeping standards directly determine whether the ship's implied warranty of seaworthiness holds up — an undisclosed defect or unrecorded repair can void the entire policy under the doctrine of utmost good faith.
  • Understanding which claims sit with H&M (physical hull/machinery damage) versus P&I (third-party liability, injury, pollution, wreck removal) tells you who to notify first, and how, after any casualty.
  • Nair frequently uses a short casualty scenario and asks you to sort the resulting costs into H&M, P&I, and General Average buckets — this topic gives you the vocabulary to do that cleanly.
📅 Historical Background
Late 1600s Lloyd's Coffee House, Tower Street, London — informal meeting place of shipowners and underwriters 1906 UK Marine Insurance Act (MIA 1906) — codifies marine insurance law, becomes the Commonwealth blueprint 1963 Indian Marine Insurance Act — modelled directly on MIA 1906 1983/1995 Institute Time Clauses – Hulls (ITCH) editions standardise H&M policy wording 2003 International Hull Clauses (IHC) issued for global market uniformity
⚖ Regulatory References
Key Instruments

Nairobi Wreck Removal Convention, 2007Compulsory insurance for wreck locating/marking/removal costs on ships ≥300 GT.
🧭 The Seven Doctrines of Marine Insurance Law
  • Utmost Good Faith (Uberrimae Fidei): the assured must proactively disclose every material fact about the vessel's condition and trade — a breach (passive non-disclosure or active misrepresentation) lets the underwriter void the policy from inception.
  • Insurable Interest: the assured must have a genuine legal/financial stake in the safe arrival of the vessel — shipowner, charterer, cargo shipper, or crew (for wages) all qualify.
  • Indemnity: the policy restores the assured to their pre-loss financial position — never better. Payout is governed by the Agreed Value versus the Insured Value.
  • Subrogation: once the underwriter pays a claim, they step into the assured's legal shoes to recover from the third party actually at fault — preventing the assured from being paid twice.
  • Contribution: if the same risk is over-insured across multiple policies, each underwriter pays only their proportional share, calculated against the total combined insured value.
  • Loss Minimisation ("Sue and Labour"): having insurance does not excuse the crew from acting prudently during an emergency — reasonable mitigation costs are recoverable under the policy.
  • Proximate Cause (Causa Proxima): the underwriter is liable only for the dominant, effective cause of a loss — not necessarily the cause nearest in time to the final damage.
🏛 H&M vs P&I — The Two Operational Frameworks
FeatureHull & Machinery (H&M)Protection & Indemnity (P&I)
Legal structureFor-profit commercial underwritersMutual, non-profit shipowner association
Core coveragePhysical damage to hull, main engine, auxiliaries, equipmentThird-party liabilities — crew injury, cargo damage, pollution, wreck removal
Policy periodFixed 12-month time policy (GMT midnight expiry)Continuous mutual policy year (traditionally from 20 February)
📄 Structural Dissection of the International Hull Clauses (IHC)

The IHC is divided into Principal Insuring Conditions, Additional Clauses, and Claims Provisions. Key clauses examinable at MEO Class 1 level:

  • Inchmaree Clause: covers consequential damage to surrounding machinery from a boiler burst, shaft breakage, latent defect, crew negligence, or barratry — but explicitly excludes the cost of repairing or replacing the defective part itself, and requires the assured to have exercised due diligence.
  • Leased Equipment Clause: covers loss/damage to onboard equipment not owned by the shipowner but installed for operational use, provided it was declared at policy inception.
  • Parts Taken Off Clause: extends cover to machinery removed for shoreside maintenance, capped at a continuous 60-day window off the vessel.
  • Pollution Hazard Clause: indemnifies the shipowner for total loss/damage caused by a government authority acting to prevent an impending pollution hazard from a casualty, provided the owner was not negligent.
⚔ The 3/4ths Collision Liability (Running Down) Clause
Total Third-Party Collision Financial Liability | +---> [3/4ths paid by H&M Underwriters] (up to 75% of insured value limit) | +---> [1/4th paid by the P&I Club mutual pool]

This clause creates a separate contract inside the H&M policy under which the underwriter indemnifies the shipowner for three-fourths of third-party legal liability from a physical collision; the remaining one-fourth is traditionally absorbed by the shipowner's P&I Club. Explicit exclusions from the H&M side of this clause (ITCH Clause 8.4): (1) removal or disposal of wrecks, obstructions, or cargoes; (2) any real or personal property or thing whatsoever except other vessels or property on other vessels — meaning Clause 8 never responds to collisions with fixed or floating objects such as buoys, jetties, or shore structures, only to other vessels; (3) cargo on board the insured vessel, and the insured vessel's own commercial engagements/loss of use; (4) loss of life, personal injury, or occupational illness; (5) pollution or environmental damage — these excluded categories are instead picked up by the P&I Club.

⚖ The Sistership Arbitration Protocol

If two vessels owned or managed by the same company collide, a company cannot legally sue itself. The Sistership Clause resolves this conflict of interest by treating the two vessels as separate legal entities, with all liability and quantum questions referred to a Sole Arbitrator mutually agreed between the underwriters and the assured.

🎓 CE Oral Tip (Nair): Nair likes to test whether you can correctly route a single casualty's costs across H&M, P&I, and General Average in one breath. Practice narrating a scenario (e.g. an engine-room fire spreading to cargo) and assigning each cost bucket out loud before you walk into the oral.
🎯 Oral Q&A
Q1. If a main engine crankshaft fails due to a long-standing latent metallurgical defect, what repair costs are covered under the Inchmaree Clause?
The Inchmaree Clause covers consequential damage to surrounding components — engine block, bedplate, connecting rods — caused by the crankshaft failure. It explicitly excludes the cost of manufacturing, shipping, and installing the replacement crankshaft itself, since that is the defective part; that cost is borne by the shipowner.
Q2. Explain the operational difference between Subrogation and Contribution.
Subrogation applies after an underwriter has settled a claim: they inherit the assured's legal right to recover from the third party who actually caused the loss, so the assured cannot collect twice. Contribution applies when the same risk was over-insured across multiple policies: each underwriter pays only its proportional share of the loss, preventing the assured profiting by claiming the full amount from every policy.
Q3. Why does the 3/4ths Collision Liability Clause exclude wreck removal and pollution costs, and who covers them instead?
The 3/4ths Clause is deliberately scoped to direct physical hull-to-hull collision damage only. Wreck removal, pollution remediation, and personal injury are high-liability, hard-to-quantify-in-advance risks, so they are carved out of the H&M clause entirely and covered instead by the Protection & Indemnity Club, which also picks up the remaining one-fourth of the collision layer.
Q4. Your vessel collides with an unmanned mooring buoy while manoeuvring in a fairway. Does the 3/4ths Collision Liability Clause respond?
No. Clause 8.4 of the ITCH expressly excludes any real or personal property or thing whatsoever except other vessels or property carried on other vessels — a mooring buoy, jetty, or any other fixed or floating object is outside the clause's scope entirely. Liability for striking a fixed or floating object is instead a Protection & Indemnity matter, not an H&M collision-clause claim, even though the physical mechanism (a collision) looks identical to a ship-to-ship case.

🧠 Memory Box
  • G-I-I-S-C-L-P — Good Faith, Insurable Interest, Indemnity, Subrogation, Contribution, Loss Minimisation, Proximate Cause: the Seven Doctrines.
  • H&M = the ship's own body; P&I = everyone else's problem — quick way to sort a casualty's costs.
  • 3/4 H&M, 1/4 P&I — the collision-liability split; wreck/pollution/injury always sit with P&I.

Scenario

A container ship suffers a main engine crankcase explosion, is towed to a port of refuge, and cargo is jettisoned en route to lighten the vessel for safe manoeuvring.

Cost Allocation

Physical damage to the engine and surrounding machinery → H&M (Inchmaree Clause, minus the defective part itself). Towage and port-of-refuge costs incurred to save the venture → General Average, shared by ship and cargo interests. Any third-party injury or pollution arising from the incident → P&I. The jettisoned cargo's value → General Average sacrifice, since it was an intentional, reasonable act for the common safety of ship and remaining cargo.

On My Vessel (Maersk A/S container tonnage)

After any engine-room casualty, my first three calls as Chief Engineer are Class (survey requirement), Technical Superintendent (H&M claim trigger), and Master (for P&I/GA notification) — keeping these three tracks separate from the outset avoids costly confusion later in the claims process.
