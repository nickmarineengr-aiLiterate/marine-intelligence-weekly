Topic 47

International Convention on Load Lines, 1966 — Freeboard, Reserve Buoyancy & Type A/B Ship Classification

Load Line Convention · Stability/Construction Uday pp. 251–256, 259–261 Examiner: Rajappan High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: The source draft's Fresh Water Allowance formula was stated as FWA = Δ/(40·TPC) mm — this denominator is wrong by a factor of 10. The correct, IMO/Barrass-standard formula is FWA = W/(4×TPC) mm, where W is displacement (tonnes) at the summer load waterline and TPC is tonnes-per-centimetre immersion at that draft. This has been corrected below; do not use the "40" version in the oral or written exam. The Tropical Allowance formula (Summer Draft ÷ 48) in the source draft is correct as stated and required no change.
📖 Definition

The International Convention on Load Lines, 1966 (as modified by the Protocol of 1988) is the statutory framework governing the calculation, assignment, and physical marking of the minimum permissible freeboard for merchant ships on international voyages. Freeboard is the vertical distance measured amidships from the waterline to the upper edge of the deck line at the freeboard deck. By capping the maximum load draft, the Convention guarantees a minimum reserve buoyancy margin, limits the shipping of green water on exposed decks, protects the hull girder from overloading, and reduces the risk of progressive flooding through deck and hull openings.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)
  • Reserve buoyancy is the un-immersed volumetric percentage of the hull above the waterline — it is what generates the righting energy the ship needs to survive heavy weather or a localized breach; freeboard is the physical mechanism by which the Convention guarantees a minimum quantity of it.
  • As Chief Engineer you do not calculate the assigned freeboard yourself, but any unauthorized hull, superstructure, or air-pipe modification you sanction can silently invalidate it — this is a recurring Rajappan trap linking engine-room modification work (e.g. adding a funnel casing vent, altering an air pipe height) back to statutory freeboard assignment.
  • Fresh Water Allowance and Tropical Allowance calculations are routine loading-computer inputs; examiners expect you to sanity-check the loadicator's output against a hand calculation, not just trust the software.

🎓 CE Oral Tip (Rajappan): Rajappan will hand you a displacement and TPC value and ask you to compute FWA on the spot — always state the formula first (W ÷ 4TPC, NOT ÷40TPC) before substituting numbers, since he specifically probes candidates who have memorized a garbled formula from secondhand notes.
🎯 Oral Q&A
Q1. Distinguish a Type A ship from a Type B ship and explain how this affects assigned freeboard.
A Type A ship is designed exclusively to carry liquid bulk cargo, with small watertight gasketed steel-covered deck openings and low cargo-space permeability, so it resists progressive flooding well and is assigned a smaller baseline freeboard, permitting a deeper draft. A Type B ship covers everything else — bulk carriers, container ships, general cargo — with larger hatch openings and higher compartment permeability, so it needs a larger baseline freeboard to preserve adequate reserve buoyancy.
Q2. A vessel has displacement 48,000 t and summer TPC 52 t/cm. Calculate the FWA.
FWA = W ÷ (4 × TPC) = 48,000 ÷ (4 × 52) = 48,000 ÷ 208 = 230.8 mm, approximately 231 mm. This is the maximum permissible sinkage below the summer mark when loading in fresh water, to be corrected back to the seawater summer draft on reaching sea.
Q3. What six factors does an assigning authority consider when determining minimum freeboard?
Adequate hull girder structural strength at maximum draft; adequate reserve buoyancy above the upper deck line; adequate watertight and weathertight integrity of hull/deck openings; both intact and damage stability criteria being met; adequate protective arrangements for crew on deck (guard rails, lifelines, catwalks); and limitation of green-water shipping through appropriate forward hull geometry and freeboard height.

🧠 Memory Box
  • "W over 4-TPC" — FWA formula; the denominator is 4×TPC, never 40×TPC.
  • S.O.B.S. — Structural strength, Opening (watertight/weathertight) integrity, Buoyancy reserve, Stability (intact & damage) — the four things assigned freeboard protects.
  • "25 mm thick, Type A goes deep" — all Plimsoll hull markings are 25 mm thick; Type A ships get the smaller freeboard (deeper draft) of the two categories.

Standard vs. Timber Load Lines

The Plimsoll disc is positioned amidships on both sides of the hull. Timber load lines (marked to the left of the disc) permit a deeper loading draft when carrying a timber deck cargo, since the deck cargo itself adds buoyancy and impact protection — shifting the effective reserve-buoyancy line upward relative to the standard lines on the right (Summer, Winter, Tropical, Fresh, Tropical Fresh, and Winter North Atlantic).

Winter North Atlantic (WNA) Line

The WNA mark provides an additional safety margin for the severe wave environment of the North Atlantic in winter. It is mandatory only for vessels not exceeding 100 m in length transiting the designated zone in winter; for vessels over 100 m, WNA is not separately marked (except in some timber-line configurations), since larger hulls span wave crests more effectively.

Line Thickness

All load line markings — deck line, disc, and seasonal lines — are welded or permanently marked at 25 mm thickness. PSC inspectors treat illegible or obscured markings as a serious statutory non-conformity.

Topic 48

Statutory Survey Engineering — Load Line Framework, HSSC Protocols & Certificate Extensions

HSSC · Statutory Surveys · Load Line Certificate Uday pp. 252, 257–261 Examiner: Paul High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: The source draft cited IMO Resolution A.1156(32) for the HSSC Survey Guidelines. A.1156(32) (adopted 15 Dec 2021) is a real and correctly numbered resolution, but it has since been superseded: A.1186(33) (2023) revoked A.1156(32), and the current version is A.1207(34), Survey Guidelines under the HSSC, 2025. The reg-box below cites the current 2025 guidelines while noting the succession chain, since Kochi MMD examiners occasionally test whether candidates are quoting a stale resolution number.
📖 Definition

Statutory survey engineering under the Load Line framework covers the mandatory inspections, physical verifications, and structural audits confirming a ship continues to meet the 1966 Load Line Convention as modified by the 1988 Protocol, throughout its operating life. These surveys verify that hull strength, watertight integrity, reserve buoyancy features, and crew-safety installations remain within their certified statutory limits despite ongoing wear, corrosion, and any structural alteration.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)
  • The Chief Engineer is the person who actually organizes survey readiness — statutory certificates, stability booklet, loading manual, and loadicator software version must all be current and cross-checked before the surveyor boards.
  • Certificate extension provisions (issue-delay, transit, short-voyage) are commonly tested because they are easy to confuse with each other on an oral exam; know exactly which situation triggers which extension and its maximum duration.
  • HSSC exists specifically so that Load Line, SOLAS, and MARPOL survey cycles run on one aligned timeline — a Paul favourite is to ask why a ship doesn't need three separate uncoordinated dockings for three separate conventions.

🎓 CE Oral Tip (Paul): Paul often asks "your Load Line Certificate expires mid-ocean — what do you do?" The correct answer chain is: this is the transit extension (max 3 months), granted only to let the ship reach the designated survey port, and only "when proper and reasonable" — the ship cannot then leave that port again without completing the renewal survey.
🎯 Oral Q&A
Q1. Your Load Line Certificate is due to expire while the vessel is transiting deep-sea, far from a survey port. What are your options?
Under Article 14 of the Load Line Convention (1988 Protocol), if the ship is not in a port where it can be surveyed when the certificate expires, the Administration may extend validity by a maximum of 3 months, solely to allow the vessel to reach the designated survey port, and only where it is proper and reasonable to do so. The ship cannot depart that port again without completing the renewal survey and obtaining a new or endorsed certificate.
Q2. What is the annual survey time window under HSSC?
A 6-month window centred on the certificate's anniversary date — from 3 months before to 3 months after — within which the annual survey must be completed.
Q3. Name the three certificate extension provisions and their maximum durations.
Issue-delay extension, up to 5 months, when the renewal survey is complete but the new certificate cannot yet be issued or placed aboard; transit extension, up to 3 months, to let a ship reach its designated survey port; and short-voyage extension, up to 1 month, for ships engaged in short voyages.

🧠 Memory Box
  • M.A.C.S. — Markings, Appliances, Calibrations, Stability logs — the four things checked in an HSSC Load Line survey.
  • "5-3-1" — issue-delay extension 5 months, transit extension 3 months, short-voyage extension 1 month.

Key Preparation Items (Selected)

  • Clean, repaint, and verify the physical measurements of the deck line, Plimsoll disc, and seasonal marks for permanent legibility.
  • Confirm any structural modifications to hull, superstructure, or deck arrangements are formally declared and recorded before the surveyor boards.
  • Verify the approved Stability Booklet and structural Loading Manual are on the bridge, legible, and current.
  • Confirm the loadicator software version matches the Type Approval certificate, and run a calibration test scenario against the manual's published results (displacement, KG, bending moment, shear force) to demonstrate computational accuracy.
  • Disassemble and test air-pipe automatic closing heads (floating balls/discs) for free movement and tight seating; check flame screens on fuel-tank vents.
  • Test hatch cover gaskets, cleats, and watertight door seals; verify scupper non-return valves and overboard discharge check valves are free and operable.

On My Vessel (Maersk A/S container tonnage)

Container ships are Type B vessels with large hatch openings across multiple bays — hatch coaming/cover integrity and air-pipe closing devices are disproportionately weighted in survey time relative to a Type A tanker, since permeability and progressive-flooding risk are inherently higher.

Topic 49

Ship Tonnage Metrics & the Hong Kong Convention — IHM Structure & the Recycling Lifecycle

Tonnage · Hong Kong Convention · IHM Uday pp. 262–269 Examiner: Nair High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: The source draft gave the Hong Kong Convention entry-into-force date as "June 25, 2025." Per the established MIW platform correction, the verified entry-into-force date is 26 June 2025 — corrected below. The three entry-into-force conditions (≥15 states, ≥40% of world merchant GT, combined annual recycling volume ≥3% of that GT over the preceding 10 years) are stated correctly in the source and are retained unchanged. IMO Resolution MEPC.379(80) (2023 IHM Development Guidelines) is correctly cited.
📖 Definition

Ship tonnage metrics establish the quantitative basis for a vessel's volumetric capacity (tonnage) and weight configuration (tonnes) under international statutory frameworks, governing commercial classification and operating limits. The Hong Kong International Convention for the Safe and Environmentally Sound Recycling of Ships, 2009, extends this lifecycle management to the vessel's end-of-life phase, requiring hazardous structural components to be systematically logged, tracked, and safely remediated at recycling.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)
  • GT/NT and DWT/Lightweight are used for entirely different purposes — port dues and manning are volume-based (GT/NT), while chartering and scrap valuation are mass-based (DWT/Lightweight) — examiners test whether you conflate the two.
  • The Chief Engineer is the person actually maintaining IHM Part I currency across the vessel's life — every machinery replacement or pipe renewal needs a matching Material Declaration and SDoC, or the ship risks a PSC non-conformity or detention at end-of-life survey.
  • The IRRC's short 3-month validity (vs. the DASR's 5-year validity) is a frequently tested contrast — know which certificate belongs to the ship and which belongs to the recycling facility.

🎓 CE Oral Tip (Nair): Nair frequently asks you to differentiate GT from DWT in one breath, then pivots straight to "what's your role in maintaining IHM Part I?" — he is testing whether you understand that tonnage is a fixed registered index while the IHM is a living document the engine room must actively update.
🎯 Oral Q&A
Q1. Differentiate Gross Tonnage from Deadweight.
Gross Tonnage is a non-dimensional volumetric index representing total enclosed shipboard volume, calculated via a logarithmic coefficient applied to the ship's cubic capacity; it is used for port dues, manning, and safety regulation. Deadweight is a mass metric in tonnes — the difference between loaded displacement at the maximum draft and the ship's lightweight — representing cargo, fuel, ballast, stores, and crew, and used to define commercial carrying capacity.
Q2. As Chief Engineer, how do you maintain IHM Part I after an auxiliary engine replacement?
I require the engine manufacturer to supply a Material Declaration and Supplier's Declaration of Conformity confirming no banned materials (e.g. asbestos, PCBs) above statutory thresholds. Once verified, I log the installation details, manufacturer data, and component locations in the IHM logbook and integrate the update into IHM Part I so the documentation remains accurate for the next HSSC survey.
Q3. When did the Hong Kong Convention enter into force, and what were the entry conditions?
It entered into force on 26 June 2025, after meeting all three conditions: ratification by at least 15 states, representing at least 40% of world merchant gross tonnage, with combined annual ship recycling volume of the ratifying states not less than 3% of their combined gross tonnage over the preceding 10 years.

🧠 Memory Box

  • "IRRC = 3, DASR = 5" — IRRC validity 3 months (ship-side), DASR validity up to 5 years (yard-side).
  • "26/6/25" — Hong Kong Convention entry-into-force date.

Commercial Tanker Size Designations (by DWT)

  • Medium Range (MR): 10,000–50,000 DWT
  • Panamax: 50,000–80,000 DWT
  • Aframax: 80,000–120,000 DWT
  • Suezmax: 120,000–200,000 DWT
  • VLCC: 200,000–320,000 DWT
  • ULCC: above 320,000 DWT

Recycling Notification Sequence

Recycling contract executed with a DASR-holding SRF → IHM Parts II/III compiled by the ship's crew under CE direction → SRF develops the Ship Recycling Plan from the full IHM → SRP submitted to Flag State/RO → Final Survey confirms physical inventory matches IHM and DASR is valid → IRRC issued (3-month validity) → vessel delivered for dismantling → Statement of Completion issued by the SRF to its national authority, owner, and Flag State → Flag State executes Deletion of Registry.

On My Vessel (Maersk A/S container tonnage)

Container ships are Type B, high-GT, high-permeability hulls — their IHM Part I is comparatively data-heavy given large numbers of reefer plugs, electrical distribution runs, and coating systems across many holds, making ongoing Part I currency a heavier administrative load than on a simpler bulk carrier.
