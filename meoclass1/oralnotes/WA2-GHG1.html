Model Answer · Written Question (15 marks)
Discuss the key elements of the 2023 revised IMO strategy on the reduction of greenhouse gas emissions from ships. Detail the short-term operational carbon intensity metrics, evaluate their enforcement mechanisms, and analyze the technical options available to a ship manager faced with a non-compliant vessel.

1 · Definition & Purpose

The 2023 IMO Strategy on the Reduction of GHG Emissions from Ships (the "2023 IMO GHG Strategy"), adopted by Resolution MEPC.377(80) at MEPC 80 on 7 July 2023, is the binding international policy framework governing the phased decarbonisation of the global merchant fleet. It supersedes the more modest 2018 Initial Strategy and commits international shipping to a pathway that peaks GHG emissions as soon as possible and reaches net-zero GHG emissions by or around (i.e. close to) 2050.

The Strategy itself is a policy instrument, not a self-executing regulation — it is implemented through binding amendments to MARPOL Annex VI. The short-term measures (EEXI, CII, enhanced SEEMP) already give the Strategy legal teeth today; the mid-term technical and economic measures (a goal-based fuel standard and GHG pricing mechanism, intended as a new Chapter 5 of Annex VI) are still working through the IMO's adoption process — see the status note in Section 4.

2 · Importance to International Shipping

The Strategy fundamentally reshapes vessel valuation, chartering, and shipboard engineering management. Its headline levels of ambition, benchmarked against a 2008 baseline, are:

  • Carbon intensity of the ship: continued reduction through EEDI/EEXI phases for new and existing ships.
  • Fleet carbon intensity: reduce CO2 emissions per transport work, across international shipping, by at least 40% by 2030.
  • Uptake of zero or near-zero (ZNZ) GHG technologies, fuels and energy sources: at least 5%, striving for 10%, of the energy used by international shipping by 2030.
  • Net-zero target: peak emissions as soon as possible and reach net-zero by or around 2050.

Indicative checkpoints support these levels of ambition: at least 20%, striving for 30%, total annual GHG reduction by 2030 versus 2008; and at least 70%, striving for 80%, by 2040. For a Chief Engineer, these targets translate directly into carbon budgets that bind a ship's commercial trading life to its measured operational efficiency.

5 · Engineering Explanation & Core Technical Mechanisms

A. Energy Efficiency Design and Existing Ship Indices (EEDI / EEXI)

EEDI (newbuilds) and EEXI (existing ships) are structural, design-verification indices — calculated once per vessel (barring major conversion) — expressing theoretical CO2 grams emitted per tonne-mile of transport work at a reference design condition:

$$EEDI\ /\ EEXI = \frac{\left(\prod_{j} f_j\right)\left(\sum_i P_{ME(i)} \cdot C_{F\,ME(i)} \cdot SFC_{ME(i)}\right) + \left(P_{AE} \cdot C_{FAE} \cdot SFC_{AE}\right) - \left(\sum_l f_{eff(l)} \cdot P_{eff(l)} \cdot C_{FME} \cdot SFC_{ME}\right)}{f_i \cdot f_c \cdot Capacity \cdot V_{ref}}$$
EEDI / EEXI Formula in Words
EEDI / EEXI  =  Net CO₂ Emitted  ÷  Transport Work Capacity
Numerator (Net CO₂ Emitted, per Voyage-Equivalent) Take the main engine's rated power, multiply by that engine's carbon factor and its specific fuel consumption — this gives main-engine CO₂ output. Do the same for the auxiliary engines and add the two together. Then subtract any CO₂ credit earned from innovative energy-saving devices (e.g. shaft generators, waste-heat recovery) that reduce the effective power draw. Finally, multiply the whole result by a correction-factor product that accounts for ship-type features such as ice-class or general cargo capacity adjustments.
Denominator (Transport Work Capacity) Multiply the ship's cargo-carrying capacity (deadweight or gross tonnage, depending on ship type) by its reference speed at that capacity, then apply the capacity- and speed-correction factors.
Spoken Version "Sir, EEDI and EEXI express grams of CO₂ emitted per tonne-mile of transport work at a reference design condition. The numerator is the net CO₂ output — main engine power times its carbon factor times specific fuel consumption, plus the same for the auxiliary engines, minus any credit for energy-saving devices, all scaled by ship-type correction factors. The denominator is the ship's capacity multiplied by its reference speed. A ship complies when its attained value is at or below the required value for its size and ship-type phase."
Same Formula Shape — So What Actually Differs? EEDI and EEXI use the identical structure above, but they are not the same calculation in practice, because the value plugged into $P_{ME}$ differs:

A ship complies when its attained EEDI/EEXI is at or below the required value set by the applicable reduction-factor phase.

Required EEDI / Required EEXI — MARPOL Annex VI, Regulations 24 & 25

The formulas above give the ship's attained value. Compliance is judged against a separate required value, defined directly in the Convention text (MARPOL Annex VI consolidated edition, as amended by Res. MEPC.328(76), MEPC.361(79), MEPC.362(79)):

$$Required\ EEDI = \left(1 - \frac{X}{100}\right) \cdot \text{Reference line value} \qquad \text{[Reg. 24]}$$ $$Required\ EEXI = \left(1 - \frac{Y}{100}\right) \cdot \text{Reference line value} \qquad \text{[Reg. 25]}$$ $$\text{Reference line value} = a \cdot b^{-c} \qquad \text{[common to both]}$$
Required EEDI / EEXI Formula in Words
Required Value  =  (1 − Reduction Factor)  ×  Reference Line Value
Reference Line Value (the common baseline) A curve fitted to historical fleet performance for each ship type, expressed as a constant $a$ multiplied by the ship's own size parameter (DWT or GT, called $b$ here) raised to a negative exponent $c$ — larger ships sit lower on the curve. Both EEDI and EEXI are measured against the same reference line for a given ship type; only the reduction factor applied to it differs.
Reduction Factor — X (EEDI) vs Y (EEXI) Subtract the reduction factor (as a percentage) from 100%, then multiply that surviving fraction by the reference line value. X (Table 1) increases through EEDI Phase 1 → 2 → 3 as build year gets later — a newbuild delivered today faces a stricter X than one delivered in 2015. Y (Table 3) is fixed at whatever percentage corresponds to the EEDI Phase 2 level for most ship types — except container ships, cruise ships, LNG carriers and gas carriers, which are held to the accelerated Phase 3 level instead (see the table above).
Spoken Version "Sir, both Required EEDI and Required EEXI are calculated the same way — one minus the reduction factor, over 100, multiplied by the EEDI reference line value for that ship type. The reference line itself is a curve, a times the ship's capacity to the power of minus c, common to both indices. What differs is only which reduction-factor table applies: X from Table 1 for EEDI, which gets stricter with each build-year phase, or Y from Table 3 for EEXI, which is generally frozen at the Phase 2 level, except for four ship types held to Phase 3."

B. Operational Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII)

Unlike the design indices, CII is a dynamic, retrospective operational metric calculated at the close of each calendar year from actual logged performance:

$$Attained\ CII_{annual} = \frac{\sum_j M_j \cdot C_{Fj}}{Capacity \cdot D_{att}}$$
Attained CII Formula in Words
Attained CII  =  Total CO₂ Emitted (year)  ÷  (Capacity × Distance Sailed)
Numerator (Total CO₂ Emitted Over the Calendar Year) For each fuel type consumed over the year, take the total mass burned and multiply it by that fuel's carbon conversion factor. Add these fuel-wise totals together to get the ship's total logged CO₂ output for the year.
Denominator (Transport Work Actually Performed) Multiply the ship's capacity (deadweight or gross tonnage) by the total distance actually sailed over the year, taken from the oil record book or GPS log.
Spoken Version "Sir, the Attained CII numerator is the total CO₂ emitted over the calendar year — the sum, across all fuels burned, of each fuel's mass multiplied by its carbon conversion factor. The denominator is the ship's capacity multiplied by the actual distance sailed that year. Dividing the two gives grams of CO₂ per capacity-mile, which is then compared against the Required CII reference line to assign the annual A-to-E rating."
Numerator Worked as a Formula — Multi-Fuel Ship (e.g. VLSFO + MDO) A container ship rarely burns one fuel all year — main engine on VLSFO (Heavy Fuel Oil family), auxiliary/boiler on MDO (Marine Diesel Oil) during ECA transits and manoeuvring. The numerator is simply each fuel's annual mass multiplied by its own $C_F$, added together:
$$\text{Numerator} = \left(M_{VLSFO} \times C_{F,\,HFO}\right) + \left(M_{MDO} \times C_{F,\,MDO}\right) + \dots$$
Using the IMO's standard $C_F$ values (Res. MEPC.281(70), Appendix IX table — same factors used for IMO DCS reporting): HFO/VLSFO family = 3.114, MDO/MGO = 3.206, LFO = 3.151, LNG = 2.750, Methanol = 1.375, Ethanol = 1.913 (all in tonnes CO₂ per tonne fuel). Worked example: a ship burning 8,000 t VLSFO and 500 t MDO in a calendar year —
$$\text{Numerator} = (8{,}000 \times 3.114) + (500 \times 3.206) = 24{,}912 + 1{,}603 = 26{,}515\ \text{t CO}_2$$
This 26,515 t CO₂ figure is then divided by (Capacity × Distance Sailed) to give the Attained CII in grams CO₂ per capacity-mile. Every additional fuel type on board — LNG, methanol, biofuel blends — simply adds one more $(M_j \times C_{Fj})$ term to the same sum; the formula structure never changes, only the number of terms.

Where $M_j$ is total mass (g) of fuel type $j$ consumed over the calendar year; $C_{Fj}$ is the carbon conversion factor for fuel $j$; $D_{att}$ is total distance travelled over ground (nautical miles), from the oil record book / GPS log. The Attained CII is compared against a ship-type- and size-specific Required CII reference line to assign an annual rating of A (major superior) through E (inferior).

Correction Factors & Voyage Adjustments (G5) — Often Omitted, Heavily Tested

Attained CII is not simply raw annual fuel mass over distance — Res. MEPC.355(78) allows specific, legitimate deductions from the numerator before the final A–E rating is struck, recognising that some fuel consumption is not representative of the ship's normal carbon efficiency:

  • Voyage adjustments ($FC_{voyage}$): fuel consumption may be excluded for voyages undertaken solely for securing the safety of the ship or saving life at sea (applies to all ships), and for voyages in ice conditions (ice-classed ships only).
  • $AF_{TankerSTS}$: a correction factor for oil tankers engaged in ship-to-ship (STS) transfer voyages, recognising the additional fuel burned manoeuvring alongside another tanker rather than on passage.
  • $AF_{TankerShuttle}$: a correction for shuttle tankers operating with dynamic positioning during offshore loading.
  • $FC_{electrical}$: deducts fuel used to generate additional electrical load for reefer containers, gas-carrier cargo handling, or LNG/tanker cargo-system equipment — cargo-driven consumption, not propulsion inefficiency.
  • $FC_{boiler}$ and $FC_{others}$: deduct fuel used for tanker cargo heating or inert gas system operation.

Each correction is applied to the relevant fuel-mass term before it enters the $\sum_j M_j \cdot C_{Fj}$ numerator — this is the practical answer to "how is Attained CII actually calculated," and a candidate who only states the raw formula without mentioning corrections is giving an incomplete answer.

"Is CII Being Combined with EEXI?" — What's Actually Happening

There is no literal merger of the two indices into a single formula — this is a common misconception worth pre-empting directly in an oral answer. What is true: MEPC 83 (April 2025) completed Phase 1 of the IMO's review of the short-term GHG measures, which examined CII, SEEMP, and EEXI together as one package, since all three share the same enforcement chain under MARPOL Annex VI regulation 28 (technical file → SEEMP Part III → Statement of Compliance). Out of that joint review: the CII G3 reduction factors were extended to 2030 (above), while EEXI itself was not substantively reopened. Phase 2 of the review is now underway — per IMO's own published timeline it runs Spring 2026 to Spring 2028, and will look at further developing CII metrics, enhancing the SEEMP framework, and — most relevant to your question — ensuring synergies between the CII/EEXI energy-efficiency framework and the IMO Net-Zero Framework (Chapter 5) once that is adopted. So: EEXI and CII remain two separate, independently certified regimes (EEXI under the one-time IEE Certificate, CII under the annual Statement of Compliance) linked only structurally — a ship's EEXI technical-file parameters (capacity, reference speed) feed conceptually into CII's denominator logic, but there is no regulatory plan to fuse them into one index. Treat any claim of a fixed "MEPC 85, October 2026" decision date on this with caution — IMO's own Phase 2 work plan gives a two-year window (Spring 2026–Spring 2028), not a single decisive session.

6 · Chief Engineer & Flag State Responsibilities

Chief Engineer Directives

  • DCS data integrity: daily logging of fuel consumption by distinct consumer (main engine, auxiliary engines, boilers), split "underway"/"not underway", from calibrated flow meters or verified soundings.
  • SEEMP Part III execution: implement the ship-specific 3-year rolling improvement plan to maintain or recover at least a 'C' rating.
  • Voyage optimisation: weather routing, main engine RPM/shaft-power limits (EPL/ShaPoLi), and trim management to minimise fuel burn without breaching safe operating limits.

Flag State / Recognized Organization (RO) Responsibilities

  • Verification: audit annual fuel-consumption data submitted under IMO DCS, due by 31 March of the following year.
  • Certification: issue the annual Statement of Compliance (SoC) for fuel consumption and carbon intensity.
  • Enforcement: require a SEEMP Part III corrective action plan for a 'D' rating three years running, or a single-year 'E' rating.

7 · Practical Shipboard Application & Case Study

Case Study — Capesize Bulk Carrier Trending to a 'D' Rating

A large bulk carrier on standard VLSFO projects a 'D' rating by Q3 year-end. Main engine: 2-stroke, electronically controlled, MCR 16,500 kW; average consumption ≈46.5 MT/day at 13.5 knots; boiler ≈1.8 MT/day in port, ≈0.5 MT/day at sea.

[Analyse Q3 DCS data] -> [Apply overridable EPL] -> [Trim & routing optimisation] -> [Re-project year-end CII]
  1. Engine Power Limitation (EPL): electronically de-rate maximum shaft output (e.g. to ~65% MCR), enforcing an eco-speed. Fuel consumption falls with the cube of speed for a given hull/propeller combination:
$$P_{new} = P_{old} \cdot \left(\frac{V_{new}}{V_{old}}\right)^{3}$$
Speed–Power Cube Law in Words
New Power  =  Old Power  ×  (New Speed ÷ Old Speed)³
Take the ratio of the new speed to the old speed, cube that ratio (multiply it by itself three times), then multiply by the old power requirement to get the new power requirement. In plain terms: a small cut in speed produces a much larger cut in power — and therefore fuel burn — because resistance grows disproportionately with speed for a fixed hull and propeller. For example, a 10% speed reduction (ratio 0.9) cuts power demand by roughly 27% (0.9³ ≈ 0.73), not 10%.
Spoken Version "Sir, the cube law states that required power is proportional to the cube of speed for a given hull and propeller. So the new power equals the old power multiplied by the ratio of new speed to old speed, raised to the power of three. This is why modest speed reduction under EPL delivers a disproportionately large fuel and CII benefit."
  1. Auxiliary load optimisation: clean boiler smoke tubes to improve heat-transfer efficiency; concentrate non-essential high electrical loads (ballast treatment, deck-crane testing) into port stays; run a single auxiliary engine at sea where safe.
  2. Trim optimisation: adjust ballast distribution to keep the bulbous bow effectively submerged and minimise wave-making resistance.

Result: reducing speed and cleaning up auxiliary consumption lowers grams of CO2 per tonne-mile enough to move the projected rating from 'D' back toward 'C' by the December 31 close — illustrating how CII is managed operationally rather than through hardware retrofit alone.

8 · Survey Requirements (HSSC) & Port State Control Focus

Survey Requirements

  • Initial survey: on delivery, or before issue of the International Energy Efficiency (IEE) Certificate — verifies the EEDI/EEXI technical file and certified SEEMP Part I.
  • Sea-trial verification (tightened from 1 May 2026): Res. MEPC.403(83) (adopted 11 April 2025, MEPC 83) amends the EEDI Survey & Certification Guidelines (Res. MEPC.365(79)) so that, for sea trials conducted on or after 1 May 2026, power curves must be built from measured ship speed and measured main engine output, corrected per the updated ISO 15016:2025 standard or ITTC Recommended Procedure 7.5-04-01-01.1 (2024) — the older ISO 15016:2015 basis is no longer valid after that date. Practical tightening: ship-length-dependent maximum wind-speed limits, mandatory wave-buoy calibration data submitted to the verifier, and a stricter, standardised wave-correction priority sequence. This is a genuine increase in verification rigor at the final (sea-trial) stage — not a shift to ongoing operational EEDI monitoring; EEDI remains a one-time design-stage index.
  • Periodical/annual audits: confirm any retrofit affecting installed power (ducting, propeller boss fins, EPL seals) matches the technical file.
  • SEEMP Part III verification: confirms an approved, ship-specific, multi-year improvement plan exists and is followed.

Port State Control Focus

  1. Document check: validity of the Statement of Compliance for the preceding calendar year.
  2. SEEMP Part III onboard verification: documented evidence the crew is executing the plan's stated optimisation measures.
  3. EPL/ShaPoLi integrity: physical check of tamper-evident seals on fuel-rack limiters, or electronic shaft-power-limiting logs.
⚠ High-Risk Detention Vectors
Missing or expired Statement of Compliance after 31 May of the reporting year.
Broken or unlogged EPL override without a documented safety justification in the engine room logbook.
No corrective action plan filed in SEEMP Part III after consecutive non-compliant ratings.

10 · Examination Question Bank

Part 1 — Strategic Architecture of the 2023 Revised Strategy

The 2023 IMO Revised GHG Strategy accelerates the regulatory path to full decarbonisation, replacing the 2018 Initial Strategy's 50% absolute-reduction goal with net-zero GHG emissions by or around 2050. Intermediate indicative checkpoints — at least 20% (striving for 30%) reduction by 2030 and at least 70% (striving for 80%) by 2040, both against 2008 — track progress, alongside a target that ZNZ energy sources reach at least 5% (striving for 10%) of total fleet energy use by 2030.

Part 2 — Mechanics of Short-Term Carbon Intensity Indicators

EEXI is a one-off design constraint (attained EEXI ≤ required EEXI), verified for the International Energy Efficiency Certificate. CII is a retrospective annual operational index producing an A–E rating from real logged performance.

Part 3 — Enforcement and Corrective Action

Enforcement runs through the Statement of Compliance: a vessel cannot trade internationally without a valid SoC. A single-year 'E' rating, or 'D' for three consecutive years, triggers a mandatory SEEMP Part III corrective action plan, which must identify the operational root causes and be audited/approved by the Flag Administration or an authorised RO before a new SoC issues.

Part 4 — Technical and Operational Remediation Pathways

  • Engine Power Limitation (EPL): mechanical or electronic fuel-rack lock capping maximum shaft output, forcing a lower, more efficient operating speed.
  • Hydrodynamic upgrades: Mewis ducts, propeller boss cap fins, low-friction anti-fouling coatings.
  • Energy-saving technologies: wind-assisted propulsion (e.g. Flettner rotors), air lubrication systems.
  • Fuel transition: biofuel blending, or conversion/dual-fuel retrofit to LNG, methanol, or ammonia.
8 marks
Explain the technical differences between EEXI and CII, and outline the administrative consequences of an 'E' rating.
  • EEXI: design constraint, attained ≤ required, single verification event tied to the IEE Certificate.
  • CII: retrospective operational metric, recalculated every calendar year from DCS-logged performance.
  • 'E' rating consequence: mandatory SEEMP Part III revision with a documented corrective action plan, audited and approved by the Flag Administration/RO before a Statement of Compliance can be reissued.
10 marks
Describe the structural updates of the 2023 IMO Revised GHG Strategy over the 2018 Initial Strategy, focusing on interim checkpoints and alternative energy targets.
  • 2018 vs 2023 headline target: 50% absolute reduction by 2050 (2018) superseded by net-zero by or around 2050 (2023).
  • Interim checkpoints: at least 20% (striving 30%) reduction by 2030; at least 70% (striving 80%) by 2040, both vs 2008.
  • Fleet carbon-intensity target: at least 40% reduction per transport work by 2030.
  • New 2030 alternative-energy target: ZNZ technologies/fuels at least 5% (striving 10%) of total shipping energy use.

11 · Summary & Memory Boxes

📌 EXAM FOCUS
EEXI is a fixed design index verified at survey milestones. CII is a dynamic operational metric recalculated annually from live vessel logs.
A single-year 'E' rating, or 'D' for three consecutive years, triggers a mandatory Flag State audit and a required SEEMP Part III update.
CE Oral Tip: Watch auxiliary boiler fuel usage closely when tracking annual carbon intensity — steam generation during extended port stays adds to total fuel mass without adding to distance travelled ($D_{att}$), which can quietly degrade an otherwise secure 'C' rating.
⚠ PSC Alert
Officers routinely check the status of the overridable Engine Power Limitation (EPL) system. If the mechanical or electronic seal is broken, ensure the exact timestamp, GPS position, and safety justification are logged in the engine room logbook — undocumented overrides are a high-risk detention item.
