Topic 33

Harmonized Common Structural Rules (H-CSR) & Goal-Based Standards (GBS)

Structural / Class Uday pp. 176–180 Examiner: Rajappan ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: All figures in the source draft checked against IACS and IMO primary text. The SOLAS II-1/3-10 GBS timeline (contract ≥ 1 Jul 2016, keel ≥ 1 Jul 2017, delivery ≥ 1 Jul 2020) and the uniform 150 m threshold for both oil tankers and bulk carriers are confirmed correct against IMO/IACS/class-society sources — no correction required. Note for candidates: the H-CSR class-rule threshold for bulk carriers (L ≥ 90 m, per IACS UR) is a separate class-specific figure from the SOLAS statutory GBS threshold (L ≥ 150 m for both ship types) — examiners frequently test this distinction, so keep the two numbers and their sources separate in your answer.

📖 Definition

The Harmonized Common Structural Rules (H-CSR) are IACS's unified minimum technical requirements for hull scantling design, calculation, and verification of oil tankers and bulk carriers, replacing the earlier separate CSR-OT and CSR-BC rule sets. Goal-Based Standards (GBS) are the IMO's higher-level statutory philosophy under SOLAS Chapter II-1, Regulation 3-10: instead of prescribing exact formulas, GBS sets the safety objectives a ship's structure must meet — including a minimum 25-year design life — and leaves classification societies to develop rules (like H-CSR) that satisfy those objectives.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)

  • H-CSR removes the old commercial incentive for class societies to compete on lighter (cheaper but weaker) steel scantlings — every H-CSR-built hull meets the same structural floor regardless of which society classed it.
  • As CE, your loading computer's SWBM/SWSF limits are derived directly from these rules — exceeding 80–100% of permissible limits during cargo operations is a direct GBS/H-CSR compliance failure, not just an operational judgement call.
  • The 25-year design-life assumption underlies every fatigue and corrosion-margin calculation in your ship's structural drawings — it is why net scantlings, not gross, define your true remaining strength as the vessel ages.
  • PSC officers now inspect structural degradation against GBS/H-CSR parameters specifically, not just generic "corrosion" — understanding the rule basis helps you pre-empt detainable findings during your own internal rounds.

🔧 H-CSR Rule Architecture & Design Load Scenarios

Static (calm water, extreme cargo density) 2. Static + Dynamic (wave-induced pressure superimposed) 3. Impact (bow/bottom slamming, sloshing) 4. Fatigue (25-year cyclic stress spectrum)

Key working stress check: σd ≤ Ry / (γm · γs) — design equivalent stress from FEA must stay within yield stress reduced by material and structural safety factors. Net scantling principle: all strength checks use tgross − tcorrosion margin, i.e. the steel that will still be there after 25 years of wastage — not the as-built thickness.

CE Oral Tip — Rajappan Sir: Rajappan will often open with "What is the difference between H-CSR and GBS?" — candidates who answer "same thing" fail immediately. State clearly: GBS (SOLAS II-1/3-10) is the IMO's statutory goal ("what" — safety objectives, 25-year life, L ≥ 150 m for both types); H-CSR is IACS's class rule ("how" — the actual scantling formulas, L ≥ 150 m tankers / L ≥ 90 m bulkers). Also expect a direct question on the alternate-hold loading case — know that Hold 2/3 and 3/4 bulkhead shear peaks are the critical check points, not just total bending moment.

🎯 Oral Q&A

Q1: State the exact GBS length threshold and implementation timeline under SOLAS II-1/3-10.
GBS applies uniformly to both oil tankers and bulk carriers of 150 m in length and above — there is no separate 90 m threshold under GBS itself (that 90 m figure belongs to the class-specific H-CSR rules for bulk carriers only). The statutory timeline: building contract placed on or after 1 July 2016; in the absence of a contract, keel laid on or after 1 July 2017; or, regardless of contract/keel dates, delivery on or after 1 July 2020.
Q2: Differentiate Net Scantling from Gross Scantling philosophy.
Gross Scantling used the nominal as-built thickness directly for strength calculations, bundling structural capacity and corrosion allowance together. H-CSR's Net Scantling Approach subtracts the corrosion margin from the gross thickness first, then performs all hull girder, buckling and fatigue checks using only the remaining net thickness — guaranteeing the ship still has adequate strength at the end of its 25-year design life, at maximum permitted wastage.
Q3: What load combinations must the loading computer evaluate under H-CSR's Design Load Scenarios?
The loading computer must combine static calm-water loads (cargo pressure, sloshing/grab impact) with dynamic wave-induced hydrodynamic loads (sagging/hogging bending, shear, external hydrostatic head), checked against both yield and buckling limits, across standard, alternate, and accidental-flooding hold conditions.
Q4: Why is alternate hold loading structurally risky on a Capesize bulker, and how does H-CSR manage it?
Loading Holds 1/3/5 and leaving 2/4 empty raises the centre of gravity (reducing snap-rolling risk) but creates sharp local shear-force steps at the transverse bulkheads between the loaded/empty holds — a fatigue-crack risk at the lower hopper knuckles. H-CSR's Part 2 Ch 1 rules mandate explicit alternate-hold-loading DLS checks (not present in legacy prescriptive rules), forcing designers to verify bulkhead shear capacity for this exact scenario rather than only the whole-ship bending moment.
Q5: What is the Chief Engineer's practical role in GBS/H-CSR compliance during cargo operations?
The CE must ensure the class-certified loading computer is calibrated against class certificates, keep SWBM/SWSF within permissible limits (ideally ≤80% during multi-stage pours), synchronise ballast stripping with the loading sequence to avoid double-bottom overload, monitor deflection against strain-gauge telemetry where fitted, and conduct visual knuckle inspections after loading — all of which protect the GBS design-life assumption in practice, not just on paper.

Why the 2006 CSR Split Existed

The original 2006 CSR was developed independently for bulk carriers (led by concerns after several capesize losses) and oil tankers (led by post-Erika/Prestige double-hull requirements), by different working groups within IACS, on different timelines. This produced two rule sets with inconsistent load assumptions and safety margins for broadly similar hull-girder problems — the harmonisation into H-CSR (post-2010s) unified the underlying wave-load and FEA methodology while retaining ship-type-specific Part 2 chapters.

Residual Strength After Damage

H-CSR requires the residual ultimate vertical bending moment Mu after grounding/collision damage to satisfy Mu ≥ γR · Msw, where Msw is the permissible still-water bending moment in the damaged condition. This is distinct from the intact-condition ultimate strength check and reflects lessons from casualties where a vessel survived initial damage but failed structurally days later under residual sea loads.

Shear Flow in Closed Sections

For a hull cross-section under vertical shear Vv, the shear flow q(s) around the thin-walled girder is q(s) = q₀ − ∫(Vv·y(s)·t(s)/Iy)ds. The q₀ term (statically indeterminate for closed loops like a double hull) is solved using compatibility of twist — this is standard thin-walled beam theory (Bredt-Batho) applied to ship structures, examined at written-paper depth rather than oral depth.

Topic 34

Classification Society Notations & the Code for Recognized Organizations (RO Code)

Class / Statutory Uday pp. 181–186 Examiner: Paul ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: RO Code adoption/entry-into-force dates verified against the primary IMO resolutions: MSC.349(92) adopted 21 June 2013 and MEPC.237(65) adopted 17 May 2013; both confirm the Code "takes effect" (in practical terms, entered into force) on 1 January 2015 upon entry into force of the related SOLAS/Load Line/MARPOL amendments. Source draft's "adopted 2013, in force 1 Jan 2015" is correct — no correction required.

📖 Definition

Class Notations are the alphanumeric codes appended to a vessel's classification entry indicating that its design, construction, and machinery meet a Classification Society's technical rules. The Code for Recognized Organizations (RO Code) — adopted under Resolutions MSC.349(92) and MEPC.237(65) — is the mandatory international framework governing how Flag States assess, authorize, and oversee Classification Societies acting as Recognized Organizations (ROs) performing statutory certification on the Flag's behalf.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)

  • Your ship's Certificate of Class is not decorative — withdrawal or suspension of a mandatory notation automatically invalidates the linked statutory certificates (Safety Construction, Safety Equipment, IOPP), making the vessel legally unfit to sail.
  • Understanding the RO Code's Part 2 independence/impartiality requirements explains why RO surveyors cannot be pressured or "negotiated with" on findings — this protects you as CE when defending a legitimate defect report against commercial push-back.
  • Knowing that an RO cannot detain a vessel or independently interpret regulations tells you exactly when to escalate a dispute to the Flag Administration rather than expecting the attending surveyor to resolve it on the spot.
  • UMS/AUT-UMS notation compromise (e.g. alarm relay failure) has an immediate, defined CE response — revert to manned watch — this is one of the most commonly oral-examined "what would you do" scenarios.

CE Oral Tip — Paul Sir: Paul frequently probes the legal boundary question: "Can the class surveyor detain my ship?" The correct answer is an unambiguous no — ROs can suspend/withdraw notations and issue Conditions of Class, but only Flag State or Port State Control hold sovereign detention power. Also expect him to test the UMS defect scenario directly — know the sequence: suspend UMS mode → manned watch → logbook entry → notify RO and Flag → arrange Condition of Class for a supervised passage to repair port.

🎯 Oral Q&A

Q1: Can an RO issue an international detention order if it finds a serious structural deficiency?
No. Detention authority is a sovereign power reserved for Port State Control or the Flag Administration. An RO that finds a deficiency invalidating statutory certificates can recommend corrective action, issue a Condition of Class, or suspend certificates — then it must notify the Flag Administration for further enforcement.
Q2: What happens to statutory certificates if a mandatory class notation is suspended?
Statutory certificates (e.g. Safety Construction Certificate) are issued on the prerequisite that the vessel remains in class. If a mandatory notation is suspended or withdrawn due to unrectified defects, the foundation for the statutory certificate is broken — the certificate becomes invalid and the ship is not legally fit to sail internationally.
Q3: Why does RO Code Part 2 emphasise surveyor independence and impartiality?
To prevent commercial conflicts of interest compromising safety decisions. Surveyors must have no commercial interest in the design, manufacture, construction or maintenance of vessels they audit, ensuring structural assessments rest strictly on technical compliance rather than owner/yard pressure.
Q4: A UMS alarm relay to your cabin fails at sea — what do you do, step by step?
Immediately suspend UMS mode and revert to a continuous three-watch manned engine room. Log the failure time, affected components, and watch officers assigned. Draft and send a formal defect notification to the RO and Flag Administration. At the next port, coordinate with the RO surveyor — since the RO cannot unilaterally waive the notation requirement, expect a Condition of Class or Memorandum permitting a supervised passage to a repair facility under manned-watch conditions.
Q5: Differentiate mandatory and optional class notations with an example of each.
Mandatory notations (e.g. the Maltese Cross, 1A) define baseline hull/machinery standards — without them the Certificate of Class cannot be issued. Optional/additional notations (e.g. ESP, CSR, BWM(T), UMS) certify specialised capability or enhanced regimes beyond the baseline and are added where the vessel is equipped/operated for that purpose — ESP is effectively mandatory in practice for bulk carriers and oil tankers specifically, since SOLAS requires the enhanced survey regime for those ship types.

Why "Recognized" Organization, Not Just "Classification Society"

Not every function a Class Society performs is statutory. A society acts as an RO only for the specific statutory certification functions a Flag State has formally delegated to it in a written agreement under RO Code Part 1. The same organisation's purely commercial classification services (issuing class certificates unrelated to a flag delegation) are not RO Code-governed in the same sense — this distinction matters when discussing liability and appeal routes.

Supplementary Surveys as an Oversight Tool

RO Code Part 3 empowers Flag Administrations to conduct independent "supplementary surveys" — spot-check inspections carried out directly by flag surveyors on ships classed with an authorized RO, specifically to verify the RO's own performance rather than the ship's condition per se. This is the primary mechanism flags use to catch systemic RO underperformance before a casualty exposes it.

Practical Note on Blue Card vs Class Notation

Do not confuse RO Code class-notation suspension with financial-security instruments like the CLC Blue Card (covered in Topic 4 of this Part) — they are governed by entirely different conventions (RO Code vs CLC 1992) even though both can result in a PSC detention if invalid.

Topic 35

The Code on Noise Levels Onboard Ships (IMO Noise Code)

Safety / Machinery Uday pp. 187–191 Examiner: Senthil Medium Frequency
🔎 Verification note: The source draft's applicability statement — "applies to all commercial vessels of 1,600 GT and above" — is corrected here. Per Resolution MSC.337(91) and SOLAS Regulation II-1/3-12, the Code is mandatory for new ships of 1,600 GT and above (contract ≥ 1 Jul 2014, keel ≥ 1 Jan 2015, or delivery ≥ 1 Jul 2018). For existing ships, only the hazard/mitigation/PPE provisions apply, and only "as far as reasonable and practicable" at the Administration's satisfaction — it is not a blanket mandatory retrofit requirement. The dB(A) limit table and the 75/85/110 rule are confirmed correct against the primary Code text.

📖 Definition

The Noise Code, adopted under Resolution MSC.337(91) and made mandatory via SOLAS Regulation II-1/3-12, sets maximum permissible noise levels across shipboard spaces to (1) prevent noise-induced hearing loss, (2) preserve audibility of alarms and communication, and (3) protect crew rest and welfare. It replaced the earlier non-mandatory Resolution A.468(XII) of 1981.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)

  • Chronic high-decibel exposure in the ECR/EIR is a documented driver of human error in complex engineering tasks — noise management is a safety-critical, not just comfort, issue for your watchkeepers.
  • A degraded acoustic boundary (failed door seal, damaged insulation) can mask a genuine machinery or fire alarm — several PSC detentions arise from ECR noise exceeding 75 dB(A) specifically because it threatens alarm audibility, not just comfort.
  • The Noise Survey Report is a statutory document you should know how to read — it tells you the baseline design values against which any later degradation (e.g. after a silencer failure) is measured.
  • Hearing-protection selection (HML method) is a real occupational-health decision the CE is expected to make correctly, matching protector attenuation to the actual frequency profile of the noise source.

CE Oral Tip — Senthil Sir: Senthil likes to give a specific dB(A) figure (e.g. "114 dB(A) in the EIR — what do you do?") and expects you to correctly classify the zone against the 85/110/120 exposure bands and state the specific protective action, not just "wear ear protection." 🎯 Oral Q&A

Q1: What operational parameters are required during a statutory noise survey sea trial?
The vessel must be in a fully loaded or heavy ballast condition, on as straight a course as practical, with main engine output sustained at not less than 80% of MCR — ensuring the survey captures a representative full-seagoing acoustic profile.
Q2: An engine room space reads 114 dB(A). What must the Chief Engineer enforce?
114 dB(A) sits in the 110–120 dB(A) zone requiring double-attenuation, occasional-entry-only protocol. The CE must restrict routine watchstanding/maintenance in that space; anyone entering for urgent work must wear dual-layer hearing protection (Class 1 earmuffs plus earplugs) with total exposure strictly time-limited.
Q3: What is the purpose of an Octave Filter Set in a noise assessment?
A standard sound level meter gives total broadband sound pressure but not its frequency composition. An octave filter set (IEC 61260 Class 1) splits the signal into frequency bands, letting engineers identify whether noise is driven by low-frequency machinery vibration or high-frequency fluid/gas dynamics — essential for both structural insulation design and correct hearing-protector (HML) selection.
Q4: Does the Noise Code apply mandatorily to an existing ship built in 2005?
Not in the same way as a new ship. The Code's full noise-limit table is mandatory only for new ships (contract/keel/delivery thresholds from 2014–2018 onward). For existing ships, only the hazard-mitigation, PPE, and warning-sign provisions apply, and only as far as reasonable and practicable to the Administration's satisfaction — it is not a blanket structural retrofit mandate.
Q5: Give the six mandatory contents of an official Noise Survey Report.
Ship particulars (name, IMO number, GT, propulsion configuration); machinery particulars (RPM, generator loads, turbocharger parameters during test); measuring instrument details (Class 1 sound level meter serials/calibration); testing conditions (sea state, wind, draft); the full sound pressure level data log across all measurement points; and the general arrangement plan marking each measuring point.

Why 1,600 GT Was Chosen

The 1,600 GT threshold roughly captures the point at which vessels carry a full complement of crew accommodation spaces subject to meaningful acoustic design choices, while excluding very small craft where noise mitigation options are physically limited by hull size. Smaller new ships may still have the Code applied voluntarily "as reasonable and practical" at the Administration's discretion.

A-Weighting vs C-Weighting

The Code's dB(A) figures use A-weighting, which approximates human ear sensitivity across frequencies (de-emphasising very low and very high frequencies). Some assessments also record C-weighted (dB(C)) values; comparing LCeq − LAeq helps classify whether a noise source is low-frequency (difference >2 dB) or high/medium-frequency (difference ≤2 dB) dominant, which directly drives HML hearing-protector selection.

Relationship to the Working Environment (National) Regulations

Several flag states (Norway is a documented example) layer national occupational-noise regulations on top of the IMO Noise Code, applying the stricter of the two limits where they diverge. As CE on a Maersk (Danish flag / DMA) vessel, be aware flag-specific circulars may add requirements beyond the bare IMO Code text.

Topic 36

International Maritime Liability Regimes: CLC 1992 & the FUND Convention

Liability Law Uday pp. 192–200 Examiner: Nair ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: All SDR figures cross-checked and confirmed: CLC 1992 first-tier cap 89.77 million SDR (via the 2000 Amendments, in force globally November 2003); base 4.51 million SDR for ships ≤5,000 GT with 631 SDR/GT scaling above that; IOPC Fund 1992 standard cap 203 million SDR, conditional cap 300.74 million SDR; Supplementary Fund Protocol aggregate cap 750 million SDR. These match the platform's standing verified figures for the IOPC Fund. No correction required — this topic is a strong, accurate draft.

📖 Definition

The Civil Liability Convention (CLC) 1992 establishes strict liability for shipowners for pollution damage from persistent-oil spills, backed by compulsory insurance. The FUND Convention 1992 is the second-tier compensation mechanism, funded by oil-receiving industry levies, which pays out when claims exceed the CLC's shipowner liability cap. Together they form a two-tier (extendable to three-tier via the Supplementary Fund Protocol) international pollution-compensation system.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)

  • Strict liability means the coastal state does not need to prove your ship was negligent — only that persistent oil escaped from it. This changes how you should document any spill event: your engineering records exist to establish an exemption defence (Force Majeure, third-party sabotage, nav-aid failure), not to prove you weren't negligent.
  • The Blue Card and CLC Certificate are statutory documents you must verify are valid before entering any port with >2,000 tonnes of persistent oil in bulk — an expired certificate is an immediate, high-severity PSC detention trigger.
  • Knowing the persistent vs non-persistent oil distinction matters practically: a light-fuel or gasoline spill is NOT covered under CLC 1992 at all — it falls to the HNS Convention instead, which changes which certificates and response protocols are relevant.
  • BDN records (Bunker Delivery Notes) are your evidence trail for the density/classification of oil actually carried — critical if a liability dispute later turns on whether the discharged product was persistent or non-persistent.

📅 Historical Background

🎯 Oral Q&A

Q1: A vessel discharges non-persistent diesel — can the coastal state claim under CLC 1992?
No. CLC 1992 applies exclusively to persistent-oil pollution damage. Non-persistent oils dissipate quickly per their distillation profile and are excluded from CLC/FUND; claims instead route to the HNS Convention or applicable national legislation.
Q2: What is a P&I Blue Card, and is the 20 February renewal date a statutory requirement?
A Blue Card is a certificate issued by a vessel's P&I Club evidencing insurance cover matching the CLC's maximum liability limits — it underpins the Flag State's issuance of the statutory CLC Certificate of Insurance. The widely cited 20 February date is an industry-standard P&I policy renewal convention, not a rule written into the CLC 1992 treaty text itself.
Q3: Outline the tiered compensation structure once CLC 1992 limits are exhausted.
Tier 1 (CLC 1992, shipowner P&I) caps at 89.77 million SDR via the 2000 Amendments. Once exhausted, Tier 2 (FUND 1992/IOPC Fund) provides a standard aggregate cap of 203 million SDR, rising to 300.74 million SDR conditionally if major-contributor thresholds are met. States that ratified the post-2003 Supplementary Fund Protocol access a Tier 3 aggregate cap of 750 million SDR across all tiers combined.
Q4: Under what conditions does a shipowner lose the right to limit liability entirely?
The right is lost only if it is proven the damage resulted from the shipowner's own personal act or omission committed with intent to cause such damage, or done recklessly with knowledge that such damage would probably result — a very high evidentiary bar, deliberately set to preserve the limitation regime's insurability in ordinary negligence cases.
Q5: Calculate the CLC 1992 liability cap for a 65,000 GT tanker.
Using Total Liability = 4.51 million SDR + [631 × (GT − 5,000)]: 4.51M + [631 × 60,000] = 4.51M + 37.86M = 42.37 million SDR — well under the 89.77 million SDR absolute maximum, so the formula result applies directly.

Why Strict Liability Instead of Fault-Based Liability

Pre-Torrey Canyon pollution claims required victims to prove shipowner negligence in ordinary tort, which was slow and often failed against well-resourced defendants. CLC's strict liability inverts this: liability attaches automatically on proof the oil escaped from the ship, subject only to the narrow listed exemptions (war, force majeure, third-party sabotage, nav-aid negligence). This dramatically speeds claim settlement for coastal communities and cleanup authorities.

Why the FUND Exists Separately From CLC

Making shipowners liable for unlimited amounts would make marine insurance commercially unworkable — P&I Clubs price cover against a capped exposure. The FUND spreads the excess-of-cap risk across the oil-receiving industry (the cargo's ultimate economic beneficiaries) via per-tonne levies on entities receiving >150,000 tonnes of contributing oil annually, rather than leaving victims uncompensated once the shipowner's cap is reached.

SDR as the Unit of Account

Special Drawing Rights (SDR) are an IMF-defined basket-currency unit, chosen specifically so liability limits remain stable in real terms across currencies and inflation over decades, rather than being fixed in a single national currency that could depreciate and erode the real value of victim compensation.
