Ship Recycling Regulations, Break-Up Voyages & End-of-Life Environmental Management

HKC · EUSRR · IHM Uday pp. 276–280 (approx.) Examiner: Nair / Senthil ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: The source draft's Hong Kong Convention entry-into-force date was given only as "June 2025"; cross-checked against the IMO's own confirmation and corrected to the exact date, 26 June 2025 (Bangladesh's ratification in June 2023 triggered the 24-month countdown to entry into force). The EUSRR (Regulation (EU) No 1257/2013) entry-into-force date is confirmed as 30 December 2018, not a generic "December 2018" — both dates now stated precisely. The 500 GT application threshold, the three-part IHM structure, and the PFOS/HBCDD EUSRR additions were checked against the IMO HKC summary and the EU Regulation text and are correct as given. No other numeric errors were found in this topic's source draft.
📖 Definition

Ship recycling is the process of dismantling a vessel at the end of its operating life to recover reusable steel, non-ferrous metals, and machinery, while systematically identifying and controlling the hazardous materials built into the ship over its lifetime. Modern regulation exists to move the industry away from the unregulated "beaching" methods historically used at yards in South Asia towards audited, worker-safe, environmentally sound facilities, governed primarily by the Hong Kong International Convention for the Safe and Environmentally Sound Recycling of Ships, 2009 (HKC).

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)
  • As Chief Engineer on any vessel approaching end-of-life, you personally own the accuracy of IHM Part I — every modification, spare, or repair fitted during your watch that contains a listed hazardous material must be logged with a Material Declaration (MD) or Supplier's Declaration of Conformity (SDoC), or the ship cannot legally recycle later.
  • Nair frequently tests whether candidates confuse the voluntary Green Passport era with the mandatory HKC/IHM era — know precisely which document is statutory and which was only guidance.
  • Bunker and sludge minimisation before the final voyage is a direct CE responsibility that affects both the environmental footprint of the breaking process and the commercial value realised by the owner.

🎓 CE Oral Tip (Nair): Nair's standard trap is "when did HKC enter into force, and why 2025 and not earlier?" He wants the tacit-acceptance mechanism explained: HKC required ratification by 15 states representing 40% of world merchant tonnage and a combined maximum annual ship recycling volume — Bangladesh's 2023 ratification was the final trigger, giving the 24-month countdown to 26 June 2025.
🎯 Oral Q&A
Q1. A vessel is being sold for scrap. What happens to her standard H&M and P&I cover, and what must the Chief Engineer verify before the final voyage?
Standard Hull & Machinery and P&I cover automatically lapses once the vessel sails with the declared intention of being broken up, since she is no longer trading commercially. The Chief Engineer must verify that the owner has arranged a specialised single-voyage Break-up Voyage Insurance Cover (also called a Decommissioning All-Risk Policy) with the P&I club/underwriters before departure — sailing without it is both an insurance and a regulatory breach.
Q2. What is the difference between an IHM under HKC and one under the EUSRR?
Both require an approved, audited IHM in Parts I–III. The EUSRR is the stricter regime: it applies not only to EU/UK-flagged tonnage but to any foreign-flagged ship calling an EU/UK port, restricts recycling to yards on the "European List" of approved facilities, and adds two extra monitored substances beyond the HKC baseline — Perfluorooctane Sulfonic Acid (PFOS) and Hexabromocyclododecane (HBCDD).
Q3. What documentation must be presented before an end-of-life vessel is authorised to enter a recycling facility?
The approved IHM (Parts I, II and III), the Ready for Recycling Certificate (RfRC) issued by the flag state, the facility's Ship Recycling Facility Management Plan (SRFMP), and the ship-specific Ship Recycling Plan (SRP). Immediately before beaching/docking, an independent marine chemist's "Gas-Free for Hot Work" certificate is also required.

Asbestos Mitigation Protocol

When ACMs are identified in IHM Part I, controls follow a strict sequence: isolate and contain under negative-pressure HEPA-filtered enclosure; saturate with surfactant-treated water before disturbance; use only hand tools or low-speed wet-cutting (high-speed dust-generating tools are prohibited); double-bag and transfer removed material directly to a dedicated hazardous-waste cell.

Pre-Arrival to Delivery Sequence (VLCC case study)

7-day prior notification to the MRCC/State Maritime Board → IHM and SRP submitted for scrutiny → tank cleaning, hot-water flushing and inert-gas purging → independent marine chemist issues "Gas-Free for Hot Work" certificate (O₂ >20.9%, LFL <1%) → beaching/docking authorised at high tide → final power/log handover → dismantling per the SRP's block sequence.

On My Vessel (Maersk A/S container tonnage)

Container ships rarely reach recycling within a normal career span at Maersk, but every drydocking or major retrofit generates the same IHM Part I discipline — any new insulation, gasket, or pipe lagging fitted must carry a signed MD/SDoC confirming zero asbestos content before it is accepted onboard.

International Ballast Water Management Convention (BWM 2004) & Technical Discharge Standards

BWM Convention · D-1/D-2 Uday pp. 281–285 (approx.) Examiner: Simon / Senthil ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: All figures in the source draft were checked against the BWM Convention text and IMO guidance and are correct: entry into force 8 September 2017 (35 states / 35% world tonnage threshold); Annex Sections A–E; the B-4 exchange distances (200 NM/200 m depth, falling back to 50 NM/200 m); the D-1 95% volumetric exchange standard; the D-2 organism limits (<10 viable/m³ for ≥50 μm, <10 viable/mL for 10–50 μm); and the D-2 indicator-microbe limits (V. cholerae <1 CFU/100 mL, E. coli <250 CFU/100 mL, intestinal enterococci <100 CFU/100 mL). The BWRB retention period (2 years onboard + 3 years company custody = 5 years total) matches Regulation B-2 guidance. No corrections were required in this topic.
📖 Definition

The International Convention for the Control and Management of Ships' Ballast Water and Sediments, 2004 (BWM Convention) is a mandatory framework to prevent, minimise and eventually eliminate the transfer of Harmful Aquatic Organisms and Pathogens (HAOPs) carried in ballast water between ecological regions. Ballast is essential for stability and structural integrity, but uncontrolled discharge is one of the four greatest threats to the world's oceans recognised by the IMO (alongside land-based pollution, overfishing, and habitat destruction).

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)
  • The Chief Engineer owns the operational readiness of the BWMS — filtration units, UV lamps, or chemical dosing pumps — and PSC officers routinely test whether the duty engineer can start the system and interpret its alarms from cold.
  • BWRB logging discipline is a classic detention trigger: PSC compares BWMS electronic logger timestamps against manual BWRB entries, and any mismatch invites deeper investigation.
  • D-1 exchange is now only a fallback; almost all trading commercial tonnage, including Maersk container vessels, must demonstrate D-2 compliance via a type-approved BWMS.

🎓 CE Oral Tip (Simon): Simon's follow-up after the B-4 distances is always "your route can't achieve 200 NM — what now?" The correct answer is not "delay the voyage": the Master requests authorisation from the destination Port State and adjacent coastal states to use a designated Port State exchange zone, with the variance fully logged in the BWRB.
🎯 Oral Q&A
Q1. What must be recorded in the Ballast Water Record Book, and for how long?
Every ballast operation — loading, internal transfer, treatment, discharge (to sea or reception facility), and any exceptional/accidental discharge — with date, time, geographic coordinates and volume. The BWRB must be retained onboard for a minimum of 2 years, then in company custody for a further 3 years — 5 years total.
Q2. What are the D-2 indicator-microbe limits, and why do they matter beyond ecology?
Toxicogenic Vibrio cholerae (O1/O139) <1 CFU per 100 mL, Escherichia coli <250 CFU per 100 mL, and intestinal Enterococci <100 CFU per 100 mL. These are public-health limits, not just ecological ones — cholera transmission via ballast discharge is a direct human health risk to coastal populations, which is why D-2 sampling is a PSC detention trigger, not merely a paperwork item.
Q3. Explain the sequential-empty-refill and flow-through methods for D-1 compliance.
Sequential empty-refill involves fully deballasting a tank, then refilling with open-ocean water — simple in concept but requires careful stability and hull-stress tracking during the empty phase. Flow-through (dilution) pumps new ocean water in at the bottom while displacing the original ballast from the top, continuing until at least three times the tank volume has been pumped through, achieving the required ≥95% volumetric exchange without the intermediate empty condition.

🧠 Memory Box
  • D-2 microbe quick-recall: Cholera <1 / E. coli <250 / Enterococci <100 (all per 100 mL).
  • "200 or 50, always 200 deep" — B-4 exchange distance fallback, depth constant at 200 m either way.
  • "2 + 3 = 5" — BWRB retention: 2 years onboard, 3 more in company custody.

Step-by-Step De-Ballasting via BWMS

Pre-operation briefing and BWRB setup → line up valves/pumps through the BWMS per the approved piping diagram → initiate discharge, engaging automation to match the system's rated treatment capacity → continuously monitor UV transmittance/chemical dosing/TRO sensors, closing the overboard valve immediately if parameters fall out of specification → log start/stop times, coordinates, volume and treatment status in the BWRB → post-operation flushing.

Survey Regime (Section E)

Initial survey before certificate issuance; annual survey within 3 months either side of the anniversary date; intermediate survey (replacing the 2nd or 3rd annual) with full operational testing and instrument calibration checks; renewal survey at intervals not exceeding 5 years.

On My Vessel (Maersk A/S container tonnage)

Large container vessels typically carry high-capacity UV or electro-chlorination BWMS units; the duty engineer's ability to demonstrate a cold start and interpret a UV-intensity-low alarm without prompting is one of the most common PSC/oral crossover checks.

The Law of Marine Salvage, Lloyd's Open Form (LOF) & the SCOPIC Framework

Salvage Convention 1989 · SCOPIC Uday pp. 286–290 (approx.) Examiner: Rajappan / Nair ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: Two figures were checked and one corrected. (1) The Article 14 uplift structure was verified against the IMO's own convention summary and independent legal commentary: the source draft's "premium of up to 30% to 100%" is correct — Article 14.2 grants a base discretionary uplift to 30% of expenses where environmental damage was minimised or prevented, which a tribunal may increase further, "if it deems it fair and just," to a maximum total increase of 100% of expenses. No change made. (2) The SCOPIC security figure in the source draft — "$2 million USD within 2 bank working days" — was checked against the published SCOPIC 2020 clause text and industry commentary (Lloyd's, ISU, P&I club circulars) and found to be incorrect on the amount: the correct, current Initial Security is US$3 million, inclusive of interest and costs, still payable within 2 working days of invocation. This figure has been corrected throughout this topic, including in the case study and Memory Box.
📖 Definition

Marine salvage covers any act to assist a vessel or property in danger in navigable waters. The modern framework — the International Convention on Salvage, 1989 — shifted the incentive structure from pure property recovery to include environmental protection, ensuring salvors are financially supported for pollution-prevention efforts even where the underlying property cannot be saved.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)
  • Once an LOF is signed, the Chief Engineer becomes the salvage master's primary technical data source — stability status, tank soundings, structural damage, and electrical distribution status must be produced accurately and fast.
  • Independent CE logs of salvage assets, timings and bunker transfers are the shipowner's main defence during subsequent SCOPIC tariff audits — this is a recurring "what do YOU personally keep a record of" oral trap.
  • Rajappan tests whether candidates understand who pays what: property underwriters fund Article 13, P&I clubs fund Article 14/SCOPIC — mixing these up is an automatic fail-point.
📅 Historical Background

🎓 CE Oral Tip (Rajappan): Rajappan's classic phrasing: "SCOPIC is invoked — how much security, and by when?" The pass answer is precise: US$3 million, inclusive of interest and costs, within 2 working days (excluding weekends/Lloyd's holidays) of written invocation notice — failing which the salvor may withdraw from SCOPIC and revert to Article 14. Candidates who answer "$2 million" are quoting an outdated or incorrect figure.
🎯 Oral Q&A
Q1. Explain "No Cure – No Pay" and how the 1989 Convention modified it.
Under the traditional rule, a salvor earned nothing if the operation failed, regardless of effort or expense. The 1989 Convention introduced Article 14 Special Compensation: a salvor who prevents or minimises environmental damage — even without saving the property — may recover out-of-pocket expenses plus a discretionary uplift, up to a maximum total increase of 100% of expenses if the tribunal deems it fair and just.
Q2. Why was SCOPIC introduced if Article 14 already existed?
Article 14 disputes over whether an environmental threat existed, and what counted as fair expenses, were legally complex and slow, and were geographically restricted to coastal/inland waters. SCOPIC replaces Article 14's case-by-case assessment with an agreed tariff, a fixed 25% uplift, unrestricted global application, and a mandatory US$3 million P&I security deposit within 2 working days of invocation — trading legal certainty for a simpler, faster mechanism.
Q3. What are the Master's and Chief Engineer's duties once a salvor arrives under Article 8.2?
The Master and owner must cooperate fully with the salvor, exercise due care to prevent or minimise environmental damage during the operation, and accept redelivery once the vessel reaches a place of safety. The Chief Engineer's specific role is providing accurate technical data — tank soundings, stability status, structural damage, and electrical distribution status — and coordinating the engineering crew to support salvage operations.

🧠 Memory Box
  • SCOPIC baseline: "3 / 2 / 25." US$3 million security, within 2 working days, fixed 25% tariff uplift.
  • Article 14 uplift: "30 up to 100." Base discretionary uplift to 30% of expenses; tribunal may extend the total increase to a maximum of 100%.

Four Criteria for a Valid Salvage Claim

(1) Real, imminent maritime danger; (2) voluntary service, with no pre-existing statutory duty or binding contract outside an LOF; (3) recognised maritime property at risk; (4) a useful result — at least partial success in preservation.

Case Study — Grounding with Engine Room Flooding

Casualty occurs, tanks sounded and danger verified → Master signs LOF with SCOPIC invoked immediately → salvor invokes tariff, P&I posts US$3 million security → CE provides stability/tank data to the salvage master → dewatering, patching and lightering executed → successful refloat and redelivery at a place of safety.

On My Vessel (Maersk A/S container tonnage)

On a large container vessel, the CE's independent salvage log should separately track main engine/auxiliary run hours, any emergency generator starts, and bunker transfers to lightering barges — all of which feed directly into both the SCOPIC tariff assessment and any subsequent General Average adjustment.

Port of Refuge Operational Protocols & Maritime Casualties

Port of Refuge · General Average Uday pp. 291–295 (approx.) Examiner: Paul / Nair Medium Frequency
🔎 Verification note: SOLAS Chapter V, Regulation 34-1 (Master's discretion for safety of life and ship security) and IMO Resolutions A.949(23)/A.950(23) (Guidelines on Places of Refuge) were checked against the IMO's published regulation summaries and are correctly cited. The Castor (2000) and Prestige (2002) casualties are correctly identified as the incidents that triggered the shift toward standardised places-of-refuge guidelines. The York-Antwerp Rules (Rule X & XI) treatment of Port of Refuge costs under General Average is consistent with the current York-Antwerp Rules 2016 framework. No numeric corrections were required in this topic.
📖 Definition

A Port of Refuge is a port or sheltered place a vessel diverts to when the Master judges it unsafe to continue the voyage due to a peril threatening the ship, cargo, or crew. Its purpose is to provide a controlled environment where a damaged vessel can stabilise, contain hazards, and undergo temporary repairs — preventing an at-sea casualty from escalating into a catastrophic loss or pollution event.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)
  • A Master's decision to divert to a Port of Refuge to preserve ship and cargo from a common peril is a General Average act — the CE's fuel, machinery-running and cost logs from that point onward directly feed the average adjustment.
  • The two-part Tender Document (technical bid + price bid) is drafted with direct CE input on welding procedures, steel grades and NDT requirements — Paul specifically tests candidates on this document's structure.
  • Statutory and class certificates are suspended immediately after a major casualty; only an Interim Certificate of Class, issued after Class-verified repairs, restores trading status.
📅 Historical Background
2000 Castor casualty — coastal states refuse refuge, vessel drifts for weeks 2002 Prestige casualty — refused refuge, vessel breaks up, major pollution 2003 IMO adopts Res. A.949(23) and A.950(23) — places-of-refuge guidelines 2016 York-Antwerp Rules 2016 — current GA cost-allocation framework
⚖ Regulatory References
Key Instruments

IMO Res. A.949(23)Guidelines on Places of Refuge — requires coastal states to balance vessel peril against local environmental/economic risk rather than issuing blanket refusal.

🎓 CE Oral Tip (Paul): Paul will ask you to name the two parts of the Tender Document without prompting: Part 1 (Technical Bid — welding procedures, steel grades, NDT scope) drafted by the technical superintendent and ship's officers, and Part 2 (Price Bid — drydock days, labour rates) from the repair yard. Confusing which part the CE contributes to is a common failure point.
🎯 Oral Q&A
Q1. What financial mechanism is triggered when a Master diverts to a Port of Refuge?
The deviation, made to preserve the ship and cargo from a common peril, constitutes a General Average act. All extraordinary costs incurred — port dues, tug assistance, repair fees, crew costs during the delay — are shared proportionally among the shipowner and cargo interests under the York-Antwerp Rules.
Q2. Describe the structure and purpose of the Tender Document.
A two-part repair specification used to obtain competitive, qualified bids from repair yards. Part 1 (Technical Bid) defines the engineering scope — steel grades, welding procedures, NDT requirements. Part 2 (Price Bid) covers commercial terms — drydock rates, labour charges, material costs.
Q3. What is the significance of an Interim Certificate of Class issued at a Port of Refuge?
Following a major casualty, statutory and class certificates are suspended. After Class-verified repairs and NDT testing are complete, the attending surveyor issues an Interim Certificate of Class, restoring the vessel's class status and enabling local authorities to grant port clearance so the voyage can resume — often with a Condition of Class requiring full permanent repair at the next scheduled drydocking.

🧠 Memory Box

  • Tender Document = "Tech + Price." Part 1 Technical Bid, Part 2 Price Bid.

Case Study — Side-Frame Failure in Heavy Weather

A 50,000 DWT geared bulk carrier suffers side-frame failure with water ingress into Hold No. 3. Sequence: immediate notification to owner/charterer/agents → CE opens a dedicated GA cost-tracking log → agent coordinates Port Authority, pilotage, health/customs clearance → H&M/P&I/Class surveyors notified → shipowner formally declares General Average on arrival → GA Bonds/Guarantees collected from cargo underwriters before cargo release → Class inspects, tender documents prepared, repairs executed → Interim Certificate of Class issued, port clearance granted.

PSC High-Risk Focus Post-Deviation

VDR data integrity (properly saved/locked immediately post-casualty) and structural maintenance logs (thickness measurement history, enhanced survey files) are the two PSC target areas. A finding that the structural failure stemmed from concealed, long-term corrosion can invalidate the GA claim entirely and expose the owner to direct liability.

On My Vessel (Maersk A/S container tonnage)

Container vessels calling major transhipment hubs have ready access to established Ports of Refuge along most trade lanes; the CE's key practical contribution is an immaculate, independently maintained fuel and running-hours log from the moment of diversion, since these figures are contested line items in nearly every GA adjustment.

The IMO Instruments Implementation Code (III Code) & Mandatory Member State Audits

III Code · MSAS Uday pp. 296–300 (approx.) Examiner: Nair / Paul Medium Frequency
🔎 Verification note: The III Code's adoption under Resolution A.1070(28) on 4 December 2013 and the MSAS mandatory commencement date of 1 January 2016 were checked against the IMO's own III Code summary and are correct as given. The evolutionary chain (Interim Guidelines 1993 → 1997 Guidelines → Self-Assessment 1999/2001 → VIMSAS 2003 → mandatory III Code/MSAS 2016) and the treaty-integration list (SOLAS Ch. XIII, MARPOL, STCW Part A, ICLL, COLREG) match IMO's published account. No numeric corrections were required in this topic.
📖 Definition

The IMO Instruments Implementation Code (III Code), codified under IMO Resolution A.1070(28), is an enforceable international standard requiring member states to consistently implement and enforce the maritime treaties they have ratified — across their three possible roles as Flag State, Port State, and Coastal State. It addresses structural weaknesses in national maritime administrations that previously allowed uneven, self-assessed enforcement.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)
  • The III Code does not audit individual ships or crews — it audits the national administration — but a poorly performing flag directly increases PSC targeting risk for every vessel flying that flag, including yours.
  • Nair uses this topic to test "why do we have PSC targeting matrices" — the answer traces back through MSAS performance data to the flag's III Code audit outcome.
  • Systemic detentions across a flag's fleet (e.g. repeated OWS or fire-damper failures) are exactly the kind of pattern that triggers deeper IMO audit scrutiny of that administration's surveyor training and RO oversight.

🎯 Oral Q&A
Q1. What is the purpose of the III Code, and when did it become mandatory?
The III Code (Res. A.1070(28)) establishes an enforceable standard ensuring member states consistently implement and enforce ratified treaties across their Flag, Port and Coastal State roles. It became mandatory on 1 January 2016 through amendments to SOLAS, MARPOL and STCW, introducing the Mandatory Audit of all Member States (MSAS).
Q2. Explain the three-stage strategy loop under Part 1 (Common Areas) of the III Code.
A member state must (1) develop an overall strategy covering its flag, port and coastal state obligations, (2) establish a methodology to monitor and assess how effectively that strategy is implemented and enforced, and (3) continuously review the strategy to maintain and improve organisational performance and capability — a continuous loop, not a one-time exercise.
Q3. How does a poorly performing flag state affect the vessels flying its flag?
Poor MSAS audit performance or weak fleet oversight leads to higher PSC targeting scores under regimes such as Paris MoU or Tokyo MoU, resulting in more frequent expanded inspections, greater detention risk, and commercial disadvantage as charterers avoid poorly-rated flags.

Operational Case Study — 8,500 TEU Container Ship under MSAS Audit

An IMO auditor accompanies a flag state surveyor for a routine shipboard inspection to evaluate the administration's field enforcement capability: documentation review (Safety Construction/Equipment/IOPP certificates, ORB, bilge alarm calibration) → safety systems testing (emergency generator auto-start, emergency fire pump) → environmental systems audit (OWS/BWMS matched against type-approval certificates) → the IMO auditor assesses whether the flag surveyor follows a structured checklist and demonstrates sufficient technical expertise.

PSC Integration

Systemic detentions across a flag's fleet for identical failures, or failures by the administration to submit casualty investigation reports, are the two flagged risk indicators that draw IMO audit attention to a flag's surveyor training and RO oversight processes.

On My Vessel (Maersk A/S container tonnage)

A well-organised engine room with current calibration logs, an up-to-date ORB, and a crew that can execute emergency drills smoothly is the single best practical demonstration a CE can offer during any flag/RO/PSC-linked inspection — it reflects positively regardless of which specific audit framework triggered the visit.
