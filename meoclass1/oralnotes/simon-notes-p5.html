Bonjean Curves — Definition, Uses & Applications
StabilityNaval Arch

Definition

A Bonjean curve is a graph that plots the relationship between the area of a ship's transverse cross-section and its draft. A separate curve is drawn for each transverse station along the ship's length.

Primary Uses

  • Displacement volume: Calculate the ship's total displacement volume at any waterline.
  • LCB: Determine the longitudinal centre of buoyancy.
  • Block coefficient: Compute the displacement block coefficient.
  • Centre of flotation: Find the longitudinal centre of the waterplane area.

Structural & Launching Calculations

  • Calculate the distribution of the buoyancy force during ship launching — critical as the stern lifts before the bow clears the slipway.
  • Determine longitudinal bending moments and shear forces for structural strength calculations in non-uniform loading conditions.
⚖ Regulatory References
IS Code 2008IMO Intact Stability Code — stability criteria that Bonjean-derived hydrostatics must satisfy
SOLAS II-1 / Pt BSubdivision and stability — hydrostatic data requirements for passenger and cargo ships
IACS UR S1Longitudinal strength standard — uses buoyancy distribution data derived from Bonjean curves
Simon Sir typically asks: "What is a Bonjean curve and where do you use it?" Key answer: graph of section area vs. draft at each station; used for displacement, LCB, and launching calculations. Simon Sir may then ask how launching calculations differ — emphasise the dynamic buoyancy shift as the stern lifts.

GZ Curve — Statical & Dynamic Stability (Page 102)
StabilityIS Code

The GZ Curve (Curve of Statical Stability)

Plots the righting lever (GZ) against angle of heel (θ) for a specific displacement (Δ) and KG. Three key features:

  • Range of Stability: Angular range over which GZ is positive.
  • Angle of Vanishing Stability: Angle at which GZ returns to zero — beyond this, vessel capsizes.
  • Point of Inflection (Contraflexure): Where the curve changes from concave to convex — occurs at deck edge immersion because the waterplane area begins to decrease.

Key Stability Formulas

Small angles (θ ≤ 10–12°): GZ = GM × sin θ

Wall-Sided Formula (large angles): GZ = (GM + ½ BM tan²θ) × sin θ

Approximate GM from curve slope: GM = GZ_θ / θ (at θ = 1 radian = 57.3°)

Dynamic Stability

  • Definition: Total work done in inclining the vessel to a specific angle of heel.
  • Calculation: Area under the GZ curve up to that angle — expressed in tonne-metre-radians.
  • Maximum energy absorption occurs at the angle of vanishing stability; beyond this, the vessel cannot right itself.

IS Code 2008 Minimum Criteria (reference)

  • Area under GZ curve: ≥ 0.055 m·rad (to 30°); ≥ 0.090 m·rad (to 40° or flooding angle); ≥ 0.030 m·rad between 30° and 40°.
  • GZ at 30°: ≥ 0.20 m; Initial GM: ≥ 0.15 m.
⚖ Regulatory References
IS Code 2008IMO Resolution MSC.267(85) — mandatory intact stability criteria for all ship types
SOLAS II-1 / Reg 5Requires stability information booklet and inclining experiment approval
SOLAS II-1 / Reg 22Damage stability — residual GZ requirements after flooding
Simon Sir typically asks: "Draw the GZ curve and explain the features." Draw axes clearly, mark the range of stability, point of inflection, max GZ, and angle of vanishing stability. He may ask: "What is the difference between statical and dynamic stability?" — Statical = GZ at a fixed angle; Dynamic = area under the curve (energy absorbed).

Smart Sensors & IOPP Certificate Supplements (Page 103)
MachineryMARPOL

Smart Sensors in the Engine Room

Definition: Advanced instruments with integrated microprocessors that process and communicate data rather than merely transmit raw signals. They act as "sensors with a brain."

  • IoT integration: Used in predictive maintenance frameworks — sensors detect early bearing wear, temperature deviations, and vibration anomalies.
  • Applications: Fuel efficiency optimisation, emission control (NOx/SOx monitoring), automated alarm management, UMS (Unattended Machinery Space) operation.

IOPP Certificate Supplements (MARPOL Annex I)

  • Form A: Supplement for ships other than oil tankers — records bilge water systems, OWS, 15 ppm alarm, ORB Part I.
  • Form B: Supplement for oil tankers — records additional crude oil washing, inert gas system, SBT/CBT arrangements, COW.
  • Must be permanently attached to the IOPP Certificate — guides PSC surveyors during inspections.

Key Contents of IOPP Supplement

  • Ship particulars: Name, Port of Registry, IMO number, GT.
  • OWS configuration and 15 ppm alarm details.
  • Sludge tank, bilge tank, and waste oil tank volumes and locations.
  • Standard Discharge Connection (SDC) specifications.
  • SOPEP (Shipboard Oil Pollution Emergency Plan) status.
⚖ Regulatory References
MARPOL Annex I / Reg 14Surveys — requirements for IOPP certificate survey and endorsement
MARPOL Annex I / Reg 38Form of IOPP Certificate and Supplement (Appendix II) — Form A and Form B
MARPOL Annex I / Reg 15Control of discharge of oil — 15 ppm bilge water discharge standard
Simon Sir typically asks: "What is the difference between Form A and Form B of the IOPP supplement?" Form A = non-tankers; Form B = oil tankers. He may also ask about smart sensors — emphasise IoT + predictive maintenance + UMS context for container vessels.

VDR, Safety Equipment Certificate & Equipment Number (Pages 103–105)
SOLASLSASurvey

Safety Equipment Certificate — Supplement Form E

Official addendum to the Cargo Ship Safety Equipment Certificate. Key contents:

  • LSA details: lifeboat/liferaft types and capacities, rescue boat specs, lifebuoy/lifejacket/immersion suit inventory, SARTs and EPIRBs.
  • Navigation equipment: magnetic compass, gyrocompass, ECDIS (with backup), radar units, LRIT, VDR/S-VDR, echo sounder.

Equipment Number (EN)

A dimensionless technical parameter used to determine the mandatory size, weight, and quantity of anchors, chain cables, mooring lines, and towing lines for new ship installations.

  • Found on the classification society's portal or in the Equipment Number Calculation document within the approved Towing and Mooring Arrangement Plan.

Voyage Data Recorder (VDR) — SOLAS Chapter V Reg 20

Maritime equivalent of an aircraft "black box." Mandatory on cargo ships ≥ 3,000 GT built on or after 1 July 2002.

  • Data recorded: Date/time, GPS position, speed, heading, bridge audio, radar/AIS captures, echo sounder, engine orders, rudder commands, wind, watertight door status.
  • Primary recording: Continuous loop covering at least 48 hours — older data overwritten.
  • Protected capsule: Retains at least 12 hours of data; withstands fire, impact, and deep-sea pressure.
  • Long-Term Recording Medium: Accessible bridge location; retains at least 30 days (720 hours).

VDR Capsule Types

  • Fixed Capsule: Hardened steel; 48-hour loop; 2-year data retention; fitted with ULB (Underwater Locating Beacon).
  • Float-Free Capsule: HRU + EPIRB integrated; 48-hour loop; 6-month retention.
  • Long-Term Recording Medium: Bridge accessible; 30-day data history.
⚖ Regulatory References
SOLAS V / Reg 20VDR requirements — mandatory for cargo ships ≥ 3,000 GT; S-VDR for existing ships
MSC.333(90)Performance standards for VDR — data parameters, capsule specifications, ULB
SOLAS III / Reg 4Surveys of life-saving appliances — Safety Equipment Certificate survey requirements
IACS UR A1Equipment number — anchor and mooring equipment determination criteria
Simon Sir typically asks: "How long does a VDR retain data?" — Give all three: 48 hours (primary loop), 12 hours (protected capsule), 30 days (long-term medium). He may also ask about the ULB — it transmits a 37.5 kHz signal to aid capsule location after sinking.

ARPA, Suez Canal Tonnage & Flag State Duties (Pages 106–107)
SOLASNavigationUNCLOS

ARPA (Automatic Radar Plotting Aid)

An automated tracking system integrated into the vessel's radar that automatically processes targets and calculates their true course, speed, CPA (Closest Point of Approach), and TCPA (Time to CPA). Replaces manual plotting to mitigate collision risks.

Suez Canal Net Tonnage (SCNT)

Derived from the historical Moorsom System's Net Registered Tonnage (NRT) with modifications specific to Suez Canal transit authorities. All canal tariffs, pilotage fees, and dues are based on the Suez Canal Special Tonnage Certificate, issued by recognized classification societies.

Flag State Duties (UNCLOS Article 94)

  • Effectively exercise jurisdiction and administrative, technical, and social control over ships flying its flag.
  • Maintain a national ship register.
  • Ensure safety at sea regarding structural construction, equipment standards, and seaworthiness.
  • Safe manning, labour conditions, and crew training in accordance with STCW.
  • Regular structural surveys by qualified surveyors; ensure navigational charts and equipment meet safety criteria.
  • Verify Master, officers, and crew hold appropriate qualifications and are present in required numbers.
  • Ensure crew is conversant with SOLAS, COLREGs, and MARPOL requirements.
  • Conduct official marine casualty investigations involving their flag vessels on the high seas when loss of life, serious injury, or major environmental damage occurs.
⚖ Regulatory References
UNCLOS Art. 94Duties of the Flag State — exclusive jurisdiction and control over ships on the high seas
SOLAS V / Reg 19Carriage requirements for navigational systems including ARPA
MSC.116(73)Revised performance standards for gyrocompasses (including radar bearing repeater integration)
Simon Sir typically asks: "What are the Flag State's duties under UNCLOS?" — State UNCLOS Article 94 clearly. Key points: maintain register, survey ships, verify STCW compliance, investigate casualties. He may link this to Port State Control — PSC inspects what Flag State is responsible to ensure.

Coastal State Rights, DG DOC & P&I Club Correspondent (Pages 108–109)
UNCLOSSOLASCommercial

Rights and Duties of a Coastal State

  • Regulate marine traffic and enforce navigational safety zones.
  • Maintain visual/electronic navigational aids and offshore installations.
  • Enforce protection over subsea cables and pipelines.
  • Conserve living marine resources within EEZ.
  • Preserve coastal environment — prevent, reduce, and control marine pollution.
  • Issue hydrographic surveys and meteorological warnings.
  • Enforce customs, fiscal, immigration, and sanitary laws without blocking innocent passage of foreign vessels.

Dangerous Goods Document of Compliance (DG DOC)

Governed under SOLAS Chapter II-2 Reg 19 and Chapter VII. Confirms vessel's construction and machinery comply with requirements for carrying dangerous goods.

  • Schedule 1: Matrix of DG classes approved for carriage and permitted stowage locations.
  • Schedule 2: Physical/structural requirements the vessel must maintain.
  • Validity: Maximum 5 years; must not extend beyond the Safety Construction Certificate.

Role of a P&I Correspondent

Local on-scene representative for P&I Clubs — the "eyes and ears" at each port.

  • 24/7 assistance during maritime incidents, collisions, structural failures, emergencies.
  • Handle local claims: crew injuries, repatriation, cargo damage, oil pollution.
  • Coordinate investigations and gather legal evidence.
  • Provide local maritime law and customs expertise.
  • Primary communication link between P&I Club HQ and shipowner/Master.
  • Appoint local legal representation for court proceedings.
⚖ Regulatory References
SOLAS II-2 / Reg 19Special requirements for ships carrying dangerous goods — DG DOC requirements
SOLAS VII / Ch ACarriage of dangerous goods in packaged form — IMDG Code compliance
UNCLOS Art. 17–26Right of innocent passage and coastal state enforcement rights in territorial sea
Simon Sir typically asks: "What is a DG Doc?" — Emphasise it's issued for 5 years and covers two schedules: what you can carry and where you can carry it. P&I Correspondent is less likely from Simon, but Rajappan or Nair may ask it in a cargo/commercial context.

ME vs MC Engine — Comparison & Components Omitted (Pages 109–110)
Machinery2-Stroke ME

Technical Design Comparison

System ComponentMC Engine (Mechanical)ME Engine (Electronic)
Timing ControlMechanical chain drive & camshaftHydraulic Power Supply (HPS) + ECU
Fuel InjectionMechanical fuel pumps & roller guidesElectronically regulated fuel pressure boosters
Exhaust ValvesMechanical cam actuating profilesElectronic actuation — FIVA / ELFI valves
Starting AirMechanical starting air distributorElectronically controlled solenoid starting valves
Speed GovernorMechanical / hydraulic governorIntegrated digital governor in ECU software
Control StandLocal mechanical maneuvering standElectronic Local Operating Panel (ELOP)

Operational Advantages of ME over MC

  • Efficiency: Precise fuel injection timing and variable exhaust valve operation → lower SFOC across all load profiles.
  • Flexibility: Injection parameters adjustable dynamically for varying fuel qualities (VLSFO, biofuels, methanol) — not possible with fixed mechanical cams.
  • Emissions: Precise combustion control reduces smoke and NOx compared to MC engines.

Components Eliminated in ME Engine (vs MC)

  • Chain drive, chain wheel frame, camshaft.
  • Fuel pump cam and roller guide.
  • Exhaust valve cam, fuel pump actuator.
  • Starting air distributor, mechanical governor, mechanical control stand.
⚖ Regulatory References
NOx Technical Code 2008Tier II/III NOx compliance — ME electronic control enables optimised combustion to meet limits
MARPOL Annex VI / Reg 13NOx emission limits — Tier I/II/III thresholds by rpm and build date
IRS / MAN B&W RulesMaker's classification rules for ME engine type approval and maintenance intervals
Simon Sir's favourite question: "Explain the difference between an ME and MC engine — what is eliminated?" List the 9 components eliminated. Then explain the FIVA (Fuel Injection & Valve Actuation) valve — this is the key component that replaces the camshaft in ME engines. He may ask: "What happens if the hydraulic oil pressure to the HPS fails?" — Safety system activates; engine goes to slow/stop; backup manual ELOP available.

Port / Place of Refuge — Protocol & Request Procedure (Pages 110–111)
SOLASISMEmergency

Definition

A Port/Place of Refuge (PoR) is a port or sheltered coastal anchorage chosen by a vessel in distress to stabilise her structural condition, ensure crew safety, or prevent environmental pollution hazards.

Triggers for Seeking PoR

  • Critical machinery breakdown, fire/explosion, severe structural damage.
  • Cargo shifting, extreme weather, hull damage, progressive flooding.

Formal Request Procedure — Form A submitted to:

  • Coastal State Competent Authority.
  • Flag State administration.
  • Vessel's insurance company (P&I Club).
  • Classification Society.

The request includes: ship particulars, nature of distress (fire/machinery breakdown/pollution), crew and passenger count, safety measures taken, and pollution/environmental risks.

Competent Authority Assessment — Factors Considered

  • Threat to life onboard and risk to public safety.
  • Possibility of oil spill or hazardous cargo pollution.
  • Fire or explosion risk; impact on nearby communities.
  • Validity of the vessel's insurance (P&I Club cover).
Master requests PoR │ ▼ Form A submitted to Coastal State / Flag / P&I / Class │ ▼ Competent Authority Assessment │ ┌──┴──┐ ▼ ▼ Approved Rejected │ ▼ Coastal State permits entry
⚖ Regulatory References
IMO Res. A.949(23)Guidelines on places of refuge for ships in need of assistance
IMO MSC-MEPC.3/Circ.3Revised guidance on the role of the Competent Authority in evaluating places of refuge
UNCLOS Art. 18Innocent passage — requires coastal states to allow passage of ships in distress
ISM Code Sec. 8Emergency preparedness — DPA and company must be notified in emergency situations
Simon Sir typically asks: "What is a port of refuge and what is the procedure?" Give the 4-party notification list: Coastal Authority, Flag State, P&I Club, Classification Society. He may ask: "Who decides if refuge is granted?" — the Coastal State Competent Authority, not the Master or the company.

Polar Code — Requirements, Certificate & CE Preparations (Pages 111–113)
SOLASSurveyPolar

Definition & Scope

The International Code for Ships Operating in Polar Waters (Polar Code) — mandatory under SOLAS XIV and MARPOL Annexes I, II, IV, V, VI. Covers Arctic and Antarctic waters.

  • Covers: ship design, construction, equipment, operational procedures, training, SAR, and protection of the polar ecosystem.

Hazards in Polar Waters

  • Severe weather; poor/unreliable charts; difficult rescue and clean-up operations.
  • Machinery affected by extreme low temperatures; increased hull and propeller loading from ice.

Ship Categories (Polar Ship Certificate)

  • Category A: Designed for heavy multi-year ice; can operate year-round in polar conditions.
  • Category B: Less robust — not capable in severe ice; summer operation only.
  • Category C: Designed to operate in open water or low ice concentration.

Requirements for Issue of Polar Ship Certificate

  • Risk Assessment: Assessment of anticipated operating conditions and polar hazards.
  • PWOM (Polar Water Operational Manual): Covers ship capabilities, limitations, decision-making, and emergency procedures for owner/operator/Master/crew.
  • Survey: Class survey to verify structural and equipment compliance.

CE Preparations Before Entering Polar Waters (key items)

  • Drain fire line and freshwater deck lines to prevent freezing.
  • Check anchor wash valves; start hydraulic pumps well before operation; run winches slowly to warm hydraulic oil.
  • Check accommodation heating coils; verify lifeboat coolant contains antifreeze.
  • Ensure fuel tanks contain winter-grade fuel; check pour point of lubricating oils.
  • Verify stern tube oil specification for low-temperature operation.
  • Plan alternate sea suction arrangements (change to low suction when ice risk increases).
  • Spread rock salt at gangways, ER entrances, emergency exits, skylight areas, mooring stations.
  • Check bridge window heaters; verify freshwater cooling systems contain antifreeze.
  • Provide adequate winter PPE: gloves, parka, insulated boots, ski goggles.
⚖ Regulatory References
SOLAS XIVSafety measures for ships operating in polar waters — Polar Code mandatory under this chapter
MARPOL Annexes I/II/IV/V/VIEnvironmental protection requirements for polar waters (HFO ban, garbage, discharge standards)
STCW Reg V/4Training and certification for officers on ships operating in polar waters
MARPOL Annex I / Reg 43AHeavy Fuel Oil ban in Arctic waters — general prohibition from 1 July 2024
Simon Sir typically asks: "What are the CE's preparations before entering polar waters?" — List at minimum 10 from the list above. Critical ones: drain lines, antifreeze in lifeboat/cooling systems, winter-grade fuel, alternate sea suction, stern tube oil check. He may also ask: "What are the three categories of polar ships?"

LSA Code Amendments — Effective 1 January 2024

  • Rescue Boat Launching: Stored mechanical power may be waived if one person can recover the rescue boat using manual crank handles, provided crank force ≤ 160 N.
  • Lifeboats with two independent propulsion systems or free-fall lifeboats: Adequate buoyancy arrangements must be provided.

LSA Code Amendments — Effective 1 January 2026

  • Single Fall Hook System: Shall be designed so it cannot support any load unless completely reset; prevents accidental release during recovery.

Lifeboat Lowering Speeds

Maximum lowering speed: 1.3 m/s (upper safety limit)

Minimum lowering speed: V = 0.4 + 0.02H Where V = speed (m/s); H = height from davit head to waterline (m)

Simon Sir typically asks: "How many chapters in the LSA Code?" — Answer: 7 chapters. He may ask the lowering speed formula — give both maximum (1.3 m/s) and the minimum formula. The 2024 and 2026 amendments are current exam hot topics — know the 160 N crank force limit and the single-fall hook requirement.

FTP Code — International Code for Fire Test Procedures (2010)

Applicable under SOLAS Chapter II-2. Provides laboratory testing, type approval, and fire test procedures for materials and products installed onboard ships.

  • Non-Combustibility Test: Material behaviour under high temperatures.
  • Smoke and Toxicity Test: Smoke density and toxic gas generation.
  • Class Divisions Test: Fire testing for "A", "B", and "F" class structural divisions.
  • Control Systems Test: Fire testing for fire door control systems.
  • Surface Flammability Test: Surface flammability properties.
  • Primary Deck Coverings: Testing deck covering materials.
  • Textiles and Films: Upholstered furniture, bedding, vertically suspended textiles.
