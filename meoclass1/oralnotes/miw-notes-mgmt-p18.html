Part 18 · Topic 1

Hull & Machinery (H&M) Policy Mechanics — Deductibles, Total Loss Classifications & Policy Typologies

Deductible/Franchise · ATL/CTL · Policy Types Uday pp. 426–432 (approx.) Examiner: Nair ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: The Constructive Total Loss (CTL) legal basis (MIA 1906, s.60(2)(ii) — repair/recovery cost expected to exceed the ship's repaired value) and the commercial contractual threshold of 80% of insured value are both confirmed against the published Institute Time Clauses – Hulls (ITC-83) Clause 19/21 text, which states a CTL claim based on cost of recovery/repair is not recoverable unless that cost exceeds 80% of the insured value. The Deductible vs Franchise mechanics and the four policy typologies (Valued, Unvalued, Open Cover, Floating Cover) reflect standard, well-established marine insurance practice and are retained as drafted.
📖 Definition

Hull & Machinery (H&M) insurance is the foundational policy protecting the shipowner's own physical asset — the hull, main engine, and onboard equipment — against physical loss or damage. In plain terms: H&M answers the question "who pays to fix or replace my ship?" This topic covers the mechanical rules that decide how much of a claim the underwriter actually pays, and how a "total loss" is legally defined.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)
  • Knowing whether your policy uses a Deductible or a Franchise changes how you report a borderline-value repair — a Franchise can mean the difference between the underwriter paying nothing and paying the entire bill.
  • The CTL threshold explains why a badly damaged but technically repairable ship is sometimes simply written off — understanding this helps a Chief Engineer interpret why management sometimes abandons a repair that looks technically feasible.
  • Nair frequently pairs this topic with a numeric mini-scenario (given a loss value and either a deductible or franchise limit, calculate what the underwriter pays) — the two formulas below are worth memorising cold.

⚖ Regulatory References

🧮 Deductible vs Franchise

A Deductible is a fixed sum the assured pays out-of-pocket on every claim; the underwriter pays only the amount above that threshold. A Franchise is a minimum trigger: if the loss falls below the franchise limit the underwriter pays nothing at all, but once the loss meets or exceeds the limit, the underwriter pays the entire loss with no deduction.

Deductible: L = max(0, V − D)   |   Franchise: L = 0 if V < F, else L = V

⚠ Actual vs Constructive Total Loss
  • Actual Total Loss (ATL): the ship is completely destroyed, ceases to be a thing of the kind insured, or the assured is irretrievably deprived of it (e.g. sunk beyond recovery).
  • Constructive Total Loss (CTL): the ship is reasonably abandoned because actual total loss appears unavoidable, or because the cost of repair/recovery would exceed the CTL threshold — the strict statutory test (MIA s.60(2)(ii)) is repair cost exceeding the repaired value (100%), but for practical claims settlement, standard commercial H&M wording contractually lowers this to 80% of the insured value.
📄 Policy Typologies
  • Valued Policy: the asset's value is agreed at inception; this Agreed Value governs the total-loss payout regardless of later market fluctuations.
  • Unvalued Policy: value is determined only when a claim arises — rarely used in modern hull practice due to the difficulty of establishing real-time asset value.
  • Open Cover: a long-term (typically 12-month) arrangement automatically covering all shipments/transits at pre-fixed premium rates.
  • Floating Cover: a generic total-sum policy without naming individual vessels/cargo upfront; the assured declares transit details as voyages occur, running down the total sum insured.
🎓 CE Oral Tip (Nair): Nair likes to give a specific dollar loss figure and a specific deductible or franchise limit and ask you to state the payout on the spot. Practice both formulas until the arithmetic is automatic — hesitating over basic arithmetic under oral pressure reads as not knowing the concept, even if you do.
🎯 Oral Q&A
Q1. A vessel is damaged to a value of $18,000. The policy has a $5,000 deductible. What does the underwriter pay?
Under a deductible system, the underwriter pays the loss value minus the deductible: $18,000 − $5,000 = $13,000. The assured absorbs the first $5,000 of every claim.
Q2. Explain the difference between the strict statutory CTL test and the commercial contractual CTL threshold.
Under MIA 1906 s.60(2)(ii), a strict CTL exists only if repair cost would exceed the ship's repaired value — effectively a 100% threshold. In practice, standard commercial H&M policy wording (ITCH) lowers this contractually so that a CTL claim is payable once repair/recovery cost exceeds 80% of the insured value, making settlement faster and less disputed than waiting to prove the stricter 100% statutory test.
Q3. Why would a shipowner prefer a Valued Policy over an Unvalued Policy for a modern container vessel?
A Valued Policy fixes the Agreed Value at inception, giving certainty on the total-loss payout regardless of later market swings in vessel values — critical for loan/mortgage servicing calculations. An Unvalued Policy requires proving the vessel's value at the time of the claim itself, which is commercially impractical for a high-value, actively trading asset and is why unvalued policies are almost never used in modern hull insurance.

Scenario

En route Rotterdam–New York, a high-temperature alarm on No.4 unit bearing is followed by a crankcase explosion, distorting crankcase doors and crosshead guides.

Master alters course to minimise rolling; Chief Engineer shuts down the engine, isolates fuel, activates fixed fire suppression if needed. The Sue and Labour duty requires the crew to act "as if uninsured" — assessing whether temporary repairs can prevent drifting aground, and calling for tug assistance if grounding risk is imminent. Management notifies underwriters and Class; a surveyor boards at the nearest port of refuge; all alarm logs, automation printouts, and damaged components are preserved as evidence.

Part 18 · Topic 2

The Legal and Operational Mechanics of Subrogation in Maritime Claims

MIA 1906 s.79 · Equitable Priority · Assignment Uday pp. 433–438 (approx.) Examiner: Nair Medium Frequency
🔎 Verification note: The statutory basis (MIA 1906, s.79(1) — subrogation rights vest automatically upon any indemnity payment, full or partial) and the Equitable Priority Doctrine from Lord Napier and Ettrick v Hunter (House of Lords) are both correctly stated and are standard, well-established English insurance case law directly applicable to marine subrogation. The worked numerical examples (equitable priority allocation and assignment profit-retention mechanics) are internally consistent and correctly illustrate the legal principle. Added for oral-exam depth: Section 79(1) actually does more than grant a right to sue third parties — once the underwriter pays a full indemnity for a Constructive Total Loss, the insurer is also entitled to take over the assured's remaining interest in whatever is left of the subject-matter insured (e.g. residual value in the wreck itself), not merely a bare right of recovery. This is a common examiner follow-up once basic subrogation mechanics have been tested.
📖 Definition

Subrogation lets an underwriter, having paid the assured for a loss, step into the assured's legal shoes to recover that money from the third party who actually caused the damage. In simple terms: the insurer pays you quickly so you're not left waiting, then goes after the real culprit to get its money back — you are not allowed to collect from both and profit twice.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)
  • Whether a recovery succeeds often comes down to the quality of technical evidence the engine room preserved immediately after the incident — bunker samples, logbook data, and isolated failed components.
  • Knowing whether your case falls under equitable subrogation, a Letter of Subrogation, or an Assignment changes whose name the lawsuit is filed under, and who ultimately keeps any recovery above the original payout.
  • Nair often tests this with a bunkering-contamination or tug-negligence scenario, asking whose name the claim is filed in and how any excess recovery is split.
📅 Historical Background
Common Law Subrogation rooted in English equity — a wrongdoer should not escape liability just because the victim had insurance 1906 Codified in Section 79, UK Marine Insurance Act 1906 Case law Lord Napier and Ettrick v Hunter — establishes the Equitable Priority Doctrine
⚖ Regulatory References
Key Instruments

🧭 The Four Typologies of Subrogation
  • Equitable (Legal) Subrogation: arises automatically by operation of law on payment — no separate contract needed. Governed by the Equitable Priority Doctrine.
  • Contractual Subrogation: formalised via a Letter of Subrogation during claims settlement, to avoid disputes and secure the assured's cooperation.
  • Subrogation-cum-Assignment: a complete transfer of the claim's ownership to the insurer, who can then sue the third party directly in its own name and keep any surplus recovery above the original payout.
  • Statutory Subrogation: arises from specific national legislation (e.g. compulsory employer injury schemes) — a general insurance-law form, not central to marine practice.
⚖ The Equitable Priority Doctrine — Worked Example

Total Loss (LT) = $400; Insurer paid a partial indemnity (R) = $200, leaving Uncompensated Loss (LU) = $200. If a third-party recovery (C) of $300 is later obtained: the assured takes priority and is paid their uncompensated $200 first; the insurer keeps only the remaining balance: $300 − $200 = $100. The insurer never gets first claim on recovered funds while the assured remains out of pocket.

🎓 CE Oral Tip (Nair): Nair's classic trap is to ask "who benefits if the third party pays more than expected?" Under standard subrogation the excess goes back to the assured; only under an Assignment does the insurer keep the entire surplus. Getting this backwards is a common candidate error.
🎯 Oral Q&A
Q1. Out-of-spec fuel damages your fuel pumps and the underwriter pays for repairs. Can they sue the supplier, and in whose name?
Yes — under MIA 1906 s.79(1), subrogation rights arise automatically once the underwriter has paid an indemnity. Under standard equitable or contractual subrogation the lawsuit is filed in the name of the assured (the shipowner), not the insurer's own name.
Q2. Explain the Equitable Priority Doctrine when an underwriter has only paid a partial settlement.
Subrogation rights vest immediately on any indemnity payment under s.79, whether full or partial. But under Lord Napier and Ettrick v Hunter, the insurer's recovery rights are subordinated to the assured — when a third-party recovery is obtained, the assured is paid first to cover their remaining uncompensated loss, and the underwriter only retains whatever balance is left over.
Q3. What is the key legal distinction between a Letter of Subrogation and an Assignment?
A Letter of Subrogation lets the insurer pursue the third party in the assured's name, but caps the insurer's retention at the indemnity amount paid — any excess goes to the assured. An Assignment is a full transfer of legal ownership of the claim, letting the insurer sue in its own name and keep the entire recovery, including any surplus above what it originally paid out.
Q4. Beyond the right to sue third parties, what else does an underwriter acquire under Section 79 after paying a full CTL indemnity?
Under s.79(1), once the underwriter pays a full indemnity for a Constructive Total Loss, they are entitled to take over the assured's remaining interest in whatever is left of the subject-matter insured — for example, any residual value in the wreck itself — not just a bare right to pursue a third-party wrongdoer. This reflects the same indemnity principle: having paid in full, the insurer is entitled to whatever residual value remains, since the assured has already been made completely whole.

Scenario

Under pilotage in an international strait, steering control is lost due to seized directional control valves; the vessel strikes a floating navigational structure. The hydraulic fluid was freshly replenished from a third-party supplier at the previous port, and testing shows grit contamination.

Evidence Protocol

Switch to auxiliary steering immediately for safety. Seal all remaining unused drums from that delivery batch as primary evidence. Draw joint samples from the seized system and the unopened drums for independent laboratory analysis to establish the contamination baseline before pursuing the supplier under subrogation.

On My Vessel (Maersk A/S container tonnage)

Any consumable delivered by a third party — fuel, lubricants, hydraulic fluid — should be sampled and the batch documentation retained until the voyage is clearly free of related issues; it is the only leverage available if a subrogated recovery becomes necessary months later.

Protection & Indemnity (P&I) Clubs — Mutual Structure, Liability Spectrum & the International Group

Mutuality · Calls · ISM Prerequisite Uday pp. 439–444 (approx.) Examiner: Nair ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: The mutual, non-profit governance structure, the advance/supplementary call funding model, the ISM Code entry prerequisite (valid Document of Compliance and Safety Management Certificate), and the 16-point liability spectrum are all standard, well-established P&I Club practice and match the source draft. One addition made to this topic (not present in the original source draft): the source did not mention the International Group of P&I Clubs (IG) by name, so the platform's standing correction on this point is added here for completeness — the IG currently comprises 12 mutual clubs (following the 2025 NorthStandard merger, down from the historical 13), which together provide the pooling and reinsurance arrangement behind the mutual system described in this topic.
📖 Definition

A Protection & Indemnity (P&I) Club is a mutual, non-profit insurance association owned and controlled by its own shipowner-members, providing collective self-insurance against third-party liabilities. In plain terms: while H&M answers "who pays to fix my ship," P&I answers "who pays when my ship damages, injures, or pollutes someone else."

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)
  • P&I entry is conditional on maintaining a valid ISM Document of Compliance and Safety Management Certificate — the Chief Engineer's logkeeping and maintenance discipline directly underpins the ship's P&I cover.
  • After any incident with third-party consequences (injury, pollution, cargo damage), the local P&I correspondent is one of your very first calls, alongside Class and the Technical Superintendent.
  • Nair frequently asks candidates to sort a single incident's costs between H&M and P&I — this topic's comparison table is your quick reference for that.
📅 Historical Background
19th Century Fixed-premium insurers fail to cover the full range of third-party risks Mid-late 1800s Shipowners form mutual "Protection" and "Indemnity" associations, later merging into unified P&I Clubs 20th Century Formation of the International Group of P&I Clubs for pooling and joint reinsurance 2025 NorthStandard merger reduces the International Group to 12 member clubs
⚖ Regulatory References

📋 Core Liability Categories (Condensed)
  • People: crew/passenger injury, illness, death; life salvage awards; stowaway/refugee handling.
  • Property & Environment: collision liability portions not covered by H&M; fixed/floating-object damage; pollution fines and cleanup; wreck location, marking and removal.
  • Cargo: loss, short-delivery, theft, or damage claims from cargo carried.
  • Operational: towage liabilities; quarantine expenses; unrecoverable General Average contributions; special compensation to salvors (e.g. under SCOPIC).
  • Legal: fines from courts/customs/port authorities; costs of official inquiries and criminal proceedings; sue-and-labour legal costs.
🎓 CE Oral Tip (Nair): Nair's favourite follow-up: "your automation fails during berthing and you strike a jetty, holing a fuel tank." Practice splitting this instantly — hull/tank repair is H&M; the jetty damage, any bunker pollution cleanup, and fines are P&I.
🎯 Oral Q&A
Q1. What is the fundamental structural difference between a P&I Club and an H&M underwriter?
A P&I Club is a non-profit mutual owned and controlled by its member shipowners, funded through advance and supplementary calls, covering third-party liabilities. An H&M underwriter is a commercial, profit-driven insurer covering only physical damage to the ship's own hull and machinery.
Q2. Your vessel deviates to land a critically ill seaman. What can be claimed from the P&I Club?
Under the diversion expenses category, the owner can claim extraordinary out-of-pocket costs directly caused by the deviation — extra fuel and lube oil consumed, port charges at the deviation port, additional provisions, crew wages during the deviation, and the seaman's medical/hospitalisation costs ashore.
Q3. Can a P&I Club deny a claim after accidental cargo damage?
Yes — coverage can be denied if the shipowner breached core technical warranties, such as sailing without a valid Document of Compliance or Safety Management Certificate under the ISM Code, or if there was willful misconduct such as knowingly sending an unseaworthy vessel to sea or ignoring outstanding conditions of class.

  • H&M = my ship; P&I = everyone else's problem.

General Average, the York-Antwerp Rules (2016) & the New Jason Clause

GA · YAR 2016 · New Jason Clause Uday pp. 445–450 (approx.) Examiner: Nair ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: The Rule Paramount and Rule A four-pillar test (Extraordinary, Intentional, Common Safety, Real Peril), Rule C's exclusion of environmental pollution cleanup costs, and Rule D (fault does not bar contribution, but does not bar counter-actions either) are confirmed against the structure and content of the York-Antwerp Rules 2016 as adopted by the CMI. The New Jason Clause mechanics (requiring cargo to contribute to GA for losses arising from any cause, negligent or not, where the carrier is statutorily exempt under COGSA/the Harter Act — but not for unseaworthiness absent due diligence) are confirmed against published legal commentary on the clause and the underlying US case law (The Irrawaddy, The Jason). The worked numerical GA apportionment example is internally consistent and correctly illustrates the proportional contribution principle.
📖 Definition

General Average (GA) is the principle that all parties in a common maritime adventure — ship, cargo, and freight interests — must share proportionally in any extraordinary sacrifice or expenditure reasonably made to save the venture from a real peril. In plain terms: if the Master jettisons your cargo to save the ship (and everyone else's cargo with it), you don't bear that loss alone — everyone whose property was saved chips in according to what they had at stake.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)
  • GA lets the Master act decisively in an emergency without first negotiating terms with hundreds of individual cargo owners — as Chief Engineer, your logs of the emergency (fuel used, damage sustained, decisions taken) become the technical backbone of the Average Adjuster's later calculation.
  • Understanding Rule A's four pillars lets you correctly judge, in real time, whether an emergency action you're asked to take (e.g. running the main engine hard aground) will actually qualify for GA treatment later.
  • Nair frequently gives a grounding or fire scenario and asks candidates to test it against the four pillars live in the oral — this is one of the highest-yield "apply the rule" topics at Class 1 level.
📅 Historical Background
Ancient Lex Rhodia (Rhodian Law) — earliest known GA-style loss-sharing principle 1890 York-Antwerp Rules first unified internationally 1994 / 2004 Periodic revisions to modernise the Rules 2016 York-Antwerp Rules 2016 — current standard, incorporated by reference into bills of lading/charter parties
⚖ Regulatory References
Key Instruments

🧮 GA Apportionment — Worked Example

Contribution Factor (Cf) = Total GA Expense (EGA) ÷ Total Saved Value (VT)

Example: a hijacking incident incurs ransom + fees totalling EGA = $110,000. Saved values: Ship $500,000, Cargo Owner 1 $300,000, Cargo Owner 2 $200,000 → VT = $1,000,000. Cf = 110,000 ÷ 1,000,000 = 11%. Each party then pays 11% of their own saved value: Ship pays $55,000, Cargo Owner 1 pays $33,000, Cargo Owner 2 pays $22,000 — proportional to what each party had at stake in the saved venture, not a flat split.

🧭 The Four Pillars of Rule A
  • Extraordinary: beyond ordinary duties — routine operations or standard cargo-delivery obligations do not qualify.
  • Intentional: deliberately executed — flooding a burning hold on purpose qualifies; accidentally striking a submerged wreck does not.
  • Common Safety: protects the entire venture, not one cargo unit — diverting for a single reefer container's breakdown does not qualify.
  • Real Peril: the danger must be genuine and imminent, not minor or imagined.

Rule Paramount sits above all of these: no allowance is made for any sacrifice or expenditure unless it was reasonable — an unnecessary commercial service ordered when the ship remained functional is excluded regardless of intent. Rule C excludes environmental pollution cleanup costs and environmental damage claims from GA entirely. Rule D preserves the right to contribution even where the casualty was caused by one party's fault — but if that fault was the shipowner providing an unseaworthy vessel, the cargo owners retain the right to counter-claim their contributions back.

📜 The New Jason Clause

Under US maritime law, shipowners historically could not claim GA contributions from cargo owners if the casualty was caused by crew negligence. The New Jason Clause overcomes this: when inserted into the Bill of Lading or Charter Party, it requires cargo to contribute to GA for losses from any cause whatsoever, negligent or not, provided the carrier is statutorily exempt from liability for that cause under COGSA or the Harter Act — chiefly errors in navigation/management of the ship, but the language extends to any statutory exemption except unseaworthiness arising from a lack of due diligence at the start of the voyage.

🎓 CE Oral Tip (Nair): Nair's classic trap question is a localised equipment failure (e.g. one reefer container compressor) versus a ship-wide threat — always check the Common Safety pillar first, since a localised problem, however serious for that cargo, is Particular Average, not GA.
🎯 Oral Q&A
Q1. A cargo hold fire is extinguished by flooding, destroying part of the cargo. Is this General Average?
Yes. All four Rule A pillars are satisfied: flooding the hold was an extraordinary, intentional action taken reasonably for the common safety of the entire vessel and remaining cargo against a real, active fire peril. The value of the water-damaged cargo and the operation's costs are shared proportionally by all saved interests.
Q2. Explain the legal scope of the New Jason Clause.
The New Jason Clause requires cargo interests to contribute to GA for losses arising from any cause whatsoever, negligent or not, provided the carrier is statutorily exempt from liability for that cause under COGSA or the Harter Act. It chiefly protects the shipowner for errors in navigation or ship management, but its wording covers any statutory exemption — so long as the shipowner exercised due diligence to make the vessel seaworthy at the start of the voyage.
Q3. A vessel diverts to a port of refuge because one reefer container's compressor fails. Can the deviation costs be claimed under GA?
No. This fails the Common Safety pillar — the breakdown threatens only that one cargo unit, not the ship or the remaining cargo as a whole. This is a Particular Average loss for that cargo owner, not a General Average act.

15A vessel runs aground; the Master orders the main engine run at full power to refloat, causing sand ingestion damage to the cooling system; the vessel is then towed to a port of refuge. Analyse this under the York-Antwerp Rules 2016 and outline how the final adjustment will be apportioned.
🧠 Memory Box
  • E-I-C-P — Extraordinary, Intentional, Common safety, real Peril: the four pillars of Rule A.
  • Rule C excludes pollution; Rule Paramount demands reasonableness above all else.
  • GA/YAR only bind if written into the Bill of Lading or Charter Party.

Scenario

A steering failure causes the vessel to shear out of a restricted channel and ground on a sandbar with the tide falling.

Sequence

Master orders tank soundings and confirms hull integrity, then declares GA given the risk of structural stress. To refloat before the next low tide, the main engine is intentionally run at maximum load alongside emergency tugs, drawing sand into the cooling system and damaging the seawater pumps and auxiliary coolers. The vessel refloats successfully and is towed to a port of refuge.

Adjustment

Because the cooling-system damage was an intentional sacrifice for the common safety of the entire adventure, it is treated as GA — all cargo owners contribute proportionally toward the repair bills and tug expenses, alongside the Ship's own proportional share.

On My Vessel (Maersk A/S container tonnage)

If ever ordered to run machinery outside normal parameters during an emergency, log explicitly that the action was ordered by the Master for the common safety of the vessel — this single logbook entry is often the deciding evidence for whether the resulting damage is accepted into the GA adjustment.
