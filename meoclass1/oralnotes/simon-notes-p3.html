COP 28 (2023) & COP 29 (2024) — Key Outcomes
Dubai & Baku — triple renewables, Loss & Damage Fund, net-zero shipping

COP 28 — Dubai, UAE (November–December 2023)

  • Energy efficiency: Reaffirmed global mandate to double the rate of energy efficiency improvements by 2030.
  • Loss and Damage Fund: Formally operationalised — developed nations contribute capital; vulnerable developing nations receive funding for climate-related losses they did not cause.
  • Fossil fuel transition: First COP to explicitly call for a formal transition away from fossil fuels within energy systems — not elimination, but managed transition.
  • Renewable energy: Member states pledged to triple global renewable energy capacity by 2030.
  • Maritime commitment: Global shipping industry reaffirmed net-zero emissions by 2050; focus on zero-emission fuel adoption.
  • Green Shipping Challenge: Launched by USA and Norway to accelerate decarbonisation of international shipping.

COP 29 — Baku, Azerbaijan (November 11–22, 2024)

  • Focused on climate finance — new collective quantified goal (NCQG) to replace the expired $100 billion/year target.
  • Agreed on rules for Article 6 carbon markets (cross-border carbon credit trading).
⚡ Simon Sir typically asks

"What happened at COP 28?" — Know the 4 key outcomes: triple renewables, Loss & Damage Fund, fossil fuel transition language, and net-zero shipping commitment. He may link this to IMO's 2023 GHG Strategy — both target net-zero by 2050 but through different mechanisms (IMO = shipping-specific regulation; COP = broad national commitments).

⚖ Regulatory References
UNFCCC / COP 28 Decision"UAE Consensus" (COP28 Decision, Dubai, 2023) — outcome of the first Global Stocktake under the Paris Agreement; first COP text referencing transition away from fossil fuels
Paris Agreement 2015"Paris Agreement" (adopted COP21, 2015) — limit warming to well below 2°C, pursuing efforts to limit to 1.5°C
IMO 2023 GHG Strategy"2023 IMO Strategy on Reduction of GHG Emissions from Ships" (adopted MEPC 80, July 2023) — net-zero by or around 2050
P.62
CBDR — Common But Differentiated Responsibilities
1992 Earth Summit — developed vs developing nations framework

Definition & Origin

A fundamental legal and environmental principle first formalised in the UNFCCC framework, adopted at the 1992 Earth Summit in Rio de Janeiro. Recognises that while all nations share responsibility for solving climate change, their historical contributions and current capacities differ significantly.

Core Framework

  • Developed nations: Emitting GHGs since the Industrial Era; account for over 50% of cumulative global emissions. Must take greater financial and technical responsibility for reduction.
  • Developing nations: Historically low emissions, limited wealth and technology for clean transition — receive support rather than bearing equal burden.

Maritime Relevance

IMO's approach has historically been flag-neutral (same rules for all ships regardless of flag state), which conflicts with CBDR. This tension is ongoing — developing nations argue CBDR should apply to maritime GHG measures. IMO's 2023 GHG Strategy acknowledges this by including provisions for technology transfer and capacity building.

⚡ Simon Sir typically asks

"What is CBDR and how does it relate to IMO?" — Origin at Rio 1992, embedded in UNFCCC/Paris Agreement. The tension: IMO applies same standards to all ships regardless of flag, while CBDR says developed nations should do more. Simon Sir may ask: "Does CBDR apply to MARPOL?" — Not explicitly, but it influences IMO mid-term measure negotiations.

⚖ Regulatory References
UNFCCC 1992 / Art. 3.1"Article 3.1 — Principles" (UNFCCC, 1992) — Common But Differentiated Responsibilities principle first codified
Rio Declaration 1992"Principle 7" (Rio Declaration on Environment and Development, 1992) — states cooperate to conserve Earth's ecosystem; developed states acknowledge their responsibility
IMO 2023 GHG Strategy"2023 IMO Strategy on Reduction of GHG Emissions from Ships" — acknowledges CBDR; includes capacity-building and technology transfer provisions

UNFCCC & COP Historical Milestones
1992 treaty → COP 3 Kyoto → COP 21 Paris → COP 28 Dubai

UNFCCC

United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change — international treaty established 1992 to coordinate global climate action. The Conference of the Parties (COP) is its supreme decision-making body, meeting annually.

Key COP Milestones

  • COP 3 (1997 — Kyoto): Adopted the Kyoto Protocol — first binding emission targets and carbon credit system.
  • COP 21 (2015 — Paris): Paris Agreement — limit warming to well below 2.0°C, pursue 1.5°C. Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs).
  • COP 26 (2021 — Glasgow): Pledges to phase down coal; methane pledge; Article 6 carbon markets rules (not finalised).
  • COP 28 (2023 — Dubai): First global stocktake; fossil fuel transition language; triple renewables; Loss & Damage Fund operational.
  • COP 29 (2024 — Baku): Climate finance — new NCQG goal; Article 6 carbon market rules finalised.
⚡ Simon Sir typically asks

"What is UNFCCC and what is COP?" — UNFCCC = the treaty; COP = the annual decision-making body. Know the 4 key COP milestones: Kyoto (1997), Paris (2015), Glasgow (2021), Dubai (2023). Simon Sir may ask how the Paris Agreement relates to the IMO strategy — both target net-zero 2050 but IMO acts through MARPOL regulations, not NDCs.

⚖ Regulatory References
UNFCCC 1992"United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change, 1992 (UNFCCC)" — establishes the Conference of the Parties (COP) as supreme governing body
Kyoto Protocol 1997"Kyoto Protocol" (adopted COP3, 1997) — binding emission targets for developed (Annex I) nations
Paris Agreement 2015"Paris Agreement" (adopted COP21, 2015) — Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) based commitments; 5-year review/ratchet cycles

IS Code 2008 — Intact Stability Criteria
GM ≥0.15 m · GZ ≥0.20 m at 30° · area limits — 5 principles

5 Fundamental Principles of IS Code 2008

  1. General Precautions Against Capsizing: GM and GZ curve criteria.
  2. Weather Criterion: Vessel behaviour under severe beam winds and rolling.
  3. Liquid Dynamics: Free surface effect in tanks; icing accumulation on superstructure.
  4. Watertight Integrity: Maintenance of internal and external watertight boundaries.
  5. Operational Guidance for Masters: Stability booklets, heavy weather procedures.

Mandatory Intact Stability Criteria

IS Code 2008 — Minimum GZ/GM Requirements
Initial GM: ≥ 0.15 m (all loading conditions)
Peak GZ (GZmax): should occur at ≥ 30°, must not occur below 25°
GZ value at 30°: ≥ 0.20 m

GZ curve area (up to 30°): ≥ 0.055 m·rad
GZ curve area (up to 40°): ≥ 0.090 m·rad
GZ curve area (30° to 40°): ≥ 0.030 m·rad
⚡ Simon Sir typically asks

"What are the intact stability criteria?" — Quote all six values from memory. He will then ask: "What is the difference between IS Code Part A and Part B?" — Part A = mandatory criteria (above). Part B = recommendatory/additional criteria (cargo ships, fishing vessels, high-speed craft). As CE on a container vessel, Part A mandatory criteria apply.

NOS-DCP — National Oil Spill Disaster Contingency Plan
Indian Coast Guard — Tier 1/2/3 — RMPRC

Definition & Authority

A comprehensive oil pollution response network developed and operated by the Indian Coast Guard (ICG). Defines roles of government agencies and maritime entities for oil spill incidents in Indian waters.

Oil Spill Volume Tiers

NOS-DCP Spill Tier Classification
Tier 1: Small localised spills up to 700 MT — port/facility level response
Tier 2: Regional incidents up to 10,000 MT — ICG regional coordination
Tier 3: National disaster exceeding 10,000 MT — full national mobilisation

Facility Contingency Mandate

All ports, offshore oil facilities, and installations within a 500-metre operational radius must maintain independent contingency plans and spill equipment rated to handle Tier 1 events.

RMPRC & ICG Hubs

  • India's first dedicated Regional Maritime Pollution Response Centre (RMPRC) — established on the Chennai coast.
  • ICG Spill notification network hubs: Mumbai (Western), Chennai (Eastern), Gandhinagar (North-West). Andaman & Nicobar via Port Blair.
⚡ Simon Sir typically asks

"What is NOS-DCP?" — Indian Coast Guard's national oil spill response plan. Know the three tiers by volume. He may ask: "What is your vessel's SOPEP obligation?" — Every ship ≥400 GT must have a Shipboard Oil Pollution Emergency Plan (SOPEP) approved by the Flag Administration, with MRCC contact numbers included.

⚖ Regulatory References

P.59
Broaching — Course Control Failure in Following Seas
Rudder out of water — wave orbital motion — extreme heel

Definition

A dangerous hydrodynamic phenomenon where a vessel running with a following sea loses directional control and turns abruptly beam-on to the waves, creating extreme heel risk.

Mechanism

  1. Ship travels down a wave face at a speed close to the wave propagation speed.
  2. Stern lifts out of the water — rudder emerges from the water, destroying steering control.
  3. Wave orbital water motion causes the vessel to swing sideways.
  4. Combined wind and wave forces induce an extreme angle of heel — capsizing risk.
⚡ Simon Sir typically asks

"What is broaching?" — Explain the mechanism: stern lifts → rudder out of water → loss of steering → beam-on. He may ask: "How do you prevent broaching?" — Reduce speed (increase period ratio ship/wave), alter course to take seas on quarter, increase engine power momentarily to maintain steerage. Broaching is covered under heavy weather manoeuvring — SOLAS V/34 Master's responsibility.

⚖ Regulatory References
SOLAS V / Reg 34"Regulation 34 — Safe navigation and avoidance of dangerous situations" (SOLAS V)
IS Code 2008 / Part B"Part B — Recommendations" (IS Code 2008) — non-mandatory guidance on vulnerability to parametric rolling and broaching-to
IMO MSC-MEPC.2/Circ.13"Interim Guidelines on the Second Generation Intact Stability Criteria" (MSC.1/Circ.1627, or successor circular) — covers broaching-to/surf-riding vulnerability criteria; verify exact current circular number, this area has been revised multiple times

Lifeboat Engine Specifications
CR 18–20 · -15°C start in 2 min · flash point >43°C · flooded operation

4 Mandatory Design Requirements

Lifeboat Engine Specifications (LSA Code / SOLAS III)
Compression Ratio: 18 to 20 (vs ~13 for standard main engines — ensures cold starting)
Cold start capability: −15°C within 2 minutes
Fuel flash point: above 43°C (safe in tropical high-temperature conditions)
Flooded operation: must run with water up to the centreline of the crankshaft
⚡ Simon Sir typically asks

"What are the specifications of a lifeboat engine?" — Quote all four criteria. He may follow with: "Why is the flash point of lifeboat fuel above 43°C and not the standard 60°C?" — Because lifeboats may be launched in tropical conditions where deck temperatures can be extreme; 43°C provides a wider safety margin than diesel fuel's natural flash point but is lower than marine fuel minimum. Also, lifeboats often use automotive-type diesel for cold starting reliability.

⚖ Regulatory References
LSA Code / 4.4.6"4.4.6 — Lifeboat Propulsion" (LSA Code Ch. IV) — engine starting, fuel flash point, and flooded-operation requirements
SOLAS III / Reg 20"Regulation 20 — Operational readiness, maintenance and inspections" (SOLAS III) — lifeboat engine maintenance/testing schedules
SOLAS III / Reg 6.2.1"Regulation 31 — Survival craft and rescue boats" (SOLAS III) — verify precise sub-paragraph for speed/launching/endurance; general lifeboat performance parameters largely derive from LSA Code 4.4

P.57
Rescue Boat — Starboard Side Placement
Right-hand propeller → port stern walk → starboard rescue boat

Design Rationale

  • Most merchant vessels use a right-handed propeller (clockwise viewed from aft).
  • When astern power is applied (e.g., MOB emergency stop), transverse thrust (propeller walk) pushes the stern to port, swinging the bow to starboard.
  • Placing the rescue boat on the starboard side positions it away from the port-swinging stern and propeller hazard.
  • This enables faster, safer launching and recovery of the person in the water.
⚡ Simon Sir typically asks

"Why is the rescue boat placed on the starboard side?" — Propeller walk: right-handed screw → stern swings port under astern thrust → starboard side is clear of stern and propeller. He may test this with: "What if your vessel has a CPP (Controllable Pitch Propeller)?" — CPP vessels can go astern without stern walk, but starboard placement remains standard by convention.

⚖ Regulatory References
SOLAS III / Reg 26"5.1.2" (LSA Code Ch. V) — stowage and launching requirements; must remain accessible in adverse conditions
LSA Code / 5.1.2"5.1.2" (LSA Code Ch. V) — rescue boat must be capable of rapid launch and recovery

Collision Bulkhead — Structural Regulations
5–8% LBP from FP · no openings · forepeak isolation valve
Statutory Location Bounds (SOLAS II-1)
Minimum distance from FP: ≥ 5% LBP or 10 m (whichever is less)
Maximum distance from FP: ≤ 8% LBP or (0.05L + 3.0 m) (whichever is greater)
Bulbous bow allowance: reference point up to 1.5% L ahead of FP

Penetration Restrictions

  • No doors, manholes, or access openings permitted — absolute structural integrity.
  • Only one pipeline penetration allowed — specifically for filling/emptying the forepeak ballast tank.
  • This pipe must be fitted with a screw-down type isolation valve operable from above the bulkhead deck or via approved remote actuator.
⚡ Simon Sir typically asks

"Where must the collision bulkhead be located?" — Quote the 5%/8% LBP limits from FP. He may ask: "Can anything penetrate the collision bulkhead?" — Only one pipeline for the forepeak tank, fitted with a screw-down valve operable above the bulkhead deck. No doors, no manholes. This is a classic Simon Sir structural question.

⚖ Regulatory References
SOLAS II-1 / Reg 11"Regulation 11 — Collision bulkheads, chain lockers, forepeak tanks" (SOLAS II-1) — verify exact regulation number for collision bulkhead location/penetration rules against current SOLAS II-1 numbering
SOLAS II-1 / Reg 13"Regulation 13 — Openings in watertight bulkheads below the margin line" (SOLAS II-1) — verify exact regulation; collision bulkhead penetrations are tightly restricted, typically to a single approved pipe

P.64
Lithium-Ion Battery Maritime Firefighting
Class 9 DG · thermal runaway · AVD & F-500 agents · SoC 30–70%

Classification

Lithium-ion batteries: Class 9 Dangerous Goods under IMDG Code. Safe transit State of Charge: 30% to 70%.

3 Core Hazards

  1. Thermal Runaway: Self-accelerating internal chemical reaction → massive temperature spike → fire/explosion. Triggered by mechanical damage or external short circuit.
  2. Explosive Potential: Internal component breakdown generates pressurised toxic gases → explosion hazard in enclosed spaces.
  3. Re-ignition Risk: Residual chemical instability causes spontaneous re-ignition long after apparent extinguishment.

Extinguishing Agents

  • Standard water mist / CO₂: Insufficient — only cools external fire, fails to stop internal oxygen-generating reaction.
  • F-500 Encapsulator Agent: Chemically encapsulates fuel molecules, suppressing vapour production.
  • AVD (Aqueous Vermiculite Dispersion): Water cools cell externally; vermiculite platelets settle to form oxygen-blocking, heat-resistant barrier — prevents re-ignition.
  • Early detection: Gas monitoring sensors + heat/smoke detectors + thermal imaging cameras.
⚡ Simon Sir typically asks

"How do you fight a lithium-ion battery fire?" — Standard CO₂ and water mist are inadequate. Know AVD and F-500 by name. Key points: thermal runaway cannot be stopped by external cooling alone; re-ignition risk means continued monitoring hours after apparent extinction. Relevant on container vessels carrying electric vehicles (EVs) — growing concern in the industry.

⚖ Regulatory References
IMDG Code / Class 9"Class 9 — Miscellaneous dangerous substances and articles" (IMDG Code) — lithium batteries UN 3480/3481; special provisions SP188/230/310/311
MSC.1/Circ.1567"Interim Guidelines for the Carriage of Electric Vehicles/Battery Electric Vehicles in Ro-Ro Spaces" or similar — verify exact MSC.1/Circ. number and title; this area (Li-ion fire guidance) is being actively developed/revised by IMO, confirm current circular before quoting
SOLAS II-2 / FSS Code"International Code for Fire Safety Systems (FSS Code)" read with SOLAS II-2 — general fire detection/suppression framework; Li-ion-specific alternative agent guidance is an emerging/evolving area, verify latest MSC circular before quoting specifics

▼

Flag State Oversight of Recognised Organisations (RO)
6 oversight methods — FSI, audits, PSC tracking, mandatory reporting

6 Methods of RO Oversight

  1. Flag State Inspections (FSI): Independent flag checkups directly on active vessels.
  2. Supplementary/Random Surveys: Direct auditing during major SEQ and SEC surveys.
  3. PSC Tracking: Reviewing RO performance metrics and deficiency trends from regional MOU reports.
  4. Regulatory Collaboration: Continuous access to RO rule databases; flag personnel involved in rule revisions.
  5. Mandatory Reporting: RO must immediately notify Flag Admin of major deficiencies, marine casualties, or safety incidents.
  6. Formal Quality Audits: Regular audits by independent Accredited Certification Bodies (ACBs) or Flag Administration audit teams.
⚡ Simon Sir typically asks

"How does the Flag State control its Classification Society (RO)?" — List all 6 methods. He may ask: "What agreement authorises the RO to act on behalf of the flag?" — The RO Agreement under SOLAS I/Reg 6(b) and the recognition under EU Regulation 391/2009 for EU flag states. Flag state retains ultimate responsibility even when delegating to RO.

IMO Res. A.739(18)"Guidelines for the Authorization of Organizations Acting on Behalf of the Administration" (Resolution A.739(18), adopted 1993)

Vessel Class Notations — System & Examples
E0/UMS · BIS · TMON · SCR TIER III — reading the Certificate of Class

Class Notation Components

  • Construction Symbol (e.g., ✠): Hull built under continuous Classification Society survey.
  • Main Class Notation: Full compliance with baseline structural rules.
  • Ship Type Notation: Design category — e.g., Container Ship, Oil Tanker.
  • Service Area Notation: Geographical/environmental operational limits — e.g., No Restriction, Coastal.
  • Descriptive/Additional Notations: Specialised equipment or automated systems.

Common Notations — Know These

  • E0 / UMS: Engine room fully automated for Unattended Machinery Space operations.
  • BIS: Approved for In-Water Survey (underwater hull inspection in lieu of drydocking).
  • TMON: Tailshaft monitoring — continuous oil-lubricated bearing parameter tracking.
  • SCR TIER III: Selective Catalytic Reduction fitted — meets MARPOL Annex VI Tier III NOₓ limits.
  • PWM(T): Approved waste heat recovery system fitted.
⚡ Simon Sir typically asks

BIS is relevant if your vessel has extended dry-docking intervals.

P.60
Quarterly Class Listing & Tacit Acceptance
Class status report · tacit acceptance rejection: 1/3 states or 50% GT

Quarterly Class Listing

A technical status report issued to shipowners to track surveys, audits, and certificate deadlines. Contains:

  • Core vessel dimensions and registry information.
  • Current class notations and structural symbols.
  • Due dates for upcoming statutory surveys.
  • Expiry dates for all classification and safety certificates.
  • Active Conditions of Class (CoCs) and memoranda items.

Tacit Acceptance Procedure

IMO adopts amendment & sets objection deadline
▼ Contracting governments remain silent during objection window
Amendment automatically enters into force for all parties at deadline
Tacit Acceptance — Rejection Threshold
Amendment blocked if objections received from:
• One-third (1/3) of all contracting governments, OR
• Governments representing ≥ 50% of world gross merchant shipping tonnage
⚡ Simon Sir typically asks

"What is the tacit acceptance procedure?" — Silence = acceptance; amendment enters force automatically unless 1/3 of states or 50% GT object within the deadline. He may ask why IMO uses this: it speeds up amendment adoption — previously amendments needed explicit ratification by each state, causing long delays.

International Legislation Hierarchy & EIF Process
Convention → Protocol → Amendment → Code → Act · 5-step EIF

Legislative Hierarchy

  • Convention: Broad multilateral treaty between nations — legally binding.
  • Protocol: Ancillary agreement introducing major changes to a convention.
  • Amendment: Minor modification — fast-tracked via tacit acceptance.
  • Resolution: Resolves protocol disputes; ongoing IMO review loops.
  • Code: Mandatory or recommendatory technical details for execution.
  • Act (domestic): National law passed by Parliament, approved by President.

How an IMO Convention Enters into Force — 5 Steps

1. Drafting — IMO technical committees draft text based on safety needs
▼
2. Adoption — Assembly conference: 2/3 quorum present; 2/3 vote in favour
▼
3. Signature — States declare intention to join; pledge not to act against the convention's spirit
▼
4. Ratification — States incorporate into domestic law; submit instrument of ratification to IMO
▼
5. Entry into Force (EIF) — Activates after fixed period once minimum tonnage and state number criteria met

"How does an IMO convention enter into force?" — All 5 steps. Then he may ask: "What is the difference between signature and ratification?" — Signature = declaration of intent; ratification = domestic law incorporation with binding effect. He may also ask: "What is accession?" — A state joining a convention already in force (not an original signatory).

4 Types of P&I Capital Calls

  • ETC (Estimated Total Call): Baseline premium calculated and billed at the start of the policy year — based on estimated annual claims.
  • Unbudgeted Supplementary Call: Additional premium levied on all members if annual claims outpace the club's financial reserves.
  • Release Call: Premium charged when a shipowner withdraws a vessel from the club — covers outstanding liability before the policy year closes.
  • Overspill Call: Catastrophic assessment levied if a massive claim exceeds the club's standard reinsurance limits — pooled across the International Group of P&I Clubs.

FMEA — Failure Mode and Effect Analysis
RPN = O × S × D · max 1000 · action threshold >100

Definition

A proactive, structured risk assessment methodology originally developed by the U.S. Military to systematically identify, analyse, and mitigate potential failure modes before they occur.

4 Standard Execution Steps

  1. Identify the system, sub-system, or component to be analysed.
  2. Identify all potential failure modes for each component.
  3. Determine the underlying causes of each potential failure.
  4. Evaluate the operational effects and consequences of each failure mode.
Risk Priority Number (RPN) — FMEA Metric
RPN = Occurrence (O) × Severity (S) × Detectability (D)
Each scored 1–10 | Maximum RPN: 1,000
Action threshold: RPN > 100 → immediate corrective action required
⚡ Simon Sir typically asks

"What is FMEA and how is RPN calculated?" — Define FMEA, list 4 steps, quote RPN = O × S × D, max 1000, action threshold 100. He may link this to ISM Code Cl. 7 (shipboard operations — risk assessment) and ask how FMEA differs from HAZID/HAZOP. FMEA is component-level; HAZOP is process/flow-level.

P.71
ISM "Apex" Safety Management Manual
Definition per MS Notice 09/2014 · document control & amendment authority explained

Definition — per the primary source

D.G. Shipping's own MS Notice 09 of 2014 (para 6.2.1.1) is the actual origin of the term "Apex Manual." It defines it as: the company's Safety Management Manual, which must contain policies, objectives, organisational structure, and responsibilities/authorities of relevant personnel, and which addresses all elements of the ISM Code. The apex manual cross-references subordinate operational manuals, instructions, checklists, and forms — but the Notice itself does not split the manual into named "Tier 1/2/3" categories, and does not assign different approval authorities to different parts of it.

Common Teaching Simplification

Many oral-coaching sources (including earlier versions of this note) describe the SMS documentation as a 3-tier ISO 9001-style hierarchy for teaching purposes:

  • Tier 1 — Policy: Company safety/environmental policy, objectives, organisational structure.
  • Tier 2 — Procedures: Specific shipboard plans and operational safety procedures.
  • Tier 3 — Forms: Working forms, checklists, and record sheets.

This structure is a useful way to organise the manual's contents — but it is not found in MS Notice 09/2014, the ISM Code, or any DGMA circular, and neither is a rule assigning specific tiers to Flag/RO vs. DPA approval.

Amendment Authority — what is actually verified
Neither MS Notice 09/2014 nor the ISM Code (Cl. 11) assigns approval of "Tier 1," "Tier 2," or "Tier 3" specifically to Flag/RO vs. DPA. Cl. 11 requires only that document changes be "reviewed and approved by authorized personnel" under the Company's own document-control procedure.

What Flag/RO (D.G. Shipping / DGMA) does control, per MS Notice 09/2014: (a) preliminary scrutiny of the apex SMS manual as part of the initial DOC application (§8.1.1.5, §8.1.2); (b) issuing/endorsing the DOC and SMC; (c) verifying, at annual/intermediate/renewal audit, that the Company's document-control system functions correctly; (d) DPA/ADPA qualification and any *change of DPA* must be reported to DGS (§7.3.1) — this is the one genuine "must-report-to-Flag" trigger in the Notice, and it concerns the *person*, not a manual tier. Day-to-day content amendments across the manual (whichever "tier" they sit in) are approved internally per the Company's own document-control procedure — subject to audit, not pre-approval, by Flag/RO.
⚡ Simon Sir typically asks

"What is the ISM apex manual?" — the top-level Safety Management Manual per MS Notice 09/2014 §6.2.1.1, addressing all ISM Code elements and cross-referencing subordinate manuals/forms. If he frames it as "3 tiers," describe Policy → Procedures → Forms as the standard way of organising it. If he probes "who approves changes to each tier," the defensible answer is: neither the Code nor MS Notice 09/2014 mandates a fixed Tier-vs-Flag/RO split — document changes are approved by "authorized personnel" under the Company's own document-control procedure (Cl. 11); Flag/RO's role is scrutiny at initial DOC application and periodic audit, not routine amendment sign-off. Be ready to describe your own company's document-control hierarchy in practice.

⚖ Regulatory References
MS Notice 09/2014"International Safety Management (ISM) Code on Indian ships" (No. ENG/ISM/59(4)/97, dated 15.05.2014) — §6.2 "SMS Manuals" is the primary source defining the "Apex Manual." Supersedes MS Notices 09/2003 & 28/2003 and 33 Engineering Circulars. Full text: indiacode.nic.in — MS Notice 09 of 2014 (PDF) · MIW condensed reference extract (§6.2 highlighted): Download PDF

NSB & Merchant Shipping Act 2025
NSB 1959 · Board = up to 22 members (6 MPs + ≤16 nominated, ≥4 women) · MSA 2025 in force 15 Mar 2026, replaces 1958 Act

NSB — National Shipping Board

  • Permanent statutory advisory body established 1 March 1959 under Section 4 of the Merchant Shipping Act 1958 (provision carried forward under the new MSA 2025 as the "Shipping Board").
  • Under the Ministry of Ports, Shipping and Waterways (MoPSW).
  • Statutory composition — Section 4, MSA 2025 (verified against Gazette text, Act No. 24 of 2025): Total up to 22 members — (a) 6 members of Parliament (4 elected by/from the Lok Sabha + 2 elected by/from the Rajya Sabha); (b) up to 16 additional members nominated by the Central Government to represent Central Government, shipowners, seafarers and other interests, of which at least 4 shall be women, with an equal number of shipowner and seafarer representatives mandated among the 16. The Chairperson is nominated by the Central Government from among the Board's own members (not necessarily an MP).
  • Current seated board (2025 reconstitution, pre-MSA 2025 rules): 16 members total (Chairman + 15) — DG Shipping/Chief Surveyor, Ministry of Petroleum & Natural Gas rep, Indian Navy rep, Indian Coast Guard rep, INSA (shipowners), ICCSA/coastal shipping assoc., NUSI/MUI (seafarer unions), IRS, an academic (IIT), and port/industry representatives — no sitting MPs on this particular board. Know both the exact statutory formula (22 max, 4 women minimum) and that the practical seated board can look different pending fresh reconstitution under the new Act's rules.
  • Advisory scope: national shipping policies, coastal/international shipping, seafarer training, welfare, marine safety, environmental protection, maritime law amendments.

Merchant Shipping Act 2025 — Key Changes (from the former MS Bill 2024)

  • Introduced in Lok Sabha December 2024; passed by Lok Sabha 6 Aug 2025 and Rajya Sabha 11 Aug 2025; received Presidential assent 18 Aug 2025 as the Merchant Shipping Act, 2025 (No. 24 of 2025); brought into force by government notification from 15 March 2026, repealing the Merchant Shipping Act 1958 (except Part XIV) and the Coasting Vessels Act 1838.
  • Structure: Condenses 561 sections (1958 Act) into 325 sections across 16 Parts.
  • DG Shipping renamed: Director-General now styled Director-General of Maritime Administration (DGMA).
  • NRI/OCI/foreign corporate ownership: Enables NRIs, OCIs, and other Central-Government-notified entities to register vessels under Indian flag; wholly-owned-by-Indian requirement relaxed.
  • Bareboat Charter Registry: Registration of foreign vessels under bareboat charter-cum-demise (BBCD) agreements, ownership intended to transfer to the Indian charterer.
  • Vessel definition expanded: Submersibles, hydrofoils, MODUs, non-displacement craft included. Registration mandatory regardless of propulsion or size — removes the old 15-ton non-mechanically-propelled coastal exemption.
  • Seafarer welfare: Extends statutory protection and social security to Indian seafarers, aligns with MLC 2006; retains National Welfare Board for Seafarers.
  • MTI regulation: DGMA empowered to approve/regulate Maritime Training Institutes and courses.
  • MARPOL/SOLAS/Wreck Removal: Incorporates SOLAS, MARPOL, Ballast Water Management Convention and Nairobi Wreck Removal Convention obligations, including shipowner-funded wreck removal costs.
  • Compliance transparency: Mandatory disclosure of all shipping charges to exporters/importers; penalties up to ₹5 lakh for non-disclosure.
⚡ Simon Sir typically asks

"What is the Merchant Shipping Act 2025 and when did it come into force?" — Key answer: repeals the 1958 Act, in force from 15 March 2026, allows NRI/OCI/foreign flag registration, expands vessel definitions, renames DG Shipping to DGMA, aligns with MLC. This is a very current topic and status changes fast — re-verify the in-force date and any pending Rules (draft MS Rules were still under stakeholder consultation as of early 2026) close to your oral date. On composition: if asked "how many members and what's the women representation?" — up to 22 total (6 MPs + up to 16 nominated), at least 4 of the 16 must be women, equal shipowner/seafarer representation among the 16 (Section 4, MSA 2025 — verified against the Gazette text).
