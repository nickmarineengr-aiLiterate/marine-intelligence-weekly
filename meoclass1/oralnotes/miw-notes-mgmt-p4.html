Topic 16

Hazardous & Noxious Substances (HNS) & Harmful Packaged Cargoes

MARPOL Annex III · HNS Convention Uday pp. 76–80 Examiner: Nair ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: The source draft's HNS definition and "no direct government contribution" statement are directionally correct but paraphrased from the 1996 HNS Convention / 2010 HNS Protocol, not verbatim treaty text — labelled below accordingly. The 100 million SDR / 250 million SDR two-tier figures are presented here as the commonly-cited indicative maximums; the exact figures depend on ship type/tonnage under the 2010 Protocol text and should be treated as typical exam figures, not quoted as fixed statute numbers. IMDG segregation distances (3 m / 6 m) are practical shipboard rule-of-thumb figures, not exact IMDG Code regulatory text — the Code itself defines segregation qualitatively (Away From / Separated From / etc.) with distances set out in IMDG Vol. 1 General Provisions §7.2.

📖 Definition

Hazardous and Noxious Substances (HNS) are, in simple terms, any substance other than oil that — if it enters the sea — could harm human health, marine life, amenities, or other legitimate uses of the sea (definition adapted from the 1996 HNS Convention, Article 1 — paraphrased, not word-for-word). Harmful Packaged Cargoes are marine pollutants carried in containers, portable tanks, or tank wagons rather than in bulk — think a container of paint thinner or pesticide rather than a tanker full of chemicals. Together, MARPOL Annex III, the IMDG Code, and the 2010 HNS Protocol form the three-layer framework covering identification/packaging, safe carriage, and financial liability respectively.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)

  • Container ships routinely carry hundreds of IMDG-classed boxes — the CE must understand ventilation, fire-fighting, and segregation implications, not just cargo planning.
  • PSC inspectors specifically target placarding, manifest accuracy, and bilge/ventilation isolation on HNS-carrying vessels — a weak answer here risks detention-related questions in the oral.
  • Class 8 corrosive or Class 3 flammable leaks in a hold directly affect engine room systems (bilge, ventilation, electrical isolation) — this is squarely CE territory, not just cargo officer territory.
  • The 2010 HNS Protocol liability regime is a favourite "why was the original convention revised" question for Nair.

📅 Historical Background

1996 → Original HNS Convention adopted — heavy reporting burden on packaged-goods receivers made ratification impractical for many states 2010 → Protocol of 2010 to the HNS Convention adopted — simplified reporting, shifted focus to major bulk receivers 2021 → 2010 HNS Protocol entered into force after reaching ratification threshold

CE Oral Tip — Nair Sir: Nair likes to test whether candidates know the HNS Fund is financed by cargo receivers, not governments — say "financed by persons in contracting states who receive contributing cargoes above threshold quantities" rather than a vague "the fund pays." If pushed on exact SDR figures, frame them as "indicative maximums under the 2010 Protocol" rather than quoting them as if reciting statute text.

🎯 Oral Q&A

Q1: What is HNS and how does it differ from oil pollution liability?
HNS is any substance other than oil which, if released into the sea, could harm human health, marine life, amenities, or legitimate sea uses. Unlike the oil pollution regime (CLC/Fund Convention), HNS liability is split into a two-tier system: shipowner strict liability (Tier 1) backed by compulsory insurance, and a post-incident industry-funded HNS Fund (Tier 2) for damages exceeding the shipowner's cap.
Q2: Why was the original 1996 HNS Convention revised in 2010?
Many states found the 1996 Convention's reporting requirements for fragmented, small-volume packaged cargo receivers operationally unworkable — tracking every small packaged HNS shipment was an excessive administrative burden. The 2010 Protocol simplified this by shifting the primary reporting obligation onto major bulk receivers, while keeping the underlying liability structure intact, making ratification practical.
Q3: Who pays into the HNS Fund, and when is it triggered?
The Fund is financed by post-incident contributions from individual receivers of HNS cargo who import quantities above specified threshold volumes — contracting governments make no direct budget contribution. It is triggered once the shipowner's Tier 1 liability cap is exhausted or if the shipowner is financially insolvent.
Q4: As Chief Engineer, what documentary check must you make before loading IMDG cargo?
I verify the vessel holds a valid Document of Compliance for the Carriage of Dangerous Goods matching the exact classes loaded, cross-check the stowage plan against the IMDG Segregation Matrix, and confirm cargo hold ventilation, bilge isolation, and gas detection systems are fully functional before cargo operations commence.
Q5: What are the four IMDG segregation categories?
"Away From" (minimum practical separation, can share a compartment), "Separated From" (different compartments/holds, wider separation on deck), "Separated by a Complete Compartment or Hold From" (a full steel bulkhead between), and "Separated Longitudinally by an Intervening Complete Compartment or Hold From" (a full intermediate hold as buffer). Exact distances are set in IMDG Code Vol. 1 General Provisions §7.2, not fixed universal numbers.

HSSC Survey Points for HNS-Certified Vessels

Initial survey covers structural fire insulation, cargo-space electrical isolation, bilge pump arrangements, and ventilation fire dampers. Annual/intermediate surveys verify containment system integrity, gas detection calibration, and documentation currency. Renewal (5-yearly) requires hydrostatic testing of hold bilge systems and full insulation-resistance testing of cargo-zone electrical equipment.

PSC Detention Risk Framing

Missing or illegible IMDG placards on a container carrying marine pollutants is commonly treated by PSC as a serious deficiency that can lead to detention under MARPOL Annex III / SOLAS Chapter VII — this is a strong practical/exam warning, but detention itself remains a discretionary PSC decision under the relevant regional MOU, not an automatic legal consequence.

Topic 17

Sewage & Garbage Management — MARPOL Annex IV & Annex V

MARPOL Annex IV/V Uday pp. 81–85 Examiner: Nair ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: Discharge distance/speed criteria and macerator particle-size figures are correct as commonly cited exam figures, but MARPOL's own food-waste text is phrased as "comminuted to pass through a screen no greater than 25 mm," not as a fixed design spec — worded accordingly below. Added the "no visible floating solids/no discoloration" condition to the sewage matrix, and clarified that Annex IV applies "on international voyages" per regulation 2, with Parties free to extend it domestically.

📖 Definition

MARPOL Annex IV governs discharge of sewage (black/grey water) from ships, while Annex V governs garbage — all solid waste generated during ship operation except cargo residues covered elsewhere. Both annexes apply distance-from-land and vessel-speed criteria to control discharge, with Annex V additionally imposing an absolute worldwide ban on plastics.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)

  • The sewage treatment plant is squarely an engine department responsibility — bacterial die-off, macerator faults, and pH imbalance are CE-level troubleshooting, not deck department.
  • Garbage Record Book and Sewage discharge logging errors are a top PSC detention trigger — CE must know exact distance/speed criteria cold.
  • Unauthorized bypass lines around treatment plants are treated by PSC as clear evidence of intentional pollution — a CE who doesn't audit for this exposes the ship (and personally, the Master/CE) to prosecution risk.

📅 Historical Background

1978 → MARPOL 73/78 Protocol — Annexes IV & V included in original convention structure 2003 → Annex IV entered into force (delayed ratification vs other Annexes) 2013 → Annex V revised — shifted to default "all discharge prohibited unless specifically permitted"
⚖ Regulatory References

CE Oral Tip — Nair Sir: Nair often asks the exact discharge distances — answer with the full condition, not just the number: "greater than 12 nautical miles, en route, at not less than 4 knots, for uncomminuted sewage." Dropping "en route" or the speed condition is a common half-mark loss.

🎯 Oral Q&A

Q1: Under what conditions can uncomminuted sewage be discharged?
Only beyond 12 nautical miles from the nearest land, while the ship is en route, proceeding at not less than 4 knots.
Q2: What additional discharge conditions apply to a type-approved sewage treatment plant?
Effluent from a certified plant may be discharged at any distance, but the discharge must not produce visible floating solids or cause discolouration of the surrounding water, and remains subject to any stricter coastal State requirements.
Q3: What is the food waste discharge rule inside a Special Area under Annex V?
Whole (uncomminuted) food waste is completely banned inside Special Areas. Comminuted food waste able to pass a 25 mm screen may only be discharged beyond 12 nautical miles from the nearest land while the vessel is en route.
Q4: What is the plastics discharge rule under Annex V?
A complete, worldwide ban — plastics of any kind, including synthetic ropes, fishing nets, and plastic garbage bags, may never be discharged into the sea, regardless of location.
Q5: As CE, how do you respond to a biological sewage plant die-off approaching an ECA?
Immediately close and lock out the overboard discharge valve, re-route sewage to the holding tank, diagnose the cause (commonly pH imbalance from harsh cleaning chemicals or fouled air diffusers), rehabilitate the chamber, and re-seed with fresh bacterial culture, running in full recirculation until a healthy foam layer confirms colony recovery before resuming normal operation.

PSC Bypass-Line Focus

PSC inspectors routinely check for unauthorized temporary hoses or newly-installed valves bypassing the sewage/bilge treatment unit. This is commonly treated as a serious deficiency pointing to intentional violation, carrying high detention and prosecution risk — always keep piping diagrams matching the as-built system with no unlogged modifications.

Chemical Sensitivity of Biological Plants

Never allow high-strength bleach or caustic commercial cleaners into cabin toilets feeding a biological plant — these kill the aerobic bacterial colony. Use only approved biodegradable, enzyme-based cleaners fleet-wide.

Atmospheric Emissions — MARPOL Annex VI: Sulphur, Incineration, VECS

MARPOL Annex VI Uday pp. 86–90 Examiner: Nair ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: Global/ECA sulphur limits and BDN/sample retention periods are correct and current. The "Min 350°C within 1 min" incinerator figure in the source diagram is a manufacturer design feature, not a regulatory figure — labelled as such below, distinct from the MEPC.76(40)-aligned statutory ≥850°C / ≥0.5 s dwell time requirement. PVC incineration is clarified: it is restricted (only permitted in a type-approved incinerator meeting the resolution) rather than a blanket prohibition on all PVC.

📖 Definition

MARPOL Annex VI regulates air pollution from ships — sulphur oxides (SOx) via fuel sulphur content limits, incinerator design/operation standards, and tanker vapour emission control systems (VECS) for volatile cargo vapours.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)

  • Fuel changeover mismanagement (thermal shock during VLSFO→MGO transition) is a real engine-room casualty risk, not just a paperwork exercise.
  • BDN retention and MARPOL sample custody are checked at nearly every PSC inspection.
  • Incinerator prohibited-materials list is frequently tested — burning the wrong material is both an environmental and a safety (toxic fume) issue.

📅 Historical Background

2005 → MARPOL Annex VI entered into force 2015 → ECA sulphur limit reduced to 0.10% m/m 2020 → Global sulphur cap reduced to 0.50% m/m ("IMO 2020")

CE Oral Tip — Nair Sir: When asked about incinerator temperature, separate the statutory figure (≥850°C combustion gas temperature, ≥0.5 s dwell time, per MEPC.76(40)) from any manufacturer-specific design feature quoted in local documentation — Nair will probe whether you can tell the two apart.

🎯 Oral Q&A

Q1: What are the current global and ECA sulphur limits?
Global limit is 0.50% m/m sulphur in fuel oil, in force since 1 January 2020. Inside a SOx Emission Control Area, the limit is 0.10% m/m, in force since 1 January 2015.
Q2: What are the BDN and fuel sample retention requirements?
The Bunker Delivery Note must be retained on board for a minimum of 3 years, and a representative fuel sample sealed at the time of bunkering must be retained for a minimum of 1 year.
Q3: Can PVC be incinerated on board?
Only in an IMO type-approved incinerator that meets MEPC.76(40) — combustion at or above 850°C with adequate dwell time. Uncontrolled incineration of PVC is prohibited due to the risk of forming corrosive and toxic halogenated combustion products.
Q4: What causes fuel pump seal failure during a rapid ECA fuel changeover, and how do you prevent it?
Too rapid a temperature drop during changeover thermally shocks fuel pump plungers and seals, causing leaks or seizure. Preventing it means limiting the temperature ramp rate — typically around 1–2°C per minute — using a properly calibrated automatic or manual changeover sequence, completed well before entering the ECA boundary.

VECS (Vapour Emission Control System)

Flow path: cargo tank striping → high-velocity P/V valves (overpressure relief) → continuous O2 analyzer (alarm/trip at ≥8% O2) → mechanical flame arrestor (stops external flashback) → VECS vapour main manifold → shore connection to terminal vapour recovery unit. This is a tanker-specific system relevant if Nixon later serves on tankers.

Topic 19

NOx Technical Code — Tiers I/II/III, SCR, EGR, Miller Cycle

Annex VI reg. 13 · NOx Technical Code Uday pp. 91–95 Examiner: Simon ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: HAM (~70%), EGR (60–70%), and SCR (≥90%) reduction figures are realistic, widely-cited manufacturer/engine-builder performance figures — not hard statutory values written into MARPOL itself — labelled "typical" below. SCR's 300–400°C recommended window is design-specific guidance, not a fixed IMO number. Any engine modification outside the approved Technical File range requires re-verification (and possibly a revised Technical File/EIAPP), not an automatic permanent voiding — phrased accordingly.

📖 Definition

The NOx Technical Code (under MARPOL Annex VI regulation 13) sets tiered nitrogen oxide emission limits for marine diesel engines above 130 kW, based on engine rated speed. Tier I and II apply globally; Tier III applies only inside a designated NOx Emission Control Area (NECA), requiring abatement technology such as SCR or EGR, or engine-design measures like the Miller Cycle.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)

  • Every 2-stroke main engine on a Maersk container vessel carries an EIAPP Certificate and NOx Technical File — CE must know what triggers re-verification.
  • SCR/EGR troubleshooting (stuck valves, ammonium bisulphate fouling) is direct engine-room casualty response material.
  • Fuel pump/injector part-number matching against the Technical File is a routine PSC and survey check.
⚖ Regulatory References

CE Oral Tip — Simon Sir: Simon likes the Miller Cycle mechanism explained precisely — Early Inlet Valve Closing (EIVC) traps air before BDC, so it expands and cools before compression, lowering peak flame temperature and thermal NOx. Say "reduces charge air temperature at start of compression," not just "reduces temperature."

🎯 Oral Q&A

Q1: What is an EIAPP Certificate?
Engine International Air Pollution Prevention Certificate — confirms a specific marine diesel engine has been factory test-bed tested and complies with the applicable Tier NOx limit under Annex VI regulation 13.
Q2: How do you use the NOx Technical File in daily engine room work?
It defines approved component specifications and allowed adjustable-setting ranges. Using the Parameter Check Method, I verify replacement parts (injector nozzles, fuel pump plungers) carry correct approved part numbers, and keep settings like injection timing within the documented range.
Q3: Explain how the Miller Cycle reduces NOx.
Using Early Inlet Valve Closing, the intake valve shuts before bottom dead centre, trapping a smaller air charge that then expands and cools during the remainder of the induction stroke before compression begins. This lowers the air temperature at the start of compression, reducing peak combustion flame temperature and suppressing thermal NOx formation.
Q4: Why must SCR urea injection be avoided below 300°C exhaust temperature?
Below the recommended operating window, urea does not fully decompose to ammonia and instead forms solid ammonium bisulphate deposits, which blind the catalyst surface, degrade reduction performance, and can raise exhaust backpressure to the point of risking engine surge.
Q5: A vessel's Tier III mode shows NOx above the NECA limit — what's your diagnostic approach?
I compare current settings against the approved Technical File limits, check exhaust gas analyzer calibration, then physically inspect the EGR/SCR abatement system — a common root cause is a sluggish or partially-stuck EGR valve actuator reducing recirculated exhaust volume, which raises combustion temperature and NOx output. Correcting the actuator restores compliant NOx levels.

SOLAS 1974, FSS/FTP Codes, Class A/B Divisions & 2024 Inert Gas System Amendments

SOLAS · FSS Code · FTP Code Uday pp. 96–100 Examiner: Senthil ⭐ High Frequency
🔎 Verification note: Class A-60/B-30 temperature figures and SOLAS tonnage/exclusion list are correct and match the FTP Code/SOLAS II-2. The "January 2024" Inert Gas System amendments (downstream non-return device, branch isolation, continuous O2/pressure recording) reflect the direction of recent FSS Code Chapter 15 amendments but the source wording is paraphrased — phrased below as "recent amendments" with a pointer to verify exact clause numbers/entry-into-force date against the current FSS Code text before quoting it as gospel in the oral.

📖 Definition

SOLAS (Safety of Life at Sea) 1974 is the primary international treaty covering ship construction, equipment, and safety of life at sea. It applies to passenger ships and cargo ships ≥500 GT on international voyages, with defined exclusions (warships, cargo ships <500 GT, wooden ships of primitive build, non-mechanically-propelled ships, fishing vessels, and pleasure yachts not engaged in trade). The FSS Code and FTP Code provide the detailed technical fire-protection and fire-testing standards that SOLAS Chapter II-2 references.

⚓ Why It Matters (CE Perspective)

  • Fire division integrity (A/B class bulkheads) is inspected constantly — a CE must know material and temperature-rise limits cold for both oral and practical survey purposes.
  • Inert Gas System design changes directly affect tanker cargo safety — even container-vessel CEs are examined on this as general engineering knowledge.
  • FSS Code chapter familiarity underpins virtually every fire-fighting equipment survey a CE will face.
⚖ Regulatory References
SOLAS Ch. I, reg. 3Application and exclusions
SOLAS Ch. II-2Fire protection, detection, and extinction — Main Vertical Zones, A/B/C divisions
FTP Code (2010)Fire Test Procedures — laboratory type-approval testing for divisions/materials
FSS Code, Ch. 15Inert Gas Systems — recently amended (verify exact clause/date before quoting in exam)

CE Oral Tip — Senthil Sir: Senthil expects the exact numbers — "A-60: average ≤140°C, single point ≤180°C; B-30: average ≤139°C, single point ≤225°C" — memorise these as pairs, not just "A is stricter than B." 🎯 Oral Q&A

Q1: What are the engineering differences between Class A-60 and Class B-30 bulkheads?
A-60 must be steel or equivalent, stiffened, holding flame/smoke integrity for 60 minutes, with unexposed-face temperature rise limited to ≤140°C average / ≤180°C at any point. B-30 can use approved non-combustible materials without requiring steel, holds integrity for 30 minutes, with unexposed-face limits of ≤139°C average / ≤225°C at any point.
Q2: What vessels are excluded from SOLAS application under Chapter I regulation 3?
Warships and troopships; cargo ships under 500 GT; ships of primitive build (wooden, non-mechanically propelled); fishing vessels; and pleasure yachts not engaged in trade.
Q3: Explain the recent (2024-direction) changes to Inert Gas System design under FSS Code Chapter 15.
Recent amendments clarify that the non-return device must be located downstream of the main IG isolating valve to reliably prevent cargo vapour backflow; each branch line requires its own effective isolation; and oxygen content plus system pressure must be continuously indicated and permanently recorded while IG is being supplied to cargo tanks. I'd confirm the exact clause number and entry-into-force date against the current FSS Code text before quoting it verbatim in the exam.
Q4: What is a Main Vertical Zone under SOLAS II-2?
A structural fire-protection subdivision of the ship's length by A-class bulkheads and decks, limiting the spread of fire and smoke to a defined zone and supporting the ship's overall damage/fire-control strategy.

  • NR device downstream — 2024-direction IG amendment, key phrase
  • S.H.I.E.L.D. — Structural, Hose, Inert-gas, Emergency pump, Low-location lighting, Dampers

Goal-Based Standards (GBS)

New oil tankers and bulk carriers over 150 m are broadly subject to Goal-Based Standards, implemented in practice through the IACS Common Structural Rules made mandatory via SOLAS II-1 Part A-1. Full CSR detail is not required for orals unless specifically asked — know the GBS/CSR linkage exists.

GMDSS & Navigation Equipment Checks (Ch. IV & V)

For Chapter IV: verify Safety Radio Certificate validity, radio logbook testing entries, GMDSS console battery reserves, HRU/battery expiry on portable survival craft VHF, EPIRB, and SART. For Chapter V: verify VDR annual performance test certificate, AIS data accuracy, ECDIS chart currency against Notices to Mariners, and magnetic compass deviation card validity.
