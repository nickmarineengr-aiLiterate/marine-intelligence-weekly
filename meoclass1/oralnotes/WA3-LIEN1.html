Model Answer · Written Question
What is a Maritime Lien? How does this lien travel with property? Explain the associated terms "In Rem" and "In Personam". Which convention deals with maritime lien? Give the order of settlement of various debtor's claims after legal action as per this convention.

1 · Definition & Purpose

A Maritime Lien is a privileged, non-possessory right or claim upon a maritime property — specifically the vessel, its cargo, or freight — for damages caused by it or services rendered to it. Unlike a common-law lien, a maritime lien does not require the claimant to hold physical possession of the property. Instead, it attaches to the physical asset (the res) automatically by operation of law from the moment the underlying cause of action arises.

The core purpose of a maritime lien is to facilitate international commerce by providing a powerful security interest to entities providing essential services to a vessel (salvage providers, crew members, repair yards) or suffering damage from it (collision victims). It effectively treats the vessel as an independent legal entity accountable for its liabilities, ensuring claimants have a direct avenue of financial recovery that survives changes in the vessel's ownership, registry, or management.

2 · Importance to International Shipping

The global maritime industry depends on the rapid movement of vessels across multiple sovereign jurisdictions. This operational model introduces unique credit and legal risks: shipowners are frequently single-purpose corporate entities located in offshore jurisdictions, making them difficult to pursue through ordinary corporate channels.

  • Securing high-risk services: Salvage operators risk capital and human life to save a vessel in distress because a high-priority salvage lien automatically attaches to the hull, guaranteeing their claim in an admiralty court.
  • Guaranteeing crew protection: Crews provide labour knowing their wages form a high-priority lien that allows the vessel to be arrested to secure payment, irrespective of the insolvency of the shipowner or charterer.
  • Facilitating credit: Bunker suppliers, shipwrights, and provisioning yards extend credit to foreign-flagged vessels because the law provides a mechanism to hold the vessel itself responsible for payment.

3 · Historical Background & Origins

The maritime lien evolved from the concept of personification of the ship, a doctrine rooted in classical maritime codes — the Roman Digests, the medieval Consolato del Mare, and the Laws of Oléron. Under this doctrine, a ship is treated not merely as property owned by an individual, but as a juristic entity capable of committing torts and contracting debts.

During the 18th and 19th centuries, English Admiralty Courts and US Federal Courts formalised this personification to meet the needs of expanding global trade. Requiring a claimant to locate and sue a foreign shipowner in their home jurisdiction was commercially unworkable, so the law adapted to allow the vessel itself to be sued, treating the hull as the primary debtor. This gave rise to the modern "secret lien" — called secret because it does not require recording in a public registry to remain valid and binding against the asset.

Plain-Language Recap — What a Maritime Lien Actually Is

Strip away the legal language and a maritime lien is simple: it's an automatic, invisible "IOU" that attaches itself to the ship the moment someone is owed money because of something the ship did or something someone did for the ship — no paperwork, no registration, nothing signed. It behaves less like a bank loan and more like a claim baked into the ship's own identity. Sell the ship, change the flag, change the company — the debt is still stuck to the hull until it's paid, the ship is destroyed, a year passes without enforcement, or a court auctions the ship and wipes the slate clean. That's really the whole concept; everything else in this chapter is detail on how that plays out in practice.

Indian Domestic Law — Admiralty Act 2017, Section 9

Alongside the international 1993 Convention, Indian courts apply their own statutory priority list under Section 9 of the Admiralty (Jurisdiction and Settlement of Maritime Claims) Act, 2017. This is the ranking a Kochi MMD examiner will typically ask for directly:

  1. Salvage costs
  2. Wages and other sums due to the master, officers, and crew — this is where the Chief Engineer's own claim sits; second-highest priority, ranking above all mortgages
  3. Loss of life or personal injury in connection with the ship's operation
  4. Damage caused by the ship (tort — collision, cargo damage)
  5. Port, canal, and pilotage dues

Registered mortgages rank below all five of these categories. Note the ordering is slightly different from the plain Article 4 list — under Indian law, salvage is placed ahead of wages at the top, whereas the 1993 Convention's Article 4 lists wages first and treats salvage's "jump the queue" priority as a separate rule under Article 5(2) (explained below) rather than a fixed first-place ranking. Both frameworks reach a broadly similar result — salvage and wages dominate the top of the table — but memorise them as two distinct lists, since an examiner may ask for either one specifically.

Article 4(2) — The Compulsory-Insurance Carve-Out

One precise exception is worth knowing: under Article 4(2) of the 1993 Convention, no maritime lien attaches under categories (b) — loss of life/personal injury — or (e) — tort damage — where compensation for that claim is already available through a separate compulsory-insurance regime. The two examples that come up in practice are oil pollution damage under the CLC (Civil Liability Convention) and nuclear or radioactive damage, both of which already carry their own dedicated strict-liability compensation schemes. The logic is straightforward: if a claimant already has a guaranteed, insurance-backed route to compensation, the Convention doesn't also hand them a maritime lien on top of it.

Article 5 — Priority Among the Liens Themselves (Full Structure)

Article 5 has four distinct sub-rules, and exam answers often lose marks by collapsing them into one. In plain terms, think of it as: liens beat mortgages (5(1)) → liens rank in listed order, except salvage jumps ahead of anything already attached (5(2)) → four of the five categories share equally within their own rank (5(3)) → and if there's more than one salvage claim, the newest one gets paid first (5(4)):

  • Article 5(1): the Article 4 liens take priority over registered mortgages, hypothèques, and charges — no other claim outranks them.
  • Article 5(2): the liens generally rank in the order listed in Article 4, except that a salvage lien (category (c)) takes priority over every other lien that had already attached to the vessel before the salvage operation took place. The reasoning: the salvage operation is what preserved the ship — and therefore preserved the very asset the earlier lienholders are claiming against — so the salvor is rewarded by jumping the queue ahead of them.
  • Article 5(3): liens falling under categories (a), (b), (d) and (e) rank pari passu (equally, sharing pro-rata) among themselves where more than one claim falls into the same category.
  • Article 5(4): where there are multiple separate salvage liens (e.g. two different salvage operations months apart), they rank in inverse chronological order — the most recent salvage operation is paid first, because it is the one that most recently preserved the value the earlier salvors' claims depend on.
CE Oral Tip: Don't confuse 5(2) with 5(4) — they answer two different questions. 5(2) asks "does salvage beat liens that existed before it?" (yes, always). 5(4) asks "if there are two or more salvage claims, which one wins?" (the newer one). Examiners frequently test whether a candidate can tell these apart.

5 · Engineering Explanation & Core Technical Mechanisms

To an engineer, a vessel is a complex assembly of structural and mechanical systems. To an admiralty court, it is a unified res. A maritime lien attaches not just to the steel hull plates, but to the entire marine engineering system — main propulsion plant, auxiliary machinery, navigational equipment, appurtenances, and even bunkers remaining on board if owned by the vessel owner.

The Mechanism of Attachment and "Travel"

The defining characteristic of a maritime lien is its ability to "travel" with the property, acting as an unrecorded, invisible encumbrance attached to the physical fabric of the ship. When a maritime tort occurs (e.g. a collision caused by a steering-gear failure) or a protected service is rendered (e.g. salvage), a maritime lien instantly attaches to the ship. If the shipowner sells the vessel to an innocent, bona fide buyer with no knowledge of the debt, the lien remains attached — the new owner acquires the asset subject to this hidden liability.

The lien can only be extinguished by:

  1. Payment or satisfaction of the underlying debt.
  2. Expiration of the one-year statutory extinction period (Article 9 of the 1993 Convention).
  3. Destruction of the res (total loss of the vessel).
  4. A formal judicial sale executed by an admiralty court of competent jurisdiction.

In Rem vs. In Personam Actions

+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+ | THE ADMIRALTY SPLIT | +-----------------------------------------------------------------------+ | | | [ ACTION IN PERSONAM ] [ ACTION IN REM ] | | Directed against the Person Directed against the | | (Shipowner / Corporate Entity) Physical Property | | (The Vessel/"Res") | | | | | | v v | | +--------------------------+ +---------------------------+ | | Sues the Owner directly | | Sues the Vessel directly | | | for breach of contract | | by name, treating the | | | or personal negligence. | | hull as the defendant. | | +--------------------------+ +---------------------------+ | | | | | v v | | +--------------------------+ +---------------------------+ | | Enforcement limited to | | Enforced via physical | | | owner's general assets | | Court Arrest of the ship, | | | globally. | | leading to Judicial Sale. | | +--------------------------+ +---------------------------+ | | +-----------------------------------------------------------------------+

Action In Personam: An ordinary lawsuit directed against a specific person or corporate entity (e.g. suing ABC Shipping Ltd. for failing to pay for a new auxiliary engine crankshaft). Liability is personal, and recovery is sought out of the defendant's general assets. If the company is insolvent or has no reachable assets within the jurisdiction, the claim may fail.

Action In Rem: A proceeding directed exclusively against the property itself — the vessel is named as the defendant (e.g. "John Doe v. The Motor Vessel Olympic Prometheus"). This action is unique to admiralty courts. It allows a claimant to arrest the ship within the territorial waters of the state where the court sits, establishing a secure asset to satisfy the claim.

Illustrative Recovery Model (not Convention text)

The relationship between the two action types can be illustrated conceptually. Let $V_s$ be the liquidation value of the vessel at judicial sale, $D_t$ the total value of the maritime claim, and $A_o$ the shipowner's general corporate assets. In a pure action in rem, recovery is capped by the value of the arrested asset:

$$R_{rem} = \min(D_t, V_s)$$

If the claim exceeds the ship's value, the claimant can pursue the remainder by a concurrent or subsequent action in personam, provided the owner is solvent and reachable:

$$R_{total} = \min(D_t, V_s) + \min(\max(0, D_t - V_s), A_o)$$

Note: this is a teaching illustration of the two-track recovery logic, not a formula published in the Convention itself.

6 · Chief Engineer & Flag State Responsibilities

Document Control and Legal Notices

Upon arrival of the Admiralty Marshal or court bailiff, a formal Warrant of Arrest is served — typically affixed to the vessel's superstructure or bridge windows, with a copy handed to the Master. The Chief Engineer must immediately log this event in the Chief Engineer's Office Logbook and the Engine Room Official Logbook, noting the exact time, date, location, and executing authority.

Technical Custodianship

An arrested vessel cannot move from its berth or anchorage without express court authorisation, but remains a living, floating engineered system. The Chief Engineer effectively becomes a technical custodian on behalf of the court and flag state, ensuring:

  • Power availability: Safe maintenance of the electrical generation plant to support essential auxiliary systems, fire pumps, bilge systems, and galley services.
  • Environmental compliance: Continuous monitoring of the OWS, holding tanks, and exhaust gas cleaning systems to ensure zero port-state environmental violations during the arrest period.
  • Manning requirements: Maintaining Minimum Safe Manning Document (MSMD) thresholds under STCW guidelines — the arrest does not relieve the flag state or crew of ISM Code safety obligations.

7 · Practical Shipboard Application & Incident Procedures

Case Study — Steering Gear Failure & Vessel Arrest

Vessel: M/V Pacific Titan (85,000 DWT Post-Panamax Bulk Carrier), approaching a major European port transit corridor.

During the approach to the pilot station, the vessel suffered a catastrophic hydraulic lock within its rotary vane steering gear, sticking the rudder hard-a-port. The vessel veered out of the navigable channel and struck a commercial automated loading pier, causing structural damage to the pier and breaching the forepeak ballast tank.

+-----------------------------------------------------------------------------+ | INCIDENT SEQUENCE & RECOVERY PATHWAY | +-----------------------------------------------------------------------------+ | | | [ STEERING GEAR FAILURE ] -> Mechanical hydraulic lock forces rudder | | hard-a-port during port approach. | | | | [ STRUCTURAL COLLISION ] -> M/V Pacific Titan strikes Port Authority pier;| | instant damage gives rise to Tort Lien. | | | | [ MARSHAL BOARDING ] -> Court bailiff boards vessel at anchor; | | affixes Warrant of Arrest to bridge window. | | | | [ SAFETY CUSTODIANSHIP ] -> Chief Engineer implements Arrest Safety Plan,| | stabilizing machinery and locking bunkers. | | | +-----------------------------------------------------------------------------+

The Port Authority quantified pier repair costs at €2,400,000 and, fearing the single-ship asset company might abandon the vessel or declare insolvency, initiated an in rem action in the local Admiralty Court, securing an arrest warrant to enforce a Maritime Tort Lien.

Step-by-Step Chief Engineer Action Plan

  1. Isolate and stabilise: Isolate the damaged steering-gear hydraulic power units; rig emergency steering (solenoid override controls) so the vessel can be safely towed or moved to a designated safe anchorage under port instructions.
  2. Fuel and resource inventory: Sound all fuel oil (HFO, VLSFO, LSMGO), lube oil, and freshwater tanks; record precisely in the Oil Record Book Part I, signed with the Master, to establish a baseline of material assets at the moment of arrest.
  3. Draft and integrity surveys: Execute complete tank soundings of all ballast tanks — specifically the breached forepeak — to confirm structural stability; monitor bilge levels daily.
  4. Enforce bunkering and supplier locks: Notify all engine room staff that no parts, tools, or residual lube oils are to be landed or transferred off the ship — removing components under an in rem arrest can be construed as reducing the value of the court's security asset.
  5. Arrest resource management plan: Shift to a single auxiliary engine configuration; run non-essential systems on an economy profile to extend fuel endurance from roughly 15 days to a targeted 45-day safety window.

8 · Survey Requirements & Port State Control (PSC) Focus

A vessel under arrest or subject to a maritime lien becomes a high-priority target for PSC inspections and Class Harmonized System of Survey and Certification (HSSC) audits. Inspectors look beyond routine technical deficiencies to evaluate indicators of owner abandonment or financial distress:

  • MLC 2006 compliance: Wage payment frequency, food provisioning, medical supply levels — wages unpaid for more than two months is a critical breach triggering a PSC detention independent of the court arrest.
  • Emergency systems readiness: Physical testing of the emergency diesel generator, emergency fire pump, and quick-closing valves.
  • Bunker sufficiency: Confirming enough fuel remains to run auxiliary plants and emergency systems through the anticipated legal lock-up period.

10 · Expanded Model Answer

1. Definition of a Maritime Lien

A maritime lien is a specialised, non-possessory right that automatically attaches to a vessel (the res), its cargo, or freight by operation of law to secure a high-priority maritime claim. It does not require physical possession by the claimant and arises immediately upon the triggering event — a collision, rendering of salvage assistance, or accrual of crew wages. It functions as an unrecorded security interest protecting key maritime service providers and tort victims.

2. Mechanics of Traveling with Property

A maritime lien adheres to the physical hull, remaining attached regardless of subsequent sales, management changes, or flag-registry changes. Even a buyer without knowledge of an outstanding claim takes the asset subject to the lien. It can only be cleared by payment of the debt, expiry of the one-year statutory time bar (Article 9, 1993 Convention), total destruction of the vessel, or a judicial sale — which wipes the title clean of all encumbrances.

3. In Rem vs. In Personam

In Rem (against the thing): brought directly against the vessel as defendant, treating the ship as a juristic entity responsible for its own debts. The primary remedy is physical arrest, and the court can sell the vessel at auction to satisfy the debt from sale proceeds.

In Personam (against the person): directed against a specific individual or corporate entity — the registered shipowner or bareboat charterer — seeking recovery from that entity's general assets. It provides no prioritised claim on the vessel itself; if the owner conceals assets or goes bankrupt, the claimant may face an unrecoverable loss.

4. Applicable Convention & Order of Priority Settlement

The principal international framework is the International Convention on Maritime Liens and Mortgages, 1993 (jointly developed by IMO and UNCTAD). When a vessel is sold via judicial auction under this Convention, proceeds are distributed hierarchically — all higher-tier claims must be satisfied in full before lower tiers receive anything:

8 marks
Explain the procedural mechanism by which an in rem action is executed via ship arrest, highlighting the technical and operational role of the Chief Engineer during this period.
  • Filing and warrant issuance: Claimant demonstrates a prima facie lien or statutory claim to an admiralty court, which issues a Warrant of Arrest.
  • Physical service: A court bailiff or Admiralty Marshal boards the vessel in territorial waters, affixes the warrant to a prominent structure (bridge window), and serves a copy to the Master.
  • Immobilisation: The vessel is prohibited from moving out of the court's jurisdiction; pilots and tug operators are notified to deny transit services.
  • Chief Engineer role: Logs the exact time of service, arresting officer, and court of origin; performs a comprehensive sounding of all fuel, lube oil, and sludge tanks to establish an inventory baseline; reconfigures the engine room for long-term standby (single-generator economy profile) while maintaining bilge systems and environmental compliance equipment.
10 marks
Explain the legal position of general statutory trade creditors — such as bunker suppliers and repair yards — under the 1993 Convention, and analyze the risks they face during a shipowner's insolvency.
  • Subordinated status: Claims for unpaid bunkers, engine room spares, and drydock repairs do not qualify as preferred maritime liens under Article 4 — they rank below court expenses, preferred liens, and registered mortgages.
  • Pro-rata sharing: If funds remain after top-tier claims and mortgages are satisfied, the balance is shared among general trade creditors proportionally if insufficient to cover all outstanding debts.
  • Financial recovery risk: Since mortgages and preferred liens often consume most of a vessel's liquidation value, Tier 4 creditors frequently recover only a fraction of their invoices — or face total loss if higher-priority debts exhaust the sale proceeds.
15 marks
A vessel is detained at a foreign anchorage after its operating company suddenly goes bankrupt. The crew has not been paid for 90 days, fuel is running low, and multiple creditors have filed claims against the ship. (a) Analyze the priority ranking between the crew's wage claims, an unpaid bunker bill, and a primary registered bank mortgage. (b) Detail the specific steps the Chief Engineer must take to protect the engine room records and maintain safety systems. (c) Outline the regulatory framework governing the repatriation of the crew during a court-ordered arrest.

(a) Priority Analysis

  1. Admiralty court costs — recovered first from arrest/mooring/maintenance expenses.
  2. Crew wages (Article 4(1)(a)) — the crew's 90-day wage claim takes top priority among maritime liens, outranking the mortgage and bunker claim.
  3. Primary registered mortgage — sits below preferred liens but ahead of general commercial debts.
  4. Unpaid bunker supplier — a general statutory trade claim, satisfied only if funds remain after costs, crew, and mortgage.

(b) Chief Engineer Directives

  • Logbook security: Daily accurate Engine Room Logbook entries — fuel soundings, machinery conditions, compliance checks.
  • Document preservation: Secure technical certificates, oil record books, and maintenance logs for class surveyors/court officials.
  • Fuel conservation: Shift auxiliaries to economy harbour standby, rotating generators to conserve fuel and minimise wear.
  • Environmental/safety controls: Lock bilge/ballast overboard valves, monitor holding tank levels daily, weekly-test the emergency generator and fire pump.

(c) Regulatory Framework for Repatriation

  • 1993 Convention: Assigns crew wages and repatriation costs top-tier lien status under Article 4, recovered early from sale proceeds.
  • MLC 2006 (2014 amendments, Standard A2.5.2): Requires shipowners to maintain financial security bonds. On abandonment (defined as 2+ months of unpaid wages, among other triggers), this security bypasses the court process, funding immediate repatriation and covering up to four months of back wages.

11 · Summary & Memory Boxes

📌 EXAM FOCUS
The Admiralty Priority Engine distributes funds sequentially — higher tiers must be paid in full before lower tiers receive any allocation.
Salvage claims among themselves rank in inverse chronological order (Article 5(4)) — the most recent salvage operation is paid first, ahead of earlier ones.
Unpaid bunker bills and repair costs are general statutory trade claims, ranking below registered ship mortgages.
CE Oral Tip: During a company insolvency, maintain transparent, detailed records of all fuel soundings and machinery operations. Accurate logbook entries protect the crew's interests and assist court authorities during an asset audit.
⚠ PSC Alert
Stranded or arrested vessels are closely monitored by Port State Control. Ensure emergency lighting, fire pumps, and lifeboats undergo routine testing and remain fully operational to prevent safety detentions.
