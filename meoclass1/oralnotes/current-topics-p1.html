Autonomous Shipping & MASS Code

1 question
1
The MASS Code was adopted at MSC 111 and enters into force 1 July 2026. Explain the four Degrees of Autonomy and where the engine room's responsibilities change.
MASS CodeMSC 111EIF 1 Jul 2026
15-Second Answer The MASS Code, adopted at MSC 111 and effective 1 July 2026, is a non-mandatory, goal-based framework defining four Degrees of Autonomy (DoA 1–4); it does not replace SOLAS, MARPOL, COLREGs or STCW.
60-Second Answer DoA 1 is automated decision-support with seafarers fully in control onboard. DoA 2 is remote control with seafarers onboard able to take over instantly. DoA 3 is remote control with no seafarers onboard, run from a shore-side Remote Operations Centre (ROC). DoA 4 is fully autonomous, with the onboard system making and executing decisions independently. The Code sets minimum safety objectives, requires Remote Operators to hold STCW-equivalent competency, mandates a "minimal risk condition" on system failure, and creates a flag-state certification pathway — but mandatory adoption is targeted for 2030 with entry into force in 2032.

Engine-Room Scope

  • Fallback control modes: machinery must default to a defined safe state on loss of remote signal or sensor confidence.
  • IAS/PMS cyber: integration with IACS UR E26 (OT segmentation) and E27 (component resilience).
  • ROC machinery data feeds: sensor validation becomes a safety input, not just a monitoring convenience.
  • UMS as foundation: existing Unmanned Machinery Space practice is treated as the operational baseline MASS builds on.

The Unresolved Question

Under SOLAS Chapter V, the Master holds absolute overriding authority. Under DoA 3/4, no Master is physically onboard — the ROC operator holds functional command, but legal responsibility under SOLAS Ch.V/ISM Code still sits with flag state, owner and ROC as defined in the SMS. This conflict is unresolved and is expected to be the central issue in the next STCW revision.

Key Numbers & Regs

EIF (non-mandatory): 1 Jul 2026 — Mandatory adoption target: 1 Jul 2030 — Mandatory EIF: 1 Jan 2032 — MSC 112: Dec 2026, re-establishes working group
Regulatory References
MSC 111 / MASS CodeGoal-based non-mandatory framework, DoA 1–4 structure
SOLAS Ch.VMaster's overriding authority for safety
IACS UR E26/E27Cyber segmentation and resilience underpinning ROC data links
ISM CodeSMS must define minimal risk condition and ROC manning/competency
CE Oral Tip: Nair and Srivastava both probe this — expect "what are the four DoA levels" followed immediately by "who is legally responsible at DoA 4?" Answer with the SOLAS Ch.V vs ROC tension explicitly; examiners want to hear that you know this is unresolved, not a confident wrong answer.

GHG, Carbon Regimes & Decarbonisation

3 questions
2
Three carbon-cost regimes are now live simultaneously — EU ETS, UK ETS and FuelEU Maritime. Explain how they differ and what a single noon report must support.
EU/UK ETSFuelEULive 1 Jul 2026
15-Second Answer EU ETS and FuelEU Maritime are assessed Well-to-Wake; UK ETS is assessed Tank-to-Wake — a vessel trading both must keep two separate accounting trails from one noon report.
60-Second Answer EU ETS reached 100% maritime compliance phase-in on 30 April 2026, covering CO2 on a Well-to-Wake basis. UK ETS for shipping went live 1 July 2026 for ships ≥5,000 GT on UK voyages, covering CO2, CH4 and N2O on a Tank-to-Wake basis, with first surrender due 30 April 2028 (2026+2027 combined). FuelEU Maritime requires a Document of Compliance from 30 June 2026 for ≥5,000 GT EU/EEA trade, evaluated Well-to-Wake. Because the boundaries differ, the same noon-report data must be split and reconciled separately for each regime, and PSC actively checks FuelEU DoC carriage.

Engineer's Action Items

  • Verify flow/torque/shaft-power meter calibration — these feed all three regimes' fuel-consumption data.
  • Keep noon-report legs separated by jurisdiction (UK / EU / non-covered).
  • Confirm SEEMP III and the vessel's Monitoring Plan explicitly cover UK ETS, not just EU ETS.
  • Cross-check FuelEU DoC issuance with the DPA and THETIS-MRV.
Examiner Trap "Aren't EU ETS and UK ETS the same calculation?" — No. EU ETS/FuelEU use Well-to-Wake (full lifecycle, including fuel production); UK ETS uses Tank-to-Wake (onboard combustion only). Conflating the two produces wrong compliance figures.
Regulatory References
EU Reg 2023/1805FuelEU Maritime — WTW GHG intensity framework
EU ETS Directive100% maritime compliance from 30 Apr 2026
UK ETS Order 2020 (amended)Live 1 Jul 2026, TTW basis, CO2/CH4/N2O
MARPOL Annex VI Reg.28SEEMP III — must reflect both ETS regimes' monitoring needs
CE Oral Tip: Srivastava and Nair like to ask "how would you explain WTW vs TTW to a junior engineer" — give the boundary definition (well/production vs tank/onboard) with a concrete example rather than reciting acronyms.

3
What is the GFI standard under the IMO Net-Zero Framework, and what is the timeline to entry into force?
GFINZFMEPC 85, Nov 2026
15-Second Answer The GFI (GHG Fuel Intensity) standard sets a Well-to-Wake intensity target with a levy on excess intensity; revenue flows to the IMO Net-Zero Fund, with adoption expected at MEPC 85 (Nov 2026) and entry into force 12 months after adoption.
60-Second Answer The Net-Zero Framework path runs: MEPC 83 (drafting continues) → MEPC 84 (further consideration) → MEPC 85, 24–28 Nov 2026 (finalisation and expected adoption) → entry into force roughly 12 months later. GFI inputs include methane slip, fuel choice at the point of bunkering, and use of shore power/renewables — all become financially relevant once the levy applies. Separately, the CII D-rating-for-3-years or E-rating-for-1-year corrective-action trigger continues to operate independently of the NZF timeline — the two systems run in parallel, not as substitutes for each other.

Why This Matters Now

This is the IMO's first mandatory global carbon levy mechanism. It sits alongside the regional EU/UK ETS and FuelEU regimes already live — meaning vessels may eventually face IMO-level and regional-level carbon costs simultaneously.

Key Numbers

MEPC 85: 24–28 Nov 2026, IMO HQ London — Expected adoption: Nov 2026 — EIF: ~12 months after adoption (Dec 2027 indicative) — CII trigger: D×3yr or E×1yr independent of NZF
Regulatory References
MEPC 83/84/85Progressive NZF drafting and finalisation sessions
MARPOL Annex VI Reg.28CII corrective-action trigger, independent timeline
CE Oral Tip: Examiners are currently testing whether candidates can distinguish NZF/GFI (IMO global levy, still pending) from CII (already in force, annual rating). Don't merge the two when answering. Free cross-reference: see the MEPC 84 → NZF entry on the MIW Decarbonisation Timeline (link below) for the full Net-Zero Framework chronology back to 1992.

4
MEPC 84 refined the BWM D-2 standard expectations. What changed, and what must the engine room evidence now?
BWM D-2MEPC 84
15-Second Answer MEPC 84 shifted BWM D-2 enforcement toward active operational performance proof, not just confirmation that a BWMS is installed — surveyors now require detailed logbook evidence.
60-Second Answer Previously, demonstrating compliance largely meant showing the BWMS was fitted and type-approved. The refined approach requires ongoing operational validation: sampling records, UV dose/lamp hours, alarm histories and consumable (chemical/filter) states must be logged and available. A BWMS that is installed but undocumented in active use is now treated as a compliance gap, not just a paperwork gap.

Practical Logbook Items

  • Sampling records at each ballast operation
  • UV dose / lamp operating hours (for UV-type systems)
  • Alarm history — any system fault or bypass events
  • Consumable state — filter condition, chemical dosing levels
Regulatory References
BWM Convention, Reg D-2Ballast water performance standard
MEPC 84Refinement toward operational performance validation
CE Oral Tip: Senthil's exact phrasing on this topic tends to be "is your BWMS installed, or is it performing?" — answer by naming the four logbook categories above, not by describing the treatment technology.

Class Society & AI Deployment

3 questions
5
Lloyd's Register issued the first Type Approval for a 100% hydrogen engine in June 2026. What safety measures does hydrogen require beyond standard IGF Code basics?
HydrogenIGF Code17 Jun 2026
15-Second Answer LR's BeHydro Type Approval (17 June 2026) makes hydrogen a class-approved propulsion fuel alongside ammonia and methanol, but it demands safety measures beyond standard IGF Code basics given its wide flammability range and near-zero ignition energy.
60-Second Answer Beyond IGF basics, hydrogen systems require double-wall piping with inert gas purge, catalytic or electrochemical hydrogen detection (since hydrogen is colourless, odourless and burns with a near-invisible flame), Emergency Shutdown systems sized for a near-zero ignition threshold, wider hot-work exclusion zones, ventilation engineered for the 4–75% flammability envelope, and embrittlement-aware material selection because hydrogen attacks certain steels over time.

Comparative Hazard Profile

  • Hydrogen: flammability range 4–75%, minimum ignition energy ~0.02 mJ — very high relative hazard, buoyant/cryogenic-adjacent handling.
  • LNG: 5–15%, ~0.28 mJ — high hazard, cryogenic, vapour cloud risk.
  • Methanol: 6–36%, ~1.14 mJ — medium hazard, toxic liquid.
  • LPG: 2.1–9.5%, ~10.0 mJ — medium hazard, heavier-than-air, explosion risk.

Key Numbers

Flammability range: 4–75% — Min ignition energy: ~0.02 mJ — TA date: 17 Jun 2026, LR/BeHydro
Regulatory References
IGF CodeBaseline safety code for gas/low-flashpoint fuels — LNG, methanol, hydrogen
IGC CodeGoverns ammonia-fuelled gas carriers — separate from IGF
LR Type Approval — BeHydroFirst 100% hydrogen engine TA, 17 Jun 2026
CE Oral Tip: Simon's favourite trap on alt-fuels is asking candidates to place a fuel under the wrong code. Memorise: LNG/methanol/hydrogen → IGF; ammonia-fuelled gas carriers → IGC, not IGF.

6
Distinguish IACS UR E26 from UR E27 and explain why both matter to your engine control systems.
CyberIACSMandatory from 1 Jan 2024
15-Second Answer UR E26 governs the vessel as a whole integrated system (network segmentation between OT and IT); UR E27 governs resilience at the individual product/vendor level — both mandatory for newbuilds contracted from 1 January 2024.
60-Second Answer UR E26 requires physical or robust logical separation — firewalls, VLANs — between Operational Technology (main engine controls, steering gear, navigation) and Information Technology (crew internet, office systems), treating the ship as a holistic cyber system. UR E27 complements it at the component level, requiring that individual third-party systems and product vendors can withstand, mitigate and rapidly recover from an active cyber incident. Together they form the technical floor referenced by the MASS Code for ROC machinery data feeds and IAS/PMS cyber resilience.
Examiner Trap "What's the difference between E26 and E27?" — System-level vs component-level. Many candidates answer with generic "cybersecurity rules" and fail to separate the two scopes.
Regulatory References
IACS UR E26Cyber security — OT/IT network segmentation, system-level
IACS UR E27Cyber resilience — product/vendor component-level hardening
MASS Code (MSC 111)References E26/E27 for ROC machinery data feed integrity
CE Oral Tip: Srivastava asks this almost verbatim. Lead with "E26 is system-level, E27 is component-level" before any further detail.

Orca AI is running at fleet scale with class notation. Can an AI alert ever authorise deferring a statutory action or overriding your watch judgement?
AI Watchkeeping2026 fleet scale
15-Second Answer No. AI tools like Orca AI (94% precision, 98.6% recall, class-notated) are cross-check aids, not a watch substitute — AI alerts never authorise deferring statutory overhauls or surveys.
60-Second Answer Orca AI is now deployed at fleet scale across operators including Gram Car Carriers and Anglo-Eastern, holding class notation from Lloyd's Register following a live trial. The principle examiners want stated explicitly: AI-flagged anomalies must be cross-referenced against manual readings before acting, document overrides are mandatory, and Type Approval (design baseline meets class safety standards) is distinct from Product Certification (specific unit verified to that design). CBM (Condition-Based Maintenance) notations do not remove statutory overhaul requirements.

SOLAS Equipment & Lifting Appliances

1 question
8
SOLAS lifting appliance and PFOS-free foam requirements entered into force 1 January 2026. What is now required for each?
SOLASLifting AppliancesEIF 1 Jan 2026
15-Second Answer Lifting appliances now require overload testing at 110% brake-holding/max line-pull at next renewal survey (Reg. II-1/3-13, MSC.532(107)); fire-fighting foam must be PFOS-free with documented batch traceability, both effective 1 January 2026.
60-Second Answer For lifting appliances — engine room cranes, gantries, davits — the new requirement is overload testing to 110% of brake-holding capacity or maximum line-pull at the next renewal survey, with manuals required either onboard or reconstructed with flag approval if missing. For foam, PFOS substances are completely prohibited; compliance can be proven via one of three routes — manufacturer declaration, laboratory test, or Type Approval under the MED — and the documentation must be traceable to the actual batch installed onboard, not a generic certificate.

Key Numbers

Overload test: 110% brake-holding/max line-pull — Trigger: next renewal survey — PFOS routes: 3 (manufacturer declaration / lab test / Type Approval-MED)
Regulatory References
SOLAS Reg. II-1/3-13Lifting appliances — overload testing requirement
MSC.532(107)Implementing resolution for lifting appliance testing
SOLAS / PFOS ProhibitionPFOS-free foam, batch-traceable documentation, EIF 1 Jan 2026
CE Oral Tip: Senthil tests the documentation trail specifically — be ready to name all three PFOS-free verification routes, not just state that PFOS is banned.

NCSR 13 — Navigation & Communications (22–26 Jun 2026)

3 questions
9
NCSR 13 finalised several navigation/communications transitions. Summarise the digital VHF transition scheme and the R-mode finalisation.
NCSR 13VHFR-mode22–26 Jun 2026
15-Second Answer NCSR 13 finalised a draft transition scheme for digital VHF voice (retaining GMDSS safety channels) and finalised performance standards for R-mode, a terrestrial GNSS-independent backup; both go to MSC 112 for approval in December 2026.
60-Second Answer The digital VHF transition scheme keeps existing analogue channels 06, 13, 16, 75, 76 plus channel 70 (DSC) and AIS-SART channels active even after digital introduction, to protect GMDSS distress/urgency/safety continuity; new digital channels will be identified within existing ITU frequency allocations, with implementation cost kept minimal. Separately, performance standards for R-mode receivers were finalised — R-mode uses existing terrestrial radio infrastructure as a GNSS-independent positioning backup, directly relevant given rising GNSS jamming/spoofing in conflict zones. Both await MSC 112 approval (December 2026).

Why It Matters for the Engine Room

GNSS-dependent systems (ECDIS positioning, DP references, some engine telemetry timestamping) are increasingly vulnerable to jamming/spoofing in high-risk areas. R-mode is the IMO's terrestrial answer; understanding it signals current awareness beyond textbook navigation theory.

Regulatory References
NCSR 13 (Jun 2026)Finalised digital VHF transition draft circular and R-mode performance standards
MSC 112 (Dec 2026)Expected approval point for both items
GMDSSSafety channel continuity protected through the VHF digital transition
CE Oral Tip: Examiners use this to test whether you follow current IMO sessions, not just the syllabus. Name the session (NCSR 13) and the approval body (MSC 112) — generic "IMO is digitising VHF" answers lose marks.

10
What progress was made on EPIRB two-way communication and S-100 ECDIS connectivity at NCSR 13?
EPIRBS-100 ECDISNCSR 13, Jun 2026
15-Second Answer NCSR 13 agreed draft amendments to MSC.471(101) adding optional two-way communication via the Galileo SAR Return Link Service to EPIRB standards, and finalised draft circulars on secure IP-based connectivity and route-exchange guidance for S-100 ECDIS — both pending MSC 112.
60-Second Answer For EPIRBs, the amendment introduces two-way communication (TWC) as an optional functionality on 406 MHz float-free EPIRBs via Galileo's SAR Return Link Service, meaning a vessel in distress could receive acknowledgement that the signal was received — guidelines for operational use were deferred pending an ICAO/IMO Joint Working Group. For S-100 ECDIS, NCSR 13 finalised draft guidance for a framework enabling secure, standardised IP-based data exchange between shore facilities and ships for next-generation chart products, plus operational guidance for digital route exchange — addressing the technical, operational and cybersecurity challenges of the S-100 transition.
Regulatory References
MSC.471(101)EPIRB performance standards — TWC via Galileo SAR RLS amendment agreed
S-100 ECDIS FrameworkIP-based data distribution and route exchange guidance, NCSR 13
MSC 112 (Dec 2026)Expected approval for both items

11
What is NAVDAT, and why was finalisation deferred at NCSR 13?
NAVDATDeferred to NCSR 14, Apr 2027
15-Second Answer NAVDAT is a digital navigational data broadcast system under adopted Performance Standards MSC.569(109); full implementation framework finalisation was deferred from NCSR 13 to NCSR 14 (April 2027) due to time constraints.
60-Second Answer NAVDAT is intended to digitally broadcast navigational and safety information as part of the modernised GMDSS architecture. Performance Standards (MSC.569(109)) and the GMDSS radio services provision (MSC.509(105)/Rev.1) are already adopted, but a nine-item action list remains before service launch — including IEC test/certification standard approval, equipment manufacturing, field trials, coast station establishment, GISIS/GMDSS Master Plan updates, an MSC circular on Terrestrial Broadcast Services, NAVTEX Manual revision, NAVDAT Manual approval, and revision of MSC.1/Circ.1645. NCSR 13 updated the roadmap but could not complete the work, deferring full finalisation to NCSR 14, April 2027.
Regulatory References
MSC.569(109)NAVDAT Performance Standards, already adopted
MSC.509(105)/Rev.1GMDSS radio services provision
NCSR 14 (Apr 2027)Expected finalisation of NAVDAT implementation framework
CE Oral Tip: Useful "watch list" answer to show currency — state clearly that Performance Standards exist but full service implementation is still pending, with a named next milestone (NCSR 14).

Nations & Security

1 question
12
The Hormuz corridor paused after a 25 June 2026 attack. What engineering precautions should you take before transiting a high-risk security corridor?
SecurityDGS Circular 33Jun 2026
15-Second Answer Over 11,000 seafarers and ~600 ships were affected when the Hormuz corridor paused after a 25 June attack; DGS Circular 33 superseded earlier advisories, lifted the blanket restriction conditional on full ISPS compliance, and engineering precautions centre on drill-verified emergency power before transit.
60-Second Answer DGS Circular 31 (13 June) was the most-urgent Gulf advisory following fatal incidents, requiring documented consent for emergency crew change and 24/7 MMDAC contact. DGS Circular 33 (26 June) superseded both Circular 09 and 31, lifting the blanket restriction but mandating full ISPS compliance and documented crew security competency. From an engineering standpoint, route and speed changes under security threat stress steering, propulsion and power systems — emergency power should be drill-verified before transit, and steering gear redundancy confirmed.

Action Checklist

  • Drill-verify emergency generator start and load acceptance before transit.
  • Confirm steering gear redundancy and changeover drill currency.
  • Maintain 24/7 MMDAC contact per DGS Circular 31/33 requirements.
  • Document crew security competency per full ISPS compliance requirement.
Regulatory References
DGS Circular 31 (13 Jun 2026)Most-urgent Gulf advisory after fatal incidents
DGS Circular 33 (26 Jun 2026)Supersedes 09 & 31; restriction lifted, full ISPS compliance required
ISPS CodeCrew security competency documentation requirement
CE Oral Tip: Examiners want the engineering angle, not current-affairs commentary — always close with the emergency power/steering redundancy drill point.
