What is the AFS Convention? Explain its scope, key prohibitions, and certification requirements.
marpolsurveyiacs
15-Second Answer: The AFS Convention, adopted 5 October 2001 and in force since 17 September 2008, bans harmful antifouling biocides — TBT globally, and cybutryne from 2023. Ships ≥400 GT need an International AFS Certificate; ships ≥24m but <400 GT need only an AFS Declaration.
60-Second Answer: Adopted 5 October 2001, entering into force 17 September 2008, the AFS Convention prohibits harmful organotin compounds (TBT) globally, with cybutryne added from 1 January 2023. Its scope extends to all vessels, including fixed/floating platforms and FPSOs, engaged in international trade. Ships ≥400 GT must undergo initial and periodic surveys to hold an International AFS Certificate (Annex 4, Reg 2). Ships ≥24m length but <400 GT only require an AFS Declaration (Annex 4, Reg 5), signed by the owner and backed by paint manufacturer data — ships under 24m need neither document. A re-survey is triggered by any change of the antifouling system, repairs affecting approximately 25% or more of the coating area, or a major conversion.

Governing Instrument

The International Convention on the Control of Harmful Anti-fouling Systems on Ships (AFS Convention), adopted 5 October 2001, entered into force 17 September 2008, phases out antifouling systems harmful to the marine environment.

Prohibited Substances & Scope

  • TBT (tributyltin): organotin compounds prohibited as biocides in antifouling systems, globally, since the Convention's entry into force (Annex 1).
  • Cybutryne: added as a controlled substance from 1 January 2023 via MEPC.331(76) — full detail in Q2.
  • Scope: applies to all ships, platforms, and floating units engaged in international trade.

Certification — Certificate vs Declaration

  • Ships ≥400 GT: require an International AFS Certificate, issued after initial and periodic surveys (Annex 4, Regulation 2).
  • Ships ≥24m length but <400 GT, on international voyages: require only an AFS Declaration, signed by the owner or authorised agent, backed by paint manufacturer documentation (Annex 4, Regulation 5) — not class/flag-issued.
  • Ships <24m length: no AFS Certificate or Declaration required under the Convention.
  • Re-survey triggers: antifouling system changed/replaced, repairs affecting approximately 25% or more of the antifouling area (per MEPC.358(78) 2022 Guidelines), or a major conversion.

Certificate vs Declaration — The Examiner's Trap

Candidates often state the threshold as simply "<400 GT," missing the ≥24m length qualifier and the fact that ships under 24m need neither document. If asked "Who issues the AFS Declaration?" — the answer is the shipowner or authorised agent, not Class or Flag, backed by manufacturer technical data.

Chief Engineer Relevance

The CE verifies the AFS Certificate/Declaration is onboard, matches the physical hull coating system, and is ready for PSC inspection — and coordinates documentation updates with drydock planning whenever hull paint specifications change.

What are the latest amendments to the AFS Convention (MEPC.331(76))?
marpolsurveyghg
15-Second Answer: MEPC.331(76), adopted 17 June 2021, in force 1 January 2023, banned cybutryne in antifouling systems. Existing cybutryne coatings must be removed or sealed within 60 months of last application; certificates held as of 1 Jan 2023 needed documentary verification by 31 December 2024.
60-Second Answer: IMO Resolution MEPC.331(76), adopted 17 June 2021 and in force 1 January 2023, added cybutryne (CAS 28159-98-0, trade name Irgarol-1051) to Annex 1's controlled substances. No application or reapplication is permitted from 1 January 2023. For existing cybutryne coatings, operators must remove the layer or apply an approved barrier/sealer coating, at the next scheduled renewal after 1 Jan 2023 but no later than 60 months from the last application. Separately, all AFS Certificates held as of 1 January 2023 required documentary verification by 31 December 2024 — a paperwork deadline distinct from the 60-month physical compliance clock. Exemptions apply to pre-2023 platforms/FSU/FPSO not drydocked since, non-international-voyage ships, and <400 GT ships with coastal State acceptance.

The 2021 Amendment

IMO Resolution MEPC.331(76), adopted 17 June 2021, entered into force 1 January 2023, added cybutryne (CAS No. 28159-98-0) to the list of controlled anti-fouling substances under Annex 1 of the AFS Convention. Cybutryne (trade name Irgarol-1051) is a biocide that disrupts photosynthesis in marine algae and is highly persistent, accumulating in sediment.

Cybutryne Prohibition & Existing-Coating Compliance

  • Application ban: no application or reapplication of cybutryne-containing antifouling systems on or after 1 January 2023.
  • Remediation options: existing cybutryne coatings must be removed, OR sealed with a barrier coating to prevent leaching.
  • Compliance window: at the next scheduled AFS renewal after 1 January 2023, but no later than 60 months (5 years) from the last cybutryne application.
  • Documentary verification: ships already holding an AFS Certificate as of 1 January 2023 required documentary verification by 31 December 2024, to update the certificate format.

Exemptions

  • Fixed/floating platforms, FSUs, and FPSOs built before 1 January 2023 and not drydocked since.
  • Ships not engaged in international voyages.
  • Ships <400 GT on international voyages, if accepted by the coastal States concerned.

Two Separate Clocks — Do Not Conflate

The 60-month physical removal/sealing window and the 31 December 2024 documentary verification deadline are two distinct compliance timelines. The 2024 deadline was strictly administrative (updating certificate templates); physical hull correction follows the separate 5-year maximum window from the system's last application.

Compliance Threshold — Biocidal Level

Per the 2022 Guidelines (MEPC.358(78)), a coating is considered compliant when cybutryne is present at a level providing no biocidal effect — originally set at a maximum of 1,000 mg cybutryne per kg of dry paint (with a 250 mg/kg / 25% tolerance range). This threshold was later revised downward to 200 mg/kg by a subsequent MEPC resolution; Material Declarations prepared on or after 26 June 2025 must use the new format reflecting this lower limit (per MEPC.405(83)).

Chief Engineer Relevance

The CE audits past drydock coating logs to confirm if cybutryne-based paint was used pre-2023, and ensures drydock planning includes full blasting or an approved barrier coat before the 60-month window expires.

Compare the main types of antifouling paint used on ships, including foul-release coatings — what are the benefits of foul-release paint?
marpoliacs
15-Second Answer: Ship hulls use Self-Polishing Copolymer (SPC) biocide paints or biocide-free Foul-Release Coatings (FRC). SPC relies on controlled biocide leaching; FRC uses a smooth, low-surface-energy surface that releases fouling hydrodynamically once the vessel is underway.
60-Second Answer: Commercial vessels typically use Self-Polishing Copolymer (SPC) paints or biocide-free Foul-Release Coatings (FRC). SPC paints erode in a controlled manner to release biocides (commonly copper), preventing growth but steadily leaching chemicals into the sea over a service life of up to about 5 years. FRCs use smooth silicone or silicone-fluoropolymer chemistries to create a low-surface-energy matrix — instead of poisoning organisms, they prevent strong mechanical adhesion, and fouling is washed off by hydrodynamic drag once the vessel exceeds a critical operating speed. FRCs offer full AFS compliance and reduced friction/fuel consumption, but are less effective during extended idle periods or slow-steaming.

Classification & Mechanism

  • Self-Polishing Copolymer (SPC): acrylic/chemical polymers with a controlled, water-activated hydrolysis mechanism — the outer layer erodes under hydrodynamic flow, continuously exposing fresh biocide (commonly copper compounds).
  • Foul-Release Coatings (FRC): non-biocidal systems using a low-surface-energy silicone or silicone-fluoropolymer matrix that minimises the mechanical adhesion strength of barnacles and slime rather than killing them.
  • Biocide-free alternatives: further covered in Q5 (class-approved modern chemistries).

Benefits of Foul-Release Coatings

  • Zero biocide leaching: free from copper, TBT, or cybutryne — removes long-term AFS Convention compliance liability.
  • Hydrodynamic efficiency: ultra-smooth hull profile reduces frictional resistance, lowering fuel consumption.
  • Extended service life: FRC matrices can remain intact for several years if undamaged, unlike SPCs which physically erode away.

Trade-Offs

FRC effectiveness is speed-dependent — self-cleaning generally requires the vessel to reach and sustain a critical operating speed regularly (approximate industry figures cite roughly 10-plus knots, though this varies by product and is not a fixed regulatory number). Vessels with extended idle or slow-steaming periods may see reduced FRC performance compared to SPC.

Chief Engineer Relevance

Paint type selection during drydock specification directly affects fuel consumption, CII performance, and future recoating cycles — the CE should weigh the vessel's typical operating speed profile against FRC's speed-dependent self-cleaning behaviour.

What is hydrogel antifouling technology and how does it work?
marpoliacs
15-Second Answer: Hydrogel antifouling is a biocide-free technology using a hydrophilic polymer matrix that absorbs seawater, creating a water barrier that prevents marine organisms from mechanically attaching to the hull.
60-Second Answer: Hydrogel coatings incorporate a network of water-attracting (hydrophilic) polymer chains. Once submerged, the matrix absorbs seawater and swells, forming a stable, water-rich boundary layer over the hull. Marine larvae and spores that rely on biological adhesives to bond to a solid substrate cannot achieve effective attachment through this water layer, preventing settlement without any chemical toxin. Because the mechanism is entirely physical rather than biocidal, hydrogel systems contain no TBT, organotin, or cybutryne, making them inherently AFS-compliant. This topic has appeared repeatedly across recent candidate batches, suggesting it is a currently trending examination area.

What Hydrogel Antifouling Is

Hydrogel antifouling is a biocide-free surface technology using a highly hydrophilic polymer network. Rather than chemically repelling or poisoning marine life, it alters the physical interface between the hull and the ocean.

Mechanism of Action

  • Water-trapping layer: upon immersion, the polymer matrix absorbs seawater, swelling to form a stable water layer against the hull surface.
  • Preventing adhesion: marine organisms rely on biological adhesives requiring a solid substrate; the water layer masks the solid hull, hindering effective settlement.

Relevance to the AFS Convention

  • Fully compliant: a physical (not chemical) mechanism means no TBT, organotin, or cybutryne — inherently meets AFS requirements without relying on Annex 1 exemptions.
  • Zero chemical leaching: does not erode or release biocides into marine ecosystems.

Chief Engineer Relevance

Hydrogel coatings can be sensitive to aggressive mechanical hull cleaning — standard stiff-bristle underwater cleaning tools can rupture the hydrogel matrix, so the CE should ensure gentle cleaning specifications are used if any underwater maintenance is required.

What new biocide-free antifouling paint types has class recently approved, and what is their base chemistry?
marpoliacs
15-Second Answer: Modern biocide-free coatings include silicone-fluoropolymer hybrids (slick, low-friction surfaces) and hard inert epoxies designed for regular mechanical hull grooming — both avoid chemical biocides entirely.
60-Second Answer: To meet AFS and broader environmental demands, several non-biocidal hull protection systems have gained class and manufacturer traction. Silicone-fluoropolymer hybrids blend hydrophobic silicone with low-surface-energy fluoropolymer chemistry to achieve a nearly frictionless, non-stick surface, similar in principle to Q3's foul-release coatings but with enhanced smoothness. Hard, inert glass-flake epoxies are also used — these contain no active ingredients but are designed to withstand frequent mechanical grooming/cleaning without degrading. Both approaches avoid biocide leaching while offering different maintenance philosophies: hybrids resist fouling passively; hard epoxies rely on active mechanical cleaning cycles.

Modern Biocide-Free Coating Categories

As environmental regulations tighten, hull coating suppliers and classification societies have developed several non-biocidal fouling-control systems beyond the hydrogel technology discussed in Q4.

1. Silicone-Fluoropolymer Hybrids

  • Base chemistry: a composite matrix combining flexible silicone elastomer with low-surface-energy fluoropolymer chemistry.
  • Mechanism: creates an exceptionally smooth, hydrophobic surface with very low micro-roughness, weakening the mechanical bond fouling organisms can form.

2. Hard Inert Epoxies (Grooming-Compatible Systems)

  • Base chemistry: high-solid, glass-flake reinforced vinyl ester or epoxy resins.
  • Mechanism: provides no chemical deterrent; instead forms an ultra-hard, smooth barrier designed to withstand regular mechanical grooming/cleaning without scratching or degrading.

Overlap with Q3 and Q4

These systems complement traditional silicone foul-release coatings (Q3) and hydrogel technology (Q4) as part of the broader biocide-free trend — the distinguishing factor is maintenance philosophy: hybrids and hydrogel resist fouling passively, while hard epoxies require an active, scheduled mechanical cleaning regime.

Chief Engineer Relevance

Silicone-fluoropolymer systems require specialised tie-coats and are unforgiving of poor surface preparation, moisture, or contamination during application — the CE should coordinate closely with the yard's technical team on these parameters during drydock specification.

Why is TBT (tributyltin) considered hazardous to the marine environment?
marpol
15-Second Answer: TBT is hazardous because it causes imposex (male sex characteristics in female marine gastropods), bioaccumulates through the food chain, and persists long-term in marine sediments.
60-Second Answer: Tributyltin (TBT) is a highly toxic organotin compound that was historically the primary biocide in self-polishing antifouling paints. Even at extremely low concentrations, TBT acts as an endocrine disruptor, triggering imposex — where female marine gastropods (whelks, oysters) develop male reproductive organs, causing reproductive failure and localised population collapse. TBT binds tightly to marine sediments rather than dissolving, allowing it to persist and bioaccumulate up the food chain from micro-organisms to commercial fish species. This combination of toxicity, persistence, and bioaccumulation was the primary driver behind the AFS Convention's global TBT ban, effective 17 September 2008.

Toxicology and Chemical Action

Tributyltin is an organometallic compound historically used as the primary biocide in self-polishing antifouling paints due to its highly effective broad-spectrum pesticide properties — however its environmental impact proved severe.

Endocrine Disruption — Imposex

Even at very low concentrations, TBT disrupts endocrine function in marine gastropods, causing imposex — the development of male reproductive organs in females — leading to widespread reproductive failure and documented population declines in affected coastal areas.

Bioaccumulation and Persistence

TBT binds strongly to marine sediment rather than dissolving and dispersing, allowing it to persist for extended periods and bioaccumulate through the marine food chain.

Chief Engineer Relevance

During drydock on older or converted vessels, the CE should be aware that any underlying legacy paint layers containing TBT must be fully removed or sealed under an approved encapsulation layer before recoating.

If the antifouling system is changed during drydock, what certificate is issued, given that the AFS Certificate itself has no fixed expiry?
marpolsurvey
15-Second Answer: A new International AFS Certificate is issued following a mandatory survey. The certificate has no fixed expiry — its validity is event-driven, ending immediately when the antifouling system changes, is replaced, or the ship transfers flag.
60-Second Answer: Unlike statutory certificates on the standard 5-year HSSC cycle (IOPP, SMC), the International AFS Certificate has no fixed calendar expiry — its validity is event-triggered. Under Annex 4, Regulation 4, the certificate becomes invalid if the antifouling system is changed or replaced, if the ship transfers flag, or on major conversion. When the coating is changed during drydock (or repairs exceed approximately 25% of the antifouling area), the vessel must undergo a survey under Annex 4, Regulation 1, present the paint manufacturer's technical data and delivery documentation, and the attending surveyor issues a brand-new International AFS Certificate with an updated Record of Anti-fouling Systems — not a routine endorsement of the old one.

Event-Driven Validity Model

The International AFS Certificate does not follow the standard 5-year expiration structure common to IOPP, SMC, or ISSC certificates. It remains valid indefinitely until a specific triggering event occurs. Under Annex 4, Regulation 4 ("Validity of an International Anti-fouling System Certificate"), the certificate ceases to be valid if:

  • The antifouling system is changed, replaced, or significantly altered.
  • The ship transfers flag to another State.
  • The vessel undergoes a major conversion affecting the hull/coating profile.

Certification Process During a Paint Change

  • Survey requirement: a survey under Annex 4, Regulation 1 is required before the ship re-enters service.
  • Documentary audit: the CE compiles paint manufacturer technical data sheets, MSDS, and paint delivery records with batch numbers and chemical composition.
  • New certificate issuance: on successful survey, the Administration/RO issues a brand-new International AFS Certificate, accompanied by an updated Record of Anti-fouling Systems.

Why AFS Doesn't Follow the Standard Certificate Cycle

Candidates frequently misread "no fixed expiry" as meaning Class simply endorses the old certificate at the end of a drydock. In fact, a paint change invalidates the old document entirely — the correct process is issuance of a new certificate, not endorsement.

Chief Engineer Relevance

The CE ensures the yard does not apply hull coatings before paint documentation is verified against the drydock specification, and confirms before departure that the newly issued AFS Certificate is filed and matches the paint delivery receipts exactly.
