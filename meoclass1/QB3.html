Background

SOLAS Chapter XII — "Additional Safety Measures for Bulk Carriers" — was introduced after a series of bulk carrier losses in the 1990s linked to structural failure following flooding of a single cargo hold. Its provisions apply to bulk carriers, with individual regulations triggered by specific combinations of length (commonly 150m and above), construction date, side-skin configuration, and cargo density — not a single blanket "150m" threshold for the whole chapter.

Key Requirements

  • Reg 5 — Structural strength: bulk carriers ≥150m, single-side-skin, designed for solid bulk cargoes of density ≥1000 kg/m³, constructed on/after 1 July 1999, must withstand flooding of any one cargo hold in all loading/ballast conditions — the "one-hold flooding" standard.
  • Reg 9 — Double-side-skin: required for new bulk carriers above 150m built after specific dates (post-2006 deliveries).
  • Reg 11 — Loading instrument (loadicator): bulk carriers ≥150m must carry a loading instrument providing hull girder shear force/bending moment information; bulk carriers <150m built on/after 1 July 2006 require the instrument to also cover intact stability (see Q8).
  • Reg 12 — Water ingress alarms (WIDAS): water level detectors in each cargo hold (alarm at 0.5m above inner bottom, and at 15% of hold depth/2m max) plus forward ballast tanks (alarm at 10% tank capacity) — audible/visual at the navigation bridge.

Why It Matters as CE

  • WIDAS (Ch XII Reg 12) is machinery the Chief Engineer maintains and tests — examiners check alarm locations, set-points, and PMS testing frequency.
  • For non-bulk-carrier multi-hold cargo ships built on/after 1 Jan 2024, the equivalent requirement sits under SOLAS II-1 Reg 25-1; single-hold cargo ships <80m fall under Reg 25-3. On a container ship, cite Reg 25-1 rather than Ch XII Reg 12.
  • SOLAS Ch XII strength requirements influence loading restrictions communicated via the loading manual / loadicator (links to Q8).

What is the difference between heel and list, and how is each corrected?
Stability Machinery
15-Second AnswerHeel is a temporary, external-force-induced inclination (wind, turning) that self-corrects once the force is removed; list is a permanent, internally-caused inclination (weight imbalance) corrected by re-distributing weight using w×d = W×GM×tanθ. Before correcting any list, always rule out angle of loll — a negative-GM condition where filling the high-side tank can capsize the ship.
60-Second AnswerHeel is caused by an external force — wind, turning, or waves — and disappears once the force is removed, the ship returning upright via its righting moment (GZ). List is caused internally — cargo shift, asymmetric ballast/bunkers, one-side flooding — and persists until corrected by transferring weight, using w×d = W×GM×tanθ where W is displacement, w the weight shifted, d the transverse distance, θ the list angle. This uses fluid GM (corrected for free surface). Critical danger: if GM is negative (excess top-weight, ice, large free surface), the ship may sit at an angle of loll — unstable equilibrium that looks like a list but isn't. Filling the high-side wing tank first can worsen GM and cause sudden capsize. Correct action: restore positive GM first (lower KG — fill low double-bottom tanks on the low side, reduce free surface) before any list-correction transfer.

Definitions

  • Heel: A temporary inclination caused by an external force — wind, turning (centrifugal effect), or wave action. Disappears once the external force is removed; the ship returns upright.
  • List: A permanent inclination caused by an internal cause — unequal weight distribution (cargo shift, asymmetric ballast/bunker consumption, one-side flooding). Persists until the internal cause is corrected.

Correction Procedures

  • Heel: No corrective action needed if transient (e.g. during a turn) — the vessel self-corrects via righting moment (GZ). If heel persists due to wind, it is monitored, not permanently corrected.
  • List: Corrected by re-distributing weight — typically ballasting/de-ballasting tanks on the listing side, or transferring fuel/fresh water between port/starboard tanks to restore symmetry. The transfer amount is calculated using the moment-to-correct-list formula.

List correction: w × d = W × GM × tanθ

Where W = vessel's displacement (tonnes, not GT), w = weight to be shifted/transferred, d = transverse distance moved, θ = list angle to be corrected.

Assumptions/Limits: this is a small-angle expression — assumes GZ ≈ GM × sinθ holds reasonably well; becomes less accurate at larger angles where the actual GZ curve must be used. GM used must be the FLUID GM (already corrected for free surface). If the transfer involves slack tanks, free surface changes during the operation and should be re-checked, not assumed constant.

Critical Danger — Angle of Loll

If GM is negative (excessive top-weight, ice accretion, or partially-filled tanks creating large free surface), the vessel may sit at an "angle of loll" — superficially looks like a list but is NOT correctable by normal list-correction. At an angle of loll the ship is in unstable equilibrium; pumping/filling a wing tank on the HIGH side first (the intuitive "correct the list" action) can reduce GM further and cause the vessel to suddenly flop over or capsize. The correct action is to first restore positive GM — typically by lowering the centre of gravity (filling low double-bottom tanks on the LOW side, or reducing free surface) — before any list-correction transfer. Always distinguish loll from list before acting.

CE Responsibility

The CE manages bunker/FW transfers for list correction and must verify the resulting GM and stability condition remains within approved loading manual limits — never correct list by an action that compromises stability, and never assume an inclination is a simple list without first ruling out angle of loll.

What is a Ship Construction File, and what does it contain?
Survey IACS / Class
15-Second AnswerA Ship Construction File is a SOLAS II-1 Reg 3-10 lifetime structural record for new bulk carriers and oil tankers — drawings, scantlings, steel grades, critical areas, coatings and survey history — formatted per IACS CSR, used as the build baseline for UTM and steel renewal decisions.
60-Second AnswerSOLAS II-1 Reg 3-10 requires new bulk carriers and oil tankers to carry a Ship Construction File compiling essential structural information, while IACS Common Structural Rules and class rules specify its detailed contents. The SCF holds structural drawings, scantling plans, steel grades and thicknesses at build, welding/NDT records, critical-area identification, PSPC coating specs, and an evolving survey/repair history. It forms the baseline against which UTM and ESP findings are compared when assessing corrosion and planning steel renewal. On non-mandated ship types (e.g. container ships), operators often keep an SCF-equivalent set of structural/coating/UTM records for the same purpose.

Definition & Purpose

The Ship Construction File (SCF) is a permanent onboard record required under SOLAS Ch II-1 for new bulk carriers and oil tankers, containing structural information critical for the vessel's lifetime maintenance, repair, and survey planning. SOLAS sets out the requirement to compile and maintain the file; the detailed format, content checklist, and update procedure are specified by the classification society's rules and the IACS Common Structural Rules — so in practice the SCF is a SOLAS-mandated record maintained to class documentation standards.

Contents (per class/IACS implementation of the SOLAS requirement)

  • Structural drawings — midship section, scantling plans, critical structural details
  • Steel grades and thickness at build (baseline for corrosion/wastage assessment)
  • Welding procedures and NDT (non-destructive testing) records from construction
  • Critical area identification — areas prone to structural failure or corrosion (per IACS Common Structural Rules)
  • Survey and repair history — to be updated throughout vessel life
  • Coating specifications (for ballast tanks, cargo holds — per PSPC)

CE Relevance, Traps, Failures & Exam Strategy
CE Relevance

The Chief Engineer ensures the SCF is kept onboard, updated after major repairs/surveys, and made available to surveyors and classification society during ESP and special surveys. The SCF underpins decisions on steel renewal, substantial corrosion and coating condition, so the CE should cross-check it when planning dockyard steel work, reviewing UTM reports and preparing ESP survey planning documents.

Trap Questions

Q: Is the SCF the same as the Stability Booklet?

A: No. SCF is the structural/construction record required by SOLAS II-1 Reg 3-10; the stability booklet is loading and stability data approved separately.

Q: Is the SCF basically just the latest UTM report?

A: No. UTM and ESP reports are life-cycle survey results. The SCF is the build baseline – original drawings, scantlings, steel grades and critical areas – against which UTM/ESP compare.

Q: If class keeps SCF data in their office, do you still need the file onboard?

A: Yes. SOLAS requires a Ship Construction File onboard; surveyors and ship staff must be able to consult it during inspections, repairs and ESP planning.

Common CE Failures

Treating the SCF as a “yard / design office file” and never opening it during planning of steel renewal, leading to ad‑hoc plate selection instead of checking original scantlings and grades.

Not updating SCF references after major structural repairs or conversions, so onboard documentation no longer matches actual structure and confuses surveyors or PSC.

Explain class notations using your last vessel as an example.
IACS / Class Survey
15-Second AnswerClass notations are codes on the class certificate showing a vessel is built and maintained to specific hull, machinery and operational standards — basic class symbol, service notation (e.g. Container Ship), and additional notations like UMS, ice class or environmental notations.
60-Second AnswerA class notation set starts with the basic hull/machinery class symbol confirming compliance with general structural and machinery rules, then adds service notations describing vessel type (Container Ship, Bulk Carrier ESP, Oil Tanker). Additional/optional notations describe extra capability — UMS (periodically unattended machinery space, linked to SOLAS II-1 Part E), ice class, environmental/'Clean' notations, fire-safety or comfort/vibration notations. These notations drive survey scope: UMS implies periodic checks of alarms, shutdowns and watchkeeping arrangements; ESP in the notation means mandatory enhanced surveys for bulk carriers/tankers. A Maersk container vessel typically carries base class, Container Ship service notation, UMS, plus relevant environmental notations.

What Class Notations Are

Class notations are codes assigned by the classification society indicating that a vessel has been built, surveyed, and maintained to specific structural, machinery, and operational standards beyond the base class symbol.

Categories of Notations

  • Mandatory/Basic Class — e.g., "✠100A1" (LR) or equivalent — confirms the vessel meets the society's rules for hull and machinery.
  • Service notations — describe the vessel type and trading restriction, e.g., "Container Ship", "Oil Tanker", "Bulk Carrier ESP".
  • Additional/optional notations — describe extra capability or compliance, e.g.:
  • SOLAS-compliant fire safety notations
  • Ice class notations (e.g., IceClass 1A)
  • Environmental notations (e.g., "Clean", "Green Passport")
  • Automation notations (e.g., "UMS" — Unmanned Machinery Space)
  • Comfort/vibration notations

Example (Container Ship, Maersk)

A typical Maersk container vessel notation would include the basic hull/machinery class, "Container Ship" service notation, "UMS" for unmanned engine room operation, and may carry environmental notations relevant to emissions compliance (linking to EEXI/CII).

CE Role

The CE must understand which notations apply to the vessel because they dictate survey scope (e.g., UMS requires periodic UMS survey of automation/alarm systems) and operational constraints.

What are the requirements for drydock planning, and what surveys coincide with a planned drydock?
Survey Machinery
15-Second AnswerDrydock planning means meeting the HSSC rule that the bottom is inspected twice in five years (max 36 months apart), while bundling in special, bottom, tailshaft and ESP surveys plus all planned repairs, steel renewals and statutory certificate renewals.
60-Second AnswerUnder the Harmonised System of Survey and Certification, a ship's bottom must be inspected at least twice in each 5-year cycle, with no more than 36 months between inspections — usually one is a drydock and, age/flag/class permitting, one may be an approved In-Water Survey. The CE plans 6-12 months ahead to line up the drydock with due surveys (special survey, bottom survey, tailshaft if due, ESP items for bulkers/tankers), compiling a work list covering hull cleaning/painting, sea valves, propeller/rudder, thrusters, main and aux engine overhauls, steel renewals identified from previous UTM, and timing statutory certificate renewals (IOPP, SAFCON) to coincide. Long-lead spares are ordered early; failure to integrate UTM/ESP findings into the dock spec causes emergency negotiations and lost dock time.

Drydocking Interval Requirements

Under the Harmonised System of Survey and Certification (HSSC), the bottom of the ship must be inspected at least twice in any 5-year special survey cycle, with the interval between successive bottom inspections not exceeding 36 months. At least one of these two inspections is normally a drydock survey; the other may, subject to flag/class approval, be an approved In-Water Survey (IWS) — though general guidance restricts how far IWS can substitute for drydocking as the vessel ages (commonly cited around the 15-year mark for general cargo ships/bulk carriers, class-rule dependent).

Drydock Planning — CE Responsibilities

  • Coordinate with the yard 6-12 months in advance — scope of work list (hull cleaning/painting, sea valve overhaul, tailshaft survey, propeller polishing, anchor chain ranging, thruster overhaul).
  • Class survey items due during the dock window must be identified early — special survey, intermediate survey items, tailshaft survey (if due).
  • Spares and rotables ordering — long-lead items for overhauls (main engine, generators, valves) planned around dock dates.
  • Steel renewal items identified from previous survey/UTM (ultrasonic thickness measurement) reports must be quoted to the yard.
  • Statutory certificate renewals timed to coincide where possible (e.g., IOPP, SAFCON renewal surveys).

Coinciding Surveys

  • Special Survey (renewal survey, every 5 years) — comprehensive hull/machinery survey.
  • Bottom Survey — hull below waterline, sea valves, anchors, propeller, rudder.
  • Tailshaft Survey (if due) — withdrawal/examination per approved scheme (continuous or 5-year).
  • ESP requirements (for bulk carriers/tankers) — UTM surveys per ESP.

What a Loadicator Is

A loadicator is an onboard computer-based loading instrument that calculates the vessel's stability (GM, GZ curve, intact and damage stability), stresses (bending moment, shear force), and trim for any given loading condition, verified against the approved stability and loading manual.

Regulatory Requirement — Three-Layer Chain

  • SOLAS carriage requirement: SOLAS Ch XII Reg 11 requires bulk carriers of 150m+ to carry a loading instrument (hull girder shear force/bending moment); for bulk carriers <150m built ≥1 July 2006, the instrument must also cover intact stability. SOLAS Ch II-1 Reg 5 sets the general stability information/loading instrument requirement for other cargo ship types.
  • IMO approval guidance: MSC.1/Circ.1229 provides guidelines for the approval of the loading instrument's software — intact and damage stability calculation methodology.
  • Class verification requirement: the classification society approves the specific software installation for the vessel and requires periodic (typically annual) function testing against a set of approved test conditions — this is the survey item the CE/Master tracks.

Latest Direction

  • The loadicator must be capable of calculating BOTH intact stability AND damage stability for the vessel's approved damage cases — not intact stability alone — where SOLAS II-1's probabilistic damage stability framework applies to the ship type.
  • Approved software is categorised as Type 1 (checks the actual loading condition against a limited set of pre-calculated damage cases — simpler, "look-up" style) or Type 3 (performs real-time damage stability calculations for the actual loading condition entered, including back-calculation of the maximum permissible KG/VCG curve). The required type depends on the vessel's keel-laying date and ship type — newer ships and certain categories require the more capable Type 3.
  • For container ships specifically — increasing integration of loadicator data with lashing computer software to verify both stability and cargo securing limits simultaneously.

CE Role

While the loadicator is primarily a deck/navigation tool, the CE must understand how loading conditions affect machinery space flooding scenarios (damage stability) and verify the loadicator's annual test/calibration is current — often a class survey item. On container ships, CE is heavily involved in agreeing viable loading conditions with deck, given machinery and systems constraints.

Survey IACS / Class STCW
15-Second AnswerAn unseaworthy ship is a legal/admiralty concept — unfit for its intended voyage due to structural, equipment, crew or stowage deficiencies, assessed at the start of the voyage. 60-Second Answer'Unseaworthy ship' is primarily a legal/admiralty law concept: a vessel not reasonably fit, in design, structure, equipment, crew competence, and stowage, to encounter the ordinary perils of the intended voyage — assessed as a condition at the commencement of the voyage, often arising in cargo claims/insurance disputes (the owner's duty of due diligence to make the ship seaworthy). 'Unsafe ship' is a statutory/PSC concept: a vessel found, on inspection at any time, to not comply with the requirements of applicable conventions (SOLAS, MARPOL, Load Lines, MLC) — e.g. missing/expired certificates, non-functional safety equipment, structural deficiencies — leading to PSC detention until rectified. The two overlap heavily in practice (e.g. a major structural defect can make a ship both unseaworthy and statutorily unsafe), but unseaworthiness is judged against the voyage undertaken, while unsafety is judged against convention compliance at the time of inspection.

Unseaworthy Ship (Legal/Admiralty Concept)

"Unseaworthiness" is primarily an admiralty law / maritime law concept — a vessel is unseaworthy if it is not reasonably fit, in design, construction, condition, equipment, or crewing, to perform the voyage or service for which it is intended. Unseaworthiness can relate to the hull, machinery, stability, documentation, or even crew competency/manning — it is assessed relative to the specific voyage undertaken. It has significant implications for cargo claims, insurance (e.g., under Hague-Visby Rules, the carrier must exercise due diligence to make the ship seaworthy before and at the start of the voyage).

Unsafe Ship (PSC / National Law Concept)

"Unsafe ship" is an operational term used in Port State Control practice and national merchant shipping law — a ship found with deficiencies that pose a danger to the ship, persons onboard, or the environment, justifying detention until rectified. PSC detention criteria are set out in the regional MOU procedures (Paris MOU, Tokyo MOU) and given legal effect through the flag/port State's national merchant shipping legislation (in India, the Merchant Shipping Act provisions on detention). The assessment is against specific convention requirements (SOLAS, MARPOL, MLC, STCW) at the time of inspection, rather than the broader "fitness for the voyage" standard used for unseaworthiness.

Key Distinction

  • Unseaworthy = a legal/contractual standard (often retrospective, used in cargo/insurance disputes) tied to fitness for a specific voyage.
  • Unsafe = a regulatory/PSC standard (prospective, operational) tied to specific convention non-compliance justifying detention.

Seaworthiness as a Shifting Standard

Seaworthiness is not an absolute, fixed property of the ship — it is relative to the specific voyage, cargo, and conditions intended. A vessel can be perfectly seaworthy for a summer coastal voyage with calm weather, yet the SAME ship, with identical equipment and crew, could be deemed unseaworthy for a winter North Atlantic crossing if it lacks adequate heating, de-icing capability, or storm-readiness for that route. This is why "due diligence to make the ship seaworthy" under Hague-Visby is assessed at the commencement of each voyage, not as a one-time certification.

Practical Overlap

A detainable PSC deficiency (unsafe condition) can also be evidence supporting an unseaworthiness claim if a casualty occurs on that voyage — the concepts overlap but serve different legal purposes.

15-Second AnswerThe EEDI reference line is Reference EEDI = a × Capacity⁻ᶜ, with a/c regression parameters from 2000-2010-built ships; for container ships, Capacity = 0.7 × DWT (volume-limited 'cube out' basis). Required EEDI = Reference EEDI × (1 − X), and Attained EEDI must be ≤ Required EEDI.
60-Second AnswerMARPOL Annex VI defines, for each ship type, a reference line: Reference EEDI = a × Capacity⁻ᶜ, where a and c are regression parameters derived statistically from a dataset of ships built 2000-2010, representing the historical average efficiency for that ship type/size. What is the significance of tribology / lube oil analysis (LOA) reports, and what actions does the Chief Engineer take based on them?
Machinery
15-Second AnswerTribology is the science of friction, lubrication and wear; routine lube oil analysis (LOA) tracks viscosity, TBN, water/fuel contamination and wear-metal content so the CE can adjust feed rates, oil change intervals and target maintenance before failures occur.
60-Second AnswerTribology is the science of friction, lubrication and wear between interacting surfaces. In practice, the CE applies it through routine Lube Oil Analysis (LOA) on main engine system oil, cylinder drain oil, generator engine oil, and gearbox/stern tube oils — sent for laboratory analysis at regular intervals per the PMS. Key parameters tracked: viscosity (shear breakdown or fuel dilution), Total Base Number/TBN (remaining alkalinity reserve to neutralise acidic combustion products — critical for cylinder oils), water content (cooling system leaks), fuel dilution, and wear-metal content (iron, copper, lead, tin, chromium, aluminium — indicating wear of liners, bearings, pistons). Trends across successive samples (not single readings) are what matter — a rising iron trend may indicate liner/ring wear before any operational symptom appears, allowing the CE to plan inspection or component renewal proactively rather than reactively.

What Tribology / LOA Covers

Tribology is the science of friction, lubrication, and wear between interacting surfaces. Lube Oil Analysis (LOA) — routine sampling of main engine, generator, and other system lube oils sent for laboratory analysis — is the practical onboard application, monitoring oil condition and machinery wear trends.

Sample Type Matters — System Oil vs Cylinder Drain Oil

LOA interpretation depends heavily on which oil system the sample is drawn from: System (sump/crankcase) oil — main engine system oil, generator sump oil: a closed, recirculated system. TBN here reflects the bulk oil's reserve alkalinity over a long change interval; wear metals accumulate and reflect cumulative bearing/journal/gear wear across the whole running period since the last change. Cylinder drain oil (2-stroke main engines) — a once-through system; the drain sample reflects the cylinder oil's performance over a single pass. TBN here indicates whether the feed rate is neutralising combustion acids from that period only, and Fe content reflects liner/ring wear rate over a short window. Conflating the two — e.g., applying cylinder-drain TBN logic to system oil, or vice versa — leads to wrong conclusions about feed rate vs overall lubricant condition.

Key Parameters Reported

  • Viscosity — oil degradation or contamination (fuel dilution, water ingress).
  • Total Base Number (TBN) — remaining alkalinity reserve to neutralise acidic combustion products (interpretation differs by sample type as above).
  • Wear metal content (Fe, Cu, Pb, Sn, Al, Cr) — indicates wear in specific components (Fe = liners/rings, Cu/Pb/Sn = bearings).
  • Water content, fuel dilution, insolubles (soot/carbon).
  • Particle count — filtration system effectiveness.

Significance

LOA trends (not single readings) reveal developing problems before they cause failure — e.g., rising Fe trend indicates accelerating liner/ring wear; rising Cu/Pb indicates bearing wear; falling TBN indicates insufficient cylinder lubrication feed rate or high-sulphur fuel acid loading.

CE Actions Based on LOA

Trend analysis — compare against previous reports and OEM limits, not isolated readings. Adjust cylinder oil feed rate based on TBN/Fe trends from cylinder drain oil (2-stroke main engines — feed rate adjustment per maker's guidance), keeping this distinct from system oil trend analysis. Schedule early inspection/overhaul if wear metal trends exceed alert limits (bearing inspection, liner measurement). Investigate root cause if abnormal trends appear suddenly — e.g., water content spike → check cooler leakage. Sample quality caution: before acting on any abnormal single result, verify the sample was drawn correctly (correct point, clean container, properly labelled with running hours/unit number) — a contaminated or mislabelled sample can produce a false trend. If a result looks anomalous against the established trend, the first action is to re-sample and re-test before committing to an overhaul or feed-rate change decision. Document findings in PMS and report to technical superintendent for any condition requiring office-level decision (e.g., bringing forward an overhaul).
