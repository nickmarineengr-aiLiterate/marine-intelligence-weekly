🛢 Bulk & Gas Cargo

2 questions
1
IGC Code — key requirements and application to gas carriers?
SOLAS STCW
15-Second AnswerThe IGC Code — International Code for the Construction and Equipment of Ships Carrying Liquefied Gases in Bulk — is mandatory under SOLAS Chapter VII Part C for gas carriers built on/after 1 July 1986, applying to all sizes. It sets four ship-type standards (1G, 2G, 2PG, 3G) by cargo hazard, and the ship carries a 5-year International Certificate of Fitness.
60-Second AnswerThe IGC Code, adopted by Resolution MSC.5(48) and mandatory under SOLAS Ch VII Part C since 1 July 1986, applies to gas carriers of any size carrying liquefied gases with vapour pressure exceeding 2.8 bar absolute at 37.8°C, and other listed products in Chapter 19. It assigns each ship a design standard by hazard: Type 1G (maximum hazard — e.g. ammonia, propylene oxide, survives damage anywhere in length), Type 2G (significant hazard — e.g. LPG, LNG), Type 2PG (≤150m, significant hazard, Type C tanks, high MARVS), Type 3G (moderate hazard, lowest design standard). Key requirements cover tank containment type and location, pressure-relief and vapour-control systems, gas detection, fire-fighting appropriate to the cargo (dry powder for LPG, water spray for ammonia), crew training under STCW V/1-2, and a Procedures and Arrangements (P&A) Manual. The ship carries an International Certificate of Fitness valid for 5 years, subject to annual surveys.

Governing Instrument & Applicability

  • Instrument: International Code for the Construction and Equipment of Ships Carrying Liquefied Gases in Bulk (IGC Code), adopted by IMO Resolution MSC.5(48), mandatory under SOLAS Chapter VII, Part C since 1 July 1986.
  • Applies to: gas carriers of any size (no GT threshold), built on/after 1 July 1986, carrying liquefied gases with vapour pressure exceeding 2.8 bar absolute at 37.8°C and other substances listed in Chapter 19 of the Code. Older gas carriers built before 1986 comply instead with the earlier GC Code (or EGC Code, pre-1976).
  • Extensively revised and consolidated in 2016 under Resolution MSC.370(93); further amendments (window fire-integrity, revised Certificate of Fitness model form) entered into force 1 January 2020.

Ship Type Classification (by hazard)

  • Type 1G — maximum hazard products (e.g. ammonia, propylene oxide); highest survivability standard — must survive damage anywhere along its length; cargo tanks located furthest inboard.
  • Type 2G — significant hazard products (e.g. LPG, LNG, ethylene) — most modern large gas carriers fall here.
  • Type 2PG — ≤150m length variant of 2G, carrying product in independent Type C tanks designed for MARVS ≥0.7 MPa (7 bar) gauge and design temperature ≥−55°C; over 150m, the ship reverts to Type 2G classification.
  • Type 3G — moderate hazard products; lowest design standard of the four.

Key Technical Requirements

  1. Cargo tank design and inboard location, matched to ship type and damage-survivability standard.
  2. Cargo containment systems — independent tanks (Types A, B, C), membrane, semi-membrane, or integral tanks depending on design.
  3. Pressure relief valves and other safety devices sized for the cargo's properties.
  4. Vapour control and gas-detection systems throughout cargo and adjacent spaces.
  5. Fire-fighting systems matched to the cargo — dry chemical powder and water spray for LPG, water spray/dilution for ammonia, appropriate systems for LNG.
  6. Crew training — STCW Chapter V, Regulations V/1-2, basic and advanced training for personnel on liquefied gas tankers.
  7. Procedures and Arrangements (P&A) Manual — ship-specific cargo handling procedures.
  8. Certification — International Certificate of Fitness for the Carriage of Liquefied Gases in Bulk.

Certificate of Fitness

The International Certificate of Fitness is valid for 5 years, subject to annual surveys to confirm continued compliance. It is the gas-carrier-specific statutory certificate, broadly equivalent in function to the Certificate of Fitness (COF) chemical tankers carry under the IBC Code.

IGC Code vs IGF Code

The IGC Code governs gas carried as cargo. The IGF Code governs gas (or other low-flashpoint fuel) used as ship's fuel. On an LNG carrier that also burns LNG as fuel (a common configuration), both codes apply simultaneously — IGC for the cargo system, IGF for the fuel gas system — and examiners test this distinction directly.

CE Oral Tip: Simon's key distinction to nail immediately: IGC Code = gas as CARGO; IGF Code = gas as FUEL. State that both apply together on an LNG-fuelled gas carrier. Then give the ship-type hierarchy from memory — Type 1G highest hazard/most restrictive design, Type 3G lowest — and the 5-year Certificate of Fitness with annual surveys. Candidates commonly fail this by only knowing "IGC = gas carriers" without the type classification or the IGC/IGF distinction, which is exactly what tripped candidates in this session.
CE Relevance, Traps, Failures & Exam Strategy
CE Relevance

CE responsibility on a gas carrier centres on cargo containment system integrity, vapour control, and fire-fighting readiness matched to the specific cargo carried — different products (LNG vs LPG vs ammonia) require materially different emergency response knowledge.

Trap Questions

Q: "Your ship carries LNG and burns LNG as fuel — which code applies?"

A: Both — IGC Code for the cargo containment/handling system, IGF Code for the fuel gas supply system. They are not mutually exclusive.

Common CE Failures

1. Confusing IGC and IGF Code purposes (cargo vs fuel).

2. Not knowing the four ship types (1G/2G/2PG/3G) or which hazard level each represents.

3. Not knowing the Certificate of Fitness validity (5 years, annual surveys).

Numbers to Memorise

SOLAS Ch VII Part C · IGC Code mandatory since 1 July 1986 · Res. MSC.5(48) · vapour pressure threshold 2.8 bar abs at 37.8°C · Certificate of Fitness — 5 years, annual surveys · Type 2PG threshold: ≤150m, MARVS ≥0.7 MPa, design temp ≥−55°C.

Casualty Link

Historic LPG/ammonia carrier cargo release incidents underline why containment-system integrity and correctly matched fire-fighting media (never plain water jet on some cargoes) are treated as core competency, not memorisation exercises.

Examiner Chain

Simon → IGC Code basics → ship types → IGC vs IGF → certificate validity → cargo containment system types (A/B/C tanks) → fire-fighting media by product.

On My Vessel

As a container vessel officer, direct IGC Code exposure is limited to reefer/hazardous container stowage under IMDG rather than bulk gas carriage — the oral answer should be framed as regulatory knowledge for the qualification, distinguished clearly from a bulk-gas-carrier's day-to-day operational context.

Dangerous cargo vs hazardous cargo — what is the difference?
MARPOL SOLAS
15-Second AnswerDangerous cargo is cargo classified under the IMDG Code's 9 hazard classes as posing an immediate physical/health risk; hazardous cargo is the broader category — including MARPOL Annex III marine pollutants and IMSBC "Materials Hazardous only in Bulk" (MHB) — that is not always classified as an IMDG dangerous good. Dangerous cargo is a subset of hazardous cargo.
60-Second AnswerThe IMDG Code classifies "dangerous goods" into 9 UN classes (Class 1 Explosives through Class 9 Miscellaneous), based on the UN Model Regulations (the "Orange Book") — this is the immediate physical/health-hazard definition. "Hazardous cargo" is broader: it includes substances hazardous to the marine environment under MARPOL Annex III (marine pollutants, some not IMDG-classified at all), and Materials Hazardous only in Bulk (MHB) under the IMSBC Code — cargoes with bulk-specific hazards (e.g. self-heating, oxygen depletion) that aren't packaged dangerous goods. So all IMDG dangerous goods are hazardous, but not all hazardous cargo is a classified dangerous good. Practical distinction: Class 3 flammable liquid in drums is both dangerous and hazardous; used oily rags are hazardous waste but not a dangerous good; a marine pollutant under Annex III may be hazardous to the environment without being IMDG-classified as dangerous to ship or crew.

Dangerous Goods — IMDG Definition

  • The IMDG Code classifies dangerous goods into 9 classes: Class 1 (Explosives), Class 2 (Gases), Class 3 (Flammable Liquids), Class 4 (Flammable Solids/spontaneously combustible/dangerous when wet), Class 5 (Oxidizing Substances/Organic Peroxides), Class 6 (Toxic/Infectious Substances), Class 7 (Radioactive Material), Class 8 (Corrosives), Class 9 (Miscellaneous Dangerous Substances, including marine pollutants).
  • Based on the UN Model Regulations ("Orange Book") classification system, universally applied across transport modes.
  • "Dangerous" implies an immediate physical or health hazard — fire, explosion, toxicity, corrosivity — during transport, handling, or stowage.

Hazardous Cargo — The Broader Category

  • MARPOL Annex III marine pollutants: substances hazardous to the marine environment — not all Annex III marine pollutants are separately classified as IMDG dangerous goods; some are hazardous to the environment without posing an immediate physical/health hazard to ship or crew.
  • IMSBC Code — Materials Hazardous only in Bulk (MHB): bulk cargoes with bulk-specific hazards (self-heating, oxygen depletion, liquefaction, corrosivity) that fall outside the IMDG classification system, which governs packaged goods.
  • Hazardous waste: items such as used lubricating-oil rags are hazardous waste requiring specific disposal handling, but are not a "dangerous good" under IMDG classification.

The Set Relationship

All dangerous goods (IMDG-classified) are hazardous, but not all hazardous cargo is a classified dangerous good — dangerous is the narrower, IMDG-specific subset; hazardous is the umbrella term covering environmental, bulk-specific, and waste-related risks the IMDG classification system does not fully capture.

Worked Examples

  • Both dangerous and hazardous: Class 3 flammable liquid in drums — IMDG-classified and hazardous to ship, crew, and environment.
  • Hazardous but NOT dangerous (IMDG): used lubricating-oil rags — hazardous waste, not IMDG-classified dangerous goods.
  • Both, from different angles: LNG or LPG carried in portable tanks — Class 2.1 (flammable gas) under IMDG AND potentially a marine pollutant/environmentally hazardous substance under Annex III.

CE Relevance — Documentation & Verification

The CE has a role in verifying cargo declarations align with the IMDG Certificate carried onboard, and understanding that undeclared or misdeclared dangerous goods (a leading cause of container-ship fires) is a documentation-integrity failure distinct from the routine handling of hazardous-but-not-dangerous materials such as garbage or waste oil.

CE Oral Tip: Simon asked this on a fail attempt where candidates conflated the terms — define both cleanly first, then explicitly state the relationship: "dangerous cargo is a subset of hazardous cargo." Give one example of each bucket: (1) dangerous — Class 3 flammable drums, (2) hazardous but not IMDG-dangerous — waste oil rags, (3) both — LNG in portable tanks (Class 2.1 + marine pollutant). Naming the set relationship explicitly is what separates a pass from a fail here.
CE Relevance, Traps, Failures & Exam Strategy
CE Relevance

Undeclared or misdeclared dangerous goods in containers is a recognised major fire-risk driver on container ships; the CE's practical role is understanding documentation requirements (IMDG Certificate, Dangerous Goods Manifest) well enough to flag anomalies, even though cargo declaration itself sits with the shipper/terminal.

Trap Questions

Q: "Is a marine pollutant under MARPOL Annex III always a dangerous good under IMDG?"

A: No. Some Annex III marine pollutants are separately IMDG-classified (often under Class 9), but the two classification systems serve different purposes — environmental harm vs immediate physical/health hazard — and don't map 1:1.

Common CE Failures

1. Treating "dangerous" and "hazardous" as synonyms rather than stating the subset relationship.

2. Not being able to give a clean worked example of "hazardous but not dangerous."

Numbers to Memorise

IMDG — 9 classes · MARPOL Annex III — marine pollutants · IMSBC — MHB (bulk-specific hazards) · UN Orange Book — base classification system.

Casualty Link

Multiple major container-ship fires in recent years have been traced to undeclared or misdeclared dangerous goods — reinforcing why examiners test precise understanding of dangerous-goods classification and documentation, not just cargo-handling procedure.

Examiner Chain

Simon → dangerous vs hazardous → worked example → PSC checks for DG compliance → undeclared DG risk on container ships → IMDG Certificate for the ship.

On My Vessel

On a Maersk container ship, dangerous goods documentation review (container DG manifest cross-checked against the stowage plan) is a standard pre-departure check; as CE, awareness of misdeclaration risk is directly relevant to engine-room and accommodation fire-risk planning given DG containers are stowed per the ship's DG stowage plan with separation/segregation requirements.
