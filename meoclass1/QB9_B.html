CAS and CAP — what are they? What is general liability vs contractual liability? What is NOSDCP?
SURVEYISM
15-Second AnswerCAS (Condition Assessment Scheme) is a mandatory MARPOL survey regime under Annex I designed to assess the structural integrity of aging single-hull oil tankers. CAP (Condition Assessment Programme) is a voluntary, commercial rating system run by classification societies to evaluate older hulls and machinery. General liability represents a legal obligation arising from a civil wrong or tort (e.g., a collision), whereas contractual liability stems strictly from breaking a specific signed agreement (e.g., a charter party breach). NOSDCP is India's National Oil Spill Disaster Contingency Plan.
60-Second AnswerCAS is a strict regulatory requirement mandated under MARPOL Annex I to verify the structural alignment and safety profiles of aging single-hull tankers before they are granted trading extensions. Conversely, CAP is a voluntary, commercial rating module utilized by older vessel owners to secure cargo charters by obtaining an objective, independent class assessment score for their hull and machinery components. In maritime law, liabilities are split by source: General liability (tort) is triggered by breaching a general duty of care independent of an agreement, such as an accidental dock collision, while Contractual liability is established when a party fails to fulfill explicit terms in a charter party or bill of lading. Both concepts link back to environmental frameworks like India’s NOSDCP, which pools national coast guard and state resources to counter catastrophic oil spill exposures.

1. CAS (Condition Assessment Scheme) vs. CAP (Condition Assessment Programme)

While both programs evaluate the physical condition of older vessels, they serve fundamentally different regulatory and commercial purposes:

  • Condition Assessment Scheme (CAS):
  • Regulatory Origin: Mandated by the IMO under MARPOL Annex I (Regulations 20 and 21).
  • Application: Specifically introduced to accelerate the phase-out of single-hull oil tankers and monitor those permitted to operate during transition periods.
  • Nature: Strict and mandatory. The survey is executed by a recognized organization on behalf of the Flag State Administration during the vessel's intermediate or renewal surveys.
  • Outcome: Failure to secure a valid CAS Statement of Compliance legally bars the tanker from international trade.
  • Condition Assessment Programme (CAP):
  • Commercial Origin: Developed independently by Classification Societies (e.g., DNV, Lloyd's Register, ClassNK).
  • Application: A voluntary, market-driven program applicable to all types of aging vessels (tankers, bulk carriers, container ships) typically over 15 years of age.
  • Nature: Commercial rather than statutory. It is requested by shipowners to provide oil majors, charterers, and underwriters with independent verification of a vessel's technical condition.
  • Outcome: Delivers a specific rating score (typically from CAP 1 down to CAP 4, where CAP 1 indicates an exceptional, near-new condition). A high CAP rating allows older vessels to remain competitive in strict charter markets.

2. General Liability vs. Contractual Liability

  • General Liability (Tortious Liability):
  • Definition: Liability imposed by law independent of any existing contract, arising when a party breaches a general duty of care and causes injury, loss, or property damage to a third party.
  • Maritime Example: A vessel breaks its mooring lines during a storm due to poor seamanship and collides with a concrete pier or an adjacent ship. The shipowner is generally liable in tort for the damages, even though no prior contract existed with the bridge or vessel owner.
  • Contractual Liability:
  • Definition: Liability that arises strictly from the breach of express or implied terms within a legally binding contract signed between two or more parties.
  • Maritime Example: A shipowner fails to maintain the contracted speed or fuel consumption metrics specified in a Time Charter Party, or delivers cargo in a damaged state under a Bill of Lading. The liability is determined directly by the text and liability limitations embedded within that specific contract.

3. NOSDCP (National Oil Spill Disaster Contingency Plan)

The National Oil Spill Disaster Contingency Plan is India's operational strategy for responding to oil pollution incidents within its maritime zones:

  • Statutory Basis: Aligns with the global OPRC 1990 convention.
  • Command Structure: The Indian Coast Guard (ICG) is designated as the Central Coordinating Authority responsible for updating and executing the plan.
  • Functional Role: It coordinates response efforts across major ports, state governments, oil refineries, and coastal administrations. The NOSDCP maintains inventories of oil containment booms, skimmers, and chemical dispersants, conducting regular national exercises (NATPOLREX) to ensure response readiness.

Trap Questions
  • Examiner: "If your vessel undergoes a voluntary CAP survey by DNV and receives a CAP 1 rating, does this certificate automatically exempt you from undergoing standard Port State Control inspections?"
  • Candidate Response: No, sir. It carries no statutory authority to bypass or override mandatory flag state or international Port State Control (PSC) inspection regimes under SOLAS or MARPOL. A CAP 1 vessel must still undergo routine, detailed PSC inspections.

What is the OPRC Convention and SOPEP/SMPEP? What is an oil pollution incident in the high seas — actions and reporting? Insurance-related aspects of
MARPOLISM
15-Second AnswerThe OPRC Convention 1990 mandates global systems for oil pollution preparedness and response, requiring all ships $\ge$400 GT to carry a SOPEP (and chemical/oil tankers to carry an SMPEP). On the high seas, an oil spill triggers immediate SOPEP mobilization, mitigation actions, and statutory reports via GMDSS to the flag state and nearest coastal authority. Insurance coverage is managed by the vessel's P&I Club, which issues the CLC Blue Card to underwrite third-party clean-up liabilities and legal claims.
60-Second AnswerUnder the OPRC Convention 1990 and MARPOL Annex I, ships must maintain a structured operational plan to handle environmental emergencies through a Shipboard Oil Pollution Emergency Plan (SOPEP) or a Shipboard Marine Pollution Emergency Plan (SMPEP) for chemical/oil carriers. If an oil pollution incident occurs on the high seas, the crew must immediately execute the SOPEP checklist: stop the discharge, contain the onboard leak, and broadcast emergency reports to the flag state and neighboring coastal authorities using standard GMDSS formats. Financial and legal exposures are covered under marine insurance. The shipowner’s P&I Club deploys local correspondents to handle clean-up costs, while the Civil Liability Convention (CLC) framework provides a direct avenue for compensation claims against the club's active "Blue Card" insurance guarantee.

1. The OPRC Framework: SOPEP and SMPEP

The International Convention on Oil Pollution Preparedness, Response and Co-operation 1990 (OPRC) provides the legal framework establishing national and shipboard emergency plans to counter marine oil spills.

  • SOPEP (Shipboard Oil Pollution Emergency Plan): Mandated by MARPOL Annex I (Regulation 37), this operational manual is compulsory for all ships of 400 Gross Tonnage (GT) and above, and all oil tankers of 150 GT and above. It contains explicit, step-by-step instructions for the crew to minimize, contain, and report oil discharges.
  • SMPEP (Shipboard Marine Pollution Emergency Plan): Mandated by MARPOL Annex II (Regulation 17), this expanded plan is required for chemical and oil tankers of 150 GT and above certified to carry noxious liquid substances (NLS) in bulk. It combines oil pollution checklists with specific chemical mitigation guidelines, addressing hazards like toxic vapor release, chemical reactivity, and cargo incompatibility.

2. High Seas Oil Pollution Incident: Action Sequence

When an operational breakdown or marine casualty causes an oil spill on the high seas, the ship’s crew must execute immediate technical and procedural actions under the SOPEP manual:

3. High Seas Reporting Framework

Because the high seas sit outside individual territorial waters, reporting must follow specific international guidelines:

  • Primary Notification Target: The Master must immediately notify the Flag State Administration and the nearest or most vulnerable Coastal State authority likely to be impacted by the drifting oil slick.
  • Standardized Report Format: Communication must utilize the DG/POLREP format via GMDSS, containing critical tracking metrics:
  • Vessel name, call sign, IMO number, position, course, and speed.
  • Exact date, time, and type of marine casualty (e.g., hull breach, overflow).
  • Type of oil spilled (e.g., heavy fuel oil, crude oil cargo, marine gas oil).
  • Estimated volume of oil discharged into the water.
  • Prevailing weather, wind speed, sea state, and projected slick drift trajectory.

4. Marine Insurance and Liabilities

Managing an oil spill requires direct coordination with the vessel's insurers to secure financial guarantees and handle claims:

  • P&I Club Activation: The shipowner’s Protection & Indemnity (P&I) Club covers third-party liabilities resulting from an oil spill, including shoreline clean-up expenses, environmental remediation costs, fisherman property damage claims, and statutory fines. The Club must be notified immediately to deploy a local correspondent and surveyor to the scene.
  • The Source Regime Rule: The legal framework for claims depends directly on the physical source of the spilled oil:
  • Persistent Tanker Cargo Oil: Governed under CLC 1992, which enforces strict liability backed by the mandatory CLC Blue Card certificate issued by the P&I Club.
  • Vessel Fuel Oil (Bunkers): Governed under the Bunker Convention 2001, using separate liability ceilings established by the LLMC 1996 Protocol for non-tanker fuel oil releases.

Demurrage and laytime — what are they? How do they apply in a charter party? What are the CE's implications?
SURVEY
15-Second AnswerLaytime is the contractually agreed time allowed to the charterer for loading and discharging cargo under a voyage charter party. Demurrage is a financial penalty rate paid by the charterer to the shipowner if this allocated time is exceeded. The Chief Engineer is directly affected because any engine room breakdown or delay in cargo operations caused by the ship's side (e.g., cargo pump failures or steam supply issues) stops the laytime clock, exposing the shipowner to financial losses.
60-Second AnswerCommercial voyage charter parties rely on structured time management parameters: Laytime represents the specified window of time negotiated by the charterer to complete cargo handling operations at the berth. If cargo operations exceed this window, the charterer must pay a predetermined daily penalty rate known as Demurrage. Conversely, if operations conclude ahead of schedule, the owner may pay Despatch to the charterer. The Chief Engineer plays a central role in this commercial framework. Any technical failure on the ship's side—such as an inert gas system breakdown, boiler steam loss, or cargo pump malfunction—stops the laytime clock. The resulting delays are logged in the Statement of Facts, which can lead to costly charter party disputes for the shipowner.

1. Commercial Definitions: Laytime, Demurrage, and Despatch

Voyage charter parties (e.g., ASBATANKVOY, GENCON) are commercial shipping agreements regulated by precise time-management rules:

  • Laytime: The contractually specified period of time allocated to the charterer for loading and discharging cargo. It is usually expressed as a set number of days, hours, or running days (e.g., "72 running hours"). Laytime is typically triggered after the vessel arrives at the port and the Master submits a formal Notice of Readiness (NOR).
  • Demurrage: A predetermined, liquidated damages rate paid by the charterer to the shipowner if the laytime window is exceeded. It is calculated as a fixed daily or hourly rate (e.g., "$35,000 USD per day") to compensate the owner for keeping the ship in port longer than planned.
  • Despatch: A financial reward paid by the shipowner to the charterer if cargo operations are completed ahead of schedule, before the allocated laytime expires. By convention, Despatch is typically calculated at 50% of the agreed demurrage rate.

2. The Operational Impact of the Statement of Facts (SOF)

Every minute spent by a merchant vessel at a terminal berth is recorded in a standardized document called the Statement of Facts (SOF). This log is compiled by the ship’s port agent and signed by the Master and the terminal representative.

  • The Laytime Dispute: The SOF logs all delays, shifting intervals, weather disruptions, and technical machinery stoppages.
  • Ship vs. Shore Delays: If a delay is caused by shore factors (e.g., terminal crane breakdowns or pipeline congestion), the laytime clock continues to run in favor of the shipowner. However, if the delay stems from a failure on the ship's side, laytime is suspended, preventing the shipowner from claiming demurrage during that period.

3. Engineering Implications for the Chief Engineer

The Chief Engineer's management of engine room systems directly influences the vessel's laytime calculation and commercial performance:

If the Chief Engineer fails to provide sufficient steam to the cargo pumps, the discharge rate will drop, and the charterer can deduct any resulting lost time from the laytime calculation.
  • Inert Gas System (IGS) Readiness: Under safety regulations, a tanker cannot discharge cargo if the tank atmosphere's oxygen levels are outside safe limits. An un-maintained IGS that breaks down during a terminal inspection will result in an immediate suspension of cargo operations, causing off-hire or laytime penalties for the owner.
  • Dry Bulk Deck Crane Maintenance: On geared bulk carriers, the Chief Engineer must ensure all deck crane hydraulics, wire ropes, and auxiliary machinery are fully operational. A hydraulic system breakdown during cargo discharge stops laytime and can lead to claims for commercial downtime.

  • Zero Laytime Credit: The status of any period where cargo operations are halted due to a verified failure of shipboard machinery.

Trap Questions
  • Examiner: "If a port crane breaks down and stops cargo operations while your ship is on demurrage, does the charterer stop paying the demurrage penalty rate during that delay?"
  • Candidate Response: No, sir. Under established maritime law, the general rule is "Once on demurrage, always on demurrage." Once the agreed laytime has expired and the vessel is on demurrage, the penalty clock runs continuously. Subsequent delays—including those caused by bad weather or shore equipment breakdowns—do not stop the demurrage clock, unless explicitly exempted by specific clauses in the charter party. The clock only stops if the delay is caused directly by a failure on the ship’s side.

Off-hire clause in a charter party — when does it apply and what are the implications for the CE?
SURVEY
15-Second AnswerOff-hire is a protective clause in a time charter party that suspends charter hire payments to the owner when the vessel is prevented from performing the contracted service. It applies during machinery breakdowns (e.g., main engine failure), drydocking, crew deficiencies, or deviations for repairs. For the Chief Engineer, any engine room failure causing off-hire is a critical incident that requires precise logging, a Root Cause Analysis (RCA), and immediate technical rectification to restart the charter hire clock.
60-Second AnswerUnder a time charter party (such as NYPE or BARECON), the charterer pays continuous hire for the use of the vessel. However, if the ship becomes fully or partially inefficient due to a listed trigger—such as a main engine breakdown, auxiliary machinery failure, drydocking, or deviation for emergency repairs—the off-hire clause is activated, and the obligation to pay hire is suspended. The Chief Engineer's primary implication is operational and financial accountability: any technical stoppage must be documented meticulously, with the exact duration of the breakdown recorded. The CE must lead the emergency repair team, conduct a comprehensive Root Cause Analysis (RCA), and maintain clear communication with the Designated Person Ashore (DPA) and the Master, as any discrepancy between the Engine Room Logbook and the Master's deck log can lead to costly maritime arbitration.

1. Legal Principle of the Off-Hire Clause

In a time charter party arrangement, the charterer purchases the operational capacity of the vessel for a defined period, paying a continuous daily "hire" fee. The Off-Hire Clause is a protective mechanism designed to balance the economic risk between the shipowner and the charterer.

  • The Threshold of Efficiency: If the vessel is prevented from working or performing the immediate service required by the charterer due to a cause explicitly listed in the charter party, the vessel is declared legally "off-hire." The charterer's obligation to pay for the ship is suspended during that exact window of inefficiency.
  • Net Loss of Time vs. Period Clause: Depending on the contract text (e.g., standard New York Produce Exchange - NYPE form), off-hire can be calculated as:
  • Period Option: Hire ceases the absolute moment the breakdown occurs and resumes only when the vessel is back in a fully efficient operational state.
  • Net Loss of Time Option: Hire is deducted only for the actual net time lost to the charterer during a voyage or cargo operation.

2. Trigger Events for Off-Hire Activation

The clause specifies a concrete list of events that can stop the hire payment clock:

  • Machinery Breakdown: Sudden failures of the main propulsion engine, auxiliary diesel generators, steering gear systems, or primary automation.
  • Structural Damage / Accidents: Groundings, collisions, or structural failures that require a suspension of operations.
  • Vessel Maintenance: Planned drydockings, underwater hull cleanings, or deviations taken to perform intermediate repairs.
  • Crew Deficiencies: Shortages of certified personnel, strikes, or crew illness/injury requiring an un-scheduled deviation to a port of refuge.
  • Breakdown Thresholds: Some custom time charter agreements include a small "window" or breakdown threshold (e.g., "no off-hire unless the breakdown exceeds 12 or 24 consecutive hours"). If exceeded, hire is deducted from the original moment of failure.

General liability vs contractual liability — what is the exact difference in a maritime context?
SURVEY
15-Second AnswerContractual liability arises strictly from the breach of an express or implied term within a legally binding agreement between specific parties (e.g., a charter party or Bill of Lading). General liability (Tortious liability) arises completely independent of a contract when a party breaches a general legal duty of care, causing damage or injury to a third party (e.g., a collision or dock impact).
60-Second AnswerCommercial maritime operations divide liabilities based on their legal origin. Contractual liability is triggered when a party fails to fulfill explicit terms in a signed shipping contract, such as an owner failing to maintain the speed warranties in a Time Charter Party or a cargo owner breaching a booking note. Conversely, General liability—often called tortious liability—requires no prior agreement. It is triggered under common law when operational negligence breaches a general duty of care, causing loss to an external third party. A classic example is an engine room blackout causing a vessel to strike a terminal berth or an adjacent ship. For the Chief Engineer, maintaining systems directly minimizes exposure to both: poor maintenance can cause cargo damage (contractual) or an environmental oil spill (tortious/general).

1. Contractual Liability Framework

Contractual liability stems directly from a voluntary, legally binding agreement entered into by two or more specific entities.

  • Legal Foundation: The scope of liability, standard of care, exemptions, and financial remedies are dictated by the written text of the contract and supplemented by specialized maritime statutes (such as the Hague-Visby Rules or Hamburg Rules for cargo contracts).
  • Privity of Contract: Legal claims can only be filed by parties who are signatories to the agreement. A third party who suffers an indirect loss cannot sue under a contract they are not party to.
  • Primary Maritime Examples:
  • Charter Party Breaches: A shipowner failing to maintain the speed or fuel consumption curves guaranteed in a Time Charter Party, or a charterer failing to pay hire on time.
  • Bills of Lading (B/L): A carrier delivering a containerized chemical cargo in a damaged, thermally degraded state due to a failure in the ship's reefer power network.
  • Ship Repair Contracts: A shipyard failing to complete a drydock overhaul within the contractually agreed timeframe.

2. General Liability (Tortious Liability) Framework

General liability arises by operation of law, completely independent of any pre-existing contractual relationship between the parties.

  • Legal Foundation: It is based on the legal principle of Tort (a civil wrong). To establish general liability, a claimant must prove three distinct elements in an admiralty court:
  1. The defendant owed the claimant a general legal duty of care.
  2. The defendant breached that duty of care through direct negligence or omission.
  3. The claimant suffered actual, quantifiable physical damage or financial loss as a direct result of that breach.
  • Primary Maritime Examples:
  • Collisions: Vessel A collides with Vessel B at an anchorage due to a failure to maintain a proper lookout or an un-drilled steering gear breakdown.
  • Fixed and Floating Objects (FFO): A vessel strikes a concrete loading pier, shore crane, or channel buoy due to poor bridge-engine room coordination.
  • Environmental Discharges: A fuel oil tank overflows during a bunkering operation, contaminating a local shoreline.

15-Second AnswerCAS (Condition Assessment Scheme) is a mandatory statutory survey regime under MARPOL Annex I designed to assess the structural integrity of aging single-hull oil tankers. 60-Second AnswerCAS is a strict regulatory requirement mandated under MARPOL Annex I to verify the structural alignment and safety profiles of aging single-hull tankers before they are granted trading extensions. Conversely, CAP is a voluntary, commercial rating module utilized by older vessel owners to secure cargo charters by obtaining an objective, independent class assessment score for their hull and machinery components. The Chief Engineer plays a central role in this commercial framework. Any technical failure on the ship's side—such as an inert gas system breakdown, boiler steam loss, or cargo pump malfunction—stops the laytime clock. The resulting delays are logged in the Statement of Facts, which can lead to costly charter party disputes for the shipowner.

1. Technical Framework of the Condition Assessment Scheme (CAS)

The Condition Assessment Scheme (CAS) is a stringent, mandatory inspection regime established under the auspices of the International Maritime Organization (IMO).

  • Regulatory Basis: Formally mandated under Regulations 20 and 21 of MARPOL Annex I.
  • Target Fleet: Developed specifically to accelerate the phase-out of aging single-hull oil tankers and tightly monitor those permitted to operate during transitional grandfathering periods.
  • Enforcement Mechanism: CAS is entirely statutory. Surveys are carried out by an authorized Recognized Organization (RO) on behalf of the Flag State Administration. The survey coincides with the vessel's Enhanced Survey Programme (ESP) intermediate or renewal timelines.
  • Operational Outcome: The survey results are compiled into a formal CAS report for the Flag Administration. If the vessel meets all structural standards, the Flag State issues a CAS Statement of Compliance. Without this document, the tanker is legally barred from international trading.

2. Technical Framework of the Condition Assessment Programme (CAP)

The Condition Assessment Programme (CAP) is an independent, non-statutory technical evaluation module developed by classification societies.

  • Commercial Basis: It is entirely voluntary and market-driven. Shipowners request a CAP evaluation to demonstrate the technical health of older tonnage to oil majors, charterers, and underwriters.
  • Target Fleet: Applicable to all classes of older merchant vessels, including tankers, dry bulk carriers, and container ships, typically once they cross 15 years of age.
  • The Rating Scale: CAP does not issue a simple pass/fail result. Instead, it assigns a specific rating score from 1 down to 4 based on detailed physical inspections and thickness measurements:
  • CAP 1 (Very Good): Structure and machinery show negligible wear, matching the standards of a newly built vessel.
  • CAP 2 (Good): Items show minor wear but are fully sound with no immediate repairs required.
  • CAP 3 (Acceptable): Items show moderate wear but meet minimum class requirements; repairs may be needed at the next drydock.
  • CAP 4 (Unacceptable): Severe structural or machinery degradation that falls below acceptable standards.

  • 15 Years: The standard age threshold where older trading vessels are routinely requested by charterers to undergo voluntary CAP evaluations.
  • CAP 2 or Higher: The minimum technical rating score typically required by oil majors (via SIRE/OCIMF inspections) to approve an older tanker for commercial chartering.
  • Reg 20/21: The specific regulatory clauses within MARPOL Annex I that establish the international baseline rules for CAS surveys.
  • 5-Year Alignment: The maximum validity period for a CAP certificate, which is typically aligned with the vessel's primary class renewal cycle.

CE Relevance, Traps, Failures & Exam Strategy
CE Relevance

The Chief Engineer must understand that a high CAP rating requires consistent technical oversight. During a CAP machinery survey, surveyors do not just perform visual checks; they evaluate objective wear data against baseline tolerances. If the Chief Engineer fails to track routine engine bearing clearances or postpones auxiliary generator maintenance, these deficiencies will be flagged in the class report, lowering the final rating. A low CAP rating can cause commercial charterers to reject the vessel, directly impacting its market value and earning potential.

---

Trap Questions
  • Examiner: "If your ship completes an extensive CAP survey and secures a CAP 1 rating from DNV, does this certificate automatically satisfy your statutory survey obligations under MARPOL Annex I?"
  • Candidate Response: No, sir. The Condition Assessment Programme (CAP) is a voluntary commercial rating system managed by classification societies for commercial leverage and chartering confidence. It carries no statutory authority to substitute or override mandatory flag state or international surveys required under MARPOL or SOLAS. Even with a CAP 1 rating, the vessel must still undergo all standard statutory surveys to maintain its core international certificates.

---

Common CE Failures

Candidates often fail by treating CAS and CAP as identical technical programs. You must clearly state that CAS is a mandatory statutory requirement under MARPOL designed for single-hull tankers, while CAP is a voluntary commercial rating service used across multiple vessel classes to satisfy charterers.

---

Casualty Link

In 1999, the structural failure and sinking of the tanker Erika off the coast of France led to severe shoreline pollution. The subsequent maritime investigation highlighted the need for more rigorous structural assessments for older tonnage, accelerating the implementation of mandatory CAS checks under MARPOL to ensure structural flaws are caught before they result in a catastrophic failure.

On My Vessel

On a Maersk vessel undergoing technical evaluation, maintaining system documentation is an important part of class compliance:

  • The Chief Engineer ensures all machinery maintenance records, performance reports, and diagnostic logs are updated within the fleet management platform to support any voluntary class CAP evaluations.
  • Coordinating with the engineering team to monitor critical machinery conditions, allowing the team to identify and resolve defects during scheduled port stays before they can impact a class assessment.
