Give a consolidated overview of SOLAS and MARPOL amendments entering into force from 2024 through 2028. Which ones affect a container vessel of your type directly?
SOLAS MARPOL GHG / Current Affairs
15-Second AnswerSOLAS amendments now run on a four-year cycle — 1 January 2024, 2026, and 2028 — while MARPOL amendments have entered force progressively since 2024. The largest single batch entered force on 1 January 2026: PFOS firefighting media ban, mandatory lifting appliance and anchor handling winch standards, vehicle/ro-ro space fire detection upgrades, oil fuel flashpoint declarations, and SASH training in Basic Training. The next major tranche is 1 January 2028, covering pilot transfer arrangements, VDES, and Emergency Towing Arrangements above 20,000 GT.
60-Second AnswerThe 1 January 2024 tranche modernised GMDSS to a multi-satellite-provider basis, introduced mooring equipment safety rules under SOLAS II-1/3-8, added water level detectors for multi-hold cargo ships, and created the new SOLAS Chapter XV / IP Code for industrial personnel (1 July 2024). The 1 January 2026 tranche — the largest — is built on Resolutions MSC.532(107), MSC.550(108), MSC.552(108), MSC.555(108), MSC.559(108) and MSC.560(108): it bans PFOS-based firefighting media, mandates SOLAS II-1/3-13 lifting appliance and anchor handling winch standards, extends fire detection to control stations and cargo control rooms (II-2/7.5.5), adds video monitoring and fire detection for vehicle/ro-ro/weather deck spaces (II-2/20), requires oil fuel supplier flashpoint declarations before bunkering, mandates electronic inclinometers on containerships/bulk carriers ≥3,000 GT, and adds SASH (sexual assault and sexual harassment) training to STCW Basic Training. MARPOL Annex VI in parallel added EEXI/CII reporting to IMO DCS (2024) and designated new ECAs — Canadian Arctic and Norwegian Sea (MEPC.392(82)) — enforcing 0.10% sulphur from 1 March 2027. The next cycle, 1 January 2028, brings pilot transfer arrangement standards (MSC.572/576(110)), VDES as an AIS-alternative tracking system, and mandatory Emergency Towing Arrangements for ships above 20,000 GT.

Why the Amendment Cycle Matters for Orals

IMO amendments to SOLAS now follow a formal four-year package cycle — 2024, 2026, 2028 — under the tacit acceptance procedure, with exceptional early-entry dates used only where urgency is justified (e.g. the 2024 package). MARPOL amendments run on their own annex-specific schedules but are increasingly harmonised to land on the same 1 January dates.

1 January 2024 — Entered Force

  • GMDSS modernisation: SOLAS Ch. II-1, III, IV, V made provider-agnostic (no longer Inmarsat-only); sea area A1–A4 definitions updated for multiple satellite operators. Why: to end the single-provider monopoly, allow competing satellite services, and future-proof distress communication as new constellations came online.
  • Mooring equipment safety — SOLAS II-1/3-8: safe mooring arrangement design, maintenance/inspection regime, and documentation, following the ISWAN/EMSA mooring injury studies. Why: mooring line/fitting failures were a leading cause of crew deaths and serious injuries on deck; the amendment forces designed-in safety rather than ad hoc fixes.
  • Watertight integrity: SOLAS II-1 parts B-1/B-2/B-4 aligned to the probabilistic damage stability approach. Why: to make progressive-flooding assumptions and collision bulkhead/watertight door rules consistent with modern damage-stability calculation methods, improving flood survivability.
  • Water level detectors — new SOLAS II-1/25-1: multi-hold cargo ships (excluding bulk carriers and tankers, which already had this). Why: to give early warning of hold flooding on ship types that previously had no mandatory detection, closing a regulatory gap exposed by cargo-hold flooding casualties.
  • Free-fall lifeboat test-launch exemption: SOLAS III/33 removed the 5-knot headway test-launch requirement for ships ≥20,000 GT. Why: studies showed the test added no meaningful data on dynamic loads at that speed while adding real risk to crew during drills — removing an unnecessary hazard.
  • MARPOL Annex VI: EEXI/CII/rating reporting to the IMO DCS made mandatory (Appendix IX); BDN must state flashpoint or confirm ≥70°C (Appendix V). Why: reporting drives transparency and accountability for the GHG reduction strategy; the flashpoint declaration reduces fire risk from mis-specified fuel.

SOLAS Chapter XV and the IP Code (Industrial Personnel) entered force separately on 1 July 2024 — why: to regulate the fast-growing transport of offshore wind and industrial workers, who fall outside standard passenger/cargo ship safety categories.

1 January 2026 — Entered Force (Largest Package)

  • PFOS firefighting media ban — MSC.532(107): use or storage of extinguishing media containing perfluorooctane sulfonic acid prohibited on ships/HSC constructed on or after 1 Jan 2026; existing ships to comply by first survey after that date. Why: PFOS is a persistent, bioaccumulative toxin harmful to crew health and the marine environment — aligning shipping with the global Stockholm Convention phase-out.
  • Lifting appliances and anchor handling winches — new SOLAS II-1/3-13, MSC.532(107): design, installation, and maintenance standards; existing equipment to comply by first renewal survey after 1 Jan 2026. Why: lifting gear had no dedicated SOLAS chapter despite being a major source of fatal crane/winch failures — this closes that gap with mandatory design and inspection standards.
  • Fire detection extension — SOLAS II-2/7.5.5, MSC.550(108): control stations, cargo control rooms, and accommodation now require individually identifiable smoke/heat detectors on new ships. Why: pinpointing which specific detector triggered speeds up the crew's response and cuts false-alarm confusion during an actual fire.
  • Vehicle/ro-ro/weather deck fire safety — SOLAS II-2/20, MSC.550(108) + FSS Code, MSC.555(108): fixed fire detection/alarm, fire patrol system, and video monitoring with 24-hour storage for vehicle, special category, and ro-ro spaces. New ships from 1 Jan 2026; existing passenger ships must retrofit key items by first survey after 1 Jan 2028. Why: directly responds to the EMSA FIRESAFE studies (2016–2018) into fatal ro-ro/car-carrier fires, which found detection and monitoring gaps let fires spread undetected in vehicle decks.
  • Oil fuel flashpoint declarations — SOLAS II-2/4.2.1, MSC.520(106)/MSC.550(108): supplier's signed declaration before bunkering plus BDN flashpoint statement, enforcing the 60°C minimum. Why: low-flashpoint fuel has caused engine-room fires and explosions; a pre-bunkering declaration puts legal accountability on the supplier, not just the ship. Don't confuse with the adjacent resolution: MSC.520(106) is the flashpoint amendment (SOLAS Ch. II-2); MSC.521(106), adopted at the same MSC 106 session, is a completely different item — the new SOLAS Chapter XV on Industrial Personnel. The two numbers sit next to each other in the same MSC 106 resolution batch, which is exactly why examiners like to test whether a candidate has them swapped.
  • Electronic inclinometers — SOLAS V/19.2.12, MSC.532(107): containerships and bulk carriers ≥3,000 GT constructed on/after 1 Jan 2026, connected to the VDR. Performance standards under MSC.363(92). Why: traces directly to the bulk carrier Jupiter bauxite cargo-shift casualty, where investigators had no recorded roll/stability data to reconstruct events.
  • Grain loading — new optional condition, MSC.552(108): "specially suitable compartment, partly filled in way of hatch opening, ends untrimmed" — requires updated/approved grain loading stability manual before use. Why: gives grain carriers a validated, safer loading configuration option without forcing a full trim, improving operational flexibility while maintaining stability margins.
  • STCW SASH training — Table A-VI/1-4, MSC.560(108): sexual assault/sexual harassment prevention and response folded into Basic Training (PSSR). Why: driven by the Joint ILO/IMO Tripartite Working Group's findings on the scale of harassment and assault at sea — embeds prevention/response training for every seafarer from day one, not just officers.
  • High-manganese austenitic steel — IGC/IGF Codes: now permitted for LNG cargo/fuel tank construction, secondary barriers, and pressure vessels under specified conditions. Why: gives yards a cheaper, more available cryogenic-grade steel alternative to 9% nickel steel, supporting LNG-as-fuel uptake for decarbonisation.
  • MARPOL Annex VI — new ECAs: Canadian Arctic and Norwegian Sea designated (MEPC.392(82)); 0.10% sulphur enforcement begins 1 March 2027. Canadian Arctic NOx Tier III applies to ships with keel laid on/after 1 Jan 2025. Why: both are ecologically sensitive, slow-recovery environments where SOx/NOx deposition does disproportionate long-term damage.
  • STCW-F Convention and Code: entered force 1 Jan 2026, setting harmonised training/certification standards for fishing vessel personnel. Why: fishing vessels have historically had the highest fatality rate of any maritime sector and were largely outside STCW's scope — this brings a minimum competence floor to that fleet.
  • Polar Code extension to non-SOLAS ships — SOLAS Ch. XIV / Polar Code Part I-A, MSC.532(107)/MSC.538(107): fishing vessels and pleasure yachts operating in polar waters brought under interim safety requirements, retroactive to existing non-SOLAS ships by 1 Jan 2027. Why: growing polar tourism and fishing activity left a safety gap for exactly the vessel types least equipped to handle ice and cold-water emergencies.

1 July 2026 — MASS Code (Current Affairs, Not in Older Study Notes)

IMO adopted the first International Code of Safety for Maritime Autonomous Surface Ships (MASS Code) at MSC 111 (May 2026) under Resolution MSC.595(111) — a goal-based code for AI-enabled and remotely operated commercial ships, formally implemented as an option for adoption by Flag Administrations from 1 July 2026 for cargo ships. Critical point to state explicitly, worded carefully: this is a non-mandatory (voluntary) Code — it does not "take effect" on the fleet in the binding SOLAS sense, and if an examiner pushes on that phrasing, the correct answer is that 1 July 2026 is when the Code became formally available for Flag States and operators to opt into, not a date on which it became legally compulsory. It launches the formal Experience-Building Phase (EBP), letting flag states and operators trial it and feed real-world data back before any mandatory version is developed. MSC 112 (December 2026) re-establishes the MASS Working Group to build the EBP framework; the mandatory MASS Code is targeted for adoption by 1 July 2030, entering force 1 January 2032. Why non-mandatory first: autonomous ship technology and operational data are still maturing — mandating binding rules before sufficient real-world experience risks locking in requirements that don't match how MASS actually operate. MASS-code vessels remain bound by SOLAS and other mandatory instruments in parallel throughout the EBP. This is a live current-affairs topic examiners are likely to probe given how recent it is.

1 January 2028 — Full Confirmed List

This is the complete package adopted at MSC 110 (June 2025) and consolidated/finalised at MSC 111 (May 2026), all entering force 1 January 2028 unless noted otherwise:

  • Pilot transfer arrangements — new SOLAS V/23 + mandatory Performance Standards, MSC.572(110)/MSC.576(110): all technical detail moved out of V/23 into a dedicated Performance Standard covering design, manufacture, installation, inspection, stowage, maintenance, and mandatory crew familiarisation; mechanical pilot hoists are banned outright and lighting standards are mandated. Applies to all ships on all voyages regardless of size, subject to the usual V/1.4 exemptions (e.g. <150 GT, fishing vessels). New installations comply immediately from 1 Jan 2028; existing arrangements by first survey after 1 Jan 2029. Why: pilot transfer accidents/fatalities have been a persistent, poorly-regulated risk — this is the most comprehensive rewrite of pilot ladder/hoist safety rules to date.
  • VDES (VHF Data Exchange System) — SOLAS Ch. IV & V, plus new MSC performance-standard resolutions: introduced as an AIS-alternative/supplement allowing higher-capacity, more secure ship-to-ship and ship-to-shore data exchange. Consequential amendments to the 1994/2000 HSC Codes. Why: AIS bandwidth and security are increasingly inadequate for modern data needs (e-navigation, MSI dissemination); VDES gives faster, more secure throughput.
  • MSI/SAR dissemination via all recognised mobile satellite services — SOLAS IV/5, V/4, V/5: requires maritime safety information and SAR-related data to be disseminated across all operational recognised satellite providers, not just one. Why: extends the same multi-provider logic from the 2024 GMDSS package to safety-information broadcast, avoiding single-provider dependency.
  • Emergency Towing Arrangements (ETA) — SOLAS II-1/3-4, MSC.549(108), supported by interim guidelines MSC.1/Circ.1691: extended to ships other than tankers, above 20,000 GT — Flag/RO-approved, rapidly deployable on power failure, fitted forward and aft, with towing load requirements tied to the ship's equipment number. Why: ETAs were historically tanker-only despite large non-tanker ships (container/bulk) posing similar drift/grounding risk after a blackout — this closes that gap.
  • IP Code (Industrial Personnel) — Part IV amendments: harmonises the industrial-personnel dimensioning weight (including PPE) at 90 kg across SOLAS Ch. I and HSC Code-certified ships, plus new subdivision/damage-stability and LSA requirements for ships carrying >240 industrial personnel/passengers. Why: the original 2024 IP Code left an inconsistency in personnel weight assumptions between ship categories, which affects stability margin calculations.
  • ESP Code (Enhanced Survey Programme) — Remote Inspection Techniques (RITs): permits RIT-based close-up surveys (drones, crawlers, etc.) for existing bulk carriers and oil tankers, formally defined and distinguished from "remote surveys." Corresponding IACS UR Z10 series to be revised. Why: close-up surveys of double-bottom/hold structure are hazardous and access-limited for surveyors; validated remote techniques cut risk and access difficulty without lowering inspection standard.
  • LSA Code — free-fall lifeboat release testing: requires simulated load-testing of the release system without launching the boat into the water, using higher safety factors to account for shock loading; supporting revisions to MSC.402(96) and MSC.1/Circ.1529. Why: release-gear failures during launch have caused fatal lifeboat drills; testing the release mechanism under simulated load without a live launch cuts drill risk while still validating the gear.
  • High-Speed Craft (HSC) Codes — life jacket harmonisation: Annex 1 of the 1994/2000 HSC Codes aligned with SOLAS Chapter III on infant life jacket numbers and accessories for large persons; pilot ladders/manropes added to the HSC Record of Equipment. Why: removes an inconsistency between HSC and standard SOLAS life-saving equipment carriage requirements.
  • IMDG Code amendments: further alignment with UN dangerous goods transport recommendations. Why: keeps sea carriage classification, packing, and labelling consistent with the latest UN model regulations used across all transport modes.
  • SPS Code & Fishermen/Fishing Vessels Code (2005): consequential Record of Equipment/certificate form updates tied to the pilot transfer and other 2028 changes.

1 July 2028 (separate date — not 1 January): draft IGF Code amendments (SOLAS Ch. II-1 clarification distinguishing "gaseous fuel" from "low-flashpoint fuel") and draft IGC Code amendments (fire safety, ventilation capacity, personnel protection clarifications) are both targeted for this date, subject to approval at MSC 111 and formal adoption at MSC 112 (December 2026) — treat as "expected," not yet locked, if asked in an oral before that adoption is confirmed. Why: the "one ship, one code" policy requires IGC/IGF to stay tightly aligned as gas-fuelled propulsion grows, so both codes are being clarified together rather than separately.

Note — LRIT free access for coastal States: updated status — MSC 111 (May 2026) approved, in principle, draft amendments to SOLAS Regulation V/19-1 introducing a free-of-charge policy for LRIT position reports to entitled coastal States, along with a draft revision of the LRIT performance standards (Resolution MSC.263(84)/Rev.1). This is a step beyond "just discussion" but is not yet formally adopted — that's expected at MSC 112 (December 2026), with a possible entry into force as late as 1 January 2032. Correct answer if asked: "approved in principle, pending formal adoption," not "settled" and not "just under discussion."

Beyond 2028 — Roadmap Context

Two related items sit just past the 2028 horizon and are useful to mention if an examiner probes "what's next": the mandatory MASS Code (autonomous ships) is targeted for development through 2028, expected adoption by 1 July 2030, and entry into force 1 January 2032 — MSC 110 was the last session to adopt amendments in time for the 2028 cycle; from MSC 111 onward, new SOLAS amendments target 2032 unless fast-tracked. MSC 112 (Dec 2026) will re-establish the MASS Working Group to build the Experience-Building Phase framework feeding into that mandatory Code. A comprehensive review of the entire STCW Convention is also underway, targeting completion around 2026–2027 with adoption expected by 2027 (see Q2).

Formula / Threshold Block

Lifeboat lowering speed (post-2026): min 1.0 m/s, max 1.3 m/s — replaces the old S = 0.4 + 0.02H formula (H = davit head to lightest seagoing draft), which varied unhelpfully with loading condition.

CE Relevance, Traps, Failures & Exam Strategy
CE Relevance

As Chief Engineer on a Maersk containership, three of these amendments are directly operational, not theoretical: the electronic inclinometer feed to the VDR (SOLAS V/19) falls under your bridge-engine room interface checks; the oil fuel flashpoint declaration (II-2/4.2.1) is a bunkering-day paperwork item you must verify before each bunker delivery; and the PFOS ban affects which foam/dry chemical extinguishing media can legally remain in your fire-fighting stores and fixed systems from 1 Jan 2026 onward.

Trap Questions
  • Examiner: "Your vessel is 8 years old — does the electronic inclinometer requirement apply to you?"
  • Candidate Answer: "Only if my ship is a containership or bulk carrier of 3,000 GT and above constructed on or after 1 January 2026, sir. An existing vessel built before that date is not required to retrofit under this amendment — this is a new-build requirement under SOLAS V/19."

Candidates quote the amendments as still "upcoming" when they are already in force, and cannot separate SOLAS's own four-year package cycle from MARPOL Annex VI's ECA/CII timeline, treating all of it as one undifferentiated "2026 amendments" blob — examiners specifically probe this distinction.

What STCW amendments are currently in force or coming, and why were they introduced?
STCW
15-Second AnswerThe 2024 amendments (MSC.560(108)) added mandatory SASH — sexual assault and sexual harassment — prevention/response training to Basic Training, in force since 1 January 2026. Digital STCW certificates became legally valid from 1 January 2025 under MSC.540(107)/MSC.541(107). A full Comprehensive Review of the entire STCW Convention is underway, targeting adoption around 2027, covering alternative-fuel training and autonomous-ship competence gaps.
60-Second AnswerThree STCW tracks are live right now. First, Table A-VI/1-4 was amended by MSC.560(108) to add a new competence — "contribute to the prevention of and response to violence and harassment, including sexual harassment, bullying and sexual assault" — to Basic Training's Personal Safety and Social Responsibilities (PSSR) module, effective 1 January 2026; existing certificates aren't invalidated, but new candidates and refresher training must include it. Second, digitisation: from 1 January 2025, Resolutions MSC.540(107) and MSC.541(107) legally recognise electronic Certificates of Competency and Proficiency, verified by Port State Control via QR code/tracking number. Third, and largest: the Sub-Committee on Human Element, Training and Watchkeeping (HTW) is conducting a Comprehensive Review of the whole 1978 STCW Convention — over 400 gaps identified — targeting completion around 2026–2027, covering training for alternative fuels (hydrogen, ammonia, methanol) and remote/autonomous vessel operation, which the current Convention doesn't address at all.

Why STCW Is Being Amended Now

STCW's last full rewrite was the 2010 Manila Amendments. Since then, three separate pressure points have built up: (1) documented harassment/assault at sea with no formal training response, (2) certificate fraud that digitisation directly addresses, and (3) a technology gap — the Convention has no provisions at all for alternative-fuel or autonomous-ship competence, both of which are now operational realities.

SASH Training — MSC.560(108), eff. 1 January 2026

  • What changed: Table A-VI/1-4 (Personal Safety and Social Responsibilities) gained a seventh competence item on preventing and responding to violence, harassment, bullying, and sexual assault/harassment.
  • Why: driven by the Joint ILO/IMO Tripartite Working Group's findings — this is now considered foundational safety training, not an optional add-on, and applies to every seafarer from Basic Training level up, not just senior officers.
  • Practical effect: seafarers boarding after 1 Jan 2026 need updated PSSR certification; those with certificates issued before 2025 will likely need gap/refresher training before their next contract.

Digital Certificates — MSC.540(107)/MSC.541(107), eff. 1 January 2025

Electronic CoCs and CoPs are now legally equivalent to paper. Why: paper certificate fraud was a persistent PSC detention driver; QR-code/tracking-number verification lets PSC officers confirm authenticity instantly against the flag state database.

Comprehensive Review of STCW (in progress, target ~2027)

  • Scope: HTW identified over 400 gaps in the current Convention — the review covers the Convention's own articles as well as the Code, an unusually broad remit.
  • Alternative fuels: new training standards being drafted for seafarers working with hydrogen, ammonia, methanol, and advanced biofuels — none of which existed as mainstream fuels when STCW was last revised.
  • Autonomous/remote operation: competence standards for MASS-type ships are being developed in parallel with the MASS Code itself (see Q1).
  • Why now: "skills fade" research and the pace of new-technology adoption on ships have both outstripped a Convention designed around conventional crewed vessels.

Latest Update — HTW 12 (23–27 February 2026)

This is the most current status, and worth quoting directly if an examiner asks "what's happened recently":

  • Phase 2 formally commenced: HTW 12 marked the start of Phase 2 (drafting/revision) of the Comprehensive Review, following MSC 110's endorsement of an updated roadmap. Phase 1 (gap identification — the 400+ gaps) is complete; Phase 2 is now actual amendment drafting.
  • Alternative-fuel training — concrete progress: HTW 12 finalised two sets of draft interim training guidelines — for seafarers on ships using methyl/ethyl alcohol as fuel and for ammonia as fuel — both built on the generic base document STCW.7/Circ.25 (approved at MSC 110) and modelled on the existing LNG-as-fuel training standard. Both were sent to MSC 111 (May 2026) for approval.
  • The parallel safety-guideline track — MSC 111 approvals: worth distinguishing from the STCW training guidelines above — MSC 111 also approved the underlying engineering/safety "Interim Guidelines for the Safety of Ships Using Hydrogen as Fuel" (arrangement, installation, control, and monitoring of machinery/equipment/systems) and the "Interim Guidelines for Use of Ammonia Cargo as Fuel on Gas Carriers" (safety outside the cargo area, supplementing IGC Code Chapter 16). These are CCC/MSC safety instruments, not STCW training instruments — the STCW training guidelines (above) exist to train seafarers on how to operate safely within these engineering guidelines, so the two tracks are companions, not the same document.
  • Work plan extended to 2029: HTW 12 agreed a work plan prioritising hydrogen fuel cells next, then LPG, hydrogen, battery power, and wind-assisted propulsion — with the explicit long-term goal of folding all of this into the STCW framework itself, not just interim guidance.
  • Autonomous/remote competence: still at the discussion stage — HTW is mapping what monitoring-automated-systems and AI-supported-decision-tool competencies should look like, but no draft text yet; this tracks alongside the MASS Code's own Experience-Building Phase (see Q1).
  • New parallel workstream — fatigue/hours of rest: HTW 12 also launched a scoping exercise (target 2027) reviewing whether current hours-of-work-and-rest provisions are fit for purpose, spanning STCW, MLC, and ISM Code effectiveness together — a signal that fatigue may become part of the next amendment wave too.
  • Certificate integrity: HTW 12 reviewed fraudulent-certificate reporting via the STCW GISIS module and linked further digitalisation work to the Comprehensive Review, building on the 2025 digital-certificate amendments (MSC.540/541(107)).

Bottom line for the oral: as of mid-2026, alternative-fuel training has moved from "planned" to "draft guidelines submitted for approval" — that's the current, defensible answer if pressed on status.

60-Second AnswerTwo distinct MLC amendment packages matter right now. This is worth stating explicitly, since it's a common examiner trap: there is no MLC amendment cycle dated 2026. 2022 Amendments — In Force from 23 December 2024

  • Social connectivity (Reg. 3.1): shipowners must, so far as reasonably practicable, provide internet access at reasonable cost. Why: COVID-era isolation exposed how much seafarer mental health depends on contact with family — this converts a "nice to have" into a standard.
  • Gender-appropriate PPE: correctly-sized helmets, coveralls, gloves, and footwear for women seafarers. Why: ill-fitting PPE designed for a male-average body was a real injury risk as female crewing has grown.
  • Urgent medical disembarkation: seafarers with serious injury, severe bleeding, broken bones, or suicide risk must be promptly landed for shore medical care. Why: closes ambiguity that had previously delayed medical landings in genuine emergencies.
  • Mandatory death reporting: all seafarer deaths recorded and reported annually to the ILO, published in a global register. Why: creates the first systematic global dataset on seafarer mortality, which previously didn't exist in usable form.
  • Abandonment/repatriation clarity: replacement crew engaged after an abandonment get the same MLC entitlements as those they replace; flag/port/labour-supplying states must cooperate on prompt repatriation.

2025 "Seafarers' Bill of Rights" — Binding from December 2027

  • Shore leave without special permits: once arrival formalities are complete, seafarers go ashore without visas/special permits; any denial needs a written reason. Why: the Seafarers' Happiness Index recorded a measurable happiness drop tied specifically to restricted shore access.
  • Explicit anti-harassment provisions: shipboard violence, harassment, sexual harassment, bullying, and sexual assault explicitly prohibited, with mandatory prevention policy and safe reporting mechanisms required of shipowners. Why: mirrors the STCW SASH training addition (Q2) at the labour-law level, closing the policy/reporting gap that training alone can't fix.

60-Second AnswerContainer ships get four categories of targeted attention. First, stability monitoring: SOLAS V/19.2.12 (MSC.532(107)) requires electronic inclinometers connected to the VDR on containerships and bulk carriers ≥3,000 GT built from 1 Jan 2026 — a direct response to cargo-shift/stack-collapse casualties where post-incident investigators lacked recorded roll data. Why Container Ships Are Singled Out

Container ships combine three risk factors other cargo types don't: very high stack weights concentrated on deck (stability sensitivity), a schedule-driven commercial model that resists slow-steaming (GHG compliance tension), and a track record of high-profile container-loss/fire incidents that have drawn direct regulatory attention.

Stability & Cargo-Loss Specific

  • Electronic inclinometers — SOLAS V/19.2.12, MSC.532(107), eff. 1 Jan 2026: containerships and bulk carriers ≥3,000 GT built from this date must have inclinometer data feeding the VDR. Why: traces to the bulk carrier Jupiter casualty but was extended to containerships because stack-collapse incidents (e.g. ONE Apus, Maersk Essen-type events) showed the same investigative data gap.
  • Container loss reporting — SOLAS V/31, V/32 amendments + MARPOL Protocol I: master must report container losses to Coast Guard, Flag State, and nearby vessels with position and count, and confirmed losses must also be entered in the IMO's GISIS database. Why: lost containers are both a navigation hazard (semi-submerged, radar-invisible) and, if carrying dangerous goods, a pollution risk — previously there was no mandatory reporting chain, let alone a central global record of how many containers are actually being lost each year.

IMDG Code Amendment 42-24 — Mandatory from 1 January 2026

Directly relevant to a container vessel engineer, this is worth naming with the exact UN numbers: the generic Class 9 entry UN 3171 "Battery-powered vehicle" is no longer permitted for lithium- or sodium-ion powered vehicles. Shipments must now use UN 3556 (lithium-ion battery powered vehicle), UN 3557 (lithium-metal battery powered vehicle), or UN 3558 (sodium-ion battery powered vehicle), each under Packing Instruction P912 and Special Provision SP962 (four-side placarding recommended). Why: the old generic entry didn't distinguish battery chemistry, but lithium battery fires require different firefighting response than a conventional vehicle fire — first responders and stowage/segregation planning need to know the chemistry before the container is opened, not after. Amendment 42-24 also tightened charcoal/carbon carriage rules — the old blanket test-based exemptions (SP925/SP223) were removed, and charcoal (UN 1361, Class 4.2 — substances liable to spontaneous combustion) now falls under new Special Provision SP978: it must be weathered for a minimum of 14 days in covered open-air storage (or steam-treated + cooled + inert-gas packed, with 24 hours' storage) before packing, and its temperature must not exceed 40°C on the day of packing, with a minimum 30 cm container headspace and block-stacking limits — all backed by a documented weathering/vanning survey record. Why: charcoal is a self-heating substance and a poor heat conductor, so trapped internal heat from incomplete cooling after production is the direct cause of the delayed-onset container stack fires (over 60 recorded between 2015–2022) that forced this change — ignition often doesn't occur until days into the voyage.

Stowage & Segregation — General Principle, Not a New 42-24 Rule

Worth a careful, defensible phrasing here: lithium-battery vehicles (UN 3556/3557, Class 9) and charcoal (UN 1361, Class 4.2) are different hazard classes with different fire behaviours — battery thermal runaway versus self-heating/spontaneous combustion — and the IMDG Code's general segregation table (Part 7.2) has long required defined separation between Class 9 and Class 4.2 goods based on standard segregation groups. Amendment 42-24 doesn't introduce a brand-new standalone rule pairing these two specific entries; rather, giving lithium vehicles their own dedicated UN numbers (instead of the generic UN 3171) makes the correct segregation *category* unambiguous at the booking and stowage-planning stage — previously, under the vague UN 3171 entry, segregation planners could miss that a battery-powered vehicle needed Class 9 handling at all. Practical takeaway for stowage planning: always check the current segregation table entry for the exact UN number being carried, rather than assuming a stowage plan template from before 2026 is still correct.

CSC Convention (Container Structural Safety) — Confirmed: No New Amendments in This Cycle

Worth stating explicitly if asked: the International Convention for Safe Containers (CSC), 1972 — the instrument governing container structural testing, the Safety Approval Plate, and periodic re-examination — has not been amended as part of the 2024–2028 SOLAS/MARPOL package. Its last substantive amendments were resolution MSC.310(88), entry into force 1 January 2012 (Safety Approval Plate specs, one-door-removed test, new Annex III on control/verification), and resolution MSC.355(92), entry into force 1 July 2014 (harmonised definitions and dimension/mass unit consequential amendments). Why no update now: the current regulatory attention on containers has gone into operational/reporting rules (loss reporting, GISIS) and ship-side stability monitoring (inclinometers) rather than the container structural-testing regime itself, which remains stable. If an examiner asks "what's new in CSC," the correct answer is that CSC itself hasn't moved — the box's structural approval regime is unchanged, only how losses of already-approved boxes are reported has changed.

Operational Reality — VDR Interface, Not Just a Fitting Requirement

Worth stating to show CE-level oversight, not textbook recall: MSC.363(92) requires the inclinometer to operate continuously in all sea-going conditions, with the roll/heel data feeding directly to the VDR. If that inclinometer-to-VDR interface fails or drops out, it isn't a trivial fault — a defective or non-functioning VDR feed is a recognised "safety of navigation" PSC deficiency, and depending on severity a PSC officer can treat it as detainable, since it degrades the ship's SOLAS V-required voyage recording capability. Practically: a Chief Engineer who notices the inclinometer has stopped reporting to the VDR should treat it the same as any other SOLAS-mandated navigation equipment fault — log it, report it, and get it rectified before the next port call, not leave it for the next scheduled survey.

Fire Safety — Fleet-Wide but Operationally Heavy on Boxships

The PFOS ban and extended fire detection to control stations/cargo control rooms/accommodation (SOLAS II-2/7.5.5) apply to all new ships, but container ships carry large firefighting media inventories and have extensive accommodation blocks, making the compliance burden proportionally larger than on, say, a tanker with a smaller crew footprint.

GHG / CII — The Structural Pressure Point

Container ships are contractually bound to schedule reliability, which pushes toward higher service speeds — directly opposed to the slow-steaming approach that most easily improves CII rating. This makes container operators disproportionately reliant on technical measures (hull/propeller efficiency, alternative fuels, WHR) rather than operational measures alone to stay compliant.

60-Second AnswerThis is the single highest-probability current-affairs question on the whole paper right now. The Net-Zero Framework — global GHG fuel intensity (GFI) targets plus a pricing/reward mechanism, proposed as new Chapter 5 of MARPOL Annex VI — was approved in principle at MEPC 83 (April 2025). Formal adoption was expected at an extraordinary session, MEPC ES.2, in October 2025, but opposition led by the US, Saudi Arabia, and other states forced a procedural vote: 57 member states voted to adjourn for a year against 49 who wanted to proceed, with 21 abstentions. The session was adjourned, not rejected — technical work on implementation guidelines continued regardless. The resumption date is now fixed: the Extraordinary Session (ES.2) reconvenes 4 December 2026, in the same week as the regular MEPC 85 (30 November–3 December 2026), with two intersessional working group meetings beforehand to try to close remaining gaps. Even if adopted in December 2026, the earliest possible entry into force is 1 March 2028, under the standard 16-month implementation window — not the March 2027 date many older notes still cite. Separately, the North-East Atlantic ECA (SOx/PM/NOx), which had also failed to adopt at the October 2025 session alongside the NZF, was successfully adopted on its own at MEPC 84 (27 April–1 May 2026) and will enter force 1 September 2027.

Why This Matters for the Oral

The Net-Zero Framework would make shipping the first global industry sector with binding, IMO-wide emissions pricing — a genuinely novel regulatory instrument. Given how politically contentious and current the story is, examiners treat correct sequencing of dates (approved-in-principle vs formally adopted vs in force) as a direct test of whether a candidate follows the news or is reciting stale notes.

The Adjournment — What Actually Happened

  • MEPC 83 (April 2025): Net-Zero Framework approved in principle by a majority of member states, drafted as new Chapter 5 of Draft Revised MARPOL Annex VI.
  • MEPC ES.2 (14–17 October 2025): convened specifically to formally adopt the amendments. Instead, Singapore moved and Saudi Arabia called a vote on adjourning the session — the vote passed 57 in favour to 49 against (21 abstentions), so the amendments were not adopted.
  • Why it failed: the US applied significant diplomatic pressure, describing the framework as an "unsanctioned global tax regime," and several states that had supported it in April (e.g. China, and briefly Greece/Cyprus) shifted to supporting the delay.

The Path Back — MEPC 84 and the December 2026 Resumption

  • MEPC 84 (27 April–1 May 2026): broad majority of member states reaffirmed support for the original NZF text as the basis for moving forward; alternative proposals from Japan and a joint Argentina/Liberia/Panama counter-proposal (which would have stripped out carbon pricing) did not gather significant support.
  • Confirmed resumption date: the Extraordinary Session (MEPC ES.2) resumes Friday, 4 December 2026, immediately following MEPC 85 (30 November–3 December 2026). Two intersessional working group meetings (September and late November 2026) are scheduled beforehand specifically to close remaining gaps, plus a dedicated workshop on "chain of custody" fuel-tracking models.
  • Revised earliest entry into force: 1 March 2028 — not March 2027. This follows from the standard ~16-month tacit-acceptance-style implementation window applied from a December 2026 adoption date, if adoption happens at all in December.
  • Member states can still submit new amendments before the December 2026 session — so the exact final text is not fully locked even now.

The Two Technical Pillars Being Negotiated

  • Global Fuel Standard: sets a declining annual greenhouse gas fuel intensity (GFI) limit through 2035, using a well-to-wake lifecycle approach; ships exceeding their GFI target must purchase remedial units or use banked credits.
  • Zero/Near-Zero (ZNZ) Fuel Reward Mechanism: ships using zero- or near-zero-emission fuels/technologies earn rewards funded by the pricing side — designed to accelerate the fuel transition rather than just penalise non-compliance.
  • IMO Net-Zero Fund: the framework's proposed vehicle for collecting GHG pricing revenue and redistributing it — for R&D, the ZNZ reward mechanism, and a "just and equitable transition" (capacity building, seafarer training, disproportionate impact mitigation on developing states). Projected to generate an estimated USD 10–15 billion per year from 2028. This is the least-developed element of the package and worth flagging as still-forming if asked for detail.

Why the Pricing Mechanism Is So Contentious — the Real Fault Lines

This is more nuanced than a simple two-bloc fight, and worth getting right: heading into MEPC 83, Pacific Island and Caribbean SIDS plus the EU (60+ nations) pushed hardest for a high, universal flat levy (proposals ranged USD 100–150/tonne CO2) applied to all emissions from the first tonne — simple, predictable, and guaranteed to generate large fund revenue. Opposing the flat levy were large emerging economies and petrostates (including some, like Argentina and Japan, who proposed their own softer reward/fund schemes), who argued a flat levy would raise shipping costs, hit food security, and disadvantage long-haul trade from developing regions. The adopted compromise — the tiered GFI/credit-trading system (the "J9 Bridge" proposal, led by Singapore, backed by China and Brazil among others) — only charges ships that miss their annual GFI intensity target, at $100–380 per excess tonne, meaning total fund revenue is structurally lower than a flat levy would generate. That's precisely why Pacific SIDS remain unsatisfied and have warned they'll revert to demanding a flat levy if the compromise is "watered down" further. The US opposition (which triggered the October 2025 adjournment) is a separate axis again — it rejects carbon pricing on shipping altogether, calling it a "global tax."

What This Means at the Manifold — GFI vs. CII

Ties the policy back to your own engine room: the new GFI metric is calculated on a well-to-wake (WtW) basis — upstream production/transport emissions plus onboard combustion — unlike the existing CII, which remains a tank-to-wake (TtW), purely operational metric and is not being replaced or removed by the NZF; the two run in parallel. Practical consequence for a CE: GFI compliance is driven overwhelmingly by which fuel is actually delivered to the manifold — its full lifecycle carbon intensity — rather than by how efficiently you burn it. A perfectly tuned main engine and a working WHR system still improve your CII score (operational efficiency), but they cannot fix a poor GFI score if the underlying fuel's well-to-wake intensity is high; conversely, a low-carbon-intensity fuel improves GFI regardless of your engine tuning. This is why the framework leans so heavily on the Zero/Near-Zero fuel reward mechanism rather than on engine efficiency incentives — the compliance lever has shifted from "how well you burn it" to "what you're burning."

North-East Atlantic ECA — Now Confirmed, Separately from the NZF

This ECA (SOx, particulate matter, and NOx) had also failed to adopt at the October 2025 ES.2 session, tangled up procedurally with the NZF vote. It was decoupled and successfully adopted on its own at MEPC 84, entering force 1 September 2027 — joining the Canadian Arctic and Norwegian Sea ECAs (see Q1) as the third major new ECA of this regulatory cycle.

Trap Warning

⚠ Trap Warning: Don't say the Net-Zero Framework "was rejected" — it was adjourned, not voted down; the underlying text still has majority support and remains on the table for December 2026. Also don't cite "1 March 2027" as the entry-into-force date — that was the pre-delay estimate; the current, correct figure is 1 March 2028 at the earliest, and even that assumes December 2026 adoption goes through.

60-Second AnswerFour DGS/DGMA instruments from 2026 are worth knowing by name and date — note the administration renamed itself mid-year, so the naming convention shifts partway through this list. DGS Order 01 of 2026 (21 January 2026) revised the age norms and qualitative parameters for Indian-flag vessel registration and for foreign-flag vessels requiring an MS Act 1958 Sec 406/407 licence; it doesn't apply to passenger vessels, FSRU/FPSO, or various specialised craft, and existing vessels regardless of current age are allowed to operate until 31 March 2029, with a possible two-year extension to 2031 subject to a future "Sustainability Indexing" review. Merchant Shipping Notice 01 of 2026 (February 2026) set out the criteria for empanelment and retention of RPSL-holding manning agents — relevant to how Indian seafarers are recruited and placed. DGMA Circular 17 of 2026 (29 March 2026) granted a 3-month (or sign-off, whichever is earlier) extension to CoC/CoP/STCW certificates for Indian seafarers, citing force majeure conditions in the Middle East/West Asia region. DGMA Circular 33 of 2026 (1 July 2026) set out precautionary measures for Indian seafarers operating in the Gulf region, part of a wider pattern of DGMA advisories responding to the elevated ship security situation around the Strait of Hormuz through 2026.

Why This Section Exists

Kochi MMD examiners — Nair, Simon, Rajappan, Senthil, Srivastava — routinely blend IMO convention knowledge with awareness of current domestic DGS instruments. A candidate who can quote IMO resolution numbers fluently but has no idea what DGS Order or Circular is currently live reads as someone who studies the syllabus but not the industry.

DGS Order 01 of 2026 — Vessel Age Norms (21 January 2026)

  • What it does: revises age norms and qualitative parameters for registering vessels under the Indian flag, and for foreign-flag vessels requiring a licence under MS Act 1958 Sections 406/407.
  • Effective: from date of issue (21 Jan 2026), superseding the prior order (DGS Order 06 of 2023).
  • Transition: existing vessels, regardless of age as of 21 Jan 2026, may continue operating until 31 March 2029; a further two-year extension to 2031 may be considered subject to a "Sustainability Indexing of ships" review to be issued later.
  • Exclusions: passenger vessels, FSRU, FPSO, highly specialised vessels (heavy-lift/crane/pipe-laying/cable-laying barges, research vessels, floating docks), dredgers, DP2 diving/well-stimulation vessels, and MODU/drilling units.
  • Operational restriction: general cargo "Other Cargo Ships" of 40+ years and Special Trade Passenger ships of 40+ years may only operate during fair-weather seasons.
  • Why: to modernise the ageing Indian-flag fleet, promote acquisition of quality tonnage, and enhance safety of life at sea — the average age of the Indian fleet has been rising even as the global fleet average declines.

Legal basis — timing trap to know precisely: the Order correctly cites MS Act 1958 Sections 406/407 because it was issued on 21 January 2026 — nearly two months before the Merchant Shipping Act, 2025 (Act No. 24 of 2025) actually came into force on 15 March 2026 (via Central Government notification S.O. 1244(E), 10 March 2026). So at the moment this Order was issued, the 1958 Act was still the governing law — this isn't an error to correct, it's a precise sequencing point worth stating if an examiner probes it: "issued under the outgoing 1958 Act, weeks before the 2025 Act's commencement." DGS itself formally became the Directorate General of Maritime Administration (DGMA) only from the 15 March 2026 commencement date onward.

Merchant Shipping Notice 01 of 2026 — RPSL Manning Agent Criteria (February 2026)

Sets out the criteria for empanelment and retention of Recruitment and Placement Service Licence (RPSL)-holding manning agents. Why relevant: this is the regulatory backbone for how Indian seafarers legally get placed on ships — a CE candidate should know this exists even without memorising every clause.

Naming Convention Trap — DGS Becomes DGMA Mid-Year

Precise sequencing worth stating if pressed: the Directorate General of Shipping (DGS) formally became the Directorate General of Maritime Administration (DGMA) from 15 March 2026, the Merchant Shipping Act 2025's commencement date (the old dgshipping.gov.in domain was fully retired by 31 March 2026, migrating to dgma.gov.in). This means DGS Order 01/2026 and MS Notice 01/2026 above, both issued before 15 March 2026, are correctly named "DGS" instruments — but anything issued after that date is a DGMA instrument, not a DGS one.

DGMA Circular 17 of 2026 — Certificate Extension, Middle East Force Majeure (29 March 2026)

Grants a 3-month extension (or until sign-off, whichever is earlier) for CoC/CoP/STCW certificates for Indian seafarers, in response to force majeure conditions in the Middle East/West Asia region. Why: regional instability disrupted normal certificate renewal logistics (travel, examination access), so the administration built in flexibility rather than let seafarers fall out of compliance through no fault of their own. Issued 29 March — two weeks after the DGMA transition, so this is correctly a DGMA circular.

DGMA Circular 33 of 2026 — Gulf Region Precautionary Measures (1 July 2026)

Sets out precautionary measures for Indian seafarers operating in the Gulf region. Why: ties directly to the elevated security situation around the Strait of Hormuz through 2026 — the same situation referenced at MSC 111, where the Committee adopted a resolution supporting the Secretary-General's efforts to facilitate safe evacuation of seafarers trapped in the Persian Gulf.

Trap Warning

⚠ Trap Warning: These are domestic Indian administrative instruments, not IMO resolutions — don't cite them as if they carry SOLAS/MARPOL force internationally. Also watch the DGS/DGMA naming split above — calling a post-15-March-2026 circular a "DGS Circular" is a small but noticeable dating error. Circular numbers and content should still be verified against the current DGMA circular list before an oral, since these are administrative documents that can be superseded or clarified without the long lead-time of an IMO amendment cycle.
