Explain the procedure for hull paint coating inspection during drydock.
surveyiacs
15-Second Answer: Hull paint inspection covers surface preparation verification (blast cleanliness to ISO 8501-1 Sa2½), environmental monitoring (steel temperature above dew point), dry film thickness gauging against the specified minimum, and a final visual/holiday inspection for defects.
60-Second Answer: Before paint application, the blasted steel surface is checked against ISO 8501-1 for cleanliness grade — typically Sa2½ (near-white metal) for hull coating systems — and the anchor profile is verified against the paint manufacturer's specification. Ambient conditions are monitored throughout application: steel substrate temperature must stay a manufacturer-specified margin above the calculated dew point (commonly cited as around 3°C, though this is a paint-manufacturer specification rather than a fixed IMO figure) to prevent moisture entrapment, and relative humidity is kept within the coating's allowable limits. During and after application, wet film thickness is checked with a notched comb, and once cured, dry film thickness (DFT) is measured with a calibrated gauge across a grid pattern, checked against the coating manufacturer's specified minimum — commonly using a statistical rule requiring the large majority of readings to meet the nominal DFT and none to fall too far below it. The finished coating is visually inspected for defects (runs, sags, pinholes) and, in critical areas, holiday-tested for microscopic voids.

Surface Preparation Verification

After grit-blasting, the surveyor/inspector checks the steel surface against ISO 8501-1 for the specified cleanliness grade — commonly Sa2½ ("very thorough blast cleaning," approximately 95% clean metal with only slight shadowing) for hull antifouling/anticorrosive systems, per the paint manufacturer's technical data sheet. The blast profile (surface roughness/anchor pattern) is also checked against the manufacturer's specification — typically expressed as a percentage of the primer's nominal dry film thickness.

Environmental Condition Monitoring

Coating manufacturers specify a minimum margin between steel substrate temperature and the calculated dew point — commonly cited in industry practice as approximately 3°C — to prevent condensation forming beneath the coating, which causes blistering and premature failure. Relative humidity is also monitored against the manufacturer's stated application limits.

Film Thickness Gauging

  • Wet film thickness (WFT): checked during application using a notched comb gauge to verify immediate coverage.
  • Dry film thickness (DFT): once cured, measured with a calibrated electromagnetic/digital gauge across a grid pattern on the flat bottom, sides, and boot-top. Readings are checked against the coating manufacturer's specified minimum and statistical acceptance criteria (industry practice commonly follows a rule where the large majority of spot readings must meet or exceed the nominal DFT, with a defined tolerance for the remainder — exact figures vary by manufacturer specification, so always confirm against the applicable coating data sheet rather than a fixed universal rule).

Visual and Defect Inspection

The cured coating is visually inspected for sags, runs, pinholes, and overspray. In critical zones (sea chests, high-stress areas), a holiday detector may be used to identify microscopic voids in the coating.

Chief Engineer Relevance

Substandard hull coating application directly affects fuel consumption (via increased hull roughness/friction) and premature fouling — the CE, alongside the attending surveyor, should confirm surface prep, environmental logs, and DFT records are complete and match the specification before the vessel undocks.

CE Oral Tip: Examiner: Not yet assigned. Lead with Sa2½ and the dew-point rule — these are the two most commonly probed specifics. Be clear that exact DFT tolerance percentages and dew-point margins come from the coating manufacturer's data sheet, not a single universal IMO regulation — this is general application/international standard-based, not an IMO/AFS Convention requirement, and don't confuse it with the PSPC (Performance Standard for Protective Coatings), which applies specifically to ballast tanks and double-side-skin spaces, not the external hull.
Deep-Dive: Traps, Failures, Numbers, Regulatory Case, On My Vessel
Trap Questions: "What happens if you paint the hull when the steel temperature is exactly at the dew point?" — Moisture condenses on the steel surface; painting over it traps water beneath the coating, causing blistering, poor adhesion, and premature failure.
Common CE Failures: Confusing wet film thickness with dry film thickness targets, or citing the PSPC (ballast tank coating standard) as governing external hull antifouling paint — it does not; PSPC covers dedicated seawater ballast tanks and double-side-skin spaces of bulk carriers only.
Numbers to Memorise: Sa2½ — standard hull blast-cleanliness grade (ISO 8501-1); dew-point margin and DFT tolerance are manufacturer-specified, not fixed IMO figures — always confirm against the specific coating's technical data sheet.
Regulatory Case: Industry data consistently attributes a majority of premature coating failures to inadequate surface preparation rather than the coating product itself — reinforcing why surface prep verification is the priority check, not an afterthought.
On My Vessel: Surface prep, environmental logs, and DFT survey records maintained in the Drydock Technical Report, cross-checked against the paint manufacturer's specification sheet for the applied system.

Source confidence: High for standards named (ISO 8501-1 Sa2½, WFT/DFT gauging practice, dew-point principle) — verified against ABS Guidance Notes on Application & Inspection of Marine Coating Systems and multiple manufacturer technical data sheets (AkzoNobel/International Paint). Medium for specific numeric tolerances (exact DFT percentage rule, exact dew-point margin in °C) — these vary by manufacturer/product and should be confirmed against the specific coating data sheet rather than quoted as a single fixed regulatory number in the oral exam.

Explain the propeller securing/locking arrangement inspection procedure during drydock.
surveymachineryiacs
15-Second Answer: Propeller securing inspection covers removal of the fairwater cone, checking the propeller nut push-up and locking arrangement (locking plate/keeper or wire locking of bolt heads), and verifying no rotational movement against alignment marks, before repacking and re-securing the cone.
60-Second Answer: "Propeller lashings" in candidate usage generally refers to the propeller nut's securing/locking arrangement, not literal rope lashings. After the fairwater cone (propeller cap) is unbolted and cleaned out, the propeller nut and locking device are exposed. Where a hydraulic (push-up) fitting is used, the nut's push-up length was originally verified by dial gauge against a calculated value based on shaft temperature and the Von Mises stress criterion, and at survey the nut/lock is checked against the reference alignment marks made on the shaft and hub to confirm no rotation has occurred in service. The locking device — a keeper/locking plate, or stainless steel wire locking two bolt heads at a time — is checked for cracking, corrosion, and secure engagement. The cone is then repacked with grease/tallow, its recesses filled with cement for a smooth streamlined finish, and the cap bolts re-secured with their own locking method.

Terminology Note

Candidates sometimes use "propeller lashings" loosely; the actual survey item is the propeller nut securing/locking arrangement — there are no rope lashings on a fitted propeller. Framing the answer around the nut/locking device is the technically correct response.

Access and Preparation

The fairwater cone (propeller cap) is unbolted and removed. Preservation grease/tallow filling the cavity is cleared to expose the shaft nut and locking arrangement.

Nut Push-Up and Rotation Verification

  • Push-up fitting (hydraulic/Pilgrim nut systems): the original push-up length was verified by dial gauge, calculated from shaft temperature and the Von Mises stress criterion for the propeller material, per the maker's design condition.
  • Rotation check: reference alignment marks on the shaft end and propeller nut are checked to confirm no rotational movement has occurred since the last securing.

Locking Device Integrity Check

  • Keeper plates/locking pins: inspected for cracking, thread deformation, and secure attachment of the retaining bolts.
  • Wire locking: where used, stainless steel locking wire is inspected for integrity, corrosion, and correct tensioning — industry practice locks bolt heads in pairs (two bolts at a time) so the wire opposes loosening rotation.

Reassembly

Following survey approval, the fairwater cone is repacked with fresh grease or tallow, recess areas around the securing bolts filled with cement for a smooth streamlined finish, and the cap secured with its own bolt-locking method.

Chief Engineer Relevance

An improperly secured propeller nut is a single-point failure risk that can lead to loss of the propeller in service — the CE should treat this as a safety-critical item during drydock handover review, not a routine cosmetic check.

CE Oral Tip: Examiner: Not yet assigned. If the examiner uses the word "lashings," don't get thrown — reframe immediately to the nut/locking device, describe removing the fairwater cone, checking alignment marks for rotation, and inspecting the keeper plate or locking wire integrity.
Deep-Dive: Traps, Failures, Numbers, Regulatory Case, On My Vessel
Trap Questions: "What secures the propeller onto the tailshaft against rotation in service?" — The interference fit from the calculated push-up (friction grip) is the primary rotational security; the locking device (keeper plate or wire) is a secondary safeguard against any loosening, not the primary retention method.
Common CE Failures: Assuming the fairwater cone itself provides torque retention for the propeller (it does not — it is a hydrodynamic fairing and grease reservoir cover only), or confusing this survey item with rope/cargo lashing procedures.
Numbers to Memorise: Push-up length and pressure are maker/class-specific figures from the shaft withdrawal survey records — not a single universal number; always reference the vessel's own shafting manual and last survey record rather than quoting a generic figure.
Regulatory Case: Loss-of-propeller incidents at sea have been linked to reused keeper-plate bolts without proper locking washers backing out under shaft resonance — illustrating why the locking-device check is treated as safety-critical, not cosmetic. Treat as illustrative of the failure mechanism; verify a specific named incident before citing it in the oral exam.
On My Vessel: Push-up records and locking device inspection results filed in the shaft/tailshaft survey record within the vessel's Class Survey Record folder.

Source confidence: High for the general procedure (fairwater cone removal, push-up/Von Mises basis, alignment mark rotation check, keeper plate/wire locking inspection, cement-filled recesses) — corroborated across multiple independent marine engineering technical references. Medium for any specific numeric values (push-up pressures, exact torque figures) — these are vessel/maker-specific and must be confirmed against the ship's own shafting manual, not quoted as a universal figure.
