Pattern to notice: P&I is the answer for almost everything EXCEPT (a) damage to your OWN ship → H&M, and (b) large-scale oil cargo pollution → CLC/Fund first (P&I sits behind CLC as the insurer funding it).
⚠ Examiner Trap"A container is damaged by your crew's careless crane operation — who pays the cargo owner?" — P&I, not H&M. H&M only responds to damage to the ship itself (and ¾ collision liability under ITC Cl.8).

Art.19(2) "Prejudicial Activities" — memorise this list: Threat/use of force · Weapons exercises · Intelligence gathering/spying · Propaganda · Launching/landing aircraft or military devices · Loading/unloading goods, currency or persons contrary to laws (customs/immigration) · Wilful and serious pollution · Fishing · Research/survey activities · Interfering with communications systems · Any activity not having a direct bearing on passage.
⚠ Examiner TrapThe CE's direct exposure item on this list is wilful and serious pollution — a deliberate OWS bypass in another state's TS doesn't just breach MARPOL, it strips the ship of innocent passage status entirely, opening the door to full coastal-state enforcement.

⚠ Examiner Trap"Heavy weather accidentally damages cargo — is that GA?" — No. GA requires a voluntary, intentional act for common safety. Accidental/inevitable damage is an ordinary loss, not GA. 12
Examiner Route Maps — The Gold Standard
This is almost exactly how Kochi MMD examiners chain questions. Rehearse these sequences out loud.
Route A — Pollution Chain (Nair favourite)
P&I→ CLC→ Fund→ Supplementary Fund→ LLMC

Story: "Who covers the cost? (P&I) → What's the first legal cap? (CLC) → What if that's not enough? (Fund) → And beyond that? (Supplementary Fund) → How does the shipowner limit overall exposure across all this? (LLMC)"

Route B — Collision Chain
Collision→ P&I→ LLMC→ Admiralty→ Ship Arrest

Story: "Two ships collide → who pays the other ship's owner? (P&I, ¼ + excess beyond H&M's ¾) → can liability be capped? (LLMC) → if the other owner doesn't pay, what's their remedy? (Admiralty action) → how is the claim secured? (Ship arrest, then security/release — Diagram 5)."

Route C — Engine Room Fire Chain (CE's home turf)
Engine Room Fire→ General Average→ Salvage→ P&I→ Hague-Visby

Story: "ER fire, you flood a hold to save the ship → cargo damaged for common safety = GA → tug arrives to assist = salvage (LOF/SCOPIC if pollution risk) → who funds GA contributions/salvage award? → P&I → does the carrier still face a cargo claim for the fire damage? → Hague-Visby fire defence (Art.IV 2(b)), but only if seaworthiness was satisfied first."

Drill technique (Huberman/Sung-style): Don't just read these routes — say each one out loud, chip by chip, filling in ONE sentence per arrow from memory before checking. Active recall + retrieval practice on the exact sequence Nair uses beats passive re-reading every time.
