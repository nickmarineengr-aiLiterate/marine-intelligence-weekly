What are a PSA and a PSSA? Give an example of each.
marpolsolasExaminer: Simon
15-Second AnswerAn Area to be Avoided (ATBA) and Special Area are routeing/discharge tools, but the only IMO-recognised term here is PSSA (Particularly Sensitive Sea Area) — an IMO-designated area needing special protection for ecological, socio-economic or scientific reasons, with an Associated Protective Measure attached. 'PSA' is not a formal IMO term. Example: Great Barrier Reef & Torres Strait.
60-Second AnswerA PSSA is an area needing special protection through IMO action because of ecological, socio-economic, or scientific significance and vulnerability to damage by international shipping, governed by IMO Assembly Resolution A.982(24). Three elements must be present: recognised attributes, vulnerability to shipping damage, and an available IMO-adoptable Associated Protective Measure (APM) — routeing measures, ship reporting, or strict MARPOL discharge/equipment application. APMs are always IMO-approved actions, never unilateral coastal-state rules. Examples: Great Barrier Reef/Torres Strait (first PSSA, 1990, extended 2015), Wadden Sea, Galapagos. PSSA designation alone has no direct legal force — the force comes from the specific APM attached to it.

Governing Instrument

IMO Assembly Resolution A.982(24) — Revised Guidelines for the Identification and Designation of Particularly Sensitive Sea Areas.

Definition

  • PSSA: area needing special IMO protection for ecological/socio-economic/scientific reasons, vulnerable to shipping damage.
  • Three-element test: recognised attributes + vulnerability to shipping + an available IMO-adoptable protective measure.

Associated Protective Measures (APMs)

  • Routeing measures — ATBA, precautionary area, recommended routes
  • Compulsory ship reporting systems
  • Strict application of MARPOL discharge/equipment requirements (Special Area status)
  • Vessel Traffic Services (VTS)

Examples

  • Great Barrier Reef & Torres Strait — first PSSA (1990), extended to SW Coral Sea 2015
  • Wadden Sea; Galapagos Archipelago; Papahānaumokuākea (NW Hawaiian Islands)

PSSA vs Special Area

A Special Area is a MARPOL-defined discharge zone (stricter Annex I/II/V standards). A PSSA is broader and can overlap a Special Area, but its protection depends on whatever APM is attached, not automatically on Special Area discharge rules.

Trap Warning — 'PSA' is not an IMO term

Simon's question uses 'PSA' — there is no IMO instrument called a 'Particularly Sensitive Area.' Correct the terminology to PSSA cleanly; do not invent a definition for 'PSA'.

Explain the HNS Convention. Is it in force? Why not? What does the HNS Fund cover?
marpolExaminer: Simon
15-Second AnswerThe HNS Convention (1996, revised by the 2010 Protocol) sets up a two-tier liability/compensation regime — shipowner liability plus an HNS Fund — for hazardous and noxious substance spills, modelled on the CLC/IOPC Fund oil regime. The 1996 Convention never entered into force (insufficient ratifications). The 2010 Protocol's ratification and cargo-reporting thresholds were confirmed met on 29 May 2026, so the 2010 HNS Convention will enter into force 18 months later, on 29 November 2027 — confirmed by IMO's own press release.
60-Second AnswerThe HNS Convention was adopted in 1996 on the CLC/Fund model for oil pollution but never entered into force due to insufficient ratifications. The 2010 Protocol was adopted to fix the practical reporting problems that blocked ratification. Its entry-into-force formula requires ratification by at least 12 States, including 4 States each with ≥2 million GT of shipping, having reported ≥40 million tonnes of contributing cargo in the preceding year — entering into force 18 months after that threshold is met. Belgium, Germany, the Netherlands and Sweden deposited ratifications on 14 April 2026, bringing the total to 12 Contracting States (9 of them above the 2M GT threshold); the 2025 cargo-reporting requirement was then confirmed satisfied, and IMO officially announced on 29 May 2026 that all conditions were met — fixing entry into force at 29 November 2027. Once in force it creates two compensation tiers: shipowner strict liability with compulsory insurance (CLC-style) up to the vessel's tonnage-based limit, plus a second-tier HNS Fund financed by post-incident contributions from cargo receivers, administered with IOPC Funds support, paying above the shipowner's limit. Total combined compensation is capped at 250 million SDR (~USD 360 million) per incident. HNS cargo covers oil, other noxious/hazardous bulk liquids, liquefied gases, liquids with flashpoint ≤60°C, and packaged dangerous goods — roughly 65,000 ships worldwide will need HNS certificates of insurance once the regime is live.

Governing Instrument

1996 HNS Convention, as amended by the 2010 HNS Protocol (together: "the 2010 HNS Convention" once in force).

Why the 1996 Convention never entered into force

  • Insufficient ratifications — the entry-into-force formula was too demanding for practical reporting systems of the time.
  • 2010 Protocol simplified contributing-cargo reporting and refined the "receiver" definition.

Entry-into-Force Formula (2010 Protocol) — Now Satisfied

  • ≥12 ratifying States — met 14 April 2026 (Belgium, Canada, Denmark, Estonia, France, Germany, Netherlands, Norway, Slovakia, South Africa, Sweden, Türkiye)
  • Including 4 States each with ≥2 million GT of shipping — met (9 of the 12 qualify)
  • Those States reporting ≥40 million tonnes of contributing cargo in the preceding year — confirmed met for 2025 reporting year
  • All conditions confirmed satisfied by IMO: 29 May 2026
  • Enters into force 18 months later: 29 November 2027

Compensation Structure

  • Tier 1 — Shipowner liability: strict liability, compulsory insurance, CLC 1992-style mechanism, limited by vessel tonnage.
  • Tier 2 — HNS Fund: funded by post-incident contributions from cargo receivers, pays above the shipowner's limit or where the shipowner is exempt/insolvent — modelled on and administered with IOPC Funds support.
  • Combined cap: 250 million SDR (~USD 360 million) per incident.

What Counts as HNS Cargo

Oil (bulk); other noxious/hazardous bulk liquids; liquefied gases; liquids with flashpoint ≤60°C; packaged dangerous/hazardous/harmful materials (IMDG Code cargo); hazardous solid bulk materials (IMSBC Code).

Key Numbers / Regs

  • 1996 = original Convention, never in force
  • 2010 = revising Protocol
  • 12 States / 4×2M GT / 40M tonnes = entry-into-force formula
  • 29 May 2026 = all conditions confirmed met
  • 29 November 2027 = confirmed in-force date
  • 250 million SDR (~USD 360M) = combined compensation cap

How do you test the high and low-low level alarms on the Inert Gas system?
marpolmachineryExaminer: Rajappan
15-Second AnswerIG alarms are tested by simulating the trip condition at the sensor — for O2 high, introducing certified calibration gas above 8% at the analyser; for pressure/temperature/level alarms, using the transmitter's built-in test/simulate function — and confirming both alarm annunciation and the automatic shutdown of blower/gas delivery to deck, logged in the PMS.
60-Second AnswerThe IG system carries several safety-critical alarms and test method depends on the parameter. O2 content high (8% by volume, SOLAS-mandated max): introduce certified calibration gas above 8% at the analyser sample line, confirming alarm and automatic shutdown of gas delivery to deck. Low pressure after blower (~250mmWG): use the pressure transmitter's simulate function or a controlled bleed of the sensing line, confirming alarm and blower shutdown. High IG temperature (~70°C): simulate via the temperature element's test input. Low deck-seal water level: lower the seal water to switch point or use the level switch test button, confirming alarm and shutdown of gas delivery to deck. All tests are logged in the PMS with before/after readings against the manufacturer's approved test procedure — never physically bypass or jumper an interlock to 'test' it; use the designed test/simulate facility only.

Governing Requirement

SOLAS Ch II-2/Reg 4.5 requires IG systems on tankers above the applicable dwt threshold, with alarm/shutdown functions per the IMO Revised Guidelines for Inert Gas Systems and the manufacturer's approved design.

Key IG Alarms and Test Methods

  • O2 content high (8% by volume): certified calibration gas at analyser sample point → confirm alarm + shutdown of gas delivery to deck.
  • Low pressure after blower (~250mmWG): transmitter simulate/test function or controlled bleed → confirm alarm + blower shutdown.
  • High IG temperature (~70°C): temperature element test input → confirm alarm + blower shutdown.
  • Low deck seal water level: lower seal water to switch point / level switch test button → confirm alarm + shutdown of gas delivery to deck.
  • Power failure: simulate loss of supply to blower/scrubber circuit → confirm alarm + shutdown.

Documentation and Frequency

Per manufacturer's manual and vessel PMS — typically before each cargo operation requiring IG use, plus scheduled functional tests at survey intervals. Log results with setpoint verification against the calibration gas certificate.

Trap Warning — testing vs defeating

Rajappan probes whether candidates confuse using the designed test/simulate facility (safe, documented) with physically bypassing a sensor or jumpering a trip to 'prove' the alarm works — the latter is a PMS/PTW violation and defeats the safety function during the test.

Ship recycling procedure in India?
** ship recyclinghkcrecertificationindian yardsExaminer: Simon
15-Second Answer

Ship recycling in India is regulated under the Recycling of Ships Act, 2019, which implements the Hong Kong International Convention for the Safe and Environmentally Sound Recycling of Ships, 2009 (HKC). The procedure requires an Inventory of Hazardous Materials (IHM), an Ready for Recycling Certificate (RRCO) issued by the administration, and a Ship Recycling Plan (SRP) explicitly developed by an authorized Indian recycling facility.

60-Second Answer

For an international vessel arriving at an Indian recycling yard (primarily in Alang), compliance with the Recycling of Ships Act, 2019 and the Hong Kong Convention (HKC) is mandatory. The Chief Engineer must maintain a fully verified and updated Inventory of Hazardous Materials (IHM). Before recycling, the ship must undergo a final survey to receive an International Ready for Recycling Certificate (IRRC). Upon arrival, the Ship Recycling Facility must prepare a yard-specific Ship Recycling Plan (SRP) approved by the National Authority (Directorate General of Shipping, India). The vessel must secure explicit port clearance, customs clearance, and a "De-contamination/Safe-for-Entry" certificate from the local maritime board prior to beaching or cutting operations.

Governing Instrument

  • International: The Hong Kong International Convention for the Safe and Environmentally Sound Recycling of Ships, 2009 (HKC).
  • National (India): The Recycling of Ships Act, 2019, and the Ship Recycling Regulations issued by the Directorate General of Shipping (DGS).

Definition or Core Concept

Ship recycling is the process of dismantling a vessel at a shore-based facility to recover scrap metal and equipment while safely managing hazardous materials (such as asbestos, PCBs, and heavy metals) to protect human health and the marine environment.

Key Requirements or Procedure

  1. Inventory of Hazardous Materials (IHM): The vessel must possess an updated, Class-approved IHM compiling Part I (Structure and Equipment), Part II (Operationally generated wastes), and Part III (Stores) prior to recycling.
  2. Ready for Recycling Survey: A final survey is conducted by the Flag State administration or Recognized Organization (RO) to verify IHM data and ensure operational wastes are accurately logged.
  3. Issuance of IRRC: The International Ready for Recycling Certificate is issued, valid for no more than 3 months.
  4. Ship Recycling Plan (SRP): The chosen recycling facility in India must generate a bespoke SRP detailing how that specific vessel will be dismantled based on its IHM.
  5. Indian Authorities Approvals:
  6. DG Shipping / Gujarat Maritime Board (GMB): Issues permission for beaching/dismantling.
  7. Customs & Explosives Department: Inspects and issues a "Hot Work / Safe for Entry" certificate, ensuring cargo tanks are thoroughly gas-freed.

Any Distinguishing Trap Point

  • The "Beaching" Contradiction: While the European Union Ship Recycling Regulation (EU-SRR) historically discouraged gravity-beaching method yards, India’s Recycling of Ships Act, 2019 permits beaching provided the yard holds a Statement of Compliance (SoC) for HKC compliance, featuring impermeable concrete floors for heavy blocks to prevent soil contamination.
  • IHM (Part I, II, & III): Documenting hazardous components.
  • IRRC: International Ready for Recycling Certificate.
  • SRP: Facility-specific Ship Recycling Plan.
  • Gas-Free Certificate: Issued by an independent, authorized chemist in India before any cutting begins.

Key Numbers / Regs

  • HKC Regulation 5: Requirements for the Inventory of Hazardous Materials.
  • HKC Regulation 11: Issuance of the International Ready for Recycling Certificate.
  • 3 Months: Maximum validity of the IRRC.
⚖ Regulatory References
  • The Recycling of Ships Act, 2019 (Indian National Legislation)
  • IMO Resolution MEPC.269(68) (2015 Guidelines for the Development of the Inventory of Hazardous Materials)
  • IMO Resolution MEPC.222(64) (2012 Guidelines for the Development of the Ship Recycling Plan)
  • Hong Kong Convention (HKC), 2009 (Entered into force globally on June 26, 2025)

Trap Questions

  • Q: Can an Indian yard begin cutting operations immediately after the vessel beaches?
  • A: No, sir. Even after beaching, no physical dismantling or cutting can commence until the Ship Recycling Facility obtains a formal "Permission to Dismantle" from the Competent Authority (e.g., GMB/DGS) and an authorized chemist issues a fresh, yard-verified Gas-Free / Safe-for-Hot-Work Certificate for all tanks and enclosed spaces.
  • Q: What happens if the IRRC expires while the vessel is waiting at Alang anchorage due to administrative delays?
  • A: The vessel cannot be delivered to the recycling facility with an expired certificate. The Chief Engineer, via the company, must apply to the Flag State or Recognized Organization for an extension or a re-survey under HKC Regulation 14, which permits an extension under specific administrative or force majeure delays for up to an absolute maximum of 3 months, or a short-term certificate must be reissued.

  • Failing to mention that the Ship Recycling Plan (SRP) is tailored specifically to the vessel by the yard, not authored by the ship’s crew.

Numbers to Memorise

  • 90 Days (3 Months): Maximum normal validity of the International Ready for Recycling Certificate.
  • 14: The Regulation under HKC governing the extension of certificates.

  • Alang Plot Accidents (Generic): Multiple historical fatalities occurred in Alang due to premature hot work on double-bottom tanks that still contained localized hydrocarbon vapours or lacked adequate ventilation. This led to the absolute mandate for strict local authority sign-offs and independent gas-freeing certification before physical cutting.

Ship recycling procedure. Cross questions.
** hkcihmrecycling surveycertificate validityExaminer: Simon
15-Second Answer

The international ship recycling procedure under the Hong Kong Convention (HKC) dictates that a ship must maintain an active Inventory of Hazardous Materials (IHM) throughout its operational life. Before being recycled, the vessel must undergo an initial/final survey to secure an International Ready for Recycling Certificate (IRRC), which ensures the ship is mapped safely for the yard's execution of its Ship Recycling Plan (SRP).

60-Second Answer

The standard ship recycling procedure is structured into four distinct phases under the Hong Kong Convention: Inventory development, Planning, Survey, and Certification. The shipowner is responsible for maintaining IHM Part I across the vessel's lifespan. Once the decision to recycle is finalized, the shipowner supplies the chosen HKC-authorized facility with the completed IHM. The yard then drafts a vessel-specific Ship Recycling Plan (SRP). The Flag State or an authorized Class Society then conducts a final survey onboard to verify compliance and issue a 3-month International Ready for Recycling Certificate (IRRC), permitting voyage and delivery to the recycling facility.

Governing Instrument

  • International Maritime Organization (IMO): The Hong Kong International Convention for the Safe and Environmentally Sound Recycling of Ships, 2009 (HKC) (Entered into force June 2025).

Definition or Core Concept

The standard recycling procedure establishes an administrative and technical bridge between the ship's operational history and its final dismantling, ensuring that hazardous wastes are declared transparently so that the recycling yard can mitigate environmental damage and occupational hazards.

Key Requirements or Procedure

``` [ Ship Operational Life: IHM Part I Maintained ] │ ▼ [ Final Decision: IHM Parts II & III Compiled ] │ ▼ [ Yard Selected: Facility Prepares Ship-Specific SRP ] │ ▼ [ Flag/RO Survey: Onboard Verification of IHM & SRP ] │ ▼ [ Certificate Issued: IRRC Granted (Max 3 Months Validity) ] │ ▼ [ Final Voyage & Delivery: Tank Cleaning, Gas-Freeing, Yard Entry ]

```

  1. Continuous Upkeep: Maintain IHM Part I (restricting materials like asbestos, ozone-depleting substances, and PCBs during routine dry docks and procurement).
  2. End-of-Life Compilation: Complete IHM Part II (operationally generated wastes like bilge water, sludge, and tank washings) and Part III (stores and consumer items) once the recycling contract is signed.
  3. Facility Authorization: The chosen facility must possess a verified Document of Authorization for Ship Recycling (DASR) issued by its local national government.
  4. Final Survey: Verification that the IRRC matches the actual physical state of the ship, and that the approved SRP reflects the true hazards present onboard.

Any Distinguishing Trap Point

  • Ownership of the SRP: Candidates frequently state that the Chief Engineer or the Shipowner writes the Ship Recycling Plan. This is a critical failure. The Ship Recycling Facility (the Yard) is solely responsible for writing the SRP, as they own the operational infrastructure and handling procedures. The shipowner only provides the underlying IHM data.
  • Part I: Maintained throughout the ship's active service life.
  • Parts II & III: Compiled specifically when the vessel is preparing for final decommissioning.

Key Numbers / Regs

  • HKC Annex Regulation 5: Requirements for the IHM.
  • HKC Annex Regulation 10: Surveys (Initial, Renewal, Additional, and Final).
  • 3 Months: Maximum validity window for an IRRC.

Chief Engineer Relevance (Onboard Detail)

Prior to the final recycling survey, the Chief Engineer must conduct an inventory audit. You must ensure the refrigerants in the main air conditioning and provisioning plants match the logbooks, trace all fluorescent lighting tubes (mercury content) stored across the ship, and verify that any modifications made to the 2-stroke Main Engine's high-pressure fuel pipes or exhaust insulation blankets are updated in the IHM if they contain specialized binding materials.

Trap Questions

  • Q: What is the fundamental difference between IHM Part I, Part II, and Part III?
  • A: Sir, Part I lists hazardous materials inherent in the ship’s structure and equipment (e.g., paint coatings, insulation) and is maintained continuously. Part II lists operationally generated wastes (e.g., sludge, oily bilge water). Part III lists stores and consumer consumables present at the time of the decision to recycle. Parts II and III are only developed at the end of the vessel's life.
  • Q: Can a ship be recycled if it holds an active IHM but the yard does not possess a DASR?
  • A: No, sir. Under the Hong Kong Convention, a ship can only be sent to a recycling facility that holds a valid Document of Authorization for Ship Recycling (DASR) issued by its respective competent national authority, proving the yard operates under clean environmental guidelines.

Common CE Failures

  • Stating that the ship’s crew develops the SRP.
  • Failing to explain that the IRRC is a short-term certificate limited to 3 months to facilitate the final delivery voyage.
  • Confusing the IHM with a simple material log, omitting its statutory nature under HKC.

Numbers to Memorise

  • 3 Months: The hard ceiling for an IRRC validity period.
  • 3 Parts: The divisions of the Inventory of Hazardous Materials (IHM).

Casualty Link

  • No specific marine casualty link. This is an administrative and socio-environmental framework established to eliminate historical open-beach dismantling disasters, which exposed laborers to chronic toxic smoke inhalation and untreated heavy metal runoffs into tidal coastal waters.

MARPOL Annex II — contents and certificates required?
** marpol annex iinlsp&a manualcargo record bookExaminer: Nair
15-Second Answer

MARPOL Annex II regulates the control of pollution by Noxious Liquid Substances (NLS) carried in bulk. It classifies liquid chemical substances into four distinct hazard categories (X, Y, Z, and OS) and mandates that any vessel carrying these substances must hold an International Pollution Prevention Certificate for the Carriage of Noxious Liquid Substances in Bulk (NLS Certificate) along with an approved Procedures and Arrangements (P&A) Manual.

60-Second Answer

MARPOL Annex II details strict structural and operational controls for ships carrying Noxious Liquid Substances in bulk to prevent marine pollution. Liquid chemical cargoes are categorized based on their environmental and human health risks: Category X (major hazard, strict mandatory prewash), Category Y (hazard), Category Z (minor hazard), and Other Substances (OS) (non-hazardous). To legally operate, a vessel must carry an International NLS Certificate (or an International Certificate of Fitness for Chemical Tankers under the IBC/BCH Code), an approved P&A Manual, and maintain a Cargo Record Book (CRB) documenting all cargo transfers, tank cleanings, ballasting, and slop discharges.

Governing Instrument

  • International Maritime Organization: MARPOL Annex II (Regulations for the Control of Pollution by Noxious Liquid Substances in Bulk).

Definition or Core Concept

Annex II governs any liquid chemical cargo carried in bulk that, if discharged into the sea from tank cleaning or deballasting operations, presents a hazard to either marine resources or human health. It establishes strict stripping, prewashing, and underwater discharge limits.

Key Requirements or Procedure

  • NLS Cargo Categorization: | Category | Hazard Level | Discharge / Operational Requirement | | --- | --- | --- | | Category X | Major Hazard | Mandatory tank prewash in port; residues discharged to a shore reception facility. | | Category Y | Moderate Hazard | Limited discharge allowed subject to strict stripping and underwater discharge limits. | | Category Z | Minor Hazard | Less stringent limits, but still requires underwater discharge. | | OS (Other) | Non-Hazardous | Outside the strict scope of Annex II control limits. |

  • Operational Discharge Criteria: Any permissible discharge of NLS residues must happen below the waterline through the dedicated underwater discharge outlet, while the ship is proceeding en route at a minimum speed of 7 knots (for self-propelled vessels), in water depths of at least 25 meters, and at a minimum distance of 12 nautical miles from the nearest land.

Any Distinguishing Trap Point

  • The Container vs. Bulk Trap: As a container ship Chief Engineer, Examiner Nair may ask if MARPOL Annex II applies to packaged chemical tanks or tank-containers (ISO tanks) on your vessel. It does not. Packaged hazardous goods are strictly regulated under MARPOL Annex III and the IMDG Code. Annex II applies only to liquids carried in built-in or deep tanks in bulk.

Key Certificates & Documents Required

  1. International NLS Certificate: Valid for a maximum of 5 years, subject to annual and intermediate surveys.
  2. Procedures and Arrangements (P&A) Manual: A ship-specific, Class-approved instruction manual detailing stripping systems, tank washing arrangements, and slop handling procedures.
  3. Cargo Record Book (CRB): Form generalized for logging operations like loading, unloading, internal transfers, mandatory prewashes, cleaning, and discharges.

Key Numbers / Regs

  • Regulation 6: Categorization of Noxious Liquid Substances.
  • Regulation 13: Control of discharges of residues of Noxious Liquid Substances.
  • 12 Nautical Miles / 7 Knots / 25 Meters Depth: The physical conditions required for permissible en-route NLS residue discharges.
⚖ Regulatory References
  • MARPOL Annex II, Regulation 6 (NLS Categorization)
  • MARPOL Annex II, Regulation 13 (Discharge Criteria)
  • The IBC Code (International Bulk Chemical Code)
  • The BCH Code (Bulk Chemical Code for older vessels)

Deep-Dive — CE Relevance, Traps, Failures, Numbers, Casualty, Examiner Chain, On My Vessel

Chief Engineer Relevance (Onboard Detail)

If serving on a vessel with an NLS notation or deep tanks designed for bulk liquid trade, the Chief Engineer must ensure the strict maintenance of the Efficient Stripping System. Under MARPOL Annex II, modern chemical tankers must be capable of stripping tanks down to a maximum residual volume of 75 liters per tank. You must ensure that the cargo pump clearances, nitrogen purging systems (for stripping lines), and the integrity of the underwater discharge piping are maintained without leaks to avoid major environmental non-compliance issues.

Trap Questions

  • Q: If a tank container (ISO tank) carrying a Category X chemical leaks into the container hold bilge on a container ship, do you record the bilge pumping in the Cargo Record Book under Annex II?
  • A: No, sir. Because the chemical was carried as packaged cargo and not in bulk, this situation is governed under MARPOL Annex III and the IMDG Code. The subsequent pumping of the contaminated bilge water must be managed as an emergency accidental discharge or retained for shore reception, and logged in Oil Record Book Part I (or a specialized bridge log) as a hazardous substance incident, not in an Annex II Cargo Record Book.
  • Q: What is the function of the P&A Manual?
  • A: Sir, the Procedures and Arrangements Manual is a mandatory, Class-approved document that provides clear operational instructions to the ship's crew regarding the exact methods for tank cleaning, slop handling, stripping, and ballasting without violating MARPOL Annex II discharge limits.

Common CE Failures

  • Confusing the carriage of chemicals in bulk (Annex II) with the carriage of chemicals in packaged form/freight containers (Annex III).
  • Forgetting the three environmental parameters required for discharging permitted residues (7 knots, 12 nautical miles, 25 meters water depth).
  • Failing to mention the Cargo Record Book as a mandatory statutory log.

Numbers to Memorise

  • 75 Liters: Maximum residual cargo quantity allowed in a tank after stripping for modern ships built after January 1, 2007.
  • 4 Categories: X, Y, Z, and OS.
  • 7 Knots: Minimum speed required during an underwater discharge.

Casualty Link

  • Bow Mariner Explosion (2004): While largely an operational safety and tank cleaning disaster (sampling residue vapours caught fire), it underscored the severe hazards of residual NLS cargo handling and tank washing procedures, reinforcing the international mandate for strict adherence to the P&A Manual and gas-free protocols.

On My Vessel

On a Maersk A/S large container vessel, the 2-stroke Main Engine uses large quantities of lube oils, but no bulk NLS cargo is handled. The closest technical interface with chemical safety involves the management of water treatment chemicals for the high-pressure auxiliary boilers and central cooling water systems, alongside the transport of dangerous goods in container slots. The Chief Engineer must ensure the crew treats all chemical container leakages as hazardous materials incidents under Annex III/IMDG guidelines, rather than applying the bulk tank cleaning procedures of Annex II.
