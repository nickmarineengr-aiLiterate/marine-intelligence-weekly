1
MS Act 2025 — why was it enacted? What are the key differences from the Merchant Shipping Act 1958?
ISM/STCW

Why the New Act Was Needed

The Merchant Shipping Act 1958 had grown to over 561 sections through decades of piecemeal amendment and had become fragmented, difficult to cross-reference, and inconsistent with contemporary IMO/ILO instruments. It lacked robust primary legislative backing for the ISM Code, MLC 2006, and STCW Manila 2010 amendments. The 2025 Act was enacted to provide a unified, contemporary legal framework aligning Indian statutory law with India's international obligations as an IMO member state — improving compliance, ease of doing business, and seafarer welfare.

Key Differences from MS Act 1958

  • Structure: 561 sections condensed to 325 clauses across 16 Parts
  • MLC 2006 integration: seafarers' welfare, rest hours, and repatriation rights are now integrated statutory provisions rather than fragmented DGS circulars
  • Statutory safety codes: ISM and ISPS Codes are explicitly embedded into the Act's framework, alongside STCW alignment
  • Digital transformation: legally validates electronic records, digital ship registration, and e-certificates; risk-based PSC inspections
  • Ownership liberalisation: broadens eligible owners of Indian-flagged vessels to include NRIs, OCIs, and a wider range of corporate structures; permits bareboat charter-cum-demise registration
  • Administration: the Director General of Shipping role is redefined and expanded as the Director General of Maritime Administration (DGMA)
  • Transparency: mandatory disclosure of shipping charges to exporters/importers/consignors/consignees

CE Relevance

As Chief Engineer, the MS Act 2025 affects statutory record-keeping (digital Oil Record Books, e-certificates), MLC-aligned rest-hour compliance for engine department staff, and the practical reality that DGS-flagged vessels now report to DGMA. Revised penal provisions apply to safety and pollution non-compliance.

15-Second AnswerThe MS Act 2025 replaces the fragmented, 561-section 1958 Act, modernising Indian maritime law to align fully with IMO and ILO conventions. Key differences: statutory integration of MLC 2006, ISM/ISPS/STCW alignment, digital documentation, liberalised vessel ownership, and the DGS role redefined as DGMA — all condensed into 325 clauses across 16 Parts.
60-Second AnswerThe 1958 Act had grown unwieldy through decades of amendment and lacked cohesive statutory backing for modern conventions. The 2025 Act — Presidential assent 18 August 2025, in force from 15 March 2026 — restructures 561 sections into 325 clauses across 16 Parts. It brings the ISM Code, ISPS Code, and STCW Manila amendments into the primary legislation, fully integrates MLC 2006 seafarer welfare provisions, digitises certification and registration, liberalises vessel ownership to NRIs/OCIs/companies, mandates transparency in shipping charges, and redesignates the Director General of Shipping as the Director General of Maritime Administration.
⚠ Examiner TrapQ: Is the MS Act 2025 in force yet? A: Yes — since 15 March 2026 (Gazette notification S.O. 1244(E)). It is not "pending" or "partially notified" as of mid-2026; the Act is fully operative and Rules under it (e.g. Limitation of Liability Rules, Coastal Waters Entry Rules) are being progressively notified.

Types of ship layup — explain cold layup, warm layup, and hot layup. Key differences and CE actions.
SurveyMachinery

Hot Layup

The vessel is in a state of full operational readiness, waiting for commercial orders. The full crew is retained, and all machinery systems remain running or on standby. It is the most expensive layup state but allows reactivation and sailing within hours to a few days.

Warm Layup

Used when a vessel is expected to be idle for several months (up to ~12). Crew is reduced to safe manning levels. Essential systems (one generator, boilers) are kept running for basic services and maintenance. The main engine is preserved but rotated periodically on turning gear to prevent seizure. Reactivation typically takes 1–3 weeks; class notification is required.

Cold Layup

A long-term (>12 months) cost-saving measure where the vessel is completely shut down. Systems are secured, drained, and blanked. Machinery spaces are protected by dehumidifiers; sacrificial anodes or impressed current systems are monitored for hull protection. Only a skeleton crew or watchmen remain onboard. Reactivation takes months and requires a full suite of class surveys, including a mandatory Reactivation Survey.

CE Responsibilities During Layup

The CE oversees machinery preservation — draining cooling water, applying corrosion inhibitors, rotating machinery periodically, and conducting insulation resistance (megger) tests on electrical systems. During warm or hot layups, critical spares must be stored in controlled environments and underwater hull inspections scheduled.

15-Second AnswerLayups are commercial decisions to idle a ship. Hot layup is short-period, full crew, active machinery. Warm layup has reduced crew with essential systems running. Cold layup is long-term storage — dead ship, skeleton crew, requiring comprehensive class surveys to reactivate.
60-Second AnswerLayups are categorised by duration, machinery readiness, and manning. Hot layup is a short-term commercial wait (up to 1–3 months) where the vessel stays fully operational with a full crew. Warm layup is for medium-term idling (up to 12 months) — manning is reduced, main engine moth-balled but turned periodically, essential auxiliary systems active for routine preservation. Cold layup is for extended periods over 12 months — the ship is completely shut down, often with only a skeleton crew, machinery drained and dehumidified, and reactivation requires significant time plus mandatory class surveys.
⚠ Examiner TrapQ: Does cold layup require class notification? What surveys are still mandatory? A: Yes — class must be notified to switch the vessel to "laid-up" status. Annual surveys may be deferred, but an extensive Reactivation Survey is mandatory before sailing.

Numbers to Memorise

Key distinguisher — reactivation time: Hot = days · Warm = weeks · Cold = months. Cold layup requires mandatory class notification and Reactivation Survey.
⚓ CE Failure LinkFailing to properly dose inhibitors or maintain dehumidification during cold layup leads to catastrophic internal corrosion of the main engine and turbochargers, driving up reactivation cost dramatically.

Taking over a vessel in cold layup — as CE, how will you proceed? Step by step.
SurveyMachinery

Pre-Joining (Desk Review)

Before physical intervention, review all certificates, surveys, and company instructions — condition of class, layup preservation logs, the outgoing CE's handover notes, overdue surveys, and pending requisitions for critical spares.

Day 1 — Physical Inspection

Focus on safety and vessel integrity: a visual walkthrough of mooring conditions, hull integrity, and accommodation spaces. Verify bilges are dry, fire detection/suppression systems are intact, and the emergency generator is ready.

Machinery Space — Systematic Check

Inspect the main engine's preservation state for internal corrosion. Check fuel and lube oil soundings against logged figures. Electrical staff must take megger readings (insulation resistance tests) of all switchboards and critical motors before any power is applied. Purifiers, turbochargers, and steering gear must be manually verified for freedom of movement.

Staged Reactivation

Step-by-step to prevent damage: begin with auxiliary systems — cooling water, control air, power generation. The main engine requires meticulous un-preservation — removing blanks, draining inhibitors, flushing the lube oil system, prolonged turning on turning gear. Finally, a Class Surveyor attends the reactivation survey, followed by basin trials and a sea trial before the vessel is declared commercially ready.

CE Handover Documentation

Sign a combined takeover report with the outgoing CE (if present) or the preservation team; establish the joining engineering staff's current competence; ensure precision instruments and passwords are handed over.

Extra Sub-Block — Takeover Checklist

  • Verify class status and outstanding conditions
  • Inspect FO/DO/LO soundings vs. logs
  • Megger-test all critical electrical systems
  • Inspect bilges, overboard valves, hull integrity
  • Review SMS and schedule the Reactivation Survey
15-Second AnswerTaking over a cold-laid vessel requires a three-phase approach: comprehensive desk review of preservation logs and class status, systematic physical inspection of hull integrity and machinery condition, and staged reactivation strictly involving a Class Surveyor and thorough sea trials.
60-Second AnswerI start with a desk review of the previous CE's handover notes, layup logs, class status, and outstanding defects. Day 1 involves a physical walkthrough for hull integrity, bilge levels, and fire/safety system status. The machinery check is systematic: main engine preservation state, turning gear freedom, electrical insulation. Reactivation is staged — restoring shore/emergency power, un-blanking systems, flushing pipelines, running auxiliaries before the main engine. Crucially, a Class Surveyor must attend the reactivation survey before any sea trials.
⚠ Examiner TrapQ: What if the class certificate has expired during layup — can you sail? A: No. If certificates have lapsed, the vessel cannot trade. The Class Surveyor must perform a full renewal or reactivation survey to reinstate the certificates before the vessel can commence its voyage.

Numbers to Memorise

Statutory certificates (IOPP, SMC) may lose validity during extended cold layup, requiring full revalidation audits under MARPOL and ISM. Always mention megger testing before energising a dead ship — condensation during cold layup severely degrades electrical insulation.
⚓ Casualty LinkEngine room fires have occurred during reactivation because electrical switchboards were energised without prior baking or megger testing, causing short circuits from absorbed moisture.
