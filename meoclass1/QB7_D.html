CO2 sequestration — what is it and how does it relate to onboard carbon capture?
MACHINERY
15-Second Answer

CO2 sequestration is the long-term, permanent storage of captured carbon dioxide in deep geological formations, oceans, or via mineral conversion. Onboard carbon capture represents the extraction step from the exhaust gas, whereas sequestration is the final downstream disposal step. Together, they complete the Carbon Capture and Sequestration (CCS) lifecycle.

60-Second Answer

The complete CCS chain consists of four primary stages:

  • Capture: Onboard extraction of CO2 from main or auxiliary engine exhaust gases.
  • Compression & Liquefaction: Cooling and pressurizing the gas into liquid CO2 (LCO2) for storage.
  • Transport: Offloading the liquefied cargo at port reception facilities.
  • Sequestration: Permanent storage via deep geological injection (depleted oil/gas fields, saline aquifers, or basalt formations), or mineral carbonation where CO2 is reacted with alkaline minerals to form stable solid carbonates.

Onboard CCS remains pre-commercial, and a significant regulatory gap exists: CO2 offloaded at port is not currently credited or recognized within the IMO Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) framework, pending an official MEPC mechanism.

Deep Geological Sequestration Mechanisms

Permanent storage relies on high-integrity geological structures to isolate the captured greenhouse gas from the atmosphere:

  • Depleted Oil and Gas Reservoirs: Highly characterized structures with proven caprock seals that previously contained hydrocarbons for millions of years.
  • Deep Saline Aquifers: Large, permeable rock formations filled with saltwater, offering the greatest theoretical storage capacity globally.
  • Basalt Formations: Rapidly reacts with injected LCO2 through mineral carbonation, permanently trapping the carbon as solid carbonate rock (e.g., calcite) within a few years, minimizing leakage risks.

```

Regulatory Framework & Policy Gaps

Under current MARPOL Annex VI regulations, the IMO CII calculation operates strictly on a Tank-to-Wake (TtW) basis. Because it only accounts for carbon mass emitted directly out of the funnel, any carbon captured and retained in liquid storage tanks onboard does not lower the vessel's calculated carbon intensity.

MEPC 80 and MEPC 81 sessions initiated intense debates regarding how to verify the custody chain of offloaded LCO2 to ensure it is permanently sequestered rather than vented down the line. A formal global accounting framework is required before CCS can directly upgrade a vessel's CII rating.

Chief Engineer's Operational Responsibilities

If a CCS system is retrofitted or installed onboard, the Chief Engineer assumes absolute statutory and operational accountability under the International Safety Management (ISM) Code:

  • System Operations: Overseeing the safety parameters of high-capacity blower systems, absorption columns, and multi-stage cryogenic compressors.
  • Inventory Management: Monitoring LCO2 tank levels, pressures, and temperatures to avoid over-pressurization or boil-off venting.
  • Discharge Logistics: Coordinating the safe transfer and document logging of liquefied carbon to dedicated shore facilities, ensuring clear entries in the engine room logbook and the ship's safety management system.

Trap Warning

Do not confuse Carbon Capture and Sequestration (CCS) with Carbon Capture, Utilization, and Storage (CCUS). In an oral exam, Simon may ask if they are interchangeable. CCS guarantees permanent disposal isolated from the biosphere. CCUS routes a portion of the captured CO2 into commercial applications (e.g., synthetic fuel production, beverage carbonation, enhanced oil recovery). If utilized in a short-cycle product, that CO2 is eventually re-released into the atmosphere, meaning it does not fulfill the criteria for a permanent carbon sink.

Carbon capture at sea — latest developments. Is onboard carbon capture the same as a scrubber?
MACHINERY
15-Second Answer

No, they are fundamentally different. A SOx scrubber removes sulfur oxides using alkaline washwater without altering CO2. An onboard carbon capture system uses chemical solvents, like amines, to isolate and compress CO2 into a liquid for storage. While both treat exhaust gases, their chemistry, engineering, and regulatory purposes are entirely separate.

60-Second Answer

A SOx scrubber uses seawater or caustic soda to chemically neutralize sulfur dioxide (SO2), producing harmless sulfates that are discharged or stored as sludge. The carbon dioxide passes through completely unaffected.

Conversely, an onboard Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS) system routes the flue gas through an absorption column where a chemical solvent (typically an amine) absorbs the CO2. The solvent is then heated in a stripper column to release pure CO2 gas, which is compressed to a liquid at high pressure (around 50 bar) and stored in insulated structural tanks.

Latest 2024–2025 pilot installations include joint ventures like Eidesvik/Wärtsilä and Mitsubishi/K-Line. However, commercial viability is limited by a 10–15% energy penalty, substantial onboard space requirements, and a total absence of port reception facilities for LCO2 offloading.

Technical Details of the Onboard Capture Process

The prevailing technology utilized in latest maritime trials is post-combustion chemical absorption:

  1. Flue Gas Cooling: Exhaust gas from the engine must be pre-treated and cooled below 50°C to optimize absorption chemistry.
  2. The Absorption Column: The cooled gas contacts a counter-current flow of lean amine solvent. The CO2 reacts exothermically to form stable chemical carbamates.
  3. The Stripper / Regenerator Column: The CO2-rich solvent is pumped to a separate column and subjected to intense thermal energy via steam or thermal oil heaters (110°C to 120°C). This breaks the chemical bonds, liberating pure gaseous CO2.
  4. Liquefaction & Storage: The pure CO2 is cooled and compressed using multi-stage compressors to transform it into a high-density liquid state, typically at pressures ranging between 15 to 50 bar, and moved into insulated pressure vessels.

``` [Exhaust Gas] ➔ [Cooling Stage] ➔ [Absorber (Lean Amine)] ➔ [Stripper (Heat Applied)] ➔ [CO2 Gas Out] ➔ [Liquefaction Plant] ➔ [LCO2 Storage]

```

Key Engineering and Commercial Challenges

  • Energy Penalty: The thermal energy required to boil and regenerate the amine solvent represents a major parasitic load. This demands massive steam generation, which increases total fuel consumption.
  • Weight and Space Penalties: The physical footprint of absorption columns, stripping towers, liquefaction skids, and heavy structural pressure tanks reduces cargo deadweight capacity.
  • Well-to-Wake Accounting & Infrastructure Gaps: MEPC 82 and 83 continue to deliberate on how to prevent double-counting of carbon. Furthermore, commercial ports currently lack the specialized infrastructure required to receive, store, or safely process tons of un-bunkered LCO2. Commercial viability is not projected until at least 2030.

Trap Warning

Do not state that a SOx scrubber can be easily converted into a CO2 capture system. Examiners will catch this. While a SOx scrubber is a vital pre-requisite (because sulfur oxides degrade amine solvents almost instantly), the chemical absorption, thermal stripping, and high-pressure cryogenic liquefaction required for CO2 capture demand an entirely separate asset footprint.

Amine separation setup in onboard carbon capture — how does it work?
MACHINERY
15-Second Answer

Amine separation uses a two-column loop. Cooled exhaust gas enters an absorber column where it reacts with an amine solvent (like Monoethanolamine) to capture the CO2. The CO2-rich solvent then pumps to a stripper column heated to 120°C via steam, breaking the chemical bond to release pure CO2 gas. The regenerated lean amine is cooled and recycled, while the gaseous CO2 is compressed into a liquid for storage.

60-Second Answer

The amine separation plant operates using a continuous two-column cycle:

  • Absorber Column: Cooled, pre-scrubbed exhaust gas enters from the bottom, traveling upward against a falling mist of lean amine solvent (such as Monoethanolamine - MEA). An exothermic chemical reaction binds the CO2 to the solvent. Crucially, the exhaust must be pre-scrubbed because any sulfur dioxide (SO2) will permanently degrade the amine.
  • Stripper / Regenerator Column: The CO2-rich amine passes through a lean/rich heat exchanger into the stripper, where it is heated by steam to approximately 110°C to 120°C. This thermal energy breaks the chemical bonds, releasing pure CO2 gas from the top of the column.
  • Liquefaction & Compression: The liberated CO2 gas is routed to a multi-stage compressor plant, liquefied at pressures between 50 to 80 bar, and stored in insulated tanks. The hot, lean amine at the bottom of the stripper is cooled down and pumped back to the absorber to repeat the process.

Detailed Process Chemistry and Mechanics

The underlying process relies on reversible chemical reactions between carbon dioxide and alkanolamines. The most common solvent used in marine testing is Monoethanolamine (MEA), chosen for its fast reaction kinetics and high initial carrying capacity.

CO2 + 2R-NH2 leftharpoons R-NH3+ + R-NH-COO-

The forward reaction occurs in the cool absorber column (40°C to 50°C), forming water-soluble carbamates. The reverse reaction is driven in the stripper column by introducing intense thermal energy (110°C to 120°C), breaking down the carbamate ions to release pure gaseous CO2.

Amine Degradation and Flue Gas Pre-Treatment

Amines are highly sensitive to contamination. Sulfur dioxide (SO2) reacts irreversibly with alkanolamines to form Heat-Stable Salts (HSS). These salts cannot be thermally broken down in the marine stripper column, leading to continuous loss of active solvent, severe chemical foaming, and aggressive internal corrosion.

Consequently, a high-efficiency SOx scrubber and a chemical wash tower must be installed upstream of the amine plant to reduce SO2 concentrations below 1–5 ppm before the exhaust gas contacts the solvent.

Chief Engineer's Safety & Environmental Obligations

Amine solvents are hazardous chemicals subject to strict safety regulations:

  • COSHH and Personal Protection: Crew members must wear advanced personal protective equipment (chemical-resistant suits, viton gloves, and full-face respirators) during any system sampling or chemical dosing.
  • Toxicity and Off-Gassing: Amines degrade into volatile nitrosamines and nitramines, which are classified as potent carcinogens. Mechanical room ventilation and gas detection arrays must be continuously maintained.
  • Bilge and Environmental Control: Amine leaks cannot be pumped into standard engine room bilges or processed via a standard 15-ppm oily water separator. Any chemical containment failure must be routed to a dedicated chemical holding tank for mandatory shore disposal.

Trap Warning

If the examiner asks how to manage an increase in absorber column temperature, do not suggest increasing the exhaust gas flow rate. High temperatures in the absorber degrade the chemical binding capacity, causing "CO2 breakthrough" where uncaptured carbon exits the stack. The correct response is to check the efficiency of the upstream flue gas cooler and increase the cooling water flow to keep the absorber stage below 50°C.

FLL (Fuel Lifecycle LCA) / Well to Tank vs Tank to Wake — explain and why does it matter for GHG accounting?
MACHINERY
15-Second Answer

Fuel Lifecycle LCA (FLL) evaluates a fuel's total environmental footprint across two phases: Well-to-Tank (upstream production, refining, and logistics) and Tank-to-Wake (onboard combustion). It is critical because evaluating emissions purely at the funnel masks the high upstream greenhouse gas footprints of fuels like fossil LNG or gray hydrogen.

60-Second Answer

Fuel Lifecycle LCA splits greenhouse gas accounting into two core metrics:

  • Well-to-Tank (WtT): Covers all emissions from feedstock extraction, refining, processing, and transport to the ship's manifold.
  • Tank-to-Wake (TtW): Covers emissions generated solely during onboard fuel combustion or utilization.
  • Well-to-Wake (WtW): The sum of WtT + TtW, representing the true total lifecycle impact.

This distinction is critical for modern regulations. For instance, the IMO Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) currently looks only at Tank-to-Wake emissions. Under this framework, LNG appears highly favorable because it emits less CO2 per unit of energy than heavy fuel oil. However, regional frameworks like FuelEU Maritime enforce a Well-to-Wake standard. When upstream methane leakage and engine methane slip are factored into a WtW assessment, LNG's net climate benefit drops significantly, creating policy misalignments that Chief Engineers must navigate.

The Methane Slip & Alternative Fuel Paradox

Evaluating fuels strictly through a Tank-to-Wake lens creates a distorted view of their actual environmental impact:

  • Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG): At the funnel (TtW), LNG yields an immediate 20% to 25% reduction in CO2 mass compared to conventional HFO. However, when upstream venting (WtT) and engine methane slip (TtW) are added, the high Global Warming Potential of unburnt methane reduces these lifecycle gains.
  • Ammonia (NH3): Burns with zero CO2 output at the funnel, yielding an exceptional TtW score. However, if that ammonia is "gray" (manufactured via steam methane reforming of natural gas without carbon capture), its WtT emissions are enormous, making its true WtW footprint worse than conventional marine gas oil.
  • Hydrogen (H2): Zero emissions at utilization, but extremely energy-intensive to compress or liquefy during the WtT phase, requiring clean energy sources to deliver any real net reduction in greenhouse gases.

Regulatory Discrepancies and Policy Tension

International shipping must balance conflicting regulatory compliance frameworks:

  • IMO CII Framework: Operates on a TtW basis (MARPOL Annex VI Regulation 28). It measures carbon emissions directly leaving the stack, treating all alternative fossil pathways equally based on basic fuel consumption data.
  • FuelEU Maritime (In Force 2025): Enforces strict limits on the total WtW greenhouse gas intensity of energy used onboard. It penalizes fuels with high upstream footprints, forcing operators to look beyond simple combustion profiles.
  • IMO Green Fuel index (GFI): Developed as part of mid-term measures to align global shipping with a full WtW lifecycle model, closing the gap between international and regional rules.

Chief Engineer's Operational Oversight

While the Chief Engineer does not manage production facilities ashore, they are directly accountable for the data that drives lifecycle compliance calculations:

  • Bunker Documentation Verification: Ensuring that the Bunker Delivery Note (BDN) and accompanying sustainability certificates accurately reflect the fuel's origin and regulatory emission factors.
  • Dual-Fuel Engine Optimization: Actively managing fuel injection, pilot fuel ratios, and operating loads to minimize combustion-related slip, protecting the ship's reported TtW footprint.
  • Data Log Integrity: Maintaining rigorous records of fuel usage by type in the Ship Energy Efficiency Management Plan (SEEMP Part 2) and Data Collection System (DCS) logs to prevent compliance penalties.

Trap Warning

Do not tell an examiner that a biofuel or e-fuel has a Tank-to-Wake emission factor of zero just because it is renewable. When burned in a diesel engine, a biofuel releases roughly the same mass of CO2 at the funnel as conventional diesel. Its carbon neutrality is achieved during the Well-to-Tank phase, where the feedstock absorbs carbon during growth, resulting in a net-zero or near-zero Well-to-Wake score.

MACHINERY
15-Second Answer

60-Second Answer

The Green Fuel Index (GFI) functions as a key component of the IMO's mid-term market-based measures. It calculates compliance by dividing total Well-to-Wake greenhouse gas emissions—including carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4), and nitrous oxide (N2O) multiplied by their respective global warming potentials—by the total energy content of the fuel in megajoules (MJ).

GFI = Total WtW Emissions (gCO2eq) / Total Energy Content (MJ)

The reference baseline is the 2008 fleet average of 93.3 gCO2eq/MJ. Mathematical Architecture of the GFI

The calculation evaluates all three major marine greenhouse gases, applying weighted factors based on their climate impact over a specific timeframe:

GHG Intensity = Σ[ Mass of Fuel × ( Emission Factor(CO2) + (EFCH4 × GWPCH4) + (EFN2O × GWPN2O) ) ] / [ Mass of Fuel × Lower Heating Value (LHV) ]

A critical feature of this maritime accounting model is the adoption of the 20-year Global Warming Potential (GWP20) for methane, which rates its climate impact at 82.5. This penalizes short-lived climate pollutants far more severely than traditional 100-year metrics, heavily affecting engines with high methane slip profiles.

Types of ammonia fuels — grey, black, green ammonia; production, issues and safety concerns?
MACHINERY
15-Second Answer

Ammonia fuels are classified by production method: Gray/Black are derived from fossil fuels without carbon capture; Blue incorporates carbon capture; Green is synthesized using renewable electricity and water electrolysis. While ammonia contains zero carbon, its use as a marine fuel is limited by extreme toxicity, material corrosion, and high nitrous oxide (N2O) emissions during combustion.

60-Second Answer

Ammonia fuel pathways are defined by their production taxonomy:

  • Black/Brown: Produced via coal gasification, carrying a heavy upstream carbon footprint.
  • Grey: Produced via Steam Methane Reforming (SMR) of natural gas without carbon capture.

Primary marine safety concerns include its extreme toxicity to humans—with a Threshold Limit Value (TLV-TWA) of just 25 ppm—and its tendency to cause stress corrosion cracking in copper, zinc, and high-strength steels. Methane sources in shipping — where does methane slip occur and why is it significant?
MACHINERY
15-Second Answer

Methane slip is the escape of unburnt natural gas (CH4) through the engine exhaust during combustion. It occurs primarily in medium-speed, 4-stroke Otto cycle dual-fuel engines. It is significant because methane's high global warming potential (GWP20 = 82.5) can completely erase the carbon advantages of switching to LNG.

60-Second Answer

Methane emissions from shipping originate from several distinct sources:

  • Engine Methane Slip: Unburnt gas bypassing combustion, primarily in 4-stroke, low-pressure Otto cycle dual-fuel auxiliary or propulsion engines (averaging 3% to 4%). Low-pressure 2-stroke engines exhibit much lower slip (0.1% to 0.2%), while high-pressure 2-stroke Diesel-cycle engines experience minimal to no slip.
  • Cargo Operations: Vapor venting or insufficient boil-off gas handling on LNG carriers.
  • Fuel Storage Systems: Impressed venting from LNG fuel tanks if boil-off gas generation outpaces engine consumption during extended port stays.

This slip is significant because methane carries a GWP20 of 82.5. Consequently, even a minor slip rate can increase a vessel's net Well-to-Wake greenhouse gas intensity, undermining its regulatory compliance under lifecycle frameworks.

Quantifying Engine Methane Slip by Combustion Cycle

The volume of unburnt methane escaping into the exhaust receiver varies considerably based on the engine design and combustion cycle:

``` [4-Stroke LP Otto Cycle] ──► Highest Slip (3.0% - 4.0%) ──► Flame quenching in piston crevice zones. [2-Stroke LP Otto Cycle] ──► Moderate Slip (0.1% - 0.2%) ──► Improved scavenging and cylinder geometry. [2-Stroke HP Diesel Cycle] ─► Minimal Slip (< 0.1%) ──► High-pressure gas injection directly into flame.

```

  • Low-Pressure 4-Stroke Otto Cycle: Gas is injected into the air intake air-stream during the induction stroke. During combustion, the flame front fails to reach the cool boundary layers near the cylinder walls and piston crevice volumes. This "flame quenching" leaves up to 4% of the fuel unburnt, which is swept directly into the exhaust manifold.
  • Low-Pressure 2-Stroke Otto Cycle: Gas is introduced mid-compression directly into the cylinder. While more efficient, slight gas losses can still occur through the exhaust ports during the final stages of scavenging.
  • High-Pressure 2-Stroke Diesel Cycle (e.g., MAN ME-GI): Liquid pilot fuel is injected first, initiating compression ignition. Gas fuel is then introduced directly into the established flame at high pressures (exceeding 300 bar). This ensures near-complete combustion, reducing methane slip to negligible levels.

Non-Combustion Shipboard Methane Sources

Beyond the main engine exhaust stack, methane emissions can occur across several operational areas:

  • Fuel Tank Boil-Off Gas (BOG) Management: When a vessel is idle or enduring low-load port operations, heat ingress causes liquid LNG in the fuel tanks to vaporize. If the vessel lacks a re-liquefaction plant or cannot burn the gas in its auxiliary systems, the excess pressure must be managed using a Gas Combustion Unit (GCU) to avoid venting raw methane directly to the atmosphere.
  • Purging and Pigging Operations: Residual gas left in bunkering lines after a transfer must be safely purged. Failure to route this gas back to a shore return line or internal recovery system can lead to fugitive emissions.

Operational Countermeasures for Chief Engineers

The Chief Engineer can implement specific operational measures to control and mitigate methane emissions:

  • Avoiding Low-Load Engine Operation: Dual-fuel engines experience significantly higher methane slip when operating at low loads due to reduced cylinder temperatures. The CE should optimize the power management system to run fewer generators at higher, more efficient loads.
  • Pilot Fuel Adjustments: Monitoring and adjusting the timing and quantity of pilot fuel injection to ensure robust ignition and complete flame propagation across the combustion chamber.
  • BOG Balancing: Coordinating auxiliary electrical and thermal loads to ensure all tank boil-off gas is consumed productively within the machinery plant, eliminating the need for venting or flaring.

The International Code of Safety for Ships using Gases or other Low-Flashpoint Fuels (IGF Code) is made mandatory under SOLAS Chapter II-1, Regulation 56. It governs vessels using fuels with a flashpoint below 60°C (such as LNG, methanol, or ethane), excluding gas carriers operating under the IGC Code.

Key safety requirements include:

  • System Isolation: Double-walled piping for all fuel lines in machinery spaces, with the outer space vacuumed or ventilated with inert gas and monitored by gas sensors.
  • Hazardous Area Classification: Strict zoning to isolate potential ignition sources from gas handling areas.
  • Automated Safety Systems: Double block and bleed valves designed to automatically isolate the fuel supply within milliseconds if a leak or power failure occurs.
  • Crew Competency: Mandatory basic and advanced STCW Chapter V training certifications for all involved crew members.

Compliant vessels are issued an International Gas Fuel Ship (IGFS) Certificate, subject to annual flag state surveys.

Statutory Foundation and Regulatory Scope

The IGF Code (IMO Resolution MSC.391(95)) provides a mandatory, goal-based international standard for machinery arrangements and fuel storage on ships utilizing low-flashpoint fuels:

``` ┌────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ SOLAS CHAPTER II-1 │ └───────────────────┬────────────────────┘ │ ▼ (Regulation 56) ┌────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ IGF CODE │ └─────────┬────────────────────┬─────────┘ │ │ ▼ ▼ ┌──────────────────────┐┌──────────────────────┐ │ PART A ││ PART A-1 │ ├──────────────────────┤├──────────────────────┤ │ • Risk Assessments ││ • Specific LNG Fuel │ │ • Goal-Based Designs ││ System Mandates │ │ • Hazardous Zoning ││ • Cryogenic Controls │ └──────────────────────┘└──────────────────────┘

```

The framework explicitly separates gas carried as fuel from gas carried as cargo. A gas carrier utilizing its own cargo boil-off gas as fuel operates primarily under the IGC Code, whereas a conventional bulk carrier, container ship, or cruise vessel utilizing LNG or methanol for propulsion must comply fully with the IGF Code.

Core Engineering and Safety Controls

To mitigate the risks of handling highly volatile, low-flashpoint fuels in shipboard machinery spaces, the code mandates several layers of defense:

  • Double-Walled Containment: Fuel piping routing through non-hazardous spaces must be double-walled. The outer pipe acts as a containment barrier. If the inner fuel line fails, the leaked gas is trapped in the outer annular space, which is continuously swept with inert gas or air and monitored by infrared gas detectors.
  • Double Block and Bleed Arrangements: The Gas Valve Unit (GVU) must feature two series-mounted automatic isolation valves and a central vent valve routed to the atmosphere. Upon gas detection, power failure, or an emergency shutdown (ESD) signal, the block valves snap shut and the bleed valve opens, isolating the fuel supply and venting residual gas safely away from the engine room.
  • Hazardous Space Ventilation: Any space housing gas fuel equipment, such as a GVU room, must be equipped with independent mechanical under-pressure ventilation capable of at least 30 air changes per hour, ensuring that any minor gas leakage is quickly exhausted before it can form an explosive mixture.

Crew Competency and Training Certifications

Because alternative fuel operations introduce unique safety challenges, the STCW Convention mandates specialized training tracks for all shipboard personnel under Chapter V (Regulations V/3 and V/4):

  • Basic IGF Training: Required for ratings and officers with specific safety or emergency response duties onboard gas-fueled ships, covering basic firefighting, toxicity hazards, and emergency bunkering protocols.
  • Advanced IGF Training: Mandatory for Chief Engineers, second engineers, and any officer with direct responsibility for the fuel storage, transfer, and utilization systems. This involves completing approved shoreside training, passing practical simulator assessments, and documenting at least one month of sea service on an IGF-compliant vessel, including participation in at least three bunkering operations.

Chief Engineer's Statutory Auditing Duties

The Chief Engineer is directly responsible for ensuring the vessel maintains its statutory compliance profile:

  • IGFS Certificate Maintenance: Preparing the machinery spaces and safety systems for annual, intermediate, and renewal surveys to maintain the validity of the International Gas Fuel Ship Certificate.
  • Bunkering Risk Management: Leading the mandatory pre-bunkering safety meeting, verifying the compatibility of the emergency release couplings (ERC), and executing the comprehensive safety checklist before opening the bunker manifolds.
  • Safety System Testing: Scheduling and logging regular operational tests of the gas detection array, the automated emergency shutdown (ESD) loops, and the ventilation failure alarms within the vessel's planned maintenance system.

FLL (Fuel Lifecycle LCA) and CE duty — explain and what is the CE's obligation?
MACHINERY
15-Second Answer

Fuel Lifecycle LCA (FLL) measures the total Well-to-Wake greenhouse gas impact of marine fuels. The Chief Engineer's primary obligation is to ensure absolute data integrity—verifying that Bunker Delivery Notes match sustainability certifications, maintaining accurate consumption logs, and updating the ship's SEEMP records.

60-Second Answer

Under the Fuel Lifecycle LCA framework, a Chief Engineer manages the operational data that verifies a fuel's actual environmental profile:

  • Documentation Audits: Confirming that all bunkered alternative fuels match their accompanying Proof of Sustainability (PoS) files and lifecycle emission factors under frameworks like FuelEU Maritime or the IMO DCS.
  • Log Integrity: Maintaining clear separation of daily consumption figures by fuel type in the engine logbooks, preventing data entry errors that could lead to non-compliance penalties.
  • System Operations: Optimizing fuel treatment setups, heating systems, and purification arrangements to handle low-carbon fuel blends safely while minimizing combustion-related losses.

Any failure to maintain accurate records constitutes an ISM non-conformity and can expose the vessel to heavy financial penalties.

Detailed Operational Duties and Administrative Controls

  • Verifying Alternative Fuel Documentation: When bunkering alternative energy options like biodiesel or e-methanol blends, the Chief Engineer must look beyond basic density and viscosity metrics. They must review the accompanying Proof of Sustainability (PoS) paperwork, ensuring it aligns with statutory guidelines (such as the Renewable Energy Directive II). If a fuel delivery lacks this verification, compliance frameworks automatically apply punitive default fossil emission factors, which can lower the ship's environmental rating.
  • Daily Consumption Log Management: Modern dual-fuel systems require logging fuel consumption across multiple categories simultaneously. The Chief Engineer must ensure that conventional pilot fuels and alternative primary fuels are measured independently using verified mass flow meters. These metrics must be recorded in the official Ship Energy Efficiency Management Plan (SEEMP Part 2) data logs to provide a clear audit trail.
  • Mid-Voyage Fuel Transition Protocols: When switching between different fuel types during an ocean passage, the engine room team must record the precise tank remaining-on-board (ROB) figures, exact geographical positions, and timing parameters. This provides clear data verification if the ship is audited at regional compliance checkpoints.

Managing Fuel Stability and Incompatibility Risks

Using modern alternative fuel blends introduces practical engineering challenges that the Chief Engineer must actively manage:

  • Preventing Fuel Incompatibility: Mixing new alternative fuel varieties with residual fossil batches in the settling or service tanks can trigger severe sludge precipitation. The Chief Engineer must enforce a strict policy of separating fuel batches until laboratory compatibility tests are completed.
  • Temperature and Viscosity Optimization: Many biofuels exhibit lower thermal stability and different viscosity curves compared to standard heavy fuel oil. The engine room team must monitor heat tracing networks and adjust purifier throughput parameters to prevent filter plugging or fuel pump scuffing.

Trap Warning

Do not tell the examiner that the Chief Engineer is responsible for calculating the fuel's lifecycle greenhouse gas intensity factor themselves. That factor is pre-determined during production and verified by shoreside certification bodies. Your actual duty is to ensure the Proof of Sustainability document matches the physical fuel delivered, log its consumption metrics exactly, and prevent data transcription errors within the ship's reporting systems.
