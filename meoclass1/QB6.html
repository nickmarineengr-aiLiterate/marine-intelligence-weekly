What is the CII — how is it calculated, what are the units, and what does the A–E rating mean?
GHG / CIIMARPOLSurvey
⚡ 15-Second Answer
CII stands for Carbon Intensity Indicator, measured in gCO₂/DWT·nm. It is calculated by dividing annual CO₂ emissions by the product of deadweight tonnage and distance sailed. Ships are rated A to E; a D for 3 consecutive years or an E in any single year triggers a mandatory corrective action plan under SEEMP Part III.
🕐 60-Second Answer
CII is an operational efficiency metric under MARPOL Annex VI Chapter 4, Regulation 28. The numerator sums all fuel types consumed multiplied by their carbon conversion factors (Cf). The denominator uses design DWT and actual nautical miles sailed. This attained value is compared against the Required CII — a ship-type specific target from MEPC.333(76) that drops approximately 2% per year. Ratings run A (superior) through E (inferior). If a ship receives D for three consecutive years, or E in any single year, the company must immediately revise SEEMP Part III to embed a corrective action plan before the next Statement of Compliance can be issued by the Administration or RO — deadline for annual data submission is 31 March of the following year.

Formula

Attained CII (gCO₂/DWT·nm) = Σ(M_fuel × Cf) / (DWT × Distance_nm)
Cf values per MEPC.364(79): HFO = 3.114 · MDO/MGO = 3.206 · LNG = 2.750 · Methanol = 1.375

Rating Bands

  • A — more than 15% better than required (Superior)
  • B — 7–15% better (Minor superior)
  • C — within ±7% (Moderate — compliant)
  • D — 7–15% worse (Minor inferior)
  • E — more than 15% worse (Inferior)

📌 Key Numbers

  • Unit: gCO₂/DWT·nm (capacity-based; for cargo ships)
  • Annual trajectory: ~2% reduction per year from 2023 to 2030 baseline
  • D × 3 consecutive years OR E in any 1 year → mandatory SEEMP Part III corrective action plan
  • Annual data deadline: 31 March to Administration/RO
  • In force: 1 November 2022
  • Applies to: ships ≥5,000 GT on international voyages
🔧 CE Relevance & Application
As CE on a Maersk Container Vessel

Your attained CII is built from ORB entries and BDNs — every fuel type, every sea day. As CE you own the numerator (fuel burned) more than any other officer. Practical levers: fuel changeover timing, boiler-off at sea via exhaust economiser, VFD pump management, cylinder pressure balancing for SFOC, and propeller inspection when slip trends exceed 10%.

On a Maersk TRIPLE-E (DWT ~165,000 t), at 80 t/day HFO × 3.114 = 249 t-CO₂/day. Reduce consumption by 5 t/day and over a 60-day voyage you save ~930 t-CO₂ from your annual numerator — a numerically significant CII shift.

What is the GFI — how is it calculated and what is its significance? How does it differ from CII?
GHG / CIIMARPOL
⚡ 15-Second Answer
GFI stands for Greenhouse Gas Fuel Intensity, unit gCO₂eq/MJ. It measures the lifecycle (Well-to-Wake) greenhouse gas intensity of the fuel mix per unit of energy. Unlike CII which measures operational efficiency per voyage (Tank-to-Wake), GFI isolates fuel cleanliness — it is a proposed IMO mid-term measure, not yet in force.
🕐 60-Second Answer
GFI aggregates Well-to-Wake emissions — covering CO₂, CH₄, and N₂O — divided by total energy content in MJ. A highly optimised ship burning HFO could score well on CII via slow steaming, yet still fail a GFI target because the underlying fuel intensity remains high. GFI prevents "problem shifting": a fuel providing low Tank-to-Wake emissions may carry large upstream production footprints. Under the 2023 IMO GHG Strategy (MEPC.377(80)), GFI forms the backbone of the proposed goal-based fuel standard within the mid-term measures framework.

Formula

GFI (gCO₂eq/MJ) = Total GHG Emissions Well-to-Wake / Total Energy Content of Fuel (MJ)
Reference baselines per MEPC.391(81) (adopted MEPC 81, March 2025):
HFO ≈ 91.7 · Fossil Methanol ≈ 89.0 · Green e-Methanol ≈ 8–12 gCO₂eq/MJ

CII vs GFI

  • CII — gCO₂/DWT·nm — operational efficiency — Tank-to-Wake — in force Nov 2022
  • GFI — gCO₂eq/MJ — fuel lifecycle quality — Well-to-Wake — proposed; expected 2027+

📌 Key Numbers

  • GFI unit: gCO₂eq/MJ (includes CH₄ and N₂O weighted by GWP100)
  • CII unit: gCO₂/DWT·nm (combustion CO₂ only)
  • GFI 2030 target: ~40% reduction from 2008 baseline fuel intensity
  • GFI 2050 target: near-zero
  • Status: Proposed mid-term measure — not yet in force
🔧 CE Relevance & Application
CE Relevance — Future Bunker Decisions

GFI is not yet in force, but CE bunker choices today determine future GFI compliance. When Maersk procurement offers methanol or biofuel, the GFI of that fuel — not just its Cf — will determine compliance credit under the Net-Zero Framework. BDNs will need to include lifecycle certification (ISCC Plus or RSB), not just sulphur content.

e-Methanol GFI ≈ 8–12 gCO₂eq/MJ vs HFO ≈ 91.7 — massive compliance incentive. But double consumption volume (calorific value ~20 vs 40 MJ/kg) means tank capacity and voyage endurance calculations change fundamentally — a CE-level engineering decision.

⚠️ Trap Questions & Strong Answers

TRAP "GFI and CII measure the same thing."
PASS Fundamentally different: CII = operational efficiency (gCO₂/DWT·nm, Tank-to-Wake, in force). GFI = fuel lifecycle quality (gCO₂eq/MJ, Well-to-Wake, proposed mid-term measure).

TRAP "GFI is already in force."
PASS GFI is a proposed mid-term measure under MEPC.377(80). Expected 2027+. Do not state it as current law.

TRAP "Green methanol has the same GFI as fossil methanol."
PASS Fossil methanol GFI ≈ 89.0; green/e-methanol GFI ≈ 8–12 gCO₂eq/MJ — 7–10× difference based on production pathway.

❌ Common CE Failures
  • Confusing GFI unit (gCO₂eq/MJ) with CII unit (gCO₂/DWT·nm)
  • Stating GFI is already in force
  • Not knowing Well-to-Wake scope vs Tank-to-Wake
  • Not knowing why MJ is the denominator (energy content, not distance — fuel quality metric)
  • Not knowing HFO baseline ≈ 91.7 gCO₂eq/MJ
📌 Numbers & Regulations to Memorise
  • GFI unit: gCO₂eq/MJ — energy-based, lifecycle
  • CII unit: gCO₂/DWT·nm — operational, Tank-to-Wake (contrast always)
  • HFO ≈ 91.7 gCO₂eq/MJ (Well-to-Wake baseline)
  • Fossil methanol ≈ 89.0 — comparable to HFO
  • Green e-methanol ≈ 8–12 — near-zero
  • MEPC.377(80) — 2023 GHG Strategy; GFI framework
  • MEPC.391(81) — 2025 LCA Guidelines for GFI (adopted MEPC 81, March 2025)
  • Status: proposed mid-term measure — NOT in force
🚨 Casualty / Incident Link

Problem Shifting — LNG as Incomplete Solution
Fossil LNG showed a ~20% Tank-to-Wake CO₂ reduction vs HFO. This is exactly the "problem shifting" that GFI is designed to prevent — a fuel with low tank-to-wake Cf but high upstream or slip emissions scores poorly on GFI even if CII looks good. The lesson directly informed the Well-to-Wake scope of the GFI mid-term framework.

What is EEXI — how is it calculated, and how does it differ from EEDI? What is the EEDI reference line for container ships?
GHG / CIIMARPOLMachinery
⚡ 15-Second Answer
EEXI is the Energy Efficiency Existing Ship Index — a mandatory one-time technical assessment for ships built before 2013. EEDI applies at the design phase for newbuilds. For container ships, the EEDI/EEXI reference line = a × DWT−c, where a = 174.22 and c = 0.201.
🕐 60-Second Answer
EEXI and EEDI share the same formula structure, measuring technical CO₂ per tonne-mile based on installed power, SFOC, and vessel capacity. EEDI is a design-phase index certified at delivery. EEXI is a retroactive, single-verification technical index for existing ships — enforcement at the first annual, intermediate, or renewal survey on or after 1 November 2022. For container ships Senthil asks about, the reference line uses the power function from MARPOL Annex VI Reg 21. Non-compliant ships must implement ShaPoLi or EPL — both authorised by Flag/Class only.

EEXI vs EEDI

  • EEDI — newbuilds; design phase; certified at delivery; Reg 20/21
  • EEXI — existing ships (built before 2013); first survey ≥1 Nov 2022; Reg 24; EEXI Technical File required

EEXI Formula

Attained EEXI ≈ [P_ME(75%) × Cf_ME × SFOC_ME + P_AE × Cf_AE × SFOC_AE] / (Capacity × V_ref)
Required EEXI = Reference Line Value × (1 − X/100)

Container Ship Reference Line

Reference Line = a × DWT−c
Container ships (>15,000 DWT): a = 174.22 · c = 0.201
Source: MARPOL Annex VI Reg 21, Table 1

📌 Key Numbers

  • EEXI enforcement: 1 November 2022 (first survey thereafter)
  • EEDI Phases: Phase 0 (2013) → Phase 1 (2015) → Phase 2 (2020) → Phase 3 (30–50% reduction depending on size)
  • Capacity parameter: 100% DWT for container ships
  • EEXI Technical File: mandatory dossier of data inputs, correction factors, and V_ref calculations submitted to RO
  • ShaPoLi: shaft power limited externally via torque sensor + governor lock
  • EPL: engine fuel index limited internally — both authorised by Flag/Class only
🔧 CE Relevance & Application
CE as EEXI Technical File Custodian

As CE you are technical custodian of the EEXI Technical File. If vessel has ShaPoLi: ensure torque sensor data log is intact and unaltered; governor lock seal is verified; any emergency override is immediately logged in the Official Log Book and reported to Flag Administration. You do not authorise ShaPoLi — Class does. But you operate it daily and must explain to the Master why full power is unavailable even in congested waters — it is a class-sealed compliance item.

TRAP "EEXI applies to newbuilds."
PASS EEXI = existing ships built before 2013. EEDI = newbuilds at design/delivery.

TRAP "EEXI is checked annually like CII."
PASS EEXI is a one-time verification — first survey on or after 1 November 2022. Not annual.

TRAP "Container EEXI reference line is a × b − c."
PASS It is a × DWT−c (power function, not subtraction). a = 174.22, c = 0.201.

TRAP "The Captain authorises ShaPoLi."
PASS Only Flag Administration / RO (Class) can authorise. Period.

What is SEEMP Part III and what does it contain? What is the International Energy Efficiency Certificate and its validity?
GHG / CIIMARPOLSurvey
⚡ 15-Second Answer
SEEMP Part III is a vessel-specific 3-year rolling CII compliance plan for ships ≥5,000 GT. The IEEC has no fixed expiry. The annual document is the Statement of Compliance (SOC) — not the IEEC.
🕐 60-Second Answer
SEEMP has three Parts: Part I (operational plan, all ships >400 GT), Part II (DCS methodology, ships ≥5,000 GT), and Part III (CII compliance plan, ships ≥5,000 GT, mandatory from 1 January 2023). Part III is a 3-year rolling roadmap specifying exactly how the vessel will achieve each year's Required CII — operational strategies, hull cleaning intervals, speed profiles, WHRS utilisation. If a D or E rating is received, Part III must be immediately updated with an audited Corrective Action Plan targeting return to at least a C rating. The IEEC, issued by the Flag Administration or RO, has no fixed expiry — it remains valid unless the ship changes flag or undergoes a major structural conversion. The annual compliance document is the SOC.

SEEMP Structure

  • Part I — Operational improvement plan; all ships >400 GT
  • Part II — DCS fuel data collection methodology; ships ≥5,000 GT
  • Part III — CII compliance plan; ships ≥5,000 GT; from 1 January 2023

📌 Key Numbers

  • IEEC validity: No fixed expiry — valid for vessel's lifetime under normal operations
  • IEEC re-issue triggers: Flag change OR major structural conversion only — NOT company change
  • SOC: Issued annually — the document that renews, not the IEEC
  • Annual data submission: 31 March each year to Administration/RO
  • Part III mandate: Mandatory from 1 January 2023; ships ≥5,000 GT
🔧 CE Relevance & Application
CE Owns SEEMP Part I; Monitors Part III

SEEMP Part I is the operational improvement plan — CE is the primary engine room contributor. You document: fuel consumption reduction measures, VFD utilisation, WHRS maintenance, propeller inspection scheduling, cylinder pressure optimisation intervals, and auxiliary machinery efficiency records.

SEEMP Part III (CII compliance plan) is developed by the company but CE must understand it — especially the corrective action plan trigger and deadlines. If vessel gets an E rating, Part III must be updated and re-verified before the SOC is issued — PSC will check this.

⚠️ Trap Questions & Strong Answers

TRAP "SEEMP Part III applies to all ships."
PASS Part III applies to ships ≥5,000 GT only. Part I applies to all ships >400 GT.

TRAP "Company change triggers IEEC re-issue."
PASS Company change does NOT invalidate the IEEC. Only flag change or major structural conversion.

What are all the greenhouse gases under MARPOL Annex VI? Is water vapour a greenhouse gas?
GHG / CIIMARPOL
⚡ 15-Second Answer
MARPOL Annex VI Chapter 4 targets CO₂, CH₄, and N₂O from shipping. Water vapour IS a greenhouse gas — the most abundant one — but is excluded from MARPOL because it is a temperature-driven feedback mechanism, not a primary anthropogenic emission. The Kyoto Protocol lists 7 gases (not 6 — NF₃ was added in 2012).
🕐 60-Second Answer

⚡ 15-Second Answer

🕐 60-Second Answer
The Cf is determined by the molecular carbon-to-mass ratio — essentially the 44/12 carbon-to-CO₂ weight ratio applied to each fuel's specific carbon content. HFO at 3.114 means every tonne burned produces over 3× its weight in CO₂. Methanol at 1.375 appears much cleaner, but because methanol's calorific value is ~19.9 MJ/kg versus HFO's ~40.2 MJ/kg, mass consumption roughly doubles for the same shaft power output. The true environmental advantage of methanol only materialises when the lifecycle (Well-to-Wake) pathway is green — i.e. e-methanol from renewable H₂ and CO₂ capture, where GFI drops to ~8–12 gCO₂eq/MJ.

Mass of CO₂ (t/day) = Fuel consumption (t/day) × Cf
80 t/day HFO × 3.114 = 249.12 t-CO₂/day ✓

3-element controller — explain. Where is it used onboard?
Machinery
⚡ 15-Second Answer
3-element controller maintains steam drum water level by measuring three variables simultaneously: (1) drum water level, (2) steam outflow rate, (3) feedwater inflow rate. It eliminates swell and shrink errors using a feed-forward loop.
🕐 60-Second Answer
A single-element controller (drum level only) fails at rapid load changes due to swell and shrink. Swell: rapid load increase → steam pressure drops → bubbles under the waterline expand → false high level → feedwater cuts back just when more water is needed. Shrink: load reduction → pressure spikes → bubbles collapse → false low level → feedwater floods in → carryover risk into superheater/turbine. The 3-element architecture uses steam flow and feedwater flow as a feed-forward compensating loop — the feedwater valve responds to load changes before the drum level can react. Drum level acts only as a final trim corrector. This eliminates hunting and protects against water-wall tube starvation (low level) and turbine blade carryover damage (high level).

Three Elements

  1. Drum Level Transmitter — hydrostatic or differential head; final trim signal
  2. Steam Flow Transmitter — main steam line; immediate feed-forward on load change
  3. Feedwater Flow Transmitter — post-regulating valve; confirms actual water injected

📌 Key Numbers

  • Swell trigger: rapid load increase → pressure drop → bubble expansion → false high level
  • Shrink trigger: load reduction → pressure spike → bubble collapse → false low level
  • Low level consequence: water-wall tube starvation → thermal burnout
  • High level consequence: liquid carryover → superheater thermal shock → turbine blade damage
  • Used on: steam LNG carriers; high-capacity WHRS boilers on container vessels
🔧 CE Relevance & Application
CE Responsibility — Boiler Control System

As CE on a steam LNG carrier: verify all three transmitters (drum level, steam flow, feedwater flow) are calibrated per PMS schedule; test controller's response to simulated load changes at sea (not during manoeuvring); ensure single-element backup controller is functional and switchable — never manage a drum level upset during heavy weather with no automation backup.

Consequences: low drum level → tube starvation → thermal burnout (hot work, class survey, drydock). High level → liquid carryover → superheater thermal shock → turbine blade damage (catastrophic and expensive).

PASS Not about accuracy — about swell and shrink false signals during rapid load changes. Single-element is accurate at steady state. 3-element is needed for dynamic load response.

TRAP "The drum level sensor is the primary control element."
PASS In 3-element control, drum level is the trim corrector, not the primary signal. The feed-forward loop (steam flow + feedwater flow) drives the primary response.

TRAP "Swell means water level is actually rising."
PASS Swell is a FALSE level rise from steam bubble expansion when drum pressure drops. Actual water inventory is NOT increasing. Single-element cuts feedwater — exactly the wrong response.

Ziegler-Nichols Method — how do you tune a PID controller using this method?
Machinery
⚡ 15-Second Answer
Set I = 0, D = 0. Increase P until sustained oscillation (Ku). Measure peak-to-peak period (Tu). Apply: Kp = 0.6 Ku · Ti = Tu/2 · Td = Tu/8. Target: quarter-amplitude decay.
🕐 60-Second Answer
Bring process to stable baseline in manual. Switch to auto with I = 0 and D = 0. Increase P gain step-by-step, applying small setpoint changes each time. When the process variable cycles in continuous, steady-amplitude oscillations, record the Ultimate Gain (Ku). Measure the period between two successive peaks — this is the Ultimate Period (Tu). Apply Z-N formulae. The target response is a quarter-amplitude decay curve — the system overshoots slightly but damps within 2–3 cycles. Use caution on live machinery: sustained oscillations in high-temperature loops can breach safety trips. Perform at reduced load or during sea trials where possible. Modern DCS/PLC auto-tune functions replicate this method electronically.

Steps

  1. Stable baseline in manual mode
  2. Switch to auto; set I = 0, D = 0
  3. Increase P gain; test small setpoint changes at each step
  4. Find Ku — gain at which system sustains constant-amplitude oscillation
  5. Measure Tu — peak-to-peak period (seconds)
  6. Apply formulae:

Kp = 0.6 × Ku
Ti (Integral Time) = Tu / 2
Td (Derivative Time) = Tu / 8
Target response: quarter-amplitude decay

📌 Applications Onboard

  • Jacket cooling water temperature control
  • HFO viscosity control (viscorator)
  • Steering gear autopilot
  • Cargo tank temperature (chemical tankers)

For safety-critical loops (steering autopilot, boiler drum level), any change must be documented in the maintenance log; class-notated systems may require surveyor notification. When a junior engineer reports a "hunting" loop, guide them through Z-N systematically — not by randomly adjusting parameters. Document Ku and Tu measurements before and after — that is what separates CE approach from guesswork.

TRAP "Z-N can be applied to any loop at any time."

PID controller — what is it, where is it used onboard, how do you tune and adjust it?
Machinery
⚡ 15-Second Answer
PID = Proportional (present error) + Integral (accumulated past error, eliminates offset) + Derivative (rate of change, dampens overshoot). Used on boiler drum level, cargo temperature, fuel viscosity, autopilot. Fast hunting → ↓P. Drifting offset → speed up I. Overshoot → ↑D.
🕐 60-Second Answer
P action: fast response proportional to current error — but leaves a steady-state offset because it needs an error to maintain a valve position. I action: continuously accumulates past error, driving the process variable to the exact setpoint — eliminates offset; if set too aggressively → Integral Windup (valve slams against limits, control lost). D action: acts on rate of error change as a braking force, dampening overshoot on fast dynamic loops like steering autopilots. D is deliberately omitted in slow thermal loops (jacket water, cargo temp) because process noise causes the derivative term to amplify minor fluctuations into erratic valve flutter. Tuning via Ziegler-Nichols gives Kp, Ti, Td from Ku and Tu measurements.

📌 Troubleshooting Rules

  • Rapid hunting / oscillation → P gain too high → reduce P (widen proportional band)
  • Slow drift, offset not correcting → I action too slow → reduce Ti (speed up integral)
  • Overshoot after setpoint change → add D (increase Td)
  • Integral windup after long upset → check anti-windup limits in DCS configuration
  • D omitted for: slow thermal loops (jacket water, cargo temp) — noise amplification causes valve flutter

Onboard Applications

  • Boiler drum water level (3-element PID)
  • Cargo tank temperature (chemical tankers) — PI only, no D
  • Fuel oil viscosity control (viscorator) — PI
  • Steering gear autopilot — all 3 terms active
  • IG main line pressure control — P or PI
🔧 CE Relevance & Application
CE Diagnostic Sequence

When junior engineer reports a problematic loop: (1) identify symptom (hunting, drifting offset, overshoot, slow response), (2) determine which PID term is causing it, (3) adjust systematically — one parameter at a time, document before and after values, (4) for safety-critical loops, do not adjust at sea without a documented risk assessment.

The D term is deliberately omitted from thermal loops (jacket water, cargo heating) because even small temperature signal noise is amplified by the derivative calculation, causing rapid valve cycling and premature wear. This is a design decision — CE must know when to leave D at zero.

⚠️ Trap Questions & Strong Answers

TRAP "Increasing I action speeds up error correction."
PASS Reducing Integral Time (Ti) speeds up I action. Increasing Ti slows it down. The convention is counterintuitive: Ti is the time constant of error accumulation — shorter Ti = faster integration = more aggressive I.

TRAP "D action is always needed for good control."
PASS D is omitted from slow thermal loops because it amplifies noise, causing valve flutter. Steering autopilots and fast dynamic loops use D. Jacket water and cargo temp loops use PI only.

TRAP "Integral Windup means the I term is too tightly wound."
PASS Integral Windup occurs when controller output is saturated (valve fully open/closed) but I term keeps accumulating error. When error finally reverses, accumulated integral causes significant overshoot. Anti-windup limits in DCS prevent this.

  • Not knowing shorter Ti = faster I action (counterintuitive convention)
  • Not knowing why D is omitted from thermal loops (noise amplification → valve flutter)
  • Cannot define Integral Windup and its consequence
  • Cannot give troubleshooting rules (hunting→↓P; drifting offset→speed up I; overshoot→↑D)
  • Cannot name at least 3 onboard PID applications
📌 Numbers & Regulations to Memorise
  • P — present error; leaves offset; hunting? → reduce P
  • I — accumulated past error; eliminates offset; integral windup risk; drifting? → speed up I (reduce Ti)
  • D — predicted future error; brakes overshoot; omit in slow thermal; overshoot? → add D
  • Integral Windup: saturation accumulation → overshoot on error reversal
  • D omitted for: jacket water, cargo temp, all slow thermal loops (noise)
  • D used for: steering autopilot, fast dynamic loops
  • Applications: boiler drum level, cargo temp, fuel viscosity, autopilot, IG pressure

VIT (Variable Injection Timing) — explain. Why is VIT not used below 40% load?
MachineryGHG / CII
⚡ 15-Second Answer
VIT advances fuel injection timing at part-load (40–85% MCR) to maintain Pmax, optimising thermal efficiency and lowering SFOC. Below ~40% MCR, turbocharger boost is insufficient → low compression temperatures → ignition delay → fuel accumulates → knock/detonation → Pmax exceedance and structural damage.
🕐 60-Second Answer
Engine thermal efficiency depends on the Pmax/Pcomp ratio. In standard fixed-timing configurations, this ratio drops at part-load as compression energy decreases. VIT counteracts by advancing the fuel injection start angle as load decreases from 85% down to ~40% MCR — keeping Pmax at its design limit and flattening the SFOC curve. Below 40%: turbocharger scavenge air pressure is minimal → compression temperatures drop → advancing injection now causes an extended ignition delay (fuel accumulates in cool air) → sudden violent detonation when auto-ignition finally occurs → massive shock loads on bearings and piston crown. VIT also maintains exhaust temperatures high enough at low loads to keep turbocharger speed stable and protect the exhaust gas economiser from soot build-up. VIT is directly relevant to EEXI and CII performance — saving fuel at slow-steaming loads (50–70% MCR) is where it delivers the most operational benefit for Maersk container vessels.

Low-Load Cutoff — Consequence Chain

  • ↓ TC boost pressure → ↓ scavenge air mass and temperature
  • ↓ Compression temperature → ↑ ignition delay
  • Fuel accumulates in cylinder before auto-ignition occurs
  • Mass ignition → severe detonation / knock → Pmax exceedance
  • Structural shock on piston crown, con-rod, bearings

📌 Key Numbers

  • Active range: ~40–85% MCR
  • Root cause of cutoff: insufficient TC boost air pressure
  • Objective: maximise Pmax/Pcomp ratio at part-load → lowest SFOC
  • CII link: lower SFOC → lower Cf × M_fuel → better attained CII
  • Implementation: mechanical rack linkage (older MC engines) or electronic common-rail solenoid (MAN B&W ME-C/ME-B)
  • NOx link: advancing timing increases peak temperature → more NOx (MARPOL AVI Reg 13 boundary)

19
FRAMO pump system — how does it work? Where is it used onboard?
MachineryMARPOL
⚡ 15-Second Answer
FRAMO = submerged centrifugal deepwell pumps driven by a central hydraulic HPU on deck at 250–300 bar. No cargo pump room — eliminates explosion risk. Double-walled cofferdam isolates hydraulic oil from cargo. Stripping to <75 L → MARPOL Annex II compliant.
🕐 60-Second Answer
The central HPU — driven by diesel or electric motors — supplies hydraulic oil through a pressurised deck ring line to each tank's deepwell pump. The hydraulic motor in the pump head drives the centrifugal impeller, lifting cargo up the central pipe stack to deck manifold. Each pump's speed is individually regulated by hydraulic flow control. The double-walled cofferdam separates the hydraulic oil supply from the cargo — the cofferdam must be regularly purged to check seal integrity: hydraulic oil in the purge = high-pressure seal failure; cargo in the purge = lower cargo seal failure. Stripping loop clears residuals to <75 litres, meeting MARPOL Annex II Reg 16 discharge requirements for noxious liquid substances — no port reception facility needed.

Architecture

  • HPU — central unit on deck; diesel or electric drive; 250–300 bar supply
  • Deck ring line — distributes hydraulic oil to each tank's deepwell pump
  • Pump head — submerged; hydraulic motor + centrifugal impeller inside central pipe stack
  • Cofferdam — double-walled barrier between hydraulic oil and cargo; purged regularly
  • Cofferdam diagnostic: oil in purge = HP seal failure; cargo in purge = lower seal failure
  • Stripping system — clears suction well to <75 L using N₂ or compressed air

📌 Key Numbers

  • HPU pressure: 250–300 bar
  • MARPOL AII stripping limit: <75 L residual (stripping trials required)
  • Used on: chemical tankers, product tankers, LNG regasification vessels
  • Key advantage: no cargo pump room → no enclosed explosion risk
  • HPU drive: diesel OR electric (vessel-type dependent)
🔧 CE Relevance & Application
CE Operating FRAMO Systems

CE critical maintenance responsibilities: (1) cofferdam purge per PMS — analyse what comes out: oil = HP seal failure; cargo = lower cargo seal failure, (2) HPU oil cleanliness monitoring — contamination causes premature pump bearing failure, (3) stripping trial documentation at each cargo discharge — prove <75 L residual for MARPOL Annex II certificates, (4) deck ring line pressure test per class interval — any drop = line leakage.

You will not pull a FRAMO pump for seal replacement at sea — but you manage the maintenance schedule and must recognise early warning signs from cofferdam samples.

⚠️ Trap Questions & Strong Answers

TRAP "FRAMO pumps have electric motors."
PASS FRAMO pumps are driven by hydraulic motors from the central HPU. No electrical cables in the cargo tank — no electrical ignition source in the hazardous cargo zone. This is the primary safety advantage.

TRAP "MARPOL Annex II stripping limit is 100 litres."
PASS The standard residue limit is less than 75 litres per cargo hold (MARPOL AII Reg 16). 100 litres is wrong — a common off-the-top-of-the-head error that Simon and Srivastava both test.

TRAP "FRAMO is only used on LNG carriers."
PASS FRAMO deepwell systems are characteristic of chemical tankers and product tankers. Some LNG regasification vessels use submerged pumps conceptually but FRAMO deepwell is the chemical/product tanker standard.
