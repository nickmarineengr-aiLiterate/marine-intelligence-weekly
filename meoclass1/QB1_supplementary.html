As Chief Engineer, what are your ISM Survey duties in the Engine Room — beyond documentation and machinery checks?
ISM CodeSOLAS IXSurvey

ISM Survey — What It Actually Tests

"In an ISM survey (initial/renewal/intermediate), the surveyor verifies that the SMS required by SOLAS IX and the ISM Code is effectively implemented on board, not just documented. For the engine room, my duties as CE include demonstrating implementation of ISM Code Chapters 7, 8, 9 and 10 in daily machinery operations, maintenance and emergency preparedness."

1. SMS Implementation — Practical Evidence

  • Demonstrate familiarity with company SMS procedures for ER: machinery operations, critical equipment failure procedures, PTW systems — verbally, not by reading from the manual.
  • Walk the surveyor through the Emergency Response Plan for machinery spaces from memory.
  • CE and Master responsibilities as defined in SMS must be clearly understood by the engine department — not just stated in a document.

2. Non-Conformities, Near Misses & Hazardous Occurrences

  • All NCs raised in the engine department must have documented corrective actions with root cause analysis and verified closure — surveyor checks if the loop was closed, not just reported.
  • Company safety circulars on lessons learned must be discussed at safety meetings — entries in safety meeting log are the evidence.
  • This addresses ISM Code Chapter 9 — reports and analysis of non-conformities, accidents and hazardous occurrences.

3. Drills & Training Records

  • Fire drills — monthly per SOLAS, engine department participation, debrief notes recorded.
  • Enclosed space entry drills — at intervals as per company SMS and SOLAS/IMO guidelines on safe entry into enclosed spaces, with engine staff participating and records filed.
  • Abandon ship drills — monthly. ERT competency for engine team verified.
  • Training Matrix for engine department — competencies, familiarization, on-job training entries current.

4. Permit to Work (PTW) System

  • Hot work, enclosed space entry, and high-voltage PTW records — recent, correctly filled, authorization chain visible.
  • Surveyor may cross-check PTW records against actual recent maintenance jobs in PMS — verifying PTW is a practiced system, not a paperwork exercise.

5. PMS and Defect Reporting

  • PMS records showing overdue items, risk assessments for deferrals, CE's approval authority for each deferral documented.
  • Critical defects reported to office via Defect Reporting System (DRS) — audit trail visible.
  • This demonstrates compliance with ISM Code Chapter 10 — maintenance of the ship and equipment.

6. Environmental Records (ORB & SEEMP)

  • ORB Part I — current, correctly completed, no blank lines, Master's signature on each completed page.
  • SEEMP Part II — CII trajectory tracked; corrective actions documented if off target.
  • These also support the company's environmental protection objectives under ISM Code Chapter 1 and MARPOL documentation requirements.

Explain the CMS (Continuous Machinery Survey) scope. What can the CE survey? What must class surveyors survey? What is NOT covered by CMS?
IACS UR Z7CMSMachinery

CMS — Principle

Under IACS UR Z7/Z7.1, Continuous Machinery Survey is an alternative to the 5-year Periodical Machinery Survey, where individual machinery items are assigned survey windows over a 5-year cycle. The ship must be accepted into CMS by its classification society, with an approved CMS list. This avoids a concentrated survey period and allows condition-based maintenance to be credited.

Items CE May Survey (Class-Delegated Self-Survey)

Under approved CMS programs, less-critical items may be surveyed by a CE holding valid MEO Class 1 / CE CoC — but delegation is strictly in accordance with the class-approved CMS schedule and written delegation conditions (CE's CoC, ship type, CMS procedure). It is not automatic for all CEs. Class always reserves the right to attend any item. Typically includes:

  • Auxiliary engines (non-propulsion generators)
  • Pumps — general service, ballast, bilge, FW/SW
  • Purifiers / fuel and lube oil separators
  • Air compressors, refrigeration compressors
  • Heat exchangers — SW/FW/LO coolers
  • Deck machinery — windlass, mooring winches (subject to class-specific rules)

Items REQUIRING Class Surveyor

  • Main propulsion engine — crankshaft, main bearings, cylinder liners, running gear
  • Propeller shafting and stern tube — tail shaft and intermediate shaft
  • Main boilers — pressure vessel survey, safety valves; set-pressure verification
  • Emergency generator — load test from dead ship condition; auto-start test
  • Rudder and steering gear — structural inspection including pintles and gudgeons

Overdue CMS Items — CE Responsibility

All CMS items have assigned due dates. If not completed and reported within the window, they become overdue CMS items and may lead to Conditions of Class or withdrawal of CMS status. As CE, I must monitor due/overdue items and document any request for postponement with a formal risk assessment — verbal deferrals are not accepted by class.

NOT Included in CMS

  • Hull surveys — separate Annual / Intermediate / Special Survey scheme
  • Fire fighting systems — separate SOLAS periodic inspection schedule
  • LSA — annual and 5-year inspection per LSA Code / SOLAS regulations
  • MARPOL equipment surveys — separate IOPP and IAPP certificate surveys

CE Oral Tip: Know your own ship's CMS program — which items are on the rolling cycle and which are currently overdue. Surveyors ask: "What is overdue on your CMS right now and what is your risk assessment for deferral?" A blank answer here is a serious red flag.

Load Line Survey preparations as Second Engineer. What are your engine room responsibilities?
Load LinesSOLAS II-1Survey

Load Line Survey — ER Scope

The Load Line Survey issues the International Load Line Certificate under the 1966 LL Convention / 1988 Protocol. In the engine room, the survey focuses on watertight and weathertight integrity of openings and sea connections which affect the vessel's assigned freeboard, in accordance with the International Load Line Convention 1966/1988. The surveyor inspects ER-side closing appliances and watertight arrangements:

1. Watertight Integrity — ER Closing Appliances

  • Engine room skylights and ventilators: All closing appliances (dogs, hinges, closures) operational; rubber seals intact; storm covers available and labelled.
  • ER access hatches (casing top): Dog bolts fully operational; gaskets not brittle or compressed flat.
  • Vent heads / air pipes: Float-type or flip-flap closures for all ER/tank vents — free-moving, not corroded; float mechanisms not stuck.
  • Storm valves on ER vents: Function-tested — very commonly found painted shut; verify each individually.

2. Sea Valves and Storm Valves

  • All sea suction and discharge valves operable from accessible positions; spindles not seized.
  • Overboard discharge non-return valves — verified non-return function.

3. Bilge System

  • Bilge alarm in ER operational; high bilge level alarm tested.
  • All bilge suction valves operable; bilge lines clear.

4. DB Tank Manholes & Vent Pipes

  • All DB tank and void space manholes — dogs in place, bolts not seized, gaskets intact.
  • Ballast tank vent pipe closures operational; sounding pipes capped.

5. Side Scuttles & Scuppers

  • Any side scuttles or scuppers within machinery spaces below the freeboard deck are checked for proper closing arrangements per Load Line requirements.
  • Note: many closing appliances for LL overlap with SAFCON checks; the LL surveyor's focus is specifically on watertight/weathertight integrity rather than fire or machinery safety.

6. Documents Ready

  • Previous Load Line Certificate and survey history.
  • Stability booklet (intact and damage) — class-approved copy.
  • Deadweight scale / hydrostatic tables.

IOPP Survey preparation as 2nd Engineer — complete checklist.
MARPOL Annex ISurvey

IOPP Survey — Scope

The IOPP Certificate is issued and maintained under MARPOL Annex I Reg. 6–8 for ships 400 GT and above, with a maximum 5-year validity, subject to intermediate and annual surveys in line with the Harmonized System of Survey and Certification (HSSC). The survey verifies physical equipment and record compliance. As 2E:

1. OWS (Oily Water Separator)

  • Full function test — run OWS, confirm overboard stop activates at or below 15 ppm.
  • 15 ppm bilge alarm: electrode cell clean; automatic three-way valve diverts to holding tank correctly.
  • Flow meter reading correct; no evidence of tampering.
  • OWS Type Approval Certificate onboard — must meet MEPC.107(49) or later standard.

2. Oil Record Book Part I

  • ORB current — all code letters A through I correctly entered; no blank lines.
  • Overboard discharge entries: date, lat/long, quantity, rate, method of disposal.
  • Bilge water retained: quantity, receiving tank identified.
  • Master's signature on each completed page.
  • ORB retained onboard for 3 years after last entry (MARPOL Annex I Reg. 17).

3. Sludge System

  • Sludge tank capacity must be adequate as per MARPOL Annex I/MEPC guidelines (commonly expressed by a formula such as V = K × C × D, considering engine size and voyage pattern); surveyor may check that installed capacity is not less than the design requirement.
  • Sludge pump operational; standard shore connection flange fitted (MARPOL Annex I Reg. 13).

4. Bilge System Integrity

  • No direct overboard discharge from ER bilges bypassing OWS.
  • Surveyor physically traces OWS pipework — know every valve position in your bilge/OWS circuit.

5. Certificates & SOPEP

  • IOPP Certificate — valid and endorsed.
  • Supplement to IOPP Certificate (Form A for non-tankers) — accurate and updated for current ship configuration.
  • SOPEP (Shipboard Oil Pollution Emergency Plan) — the surveyor may also check that SOPEP/SMPEP equipment and arrangements (discharge connections, emergency pumping) align with the IOPP Supplement information.

NOx Technical File — what is it, what does it contain, and what does the surveyor check?
MARPOL Annex VISurveyMachinery

NOx Technical File (NTF) — Purpose

The NTF is mandatory under MARPOL Annex VI, Regulation 13 for each diesel engine with output power more than 130 kW. It is an engine-specific document — every such engine has its own NTF and EIAPP Certificate issued or endorsed by the Administration/RO. Per-engine, not per-ship. Every diesel engine above the threshold has its own NTF approved by the Flag Administration.

Contents

  • Engine identification: Maker, model, serial number, rated power (kW), rated speed (RPM).
  • Applicable NOx Tier: Tier I (pre-2011 keel lay), Tier II (2011 onward), Tier III (NECA-applicable, post-2016). Important: Tier III limits apply only when the ship operates in designated NOx Emission Control Areas (NECAs); outside NECAs, Tier II limits apply even for Tier III-capable engines. Many engines have both Tier II and Tier III operating modes (e.g. SCR on/off, EGR active/bypass).
  • Engine cycle data — the critical part: Fuel injection timing, injection pressure, valve timing, charge air pressure and temperature — the exact settings defining the emission control configuration.
  • Approved components list: Fuel injection pumps, injectors, turbocharger model — and acceptable variation ranges.
  • Test report reference: Reference to NOx emission test accepted by Flag Administration.
  • Approved modifications: Any post-delivery modification affecting NOx emissions — recorded and separately approved.

What the Surveyor Checks

  • NTF onboard and valid — signed by Flag Administration or Recognised Organisation (RO).
  • Engine settings match NTF values — injection timing may be physically verified.
  • Approved components in use — unapproved replacements require Administration notification.
  • EIAPP Certificate — cross-checked with NTF.
  • ONVP (if applicable) — procedure document and recent verification records checked.
  • For Tier III engines: surveyor verifies that the correct operating mode (e.g. SCR/EGR in service) is used when operating in a NECA, and that mode-changeover procedures and records follow the NTF and ONVP.
⚖ Regulatory References
MARPOL Annex VI / Reg. 13NOx — Tier I/II/III limits and Technical File requirement
NOx Technical Code 2008Technical Code on Control of NOx from Marine Diesel Engines — MEPC.177(58)
MARPOL Annex VI / Reg. 5Surveys and certification — EIAPP Certificate
MEPC.1/Circ.795Guidance on NOx Technical File record keeping
CE Oral Tip: Know your main engine's NOx Tier. Most Maersk container vessels post-2011 are Tier II. For ECA operations (North Sea, Baltic, US ECA), know whether your engine is Tier III compliant (EGR or SCR fitted). Examiner will ask: "What Tier is your main engine and how do you verify compliance at an ECA boundary?"

What is a SAFCON (Safety Construction) Survey? What does the surveyor check in the engine room?
SOLAS IISurveyMachinery

SAFCON Survey — Purpose

The Safety Construction Survey under SOLAS Chapter I Reg. 10 issues the Cargo Ship Safety Construction Certificate (CSSCC) for vessels 500 GT and above. It covers compliance mainly with SOLAS Chapters II-1 (structure, machinery, electrical) and II-2 (fire protection, detection and extinction) as they relate to hull and machinery safety. The survey regime follows the HSSC pattern (initial, annual/intermediate, renewal every 5 years) in accordance with SOLAS I/14. Note that the CSSCC is part of the Cargo Ship Safety Certificate package alongside the Safety Equipment and Safety Radio certificates.

A. Emergency Stops & Remote Controls

  • Remote emergency shutdown — fuel oil service pumps, forced draught fans, purifiers — tested from outside machinery spaces.
  • Emergency generator — auto-start tested; load test from dead ship condition.
  • Quick-closing fuel oil valves — remote operation from outside ER tested.

B. Fire Safety in Machinery Spaces (SOLAS II-2)

  • Fixed CO2 / equivalent fire extinguishing system — cylinder weights within limits; last discharge certificate present.
  • ER boundary ventilation dampers — quick-closing tested from outside ER.
  • Fire detection system — smoke/heat detectors tested; panel functional; alarm audible on bridge.
  • Main and emergency fire pumps — pressure and flow tested.

C. Steering Gear (SOLAS II-1 / Reg. 29)

  • Main and auxiliary steering gear — functional test to full angle rate.
  • Emergency steering from steering gear room — tested; bridge communication confirmed.

D. Electrical Safety

  • Main switchboard insulation resistance measured.
  • Emergency switchboard automatic transfer tested.
  • Shore connection available and correctly marked.

E. ER Structural Integrity

  • ER watertight boundary — closing appliances, hatch covers operational.
  • Shaft tunnel access door — watertight, dogs operational, passage unobstructed.

State the Intact Stability Criteria — IS Code 2008 (MSC.267(85)). Give all numerical values.
StabilityIS Code 2008

Intact Stability Criteria — IS Code 2008, Chapter 2.2

For conventional cargo and passenger ships, the IS Code 2008 (Res. MSC.267(85), Part A, Chapter 2.2) specifies the following minimum intact stability criteria. All six must be satisfied simultaneously at every loading condition — none may be reduced. Note: additional or modified criteria may apply to specific ship types such as containerships, fishing vessels, and high-speed craft.

  1. Area 0° to 30° ≥ 0.055 m·rad
  2. Area 0° to 40° ≥ 0.090 m·rad (or to angle of flooding θf if θf < 40°)
  3. Area 30° to 40° ≥ 0.030 m·rad (or between 30° and θf)
  4. GZ at 30° ≥ 0.200 m
  5. Maximum GZ at angle ≥ 25° (preferably ≥ 30°)
  6. Initial GM₀ ≥ 0.150 m

θf = angle of flooding — heel angle at which water ingress can occur through any unprotected opening. If θf < 40°, criteria 2 and 3 are measured to θf instead of 40°.

Weather Criterion (IS Code 2008, Section 2.3)

Under the weather criterion, the ship must have sufficient dynamic stability to withstand steady wind plus rolling. The pass condition is that the leeward residual area (b) under the GZ curve must be at least equal to the wind heeling energy area (a). Wind heeling is calculated using a standard pressure of 504 Pa for steady wind, with gust pressure taken as 1.5 times the steady wind value. Rolling amplitude (θ1) is derived from an empirical formula based on the ship's beam, draught, block coefficient, and rolling period.

State the Damage Stability Criteria for cargo ships under SOLAS 2009 amendments. What is A ≥ R?
StabilitySOLAS II-1

Damage Stability — Regulatory Framework

SOLAS Chapter II-1, Part B-2 governs damage stability. The 2009 amendments (MSC.216(82)) introduced a probabilistic framework for cargo ships ≥80m with keels laid on or after 1 January 2009.

The Probabilistic Method — A ≥ R

  • Attained index (A): Calculated from the ship's actual geometry — summing probability-weighted contributions from all possible compartment flooding scenarios. The calculation of A includes the probability of damage (location and extent) and the probability of survival for each damage case, combined over all cases. Higher A = better inherent damage resistance.
  • Required index (R): Prescribed by SOLAS as a function of ship length and type. Generally 0.5 to 0.8 for cargo ships, increasing with length.
  • Compliance: A ≥ R. If A falls below R, the subdivision is inadequate and the ship cannot be certified.

Residual Stability After Flooding (Final Stage)

For each damage case considered in the probabilistic calculation, the ship must meet defined survival criteria in the final stage: there must be adequate range of positive righting levers (GZ), sufficient maximum GZ, and acceptable equilibrium heel and trim. Openings that could lead to progressive flooding must remain above the final waterline. The detailed limits are given in SOLAS II-1 Part B-1/B-2 and the associated explanatory notes — they vary by damage case and ship type and should not be reduced to a single universal figure.

Older Ships — Deterministic Method

Pre-2009 SOLAS cargo ships generally use the deterministic method — damage is assumed to fixed prescribed lengths (one-, two-, or three-compartment flooding based on ship length), and the ship must meet prescribed residual stability criteria for each standard damage case. The probabilistic method replaced this for new ships because it assesses survival probability over all realistic damage scenarios, producing a more accurate safety measure.

What is the Factor of Subdivision? How is it determined and what does a lower value indicate?
StabilitySOLAS II-1

Factor of Subdivision (F) — Definition

F is the maximum permissible length of a compartment expressed as a fraction of the floodable length. It governs how finely a ship must be subdivided to meet statutory damage stability requirements.

Permissible compartment length = F × Floodable length

How F is Determined

F is calculated from the criterion numeral (C), which depends on:

  • Ship length (L) — longer ships get stricter subdivision.
  • Number of persons carried — Cs (service numeral) combines a length factor with a passenger factor.
  • Two values Cs and Cp are calculated; F is interpolated between them using the SOLAS II-1 Reg. 6 formula.

Interpretation

  • F = 1.0 — minimal subdivision; compartment may equal full floodable length. Simple cargo ships below certain criteria.
  • F = 0.5 — compartment may be only 50% of floodable length; stricter.
  • F = 0.33 or lower — very high subdivision; large passenger ships.

These F values are indicative only; the actual F for a particular ship is obtained from the SOLAS II-1 Reg. 6 tables/formulae once C (criterion numeral) is calculated.

Lower F = more bulkheads required = more stringent subdivision = safer ship against flooding.

For ships still under the deterministic regime, subdivision bulkhead spacing must satisfy "permissible compartment length = F × floodable length" along the ship's length. For newer cargo ships, overall subdivision is demonstrated by the probabilistic index A ≥ R instead.

Draw and explain the GZ / Statical Stability Curve. Label all key points.
StabilityIS Code 2008

GZ Curve — What It Represents

The GZ curve (righting lever curve) is the basic statical stability curve used in the IS Code 2008 to check compliance with intact stability criteria (areas and GZ values). It plots the righting lever (GZ in metres) against angle of heel (degrees) at a specific displacement and KG. GZ is the horizontal distance between vertical lines through G and through the centre of buoyancy B — it is the arm of the restoring couple. Larger GZ = more righting moment = more stable.

Key Points — Label These When Drawing

  • Origin (0°, GZ=0): Ship upright. Zero righting lever.
  • Initial slope: For small angles, GZ ≈ GM₀ × sin θ (≈ GM₀ × θ in radians), so the initial slope of the curve at 0° is directly proportional to GM₀. Steeper slope = larger GM = stiffer ship.
  • Maximum GZ point: Peak of the curve. IS Code requires this at ≥ 25° (preferably ≥ 30°); GZ at 30° must be ≥ 0.20m.
  • Angle of flooding (θf): Angle at which an unprotected opening submerges. If θf < 40°, it cuts off the effective range.
  • Angle of vanishing stability (θv): GZ returns to zero — beyond this, ship capsizes. Range of positive stability = 0° to θv.

Area Criteria (Mark as Hatched Zones on Drawing)

  • Zone 1: 0° to 30° = 0.055 m·rad minimum.
  • Zone 2: 0° to 40° = 0.09 m·rad minimum (whole zone).
  • Zone 3: 30° to 40° = 0.03 m·rad minimum.
⚖ Regulatory References
IS Code 2008 / 2.2Design criteria — GZ curve requirements; MSC.267(85)
IS Code 2008 / Part A / 1General requirements — mandatory provisions for all ship types
SOLAS II-1 / Reg. 5Required stability information to be supplied to Master
CE Oral Tip: You will almost certainly be asked to draw the GZ curve at Kochi MMD. Draw a clean arc starting at origin, initial slope proportional to GM, peak near 40°, descend to cross zero at ~70–80°. Mark θf if asked. Hatch the three area criteria zones. Label maximum GZ point with "≥ 0.20m at 30°". A well-drawn, labelled curve with all six criteria noted scores full marks.

What are Cross Curves of Stability (KN Curves)? How are they constructed and used?
Stability

Cross Curves — Purpose

Cross Curves of Stability are a set of curves derived from ship hull geometry alone, independent of any specific loading condition. They allow the GZ curve to be rapidly constructed for any loading condition without repeating full hull calculations from scratch.

Construction

  • At each fixed angle of heel (typically 10°, 20°, 30°, 40°, 50°, 60°, 75°, 90°), KN is calculated — the distance from keel (K) to the line of action of buoyancy (N) measured perpendicular to the centreline plane; it is a purely geometric lever independent of loading — across a range of displacement values.
  • Each fixed heel angle produces one curve of KN vs. displacement.
  • Result: a family of curves (one per heel angle) plotted on one graph against displacement. Calculated once at design stage; reused throughout the ship's life.

Using the Cross Curves to Find GZ

For a given loading condition (known displacement Δ and KG):

  1. Enter the cross curves at the actual displacement — read off KN at each angle.
  2. Apply correction: GZ = KN − KG × sin θ
  3. Plot GZ vs. angle — this gives the actual GZ curve for that loading condition.

Both KN and GZ are in metres; angles are in degrees (or radians in the trigonometric functions). This formula separates geometry (KN — fixed by hull form) from loading (KG — changes with cargo). This is the practical utility of cross curves.

Separating them allows one set of hull-form calculations to serve all loading conditions throughout the ship's operational life.

Explain Free Surface Effect — formula, effect on GM, and how to reduce it.
Stability

Free Surface Effect — Principle

When a tank is partially filled, the free surface of the liquid shifts transversely as the ship heels, moving the centre of gravity of that liquid in the direction of heel. This effectively raises the ship's virtual centre of gravity and reduces the righting lever GZ. FSE does not physically move G — it creates a virtual rise of G equivalent to the loss of stability from the shifting liquid surface.

Formula

GG₁ = (i × ρ_liquid) / (V × ρ_water)

Where: i = second moment of area of the free surface about its longitudinal centroidal axis (m⁴) | ρ_liquid = density of liquid in tank | V = ship's displacement volume (m³) | ρ_water = density of water ship floats in. If the liquid density is close to seawater, GG₁ ≈ i / V — this is the free surface correction added to KG (or subtracted from GM).

Effect on GM

GM (effective) = GM (solid) − GG₁

FSE always reduces effective GM. If total GG₁ > GM (solid) → effective GM becomes negative → potential instability.

Three Key Points

  • FSE is proportional to breadth³ of the tank (i = lb³/12) — wide tanks are far more dangerous than narrow ones.
  • Splitting a tank longitudinally into two equal halves reduces FSE to 1/4 of original.
  • FSE depends on tank dimensions, not on quantity of liquid — a tank 10% full has nearly the same FSE correction as one 90% full.
⚖ Regulatory References
IS Code 2008 / 2.2.3Correction to GM for free surfaces of liquids — mandatory in all stability calculations
SOLAS II-1 / Reg. 5Stability information — FSC corrections to be included in loading manual
CE Oral Tip: When asked "how do you reduce free surface effect?", give three practical answers: (1) press up tanks or empty them completely, (2) use longitudinal divisions in wide tanks, (3) load ballast in DB tanks first (lowest, widest tanks have least effect on KG). These three show operational understanding, not just formula recitation.

What is Angle of Loll? Cause, recognition, and correct corrective actions — in full.
Stability

Angle of Loll — Definition

Angle of loll is the angle at which a ship with negative initial GM (G above M) finds a position of unstable equilibrium. The ship lolls to one side and remains there. If disturbed, it may snap to the other side or capsize. Key distinction from a list: with loll, G is still on the centreline — the cause is GM < 0, not an off-centre G.

Cause

GM₀ < 0 — typically from:

  • Excessive topweight — heavy cargo or equipment loaded high.
  • Large free surface effects reducing effective GM below zero.
  • Consuming DB tank ballast, raising centre of gravity above metacentre.

Recognition — GZ Curve at Loll

At upright the ship is in unstable equilibrium (GM < 0); it cannot remain safely upright and will "fall" to one side until it reaches the angle of loll where GZ becomes zero again and the effective GM is just positive. The GZ curve has a negative slope at 0°, crosses zero at angle of loll (θl), shows positive GZ beyond θl, then falls back to zero at vanishing stability.

Corrective Actions — CRITICAL SEQUENCE

  • Step 1 — Identify correctly: Confirm it is loll (G on centreline, negative GM) not list (G off centreline, positive GM). Treatment is different.
  • Step 2 — Do NOT fill high tanks first. Adding liquid to a high tank temporarily increases FSE, further raising virtual G — can capsize the vessel before the tank fills.
  • Step 3 — Fill Double Bottom (DB) tanks. This lowers G (adds weight low) and increases KM (less displacement draws more KM), restoring positive GM.
  • Step 4 — Fill the DB tank on the low (loll) side first. If you fill the high side first, the initial effect is to add weight to the high side, potentially snapping the ship to the other side before the FSE is eliminated.
  • Step 5 — Shift heavy cargo downward if operationally possible.

Key distinction: in a list, GM is positive and G is off the centreline due to asymmetric loading; in a loll, GM is negative and G is on the centreline. This is why the treatment is completely different — do not confuse them.

⚖ Regulatory References
IS Code 2008 / 2.2GM ≥ 0.15m minimum criterion — to prevent loll situations in normal operation
SOLAS II-1 / Reg. 5Stability information — remedial action guidance to be included in stability booklet
CE Oral Tip — The Classic Killer Question: Examiner: "10° loll to starboard. What do you do?" Answer: (1) Confirm it is loll not list. (2) Fill DB tanks — starboard (low) side first. (3) State WHY NOT high tanks — "filling a high tank temporarily increases FSE, raising virtual G further and risking capsize before the tank is full." This three-point answer with the reason will satisfy the examiner completely.

What is Dynamic Stability? How does it differ from static stability and why does it matter?
Stability

Dynamic Stability — Definition

Dynamic stability is the work done in heeling the ship to a given angle — the ship's capacity to absorb energy from waves, wind gusts, or cargo shifts without capsizing.

Dynamic Stability (m·rad) = Area under the GZ curve × Displacement × g

In practice, we often express dynamic stability simply by the area under the GZ curve in m·rad, which is proportional to the energy per tonne the ship can absorb. Multiplying by displacement and g gives the actual work in kN·m (Joules). The IS Code 2008 intact stability criteria (0.055, 0.09, 0.03 m·rad) are therefore dynamic stability requirements — they ensure a minimum reserve of energy against capsize, not just a minimum GZ at one angle.

Static vs. Dynamic

  • Static stability: GZ at one fixed angle — the righting moment if the ship is slowly and statically held at that angle. A single-point measure. Does not account for energy required to reach that angle.
  • Dynamic stability: Area under the GZ curve to that angle — the energy the ship can absorb while rolling through those angles in real sea conditions. Ships roll dynamically, not statically — this is the more realistic measure.

Practical Significance

A ship could have adequate GZ at 30° (adequate static stability) but a small area under the curve (low dynamic stability) — meaning it cannot survive a wave imparting sufficient rolling energy to reach that angle. The IS Code area criteria and the weather criterion (area b ≥ area a) are both essentially dynamic stability requirements expressed numerically.

⚖ Regulatory References
IS Code 2008 / 2.2Area criteria — the IS Code's expression of dynamic stability requirements
IS Code 2008 / 2.3Weather criterion — combines static wind moment and dynamic rolling energy
CE Oral Tip: If asked "what is the area under the GZ curve?", state: it represents the dynamic stability — the energy absorbed during heeling. This connects the abstract criteria numbers to a real physical meaning and shows the examiner you understand the purpose of the criteria, not just the values.

What is an Inclining Experiment? Purpose, principle, pre-inclining checks, and when must it be repeated?
StabilitySOLAS II-1Survey

Inclining Experiment — Purpose

The Inclining Experiment (IE) is the only direct method of determining a ship's actual lightship displacement and KG. The IE is carried out under an Administration/class-approved procedure; the resulting lightship displacement and KG are approved and become the reference values in the approved stability booklet — all loading condition GZ curves are derived from these values. Mandatory for all new ships before the stability booklet is approved (SOLAS II-1 Reg. 5-1).

Principle

Known masses are moved known transverse distances on deck. The resulting heel angle is measured:

GM = (w × d) / (W × tan θ)

Where: w = mass moved (t) | d = transverse distance (m) | W = ship's displacement (t) | θ = angle of heel produced.

Then: KG = KM − GM (KM from hydrostatic tables at surveyed draught).

Pre-Inclining Checks

  • Ship as close to lightship as possible — all cargo, consumables removed or precisely recorded.
  • All tanks pressed up or empty — free surfaces minimised.
  • No personnel moving on deck during readings; gangway singled up; mooring lines slack.
  • Weather: wind < Beaufort 3; ship floating freely, not touching bottom.
  • Pendulums or digital inclinometers calibrated and positioned per approved procedure.

When Must the IE Be Repeated?

  • After major structural modification (new deck, superstructure addition, major conversion).
  • When actual lightship displacement or LCG differs from booklet values beyond class tolerances — typically 2% in displacement or 0.5% of ship length in LCG. If a lightweight survey shows small differences within tolerances, a full IE may not be required; if differences exceed limits, a new IE is mandatory.
  • If Administration or class society requests it due to suspect stability performance.
⚖ Regulatory References
SOLAS II-1 / Reg. 5-1Information on stability — inclining test required for all ships
MSC.1/Circ.1229Guidelines for preparation and conduct of the inclining experiment
IS Code 2008 / Part BExplanatory notes — inclining experiment guidance
CE Oral Tip: When asked "when do you repeat the inclining experiment?", be specific: after major structural modifications, or when deviation from booklet values exceeds 2% in displacement or 0.5% of ship length in LCG. Generic answers like "after any modification" will not satisfy the examiner.

Contents of the Damage Control Plan. What must it contain per SOLAS, and where must it be kept?
Damage ControlSOLAS II-1

Damage Control Plan — SOLAS Requirement

SOLAS Chapter II-1 Regulation 19 requires that approved Damage Control Plans and Booklets be available to officers of cargo ships and passenger ships, showing the ship's watertight subdivision and means for controlling flooding.

Required Contents

  • General arrangement plan: All watertight bulkheads, decks, tank tops; flooding boundaries clearly marked.
  • Compartment identification: Each watertight compartment numbered/labelled with its free flooding volume and permeability factor.
  • Flooding scenarios: For each scenario — resulting waterline, heel, trim, reserve buoyancy.
  • All watertight door locations: Type (hinged or sliding), remote/local operation, bridge position indicator.
  • Cross-flooding arrangements: Cross-flooding duct locations (if fitted), to equalise flooding and reduce heel.
  • Bilge pumping arrangements: Pump capacities, suction positions, cross connections.
  • Residual stability: GZ and waterline for each flooding scenario — compliance with damage stability criteria shown.
  • List correction procedures: Ballasting sequence to correct heel after damage.

Location & Accessibility

The plan must be permanently displayed or readily available — typically at the bridge and/or entrance to accommodation. Plans must be in a language understood by the officers and, where necessary, accompanied by translations. They must be readily useable in an emergency — not filed away. Surveyors check their presence and accessibility during SAFCON and CSSCC surveys.

SOLAS II-1 / Reg. 19Damage control information — plans and booklets required to be kept on board

MSC.1/Circ.1245Guidelines for preparation of damage control documentation

What is the Shell Expansion Plan? How is it used, and what is Frame Number 0?
IACS / ClassSurvey

Shell Expansion Plan — Definition

The Shell Expansion Plan (SEP) is a plan view of the ship's outer shell plating — the hull "unrolled" and flattened. It shows every shell plate with:

  • Strake designation (A-strake at keel, ascending alphabetically upward)
  • Plate number within each strake
  • Plate material grade (A, B, D, E, or higher-strength AH, DH, EH)
  • Plate thickness as-built; updated with UT measurement records from surveys
  • Frame positions along the length; weld locations (butt and seam welds)
  • Insert plates, doublers, or recorded repairs

Used by class surveyors during Special Survey / Close-Up Survey to record UT measurements, document wastage against original thickness, identify areas needing steel renewal, and track structural condition over the ship's life. Surveyors and shipyards use the SEP together with midship section and framing plans to decide allowable wastage, required renewals, and to record steel repairs against original thickness and material grade.

Frame Number 0

Frame No. 0 is the Aft Perpendicular (AP) — the vertical reference line at the centreline of the rudder stock at the design waterline. Frames are numbered sequentially forward:

  • Frame 0 = Aft Perpendicular (AP)
  • Frame N = N × frame spacing forward of AP
  • Forward Perpendicular (FP) = highest frame number

This system allows any structural location to be precisely identified: "Strake D, Plate 3, Frames 24–28, Port Side" is an unambiguous location for steel renewal records.

IACS UR S31Thickness measurement of hull structure — use of shell expansion plan
SOLAS II-1 / Reg. 3-2Construction documents — plans to be maintained on board for entire service life
IRS Rules Part 2 / Ch. 1Ship drawings and plans required on board — IRS classification

SOLAS requirements for the Collision Bulkhead: position from FP, height, and permitted openings.
SOLAS II-1 Reg. 12

Collision Bulkhead — Purpose

The collision bulkhead is the foremost watertight transverse bulkhead. Its purpose: contain flooding to the forepeak compartment in the event of bow collision or grounding, protecting the rest of the ship.

Position (SOLAS II-1 / Reg. 12)

  • For cargo ships: not less than 0.05L from the Forward Perpendicular (FP)
  • Not more than 0.08L from the FP
  • Where L = ship's rule length between perpendiculars
  • Exception: large bow thruster tunnel or long forward structure → up to 0.10L from FP, with Administration approval.

Height

  • Must extend watertight to the freeboard deck (the main weathertight deck).

Permitted Openings

  • No doors, manholes, or access openings below the freeboard deck are permitted in the collision bulkhead.
  • Only pipe penetrations serving the forepeak tank are allowed — fitted with remotely controlled screw-down valves operable from above the freeboard deck.
  • Anchor chain pipes (hawse pipes) — must have effective closures (chain locker plugs, close-fitting stoppers) to prevent flooding progression.
  • Above the freeboard deck, doors/openings in any extension of the collision bulkhead may be permitted, provided they are weathertight and comply with Load Line/SOLAS requirements.
⚖ Regulatory References
SOLAS II-1 / Reg. 12Collision bulkhead — position and construction requirements
SOLAS II-1 / Reg. 13Forepeak and afterpeak tanks — additional requirements
IACS UR S19Evaluation of scantlings of the collision bulkhead

What is Propeller Blade Pitch Ratio (P/D)? Effect on efficiency and what is positive slip?
MachineryClass

Pitch Ratio — Definition

Pitch (P): Theoretical distance a propeller would advance in one revolution through a solid medium (no slip) — a geometric property of the blade angle.

Pitch Ratio = P / D

Dimensionless ratio of pitch to propeller diameter. Typical range: 0.6 to 1.4. Optimal range for commercial vessel efficiency: 0.8 to 1.0.

Effect on Efficiency

  • Low P/D (< 0.6): Near-flat blades, low thrust per revolution. Suited for tugs (high bollard pull, low speed).
  • Optimal P/D (0.8–1.0): Best balance of thrust and efficiency for normal merchant service speeds — peak propulsive efficiency η_p.
  • High P/D (> 1.2): High advance per revolution; suited for high-speed vessels, but cavitation risk increases at high power loading.

Propeller Slip

Apparent Slip = (P × N − V_a) / (P × N)

P = pitch | N = RPM | V_a = ship speed through water.

  • Positive slip (normal): Propeller turns faster than ship advances — normal in all ahead conditions due to wake and propeller losses.
  • Negative slip: Ship advances faster than P × N predicts — occurs in following seas when waves push the ship forward.
  • Note: The formula above gives apparent slip, based on ship speed through water. True slip uses the speed of advance in the propeller race (accounting for wake fraction); in practice, apparent slip is what is calculated and logged.

P/D and Design Point

In design, P/D is chosen so that at the ship's design speed and power, the propeller operates near its peak open-water efficiency. Large changes in service speed (e.g. slow-steaming) move the propeller away from this optimum — one reason modern container vessels select large-diameter, low-RPM, fixed-pitch propellers capable of reasonable efficiency across a range of operating speeds.

⚖ Regulatory References
IACS UR M68Propellers — manufacturing and testing requirements
ISO 484-1:2015Ship screw propellers — manufacturing tolerances (Class I and Class II)
CE Oral Tip: For container vessels, know your propeller's P/D ratio and diameter. Maersk vessels use large fixed-pitch propellers optimised for design speed (~22 kts). At current slow-steaming (12–16 kts), the propeller operates off its design point — this is one reason large-diameter, low-RPM propellers were chosen for the slow-steaming era. Shows examiner you understand operational context.

Tail Shaft Survey — what is it, when is it due, and what is the complete procedure?
MachineryIACS UR M10Survey

Tail Shaft Survey — Purpose

The Tail Shaft Survey is a mandatory class survey to inspect the propeller shaft (tail shaft) and stern tube assembly for wear, corrosion, cracking, and bearing condition. It verifies the shaft is fit for continued service. Governed by IACS UR M10 — the primary reference accepted by all major classification societies.

Survey Intervals

  • Traditional stern tube (oil-lubricated, bronze liner, white metal bearing): every 5 years — shaft must be withdrawn for inspection.
  • Approved systems (e.g., Wartsila Enviroseal, Thordon COMPAC, stainless solid shafts with water-lubricated polymer bearings): where condition-monitoring records are satisfactory, IACS UR M10 allows no-withdrawal or partial withdrawal surveys at some anniversaries, with interval extended to 7.5 or 10 years — subject to class criteria and satisfactory oil analysis, vibration monitoring, and seal leakage records.

Procedure — Traditional Shaft Withdrawal

  1. Dry docking (or shaft pulling facility).
  2. Propeller removed — nut and locking arrangements removed.
  3. Shaft withdrawn aft — stern tube and seals exposed.
  4. Visual inspection: Shaft surface for corrosion, pitting, cracks, scoring at bearing areas.
  5. Dimensional checks: Shaft diameter at bearing locations; wear-down measured (bearing clearance).
  6. NDT — dye penetrant or MPI at keyway, propeller cone taper, and any suspect areas.
  7. Stern tube bearings (white metal or polymer) — clearance measured; replaced if beyond limits.
  8. Stern tube seals (forward and aft) — replaced at each survey.
  9. Reassembly — clearances recorded; oil system filled and leak-tested.

Documents Required

  • Shaft withdrawal report with full dimensional records (journal diameters, bearing clearances, NDT results).
  • Class survey report and updated CMS / survey status from classification society.
  • Where the shaft is included in a CMS programme, each tail shaft survey (withdrawal or in-situ) must be recorded against the CMS item in the class survey status, with measured clearances and NDT results kept for trend monitoring across survey cycles.
