QB9_D Cheat Sheet

Commercial Law & Maritime Liability (D)

Rapid pre-exam recall — 15-second answers, key regulatory references, and CE Oral Tips for all 10 questions. Full answers in QB9_D.html.

Q1.P&I Club — what is it, 'call' explained, how does it work?
A P&I (Protection & Indemnity) Club is a mutual, non-profit insurance association of shipowners that covers third-party liabilities not protected under standard Hull & Machinery (H&M) policies. These liabilities include crew injury, pollution, cargo damage, and collision excess. Instead of fixed premiums, members pay "calls"—which are financial contributions to a shared pool based on estimated and actual claims during a policy year.
CLC Convention 1992LLMC Convention 1996Bunker Convention 2001ISM Code Element 9 & 10
Tip: When answering Simon at Kochi MMD, emphasize the word "Mutual" immediately within your first sentence. Clearly explain that the shipowner is both a customer and a shareholder of the club...
Q2.IG Group (International Group of P&I Clubs) — what is it and its pooling arrangements?
The International Group of P&I Clubs (IG Group) is an association of 12 member P&I clubs that collectively provide liability cover for approximately 90% of the world’s ocean-going tonnage. It operates a tiered pooling arrangement that allows member clubs to share catastrophic, high-value claims that exceed the financial retention capacity of any single club.
IG Group ConstitutionLOF / SCOPIC ClauseAthens Convention 2002Nairobi Wreck Removal Convention 2007
Tip: When Senthil asks about the IG Group, do not just say it is a collection of insurance companies...
Q3.Flag of convenience vs open registry — difference and examples?
An Open Registry allows shipowners of any nationality to register their vessels under its flag. A Flag of Convenience (FOC) is a derogatory term used by bodies like the International Transport Workers' Federation (ITF) for registries where there is no "genuine link" between the beneficial owner and the flag state, often characterized by low taxes and relaxed labor oversight. All FOCs are open registries, but not all open registries are FOCs.
UNCLOS Article 91IMO RO CodeITF FOC DeclarationParis MOU Targeting Matrix
Tip: Simon heavily tests the nuance between an FOC and a high-quality open registry. Do not generalize them as the same thing. Use Norway (NIS) or Singapore as your prime example of an open registry that is not an FOC...
Q4.Bharat Maritime Insurance Pool — what is it and its significance?
The Bharat Maritime Insurance Pool (BMIP) is an Indian domestic marine insurance initiative established around 2022 under GIC Re (General Insurance Corporation of India). Its primary purpose is to provide local Hull & Machinery (H&M), Protection & Indemnity (P&I), and war risk cover, thereby reducing the Indian shipping sector's total reliance on the London and Western insurance markets.
GIC Re Operational CharterMaritime Amritkal Vision 2047Sagarmala Programme DirectivesDGS Merchant Shipping Notices
Tip: This is a highly regarded national topic at Kochi MMD. When Simon asks about BMIP, always explicitly mention "GIC Re" and connect the answer to "reducing dependence on the London market"...
Q5.GIFT City — what is it and its relevance to maritime finance and insurance?
GIFT City (Gujarat International Finance Tec-City) is India's premier International Financial Services Centre (IFSC). Regulated by the IFSCA, it provides a specialized, tax-incentivized environment designed to handle USD-denominated marine insurance, ship leasing, ship registration, and maritime arbitration, competing directly with hubs like Singapore and Dubai.
IFSCA Act 2019IFSCA (Framework for Ship Leasing) 2021Income Tax Act Section 80LAMaritime Amritkal Vision 2047
Tip: Simon tests this specifically to check your awareness of contemporary maritime developments...
Q6.MS Act in detail — RPSL vs MS Act: differences and application?
The Merchant Shipping (MS) Act is the primary parent legislation passed by Parliament covering India's entire maritime domain. The Recruitment and Placement of Seafarers Regulations (RPSL) are a set of specific, subordinate rules enacted under Section 95 of the MS Act to regulate seafarer manning and placement agencies.
Merchant Shipping Act (India)MS (RPSL) Regulations 2018MLC 2006 Regulation 1.4MS Act Section 76 & 87
Tip: Simon uses this question to check if you understand the structural hierarchy of maritime law...
Q7.What is a contagious zone in maritime context?
A contagious zone is a designated quarantine or health-restricted area declared by a coastal state under the International Health Regulations (IHR 2005) to control infectious disease outbreaks. Vessels arriving from these zones face strict health screenings, quarantine mandates, and must be granted "Free Pratique" via the standard Q-flag signal before any shore interaction can commence.
International Health Regulations (IHR 2005)SOLAS Chapter V Regulation 20WHO Ship Sanitation GuidelinesMerchant Shipping (Port Health) Rules
Tip: Senthil highly respects precision regarding the Q-flag and Free Pratique. Open your answer by stating that the yellow flag means the ship is healthy but demanding a health clearance...
Q8.ELSA incident — where did it happen and how is the case handled? Under what laws?
The MSC Elsa 3 was a Liberian-flagged container vessel that capsized and sank 14.6 nautical miles off the coast of Kerala, India, on May 25, 2025. The disaster was caused by a catastrophic failure of its ballast water handling and heeling correction systems, combined with structural neglect and crew errors. The case was handled under India's Merchant Shipping Act, 1958 (the law in force at the time of the incident, 25 May 2025; since replaced by the Merchant Shipping Act, 2025 with effect from 15 March 2026), the Admiralty Act 2017, and — because India has not ratified the Bunker Convention 2001 or the Nairobi Wreck Removal Convention 2007 — the shipowner's liability limitation instead falls back on the LLMC 1976/1996 Protocol, to which India is a party.
UNCLOS Article 56 & 58LLMC 1976/1996 ProtocolAdmiralty Act 2017 (India)Territorial Waters, Continental Shelf, and EEZ Act 1976
Tip: Senthil expects you to know this case intimately because it occurred locally in Kerala waters...
Q9.Act vs rule — difference in Indian legal context with maritime examples?
An Act is primary legislation debated and passed by the Parliament of India that establishes broad legal frameworks. A Rule is subordinate, delegated legislation drafted by the Executive branch (Ministries/DGS) to provide the specific, technical mechanisms required to implement that parent Act.

Q10.Coastal state duties — obligations under UNCLOS?
Under UNCLOS, a coastal state possesses sovereign rights but is bound by strict duties across its maritime zones. In its Territorial Sea (up to 12 nm), it must guarantee and not hamper the right of innocent passage, publish all known navigational hazards, and levy no transit charges. In its EEZ (up to 200 nm), it must protect and preserve the marine environment, manage living resources sustainably, and allow freedom of navigation to foreign vessels.
UNCLOS Article 17-26UNCLOS Article 56 & 58UNCLOS Part XII (Articles 192-196)Territorial Waters, Continental Shelf, and EEZ Act 1976 (India)
Tip: When Paul asks this question, ensure you emphasize that you are detailing Duties, not rights. Candidates often start listing resource rights (fishing, oil drilling), which is the opposite of what he asked...
