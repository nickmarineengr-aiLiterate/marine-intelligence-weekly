SEEMP Part III — why was it added and what are its specific contents?
GHG / CII MARPOL Survey
⏱ 15-Second Answer SEEMP Part III was added under MARPOL Annex VI, Regulation 26 by MEPC.328(76) to enforce operational compliance with the Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) framework. Unlike EEXI which is a one-time technical design check, SEEMP Part III is a mandatory operational management plan for ships ≥5,000 GT, detailing how the vessel calculates, monitors, and improves its annual carbon efficiency rating.
🎯 60-Second Answer SEEMP Part III bridges the gap between fixed structural design metrics and real-time operational fuel efficiency. The EEXI is a one-off technical certificate; the CII requires continuous operational tracking — hence Part III was mandated as the dynamic management tool. For all vessels 5,000 GT and above, Part III specifies the methodology to calculate the attained CII, states the required annual target values from 2023–2030, and lists operational reduction measures such as weather routing and engine power optimisation. Critically, it must incorporate an approved Corrective Action Plan if the vessel receives a D rating for three consecutive years or an E rating in any single year.

1. Why SEEMP Part III Was Needed

SEEMP Part III was adopted via amendments to MARPOL Annex VI, Regulation 26, entering into force 1 January 2023.

  • The technical–operational gap: EEXI evaluates a vessel's structural energy efficiency (shaft power limits, hull form) as a one-time certification. It does not track how the ship is operated in service.
  • CII dynamic mandate: CII measures actual operational GHG emissions per unit of cargo capacity and distance sailed each calendar year. Because performance varies with trade route, speed, and fuel choice, IMO mandated SEEMP Part III as the live management tool to track and improve CII compliance year on year.

2. Application Scope

  • Applies to: All ships ≥5,000 GT engaged in international voyages — same scope as MARPOL Annex VI CII reporting.
  • Certification cycle: Unlike Parts I and II, Part III must be verified and certified by the Flag State or authorised RO before each three-year implementation cycle.

3. Mandatory Contents of SEEMP Part III

A certified Part III document must contain five structural elements:

  • Methodology for attained CII calculation: Specifies the data streams (IMO DCS fuel monitoring) and mathematical approach used to compute the annual attained CII.
  • Required annual CII target values: Lists the vessel's mandatory reduction boundary values from 2023 to 2030, showing the step reductions required each year.
  • Operational carbon reduction measures: Detailed action plan — speed optimisation profiles, weather routing integration, hull cleaning schedules, propeller polishing, auxiliary load management in port.
  • Self-evaluation and review mechanism: Framework for how shipboard management and the company track energy efficiency trends and identify performance gaps during the reporting year.
  • Corrective Action Plan (when triggered): Appended when CII rating triggers the enforcement mechanism (see below).

4. Corrective Action Plan — Trigger and Obligations

  • Trigger — E rating: Any single-year E rating immediately requires a Corrective Action Plan.
  • Trigger — D rating: Three consecutive years at D rating requires a Corrective Action Plan.
  • Plan content: Root-cause analysis of poor efficiency, specific technical or operational modifications (ESDs, fuel switch), and a clear timeline to restore rating to C or above.
  • Consequence of non-compliance: Flag State withholds the Statement of Compliance (SoC). Without a valid SoC, the vessel is subject to port state detention and barred from international commercial trading.

⚠ Trap Questions

"If your ship gets a D rating this year, do you need to update SEEMP Part III immediately?" — No. A single D rating does not trigger a mandatory Corrective Action Plan update. The trigger is D for three consecutive years or E in any single year. Candidates who answer "yes" to a single D fail this question.

"Is SEEMP Part III the same as EEXI?" — No. EEXI is a one-time technical design metric (structural certification). SEEMP Part III is a dynamic operational management plan updated annually and formally verified before each three-year cycle.

Onboard carbon capture — how does it work? What is carbon sequestration?
GHG Machinery
⏱ 15-Second Answer Onboard Carbon Capture and Storage (OCCS) captures CO₂ from engine exhaust before atmospheric release. The primary method is post-combustion chemical absorption (amine scrubbing) — exhaust passes through an absorber column containing monoethanolamine (MEA) solvent, which binds CO₂. The captured gas is liquefied under pressure and stored in cryogenic tanks for discharge ashore. Carbon sequestration is the permanent underground storage of that liquid CO₂ — in depleted oil fields, deep saline aquifers, or via mineral carbonation in basalt rock.
🎯 60-Second Answer OCCS removes CO₂ directly from main engine exhaust. In amine scrubbing: exhaust gas is cooled to approximately 40–50°C, then enters an absorber column where MEA solvent chemically binds the CO₂. The CO₂-rich solvent passes to a stripper column where auxiliary boiler steam at 120–140°C breaks the bonds, releasing pure CO₂ and regenerating the lean amine for reuse. The released CO₂ is compressed to 15–20 bar, chilled to approximately −30 to −50°C, and stored as liquid CO₂ in cryogenic tanks onboard. Note: every tonne of marine fuel burned produces approximately 3.1 tonnes of CO₂ — so storage demands are significant. Carbon sequestration is the final step where offloaded liquid CO₂ is injected permanently into geological formations: depleted reservoirs, deep saline aquifers, or mineralised in basalt structures.

1. Post-Combustion Amine Scrubbing — Step by Step

  • Pre-cooling: High-temperature exhaust from main engine / auxiliary diesels passes through a particulate filter and cooling tower, reducing temperature to 40–50°C to protect solvent stability downstream.
  • Absorption column: Cooled exhaust enters the bottom of a vertical absorber tower, flowing upward against a counter-current spray of Monoethanolamine (MEA) or piperazine formulations. The amine chemically bonds with CO₂ molecules. The remaining clean exhaust (N₂, H₂O vapour) vents safely from the top.
  • Stripping and regeneration: CO₂-rich solvent is pumped through a heat exchanger into a stripper column. Auxiliary boiler steam at 120–140°C breaks the chemical bonds, releasing pure CO₂ gas from the top while regenerating lean amine, which is cooled and recycled to the absorber column continuously.

2. Liquefaction and Onboard Storage

  • Compression: Pure CO₂ gas exits the stripper and is routed through multi-stage compressors to approximately 15–20 bar.
  • Cryogenic refrigeration: Pressurised gas is chilled to approximately −30 to −50°C, converting it to liquid CO₂ (LCO₂).
  • Storage: LCO₂ is transferred to vacuum-insulated Type-C cryogenic containment tanks on deck or in a dedicated compartment, maintained throughout the voyage.
  • Mass penalty: Every tonne of marine fuel burned produces approximately 3.1 tonnes of CO₂. Storage tanks must accommodate this expansion — and the added weight reduces commercial deadweight capacity and impacts vessel stability.

3. Carbon Sequestration — Three Permanent Storage Pathways

  • Depleted offshore oil/gas reservoirs: LCO₂ is injected under pressure into depleted fields where impermeable cap rock traps the gas indefinitely.
  • Deep saline aquifers: Injected thousands of metres into permeable saltwater sand formations; CO₂ dissolves into brine and sinks due to density differences.
  • Mineral carbonation in basalt: CO₂ reacts with basaltic rock, chemically mineralising into solid carbonate (limestone-like) structures within years — effectively permanent.

4. Energy Penalty and Engineering Constraints

  • Auxiliary energy demand: Amine regeneration requires continuous auxiliary boiler steam; compressor operation draws significant electrical load. Overall fuel consumption penalty estimated at 10–15% — a significant negative when assessing lifecycle GHG benefit.
  • Capture efficiency target: Advanced marine amine installations target >80% CO₂ capture efficiency from exhaust streams.
  • Space and weight: LCO₂ at 3.1:1 mass ratio relative to fuel burned requires large dedicated tank space and heavy structural supports — engineering and stability implications are considerable.
⚠ Examiner Trap "When you capture 10 tonnes of CO₂ from the exhaust, does it store as 10 tonnes in your tanks?" — Yes, mass is conserved — 10 tonnes stays 10 tonnes by weight. The trap is volume: for every 1 tonne of marine fuel burned, combustion produces approximately 3.1 tonnes of CO₂. So on a per-fuel-burn basis the storage demand is threefold higher than the fuel consumed, not the CO₂ captured. Distinguish clearly between mass conservation and the fuel-to-CO₂ mass multiplier.

🔧 CE Relevance

As 2/E managing an OCCS installation, you are responsible for: maintaining chemical dosing pumps and monitoring solvent purity levels (MEA degradation from SO₂ and O₂ contaminants); monitoring multi-stage CO₂ compressors; managing cryogenic refrigeration loops and tank pressure/temperature; and monitoring differential pressure across absorber column packing. Any absorber foaming event (from contaminants or surfactant ingress) can cause solvent carry-over up the exhaust stack, fouling downstream equipment. Cryogenic LCO₂ leaks onto standard carbon steel deck structures risk localised freezing and brittle fracture of structural members.

⚠ Trap Questions

"Is an onboard carbon capture system the same as a scrubber?" — No. A scrubber (EGC system) removes SO₂ from exhaust using seawater or caustic solution for MARPOL Annex VI sulphur compliance. OCCS removes CO₂ using amine chemistry for GHG reduction. Completely different chemical reactions, different exhaust streams targeted, different regulatory frameworks.

"Does OCCS make a ship carbon neutral?" — No. OCCS only captures exhaust CO₂; it does not address methane slip, nitrous oxide emissions, or the fuel lifecycle upstream emissions. IMO classification requires lifecycle (Well-to-Wake) assessment for GHG credit.

  • Missing the energy penalty — failing to mention the 10–15% fuel consumption increase from auxiliary boiler and compressor demand

EU MRV, FuelEU Maritime, and EU ETS — explain each and their cross-linkage with IMO's CII framework.
GHG / CII MARPOL Survey
⏱ 15-Second Answer The EU MRV monitors and verifies fuel consumption and emissions on EU port calls — it provides the data foundation. The EU ETS uses that data to impose carbon allowance purchase obligations — phased to 100% by 2026. FuelEU Maritime mandates a progressive reduction in fuel greenhouse gas intensity — starting at 2% below 2020 baseline from 2025, reaching 80% by 2050. The global IMO CII framework runs in parallel — same data streams, different scope, no direct financial penalty.
🎯 60-Second Answer The EU operates a three-layer carbon management system for ships above 5,000 GT calling at EEA ports. The EU MRV (Regulation 2015/757) mandates monitoring and third-party verification of fuel consumption and CO₂ emissions on voyages to, from, or between EU ports — producing a mandatory Document of Compliance onboard. This data feeds the EU ETS (Directive 2003/87/EC), effective from 1 January 2024, requiring shipping companies to surrender European Union Allowances (EUAs) for verified emissions — 40% in 2024, 70% in 2025, 100% from 2026. Running in parallel, FuelEU Maritime (Regulation 2023/1805), effective 1 January 2025, mandates reduction in the well-to-wake Greenhouse Gas Intensity of shipboard energy (its own 91.16 gCO2eq/MJ 2020 baseline — not to be confused with the separate IMO GFI metric, 93.3 gCO2eq/MJ 2008 baseline) — 2% reduction 2025–2029, rising to 80% by 2050. These EU instruments interface with the IMO CII via shared fuel data but differ fundamentally: CII grades operational efficiency without financial penalties, while the EU applies direct financial obligations on top.

1. EU MRV — The Data Foundation

  • Instrument: EU Regulation 2015/757 (Monitoring, Reporting and Verification of CO₂ Emissions from Maritime Transport)
  • Applies to: Ships >5,000 GT calling at EEA ports, for voyages to, from, or between EU ports
  • What is monitored: Fuel consumption per fuel type, CO₂ and other GHG emissions, distance sailed, time at sea, cargo carried (for efficiency metrics)
  • Verification: Data must be independently verified by an accredited third-party verifier annually
  • Output document: A Document of Compliance (DoC) for EU MRV must be carried onboard the vessel
  • Data use: EU MRV data feeds directly into EU ETS allowance surrender calculations

2. EU ETS — The Carbon Tax Mechanism

  • Instrument: EU Directive 2003/87/EC (Emissions Trading System) — extended to maritime shipping from 1 January 2024
  • Mechanism: Cap-and-trade system — shipping companies must purchase and surrender European Union Allowances (EUAs) for verified GHG emissions. 1 EUA = 1 tonne of CO₂ equivalent
  • Phased compliance timeline:
    • 2024: 40% of verified emissions must be covered by EUAs
    • 2025: 70% of verified emissions
    • 2026 and beyond: 100% of verified emissions
  • Voyage coverage: 100% of emissions on voyages between EU ports; 50% of emissions on voyages arriving from or departing to non-EU ports
  • Non-compliance: Excess emissions penalty + port entry ban after two consecutive years of non-compliance

3. FuelEU Maritime — Fuel Carbon Intensity Limits

  • Instrument: EU Regulation 2023/1805, effective 1 January 2025
  • What it regulates: The well-to-wake Greenhouse Gas Intensity of the energy used onboard — measured in grams of CO₂ equivalent per megajoule (gCO₂eq/MJ), against FuelEU's own 91.16 gCO2eq/MJ 2020 baseline (do not label this "GFI" — that acronym belongs to the separate IMO Net-Zero Framework metric, baselined at 93.3 gCO2eq/MJ off 2008 data)
  • Baseline: 2020 reference GFI value for fossil marine fuels
  • Reduction pathway:
    • 2025–2029: Mandatory 2% GFI reduction below 2020 baseline
    • 2030–2034: 6% reduction
    • 2035–2039: 14.5% reduction
    • 2040–2044: 31% reduction
    • 2045–2049: 62% reduction
    • 2050 onwards: 80% reduction
  • Penalty for non-compliance: Financial penalty calculated based on the energy deficit multiplied by a reference cost of alternative low-carbon fuels

🔧 CE Relevance

As 2/E on a Maersk container vessel operating regular Europe calls, EU ETS compliance is directly relevant to your documentation duties. You must ensure fuel oil mass flow meter readings are accurately recorded by consumer (main engine, auxiliaries, boiler), and that fuel changeover records for bio-blends or LNG are precise. ⚠ Trap Questions

"If your ship achieves an IMO CII A-rating, is it exempt from buying EU ETS allowances?" — No. CII rating and EU ETS obligations are entirely separate frameworks. An A CII rating means the vessel operates efficiently — it does not reduce the financial obligation to purchase EUAs for actual CO₂ emitted on EU-touching voyages.

"Does FuelEU Maritime apply only to ships trading within EU ports?" — It applies to voyages to, from, or between EEA ports. It is not limited to intra-EU routes. A ship arriving from Singapore to Rotterdam is subject to EU MRV and ETS obligations (50% EUA coverage for the international leg).

  • Stating a CII A-rating exempts from EU ETS — it does not
  • Confusing FuelEU Maritime's Well-to-Wake GFI metric with the CII's Tank-to-Wake (operational only) metric
