Concept Map

Oil-pollution civil-liability chain: CLC (tanker cargo oil) hands off to the Fund Convention for top-up compensation, with the Bunker Convention plugging the fuel-oil gap CLC leaves open, and LLMC as the general fallback limit.

Confusable Pairs — Examiner Favourites

⚠ Confusable Pair: CLC vs Bunker Convention
CLC covers oil carried as cargo on tankers (persistent oil spills). Bunker Convention 2001 covers oil carried as fuel on any ship, any cargo type — created because CLC left non-tanker fuel spills uncovered.
⚠ Confusable Pair: Tort vs Crime
A tort is a civil wrong (compensation to the injured party); a crime is an offence against the state (prosecution, punishment). The same act — e.g. a collision from negligence — can trigger both simultaneously.
⚠ Confusable Pair: Blue Economy vs Marine Spatial Planning
Blue Economy is the economic concept — sustainable use of ocean resources for growth. Marine Spatial Planning is the practical tool used to allocate sea-space between competing uses (shipping lanes, fishing, wind farms) to make that economy work without conflict.

Insurance & Civil Liability
Q3. War Time Marine Insurance & Extra Premiums
Tap to reveal the 15-second answer →
War Risk Insurance covers damage and liabilities arising from acts of war, terrorism, sabotage, and political seizures, which are excluded from standard Hull and Machinery (H&M) policies. The liability for the additional premium (known as the Breach Premium or War Risk Additional Premium) depends on the Charter Party agreement; under standard Time Charters, the Charterer covers this cost if they direct the vessel into an active, underwriter-designated war risk zone.
Q4. CLC and its Explanation
Tap to reveal the 15-second answer →
The CLC (International Convention on Civil Liability for Oil Pollution Damage) establishes a framework of strict liability and compulsory insurance for shipowners in the event of persistent oil pollution damage originating from tankers. While the CLC specifically covers oil carried as cargo or in the bunkers of a laden tanker, the International Convention on Civil Liability for Bunker Oil Pollution Damage (Bunker Convention) extends a similar strict liability framework to non-tankers, ensuring coverage for any fuel oil spill from vessels like container ships.
Q5. What is Insurance?
Tap to reveal the 15-second answer →
Marine insurance is a commercial contract whereby the underwriter (insurer) undertakes to indemnify the shipowner or cargo owner (assured) against maritime losses incident to a marine adventure. It relies on critical legal principles: insurable interest, indemnity, subrogation, proximate cause, and utmost good faith (uberrimae fidei).
Q7. P&I Club Coverage in Hormuz
Tap to reveal the 15-second answer →
Standard P&I Club rules contain a Mutual War Risk Exclusion clause, meaning regular third-party liabilities arising from acts of war are excluded during a transit through the Strait of Hormuz. To maintain coverage, shipowners must secure P&I War Risk cover (either through a specialized hull war underwriter or a P&I Club war risk extension). Underwriters can cancel or amend terms for high-risk transits by issuing a formal Notice of Cancellation, typically requiring 7 days' notice.
Q12. Inchmaree Clause in Marine Insurance
Tap to reveal the 15-second answer →
The Inchmaree Clause is a standard provision in Marine Hull & Machinery (H&M) insurance policies that extends coverage to losses caused by the negligence of the master, officers, or crew, as well as bursts in boilers, breakages of shafts, or latent defects in the hull and machinery, provided such loss did not result from a lack of due diligence by the shipowner or managers.
Legal & Regulatory Frameworks
Q1. TMSA
Tap to reveal the 15-second answer →
TMSA stands for Tanker Management and Self Assessment, a tool introduced by the Oil Companies International Marine Forum (OCIMF) to help vessel operators assess, measure, and continuously improve their safety management systems. It uses a structured framework across 13 elements, shifting focus from baseline compliance to continuous performance excellence. For a Chief Engineer, it serves as the operational benchmark for safety, environmental leadership, and preventative maintenance regimes.
Q2. Latest MS Act Updates & DG Website Differences
Tap to reveal the 15-second answer →
The latest updates to the Merchant Shipping (MS) Act center around consolidating maritime laws, digitizing statutory services, implementing stricter marine environmental penalties, and codifying advanced MLC requirements. The transition from the old DG Shipping website to the new e-governance portal shifts the framework from a fragmented, manual-upload document repository to an integrated, single-window database that verifies data in real-time.
Q8. MS Act Provisions for Competency Exams
Tap to reveal the 15-second answer →
The statutory provisions enabling and regulating certificates of competency for seafarers in India are contained within Part VI of the Merchant Shipping Act, 1958, specifically Sections 78, 79, and 87. These sections empower the Central Government to define officer grades, conduct examinations, appoint examiners, and formulate the Merchant Shipping (Standards of Training, Certification, and Watchkeeping for Seafarers) Rules.
Q11. Difference Between Tort and Crime in Maritime Context
Tap to reveal the 15-second answer →
A tort is a civil wrong independent of a contract where one party's negligence or wrongful act causes harm to another, resulting in a civil lawsuit for monetary damages. A crime is an offense against the state or public law, prosecuted by the government, resulting in criminal penalties such as fines or imprisonment.
Policy & Sustainability
Q6. Blue Economy in Detail
Tap to reveal the 15-second answer →
The Blue Economy refers to the sustainable use of ocean resources for economic growth, improved livelihoods, and jobs, while preserving the health of ocean ecosystems. For the maritime sector, it demands a shift toward zero-emission propulsion, eco-efficient port operations, waste minimization, and alignment with national strategies like India's SAGAR vision.
Q9. Marine Spatial Planning
Tap to reveal the 15-second answer →
Marine Spatial Planning (MSP) is a public, multi-sectoral process that analyzes and allocates the spatial and temporal distribution of human activities in marine areas to achieve ecological, economic, and social objectives. It prevents conflicts between competing ocean users—such as commercial shipping lanes, offshore wind farms, and naval zones—while preserving fragile ecosystems.
Q10. Blue Economy
Tap to reveal the 15-second answer →
The Blue Economy represents a sustainable maritime development model that reconciles industrial ocean usage with environmental protection. For modern shipping companies, it demands aggressive decarbonization, strict energy efficiency tracking, and clean ballast and waste management to preserve the marine biosphere while maintaining global trade.
