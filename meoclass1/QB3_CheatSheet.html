📐 GZ Curve & Angle of Loll — The Three GM States

The single most commonly hand-drawn diagram in MMD orals (Rajappan). Shows the righting arm (GZ) vs heel angle for positive GM (stable ship), zero GM (neutral, "tender"), and negative GM (angle of loll — unstable, the trap case). (Relates to QB3 Q2.)

Positive GM — ship rights itself (normal stable condition) Zero GM — neutral, minimal restoring force ("tender" ship) Negative GM — angle of loll, GZ=0 at a non-zero angle (the trap)

📈 EEDI Reference Line — Container Ship Adjustment

Reference EEDI = a × Capacity⁻ᶜ. For container ships, Capacity = 0.7 × DWT (volume-limited "cube out" basis) — the detail examiners probe for. Required EEDI sits below the reference line by the phase reduction factor (1−X); Attained EEDI must fall at or below the Required EEDI line. (Relates to QB3 Q16.)

Reference line (historical fleet average, by ship type) Required EEDI (reference shifted down by phase factor) Container-ship-specific capacity adjustment

⚡ Quick-Fire 15s Index — All 18 Questions

The 15-second answer for every QB3 question, for rapid-fire pre-oral revision. Click through for the full structured answer, reg-box and CE tip.

1SOLAS Chapter XII — what does it cover, and why is it relevant to bulk carriers?
SOLAS Chapter XII, "Additional Safety Measures for Bulk Carriers," sets structural strength, double-side-skin, loadicator and water-ingress alarm (WIDAS) requirements for bulk carriers above 150m, triggered after the one-hold flooding standard was introduced post-Derbyshire. On my container ship, the equivalent water-ingress alarm requirement is SOLAS II-1 Reg 25-1.
Full answer →
2What is the difference between heel and list, and how is each corrected?
Heel is a temporary, external-force-induced inclination (wind, turning) that self-corrects once the force is removed; list is a permanent, internally-caused inclination (weight imbalance) corrected by re-distributing weight using w×d = W×GM×tanθ. Before correcting any list, always rule out angle of loll — a negative-GM condition where filling the high-side tank can capsize the ship.
Full answer →
3What is a Ship Construction File, and what does it contain?
A Ship Construction File is a SOLAS II-1 Reg 3-10 lifetime structural record for new bulk carriers and oil tankers — drawings, scantlings, steel grades, critical areas, coatings and survey history — formatted per IACS CSR, used as the build baseline for UTM and steel renewal decisions.
Full answer →
4Explain class notations using your last vessel as an example.
Class notations are codes on the class certificate showing a vessel is built and maintained to specific hull, machinery and operational standards — basic class symbol, service notation (e.g. Container Ship), and additional notations like UMS, ice class or environmental notations.
Full answer →
5What are the requirements for drydock planning, and what surveys coincide with a planned drydock?
Drydock planning means meeting the HSSC rule that the bottom is inspected twice in five years (max 36 months apart), while bundling in special, bottom, tailshaft and ESP surveys plus all planned repairs, steel renewals and statutory certificate renewals.
Full answer →
6Explain the Enhanced Survey Programme (ESP) — what does it cover and what does the ESP file contain?
ESP (Enhanced Survey Programme) applies close-up surveys and UTM thickness measurements to bulk carriers and oil tankers at intermediate and special surveys, compiling findings into the ESP file — survey reports, UTM records, CER and repair history.
Full answer →
7What is the recent direction of ESP survey tightening, and what drives it?
ESP survey tightening reflects increased UTM coverage, more critical-area focus, and stricter CER follow-up, driven by ongoing bulk carrier/tanker structural casualties and IACS Common Structural Rules updates.
Full answer →
8What is a Loadicator, and what are the latest regulatory requirements/amendments concerning it?
A loadicator is an onboard computer that calculates stability (GM, GZ, intact and damage), bending moment/shear force and trim for any loading condition. SOLAS Ch XII Reg 11 / II-1 Reg 5 mandate it; MSC.1/Circ.1229 governs software approval; latest amendments require Type 3 (real-time damage stability) capability on many newer ships.
Full answer →
9What certificates are issued under the AFS Convention, who issues them, and what is their validity?
The AFS Convention requires an International Anti-fouling System Certificate (IAFSC) confirming the hull coating contains no prohibited biocides (e.g. organotin/TBT compounds); issued by flag/class on survey, valid for the vessel's life unless the system changes.
Full answer →
10What is the difference between an "unsafe ship" and an "unseaworthy ship"?
An unseaworthy ship is a legal/admiralty concept — unfit for its intended voyage due to structural, equipment, crew or stowage deficiencies, assessed at the start of the voyage. An unsafe ship is a statutory/PSC concept — fails to meet specific convention/certificate requirements, identified at any time via inspection, leading to detention.
Full answer →
11What are the steel plate renewal criteria for hull structural members?
Steel renewal is governed by allowable diminution — class/IACS limits on permissible thickness loss (as % of as-built thickness) per structural member. UTM readings below the limit mandate renewal; approaching the limit (~75% of margin consumed) flags 'substantial corrosion' (Q12) for closer monitoring.
Full answer →
12What is "substantial corrosion" and how is it assessed?
Substantial corrosion is a class/IACS early-warning classification for an area approaching, but not yet exceeding, the allowable diminution limit (Q11); identified via UTM, it triggers closer monitoring and CER follow-up rather than immediate renewal.
Full answer →
13What do you check during a ballast water tank inspection?
Ballast tank inspections check coating and structural condition, fittings, anodes and cleanliness against PSPC, while strictly following enclosed-space entry procedures, because ballast tanks are high-corrosion, high-risk spaces.
Full answer →
14How do Ship Construction File (SCF) and ESP/UTM interact in practice?
The SCF (build baseline — drawings, scantlings, steel grades, critical areas) and ESP/UTM (life-cycle survey results) interact as reference vs. measurement: UTM readings are compared against SCF baseline thicknesses to calculate diminution, classify substantial corrosion, and plan steel renewal.
Full answer →
15What is the SEQ survey, and what is the supplement to the Cargo Ship Safety Equipment Certificate?
The SEQ (Safety Equipment) survey covers life-saving and fire-fighting appliances under the Cargo Ship Safety Equipment Certificate; its supplement (Form E / Record of Equipment) lists the actual equipment fitted, types, quantities and locations, which the CE must keep matching reality.
Full answer →
16EEDI reference line and container ships (context add‑on)
The EEDI reference line is Reference EEDI = a × Capacity⁻ᶜ, with a/c regression parameters from 2000-2010-built ships; for container ships, Capacity = 0.7 × DWT (volume-limited 'cube out' basis). Required EEDI = Reference EEDI × (1 − X), and Attained EEDI must be ≤ Required EEDI.
Full answer →
17What is the class quarterly listing / survey status report, and how does the CE use it?
The class quarterly listing / survey status report lists all certificates, survey due dates, conditions of class and outstanding memoranda for the vessel; the CE uses it as a live planning tool to ensure no statutory or class survey deadline is missed and to schedule drydock/maintenance accordingly.
Full answer →
18What is the significance of tribology / lube oil analysis (LOA) reports, and what actions does the Chief Engineer take based on them?
Tribology is the science of friction, lubrication and wear; routine lube oil analysis (LOA) tracks viscosity, TBN, water/fuel contamination and wear-metal content so the CE can adjust feed rates, oil change intervals and target maintenance before failures occur.
