Q1. SEEMP Part III

🌍 GHG Management

SEEMP Part III — why was it added? What are its contents? What is the CE's role?
GHG / CII MARPOL Survey
15-Second AnswerSEEMP Part III was added by MEPC 76 (2021, in force 1 January 2023) to operationalise CII compliance — it is the ship's rolling operational carbon-intensity plan, mandatory for ships ≥5,000 GT, because Parts I and II collected data but created no enforceable target or consequence.
60-Second AnswerBefore 2021, SEEMP Part I (voluntary efficiency measures) and Part II (mandatory IMO DCS fuel data collection) existed, but neither created an enforceable operational target — ships reported data with no required response to poor performance. MEPC 76 added Part III, guided by the 2022 Guidelines (MEPC.346(78)), to close that gap: it sets the required CII rating for the current and following years, documents planned/implemented operational measures, defines the self-evaluation methodology, and — critically — requires a Corrective Action Plan if the ship receives an "E" rating in any year or a "D" rating for three consecutive years. Applicable to ships ≥5,000 GT from 1 January 2023, verified with a Confirmation of Compliance / Statement of Compliance (SoC). The framework was further updated by MEPC.395(82) (2024 Guidelines), which standardised the SEEMP II/III format and expanded reporting — transport work, power at berth, fuel consumption by emission source — effective from 2026 reporting.

Why Part III Was Added — The Legislative Gap

SEEMP Parts I and II pre-existed the CII framework: Part I was a voluntary energy efficiency plan (mandatory to carry, but not verified against a target), and Part II was the mandatory IMO Data Collection System (DCS) for fuel consumption, in force since 2019. Neither created an enforceable consequence for a ship performing poorly — data was collected but no rating or corrective obligation followed. The Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII), introduced by MEPC 76 (June 2021) as part of the 2021 revision of MARPOL Annex VI, created the missing enforcement mechanism, and Part III is where that mechanism lives operationally — a binding plan linking the ship's CII rating to a mandatory response.

Contents of SEEMP Part III

  • The required CII for the current year and the following two years — a rolling 3-year planning horizon.
  • An implementation plan — operational measures already in place and planned (speed optimisation, hull/propeller cleaning schedule, trim optimisation, weather routing, waste heat recovery, VFDs, fuel switching) to achieve the target CII.
  • The methodology for onboard data collection and CII calculation.
  • A self-evaluation and improvement procedure — annual review cycle against the plan.
  • A Corrective Action Plan (CAP), required only if the ship receives an "E" rating in any single year, or a "D" rating for three consecutive years — describing specific measures, timeline, and responsible parties to restore at least a "C" rating.

When Part III Must Be Revised

  • At the start of each rolling 3-year cycle.
  • Mandatorily, if a CAP is triggered — the CAP must be submitted together with, and no later than one month after, reporting the attained annual CII.
  • On company or flag change, or a major conversion altering the CII baseline.
  • Revised plans require re-verification by the Administration or Recognized Organisation before a new Statement/Confirmation of Compliance is issued.

2024 Update — MEPC.395(82)

The 2024 Guidelines (MEPC.395(82)) standardised and expanded the SEEMP Part II/III reporting format — adding mandatory transport-work (cargo) reporting, fuel consumption reported separately by emission source (main engines, auxiliaries, boilers), power-at-berth/shore-power monitoring, and event-based voyage reporting (BOSP, EOSP, FAOP). Existing ships continue under the previous format until the transition date, then move to the expanded MEPC.395(82) format — a candidate should be aware this superseded elements of the original MEPC.346(78) guidance rather than replacing SEEMP Part III itself.

CE's Role

  • Primary executor of the operational measures listed in Part III — accurate fuel data recording, main/auxiliary engine efficiency monitoring (SFOC, cylinder pressures), hull and propeller condition tracking and cleaning scheduling.
  • Reports deviations from planned measures to the shore performance team promptly, not retrospectively.
  • Participates directly in Corrective Action Plan development if the ship is rated D (3 consecutive years) or E (any year).

Statement of Compliance vs IEEC

The IEEC (International Energy Efficiency Certificate) has no fixed expiry and is issued once when EEXI is verified. The Statement/Confirmation of Compliance for SEEMP Part III is issued annually, after the year's CII data has been submitted and verified. Both are carried onboard and both are checked during Port State Control.

CE Oral Tip: Nair's angle is specifically "why was Part III added" — the legislative-logic question, not just its contents. Answer: "Sir, Parts I and II existed before 2021 but created no enforceable operational consequence for poor performance. Part III was added by MEPC 76 to close that gap — a binding 3-year operational roadmap linking CII rating to a mandatory Corrective Action Plan. A ship rated D for three consecutive years, or E in any year, must revise Part III with a CAP before it can receive the next Statement of Compliance." If pressed further, mention that MEPC.395(82) (2024) has since updated the reporting format itself.
CE Relevance, Traps, Failures & Exam Strategy
CE Relevance

The CE's fuel data recording and machinery efficiency monitoring feed directly into the annual CII calculation — inaccurate ORB/DCS data doesn't just risk a MARPOL deficiency, it can distort the ship's CII rating and trigger an unwarranted Corrective Action Plan.

Trap Questions

Q: "Does SEEMP Part III expire? What happens if the Statement of Compliance is not renewed?"

A: SEEMP Part III itself doesn't expire, but the Statement/Confirmation of Compliance must be renewed annually after each year's CII data is verified. Without a current SoC, the vessel faces a Port State Control deficiency.

Q: "How is the 2024 update (MEPC.395(82)) different from Part III itself?"

A: MEPC.395(82) doesn't change the existence or purpose of Part III — it standardises and expands the SEEMP II/III reporting format (transport work, power at berth, fuel by emission source), improving the data quality feeding into the CII rating.

Common CE Failures

1. Confusing Part III with Part I (calling it "just the efficiency plan").

2. Not knowing the specific D/E rating trigger for a Corrective Action Plan.

3. Not knowing the 3-year rolling planning horizon or the 2024 format update.

Numbers to Memorise

MEPC 76 — June 2021, in force 1 Jan 2023 · MARPOL Annex VI Reg 26 (SEEMP) & Reg 28 (CII) · MEPC.346(78) — 2022 Guidelines · MEPC.395(82) — 2024 updated Guidelines · ships ≥5,000 GT · 3-year plan horizon · CAP trigger: 1×E or 3 consecutive D · SoC issued annually.

Casualty Link

No direct casualty; commercial consequence is the operative link — ships with sustained E/D ratings face charterer refusal, commercial pressure, and reputational risk under increasing cargo-owner and financier scrutiny of carbon performance.

Examiner Chain

Nair → why Part III added → contents → what if ship gets an E rating → Corrective Action Plan → SoC renewal → PSC check → 2024 reporting update.

On My Vessel

Maersk container tonnage is well above the 5,000 GT threshold, so SEEMP Part III is mandatory fleet-wide. Fuel and performance data is reported to Maersk's shore performance management team; the CE's practical duty is accurate, timely fuel and engine data recording feeding that reporting chain, consistent with Maersk's stated decarbonisation roadmap and CII targets.
